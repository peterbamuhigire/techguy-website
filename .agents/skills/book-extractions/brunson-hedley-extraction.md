## OVERARCHING TESTING PHILOSOPHY

**Core principle:** Look at the whole funnel, not just one or two pages. A page converting at 0.76% will kill profits even if squeeze page converts at 38% and upsells convert at 25%.

**Testing mindset:** The numbers tell you exactly what is broken. Direct response marketing lets you look at each step and fix the problem rather than guess.

**Test each traffic source independently.** Warm traffic (your list) and cold traffic (banner ads, PPC) behave differently. A 3-step header wins with warm traffic but intimidates cold traffic.

**Model success, do not re-invent the wheel.** Find what is working in your market and model it before testing variations.

**What to test first (highest impact, fastest results):**
1. The offer itself — biggest lever of all
2. The headline
3. The call to action / order button timing
4. Price point
5. Video vs. text vs. layout

**Testing velocity:** Run tests until you have statistical significance. Brunson's team developed their "control" after 100+ tests across multiple markets. Start with 5 banner styles before narrowing down. Build a control, then try to beat it.

**The $9.45 → $16.50 DPL lesson:** Two words ("don't you agree") inserted as trial closes throughout a webinar increased Dollars Per Lead by 74.61%.

## CATEGORY: HEADLINES

### Test 1 — Specific vs. Round Numbers in Headlines
- **A:** "Add 1,000 people a day to your list"
- **B:** "Add 1,198 people a day to your list"
- **Winner:** B — specific number
- **Lift:** +33.56%
- **Principle:** Round numbers look like guesses. Specific numbers feel like verified data. Always use exact figures.
- **Page type:** Squeeze page / lead magnet
- **Rule:** Replace all round numbers in headlines with the specific real number from your data.

### Test 2 — "This Very Blog" vs. Generic Claim Headlines
- **A:** "Discover How You Can Grow Your Blog to 10,000+ Subscribers in 12 Months"
- **B:** "My Free PDF Reveals How to Get 10,000+ Blog Subscribers in 12 Months"
- **C:** "Discover How This Very Blog Grew to 10,000+ Subscribers in 12 Months"
- **Winner:** C — specificity to real event
- **Lifts:** C: 58% over A, B: 34% over A
- **Principle:** Concrete past events are perceived as true. Generic claims read as fabricated.
- **Rule:** Ground headlines in specific real events or data, not generic promises.

### Test 3 — Red vs. Blue Headlines
- **A:** Blue headline (matches site design)
- **B:** Red headline (clashes with design)
- **Winner:** Red
- **Lift:** 313% more revenue
- **Principle:** Red creates urgency and stops the eye, even when it looks ugly.
- **Rule:** Test red headlines on all sales pages. Aesthetics lose to conversion.

### Test 4 — Curiosity-Based vs. Feature-Focused Headlines (37Signals)
- **Winner:** Headline emphasising the 30-day trial and speed of sign-up
- **Lift:** 30% over control
- **Principle:** Benefits and ease of getting started outperform feature descriptions.

### Test 5 — Animated vs. Static Headlines
- **Results vary by product.** On pornography addiction product: static won by 28.52%. On email marketing product: animated won by 134%.
- **Rule:** Always test both. Never assume animated wins.

### Test 6 — Headline with Defocused/Transparent Image Background
- **A:** Control headline with human face next to it
- **B:** Headline alone (no image)
- **C:** Headline over light blue transparent image
- **D:** Headline over dark blue transparent image
- **Winners:** C (+230%), D (+112%), B (+7-20%)
- **Principle:** Human faces pull eye-gaze away from the headline. Background images that frame copy without competing with it lift conversions dramatically.
- **Rule:** Never place a human face directly next to a headline. Use faces that look toward the copy, not at the camera. Use soft/defocused background images for headline sections.

### Test 7 — Time Continuum Language
- **Concept:** Present tense is perceived as true. Past tense = expired. Future tense = unproven.
- **Example winning copy:** "Simple Email Tips Putting Money In Bank Accounts RIGHT NOW..."
- **Rule:** Write all headline and CTA copy in present tense. Eliminate future conditional language ("you will", "you'll be able to").

## CATEGORY: CTAs AND BUTTONS

### Test 8 — Red vs. Green Button
- **Winner:** Red
- **Lift:** +34%
- **Secondary test:** "Get Started Now" vs. "Signup For Free" — "Get Started Now" won by 7%
- **Principle:** Red creates urgency. "Get Started Now" implies less commitment than "Signup."
- **Rule:** Use red buttons. Use action-forward text like "Get Started Now," "Get Access," "Claim Your Copy."

### Test 9 — "It's Free" Two Magic Words
- **Test:** Adding the words "it's free" next to the button on Soocial.com
- **Winner:** With "it's free"
- **Lift:** +28%
- **Rule:** Wherever a free offer exists, add "it's free" near the button or CTA.

### Test 10 — Add to Cart Button ABOVE the Video
- **A:** Add to Cart button below the sales video (standard)
- **B:** Add to Cart button above the video
- **Winner:** B — above the video
- **Lift:** +38%
- **Principle:** Engaged viewers never scroll down. Placing the button above ensures maximum visibility.
- **Rule:** Test placing your primary CTA above the video for high-engagement video sales letters.

### Test 11 — Order Button Delay (Multiple Tests, Consistent Result)
- **Test A (pages 12-13):** Showing order form immediately vs. hiding until price reveal — **showing immediately won by 43-66% in EPC**
- **Test B (page 39):** No delay vs. delayed order form — **no delay won by 92%**
- **Test C (page 50):** No delay won by 30.36%
- **Test D (page 66):** No delay on upsell — **won by 188%**
- **Test E (page 73):** No delay vs. 11-minute delay — **no delay won by 50%**
- **Consistent winner:** Show order button immediately, every time.
- **Nuance (page 12):** People want to see the button early BUT still want the choice to click it themselves — so show the button but keep it as a button (not an auto-open form).
- **Rule:** Always show the order button immediately. Never delay it until price reveal.

### Test 12 — Copy Above Order Button
- **A:** "Limited Time Offer"
- **B:** "Get Cloud Blogging"
- **Winner:** B — action-forward copy above order button
- **Lift:** +39%
- **Rule:** Replace urgency-only copy above order buttons with benefit-action copy describing what they get.

### Test 13 — Big Yellow Button at Top of Long-Form Free Trial Page
- **A:** No button at top; button only appears after price reveal
- **B:** Big yellow CTA button added at top of long sales letter
- **Winner:** B
- **Lift:** +33%
- **Rule:** For free trial offers, place the CTA at the top of the page. Remove friction by making the offer visible immediately.

## CATEGORY: FORMS

### Test 14 — Mad Libs Style Forms
- **A:** Standard web form
- **B:** "Mad Libs" style (fill-in-the-blank sentence format)
- **Winner:** Mad Libs style
- **Lift:** 25-40% increase across 12+ sites tested
- **Principle:** Conversational, interactive form fields feel less intimidating and more engaging.
- **Rule:** Test Mad Libs-style opt-in forms, especially for lead generation.

### Test 15 — HTTPS on Squeeze Pages
- **A:** HTTP squeeze page
- **B:** HTTPS secure squeeze page
- **Winner:** HTTPS
- **Lift:** +29.26%
- **Rule:** Make every page in every funnel HTTPS. Implement immediately; it is a low-effort, high-return change.

### Test 16 — Two-Step Order Form
- **Benefits found:** Higher conversion from step 1 (lead capture), ability to email non-buyers, ability to call partial leads
- **Call centre follow-up:** Converted 10%+ of partial leads into sales by answering one or two questions
- **Rule:** Use two-step order forms. Collect name/phone/address first, payment details second. Follow up with everyone who completes step 1 but not step 2.

### Test 17 — Micro-Commitment Squeeze Page (Daegan Smith Style)
- **A:** Standard opt-in form (ask for email immediately)
- **B:** Micro-commitment: ask "How did you hear about us?" first, then ask for email
- **Winner:** B
- **Lift:** +95% in opt-ins, nearly all sales came from B version
- **Rule:** Start with a small commitment (a survey question) before asking for email. This is the Brunson/Daegan "control" squeeze page format.

### Test 18 — "Other" as Fourth Option in Mini-Survey
- **A:** Four specific options (TV, radio, friend, search)
- **B:** Three specific options + "Other"
- **Winner:** B with "Other"
- **Lift:** +206% in EPC
- **Principle:** If visitors can't answer the survey correctly, they drop off. "Other" gives everyone a valid path forward.
- **Rule:** Always include "Other" as an option in micro-survey squeeze pages.

### Test 19 — Facebook Connect Only vs. Facebook + Email
- **A:** Facebook Connect button only
- **B:** Facebook Connect + email opt-in option
- **Winner:** B
- **Lift:** +65.27% in registrations, +49.92% in upsell sales
- **Rule:** Never use Facebook Connect as the only sign-up option. Always offer an email alternative.

### Test 20 — Order Form Placement: New Page vs. On-Page Inline
- **A:** Button takes visitor to new order page
- **B:** Order form opens inline on same page as sales video
- **Winner:** Inline
- **Lift:** +75% in order form conversions (1.4% → 2.5%)
- **Rule:** Use inline order forms on the sales page whenever technically possible.

### Test 21 — Order Form: Credit Card Info on Left vs. Right
- **Winner:** Left side
- **Principle:** May relate to natural reading direction and proximity to content.
- **Rule:** Place credit card fields on the left side of the order form.

## CATEGORY: PRICING

### Test 22 — Lower Price vs. Higher Price
- **Test:** $47 vs. $97 for pornography addiction product
- **Winner:** $47
- **Result:** 4x more sales at $47 = nearly double the revenue ($47 × 4 = $188 vs. $97 × 1 = $97)
- **Lift:** 616% more conversions at lower price
- **Principle:** Never assume higher price = more profit. Test both.
- **Rule:** Test pricing systematically. Lower prices can multiply unit volume enough to exceed higher-price revenue.

### Test 23 — Payment Plans for Facebook Traffic
- **A:** $99 one payment (50% off $199)
- **B:** Three payments of $33 each
- **Winner:** Three payments
- **Lift:** +75.6%
- **Principle:** Facebook cold traffic has not pre-committed to spending money. Breaking the price into smaller instalments dramatically lowers the perceived barrier.
- **Rule:** For cold social traffic, test payment plan pricing as the primary offer rather than as an alternative.

### Test 24 — Split Pay Option Added to High-Ticket Offers
- **$497 vs. two payments of $248.50:** Adding the 2-pay option tripled conversion rate (+217%)
- **$197 with split pay, full video controls:** Increased sales 134% over best non-pay-plan version
- **Rule:** For any price above $100, test offering a 2 or 3-payment plan. Pre-select the full payment as the "preferred" option to anchor the choice.

### Test 25 — "3x$37" vs. Other Price Points (DC Fawcett)
- **Tested:** $37, $47, $77, $97, 2x$37, 2x$47, 2x$97, 3x$37, 3x$47
- **Winner:** 3x$37 — $16.15 EPC, $2,664 total revenue
- **Rule:** Three-part payment plans at lower per-payment amounts often outperform lump-sum equivalents.

### Test 26 — Disappearing Decoy / Too Many Options
- **Three-bottle option ($129.95) removed:** +83% profit
- **Six-bottle option ($229.95) removed:** -15% profit
- **Principle:** The six-bottle decoy pushed buyers toward the one-bottle. The three-bottle split buyers between options and lowered average order value. One high-priced anchor + primary offer = optimal.
- **Rule:** Keep 1-2 options on pricing pages. Use one high-price decoy to make the primary offer look reasonable. Remove middle options that split the decision.

### Test 27 — "Dollars Off" vs. "Percentage Off"
- **Winner:** Dollar amounts ("$10 off") beat percentage amounts ("10% off")
- **Lift:** 22% better conversion
- **Rule:** Always express discounts in dollar amounts, not percentages.

## CATEGORY: VIDEO

### Test 35 — Male vs. Female Voice in Sales Video
- **Winner:** Male voice
- **Principle:** In the weight loss niche at the time, masculine voice consistently outconverted female voice.
- **Rule:** Test voice gender, especially in health and finance niches.

### Test 36 — Video Type: Animation vs. Hand-Sketched vs. PowerPoint
- **Winner in this test:** PowerPoint (without music for female, with music for male)
- **Brunson's advice:** Start with PowerPoint, get it converting well, then hire an artist to try to beat the control.

### Test 37 — Video Size: 720x400 vs. 640x360
- **Winner:** 720x400
- **Lift:** +31%
- **Rule:** Use 720-pixel wide video as default starting point.

### Test 38 — FAQ Section at End of Sales Video

- **Winner:** B
- **Lift:** Doubled conversion rate
- **Principle:** Answering objections in the video keeps viewers in a buying state. They do not leave to contact support, which breaks momentum.
- **Rule:** Review your support tickets, find the top 5 questions, answer them positively at the end of every sales video after the final CTA.

### Test 39 — Video OTO: Lesson Preview + Timed Order Menu
- **A:** Long copy OTO page
- **B:** OTO with actual product lesson video, order menu appearing after a few minutes of watching
- **Winner:** B
- **Lift:** +33%

### Test 40 — Webinar "Watch Now" Option
- **Adding "Watch Replay of Yesterday's Event Now" as top default option:** 25% of registrants chose it, generating 50% of all sales
- **Rule:** Always provide an immediate "watch now" option on webinar registration pages. Move it to the top default option.

## CATEGORY: LAYOUT AND DESIGN

### Test 41 — Simplified Design vs. Too Many Options
- **A:** 25 above-the-fold clickable options
- **B:** 5 options only
- **Winner:** Simplified
- **Lift:** +21% average across two tests
- **Principle:** A confused mind always says no.
- **Rule:** Reduce choices on any page to the absolute minimum needed.

### Test 42 — Removing Extra Content Blocks
- **A:** Sales page with symptom block + ingredient block below the video
- **B:** Same page with those blocks removed
- **Winner:** Stripped version
- **Lift:** +80%
- **Principle:** Over-selling creates doubt. Some audiences just need the basic information to decide.
- **Rule:** Remove any section that does not directly advance the buying decision. Test stripped-down versions of complex pages.

### Test 43 — Light Box Pop-Up for Email Opt-In
- **Winner:** With light box pop-up
- **Lift:** +67%
- **Best practices:** Appears after 10 seconds or scroll depth; matches site design; includes product image; shows download count; never appears for existing subscribers; never used on Google landing pages.
- **Rule:** Implement timed light box pop-ups on content pages (not landing pages). Configure them to trigger on engaged visitors, not immediate entry.

### Test 44 — "Hello Bar" on Blog
- **Result:** 3.45% of all blog visitors clicked the Hello Bar, 2.76% registered for the webinar → $276 extra per 1,000 visitors
- **Plus:** 6.13% lift in regular opt-ins (no cannibalisation)
- **Rule:** Add a Hello Bar to blog and content pages pointing to your primary lead magnet or webinar.

### Test 45 — Mobile Responsive Version
- **A:** Regular desktop version
- **B:** Mobile responsive version
- **Surprising result:** Mobile responsive version got +7% more opt-ins but -83% fewer purchases
- **Principle:** Responsiveness may change layout, fold, and presentation in ways that kill the sales sequence.
- **Rule:** Test your mobile experience thoroughly before deploying. Do not assume responsive = better. A page that works on mobile without responsive CSS may outperform a poorly adapted responsive design.

### Test 46 — Trust Guard Logos on Webinar Registration Page
- **Result:** Adding Trust Guard logos decreased conversions by 13-14%
- **Principle:** Security badges on non-payment pages create distraction, not trust.
- **Rule:** Trust seals belong on order forms and checkout pages, not opt-in or webinar registration pages.

### Test 47 — CTA Button on Right Side of Video vs. Below
- **A:** Add to Cart on right side of video
- **B:** Add to Cart below video
- **Winner:** B (below)
- **Principle:** Right-side CTA placement "bombed completely" — Brunson estimates losing money on two prior launches using this layout.
- **Rule:** Place CTA buttons below video, not to the side.

### Test 48 — The "Ugly Blue Version"
- **A:** Expensive, well-designed webinar registration page
- **B:** Simple "ugly blue" text-heavy version
- **Winner:** Ugly blue version — by 11%
- **Short copy won within ugly version:** +26% more opt-ins vs. long copy
- **Rule:** Simple, plain, ugly pages often outperform expensive designed ones. Test your ugly version. For free webinar/lead magnet pages, keep copy minimal.

### Test 49 — Three-Step Header for Cold vs. Warm Traffic
- **Warm traffic (own list):** Three-step header increases conversions
- **Cold traffic (banners/PPC):** Three-step header decreases conversions by ~10%
- **Rule:** Test traffic-source-specific landing pages. Remove complexity from cold traffic pages.

## CATEGORY: BACKEND / TRAFFIC TESTS

### Test 66 — Phone Order Option / Exit Pop for Phone Orders
- **Adding phone number to header:** Immediate phone sales began
- **Exit pop offering discount:** Near zero response
- **Exit pop asking "Are you nervous to order online? Call us":** +65% lift in phone sales
- **Rule:** For older demographics, add a phone option in three places: page header, exit pop (anxiety-framed), and email follow-up sequence.

### Test 67 — The "Toilet Letter" — Inbound vs. Outbound Leads
- **Outbound call (you call them):** $150 DPL
- **Inbound call (they call you):** $450 DPL — 3x more valuable
- **Method:** Send a direct mail letter offering a free CD. They call a number to claim it. Your sales team closes while they have them on the phone.
- **Rule:** Create mechanisms for buyers to call you. Inbound calls convert 3x better than outbound calls. Use "toilet letters" or similar to trigger inbound calls.

### Test 68 — Call Centre Timing
- **Sweet spot for calling buyers:** Days 14-28 after purchase
- **Rule:** Do not call too early (product not received) or too late (forgotten you). Call between 14-28 days.

### Test 69 — Webinar Questions Slide
- **Adding a slide inviting viewers to leave their credit card on voicemail to lock in a position:**
- **Lift:** +25% profit
- **Rule:** In webinars, create a mechanism for fence-sitters to "lock in" by leaving contact details, even before they are ready to buy.

### Test 70 — Facebook Newsfeed Ads vs. Sidebar Ads
- **CPA with sidebar ads:** ~$300
- **CPA with newsfeed ads:** Below $100 (66% reduction)
- **CTR advantage:** Newsfeed: 1.451% vs. sidebar: 0.110% (+1,219%)
- **Rule:** Run Facebook ads in the newsfeed, not the right column. Set up newsfeed campaigns separately from sidebar campaigns.

### Test 71 — Facebook Fanpage Tab vs. External URL
- **A:** Ad sends traffic to external landing page — $0.44 CPC, 0.072% CTR, CPA $10.63
- **B:** Ad sends traffic to Facebook fanpage tab (iframe redirect) — $0.17 CPC, 0.792% CTR, CPA $2.87
- **Lift:** +1,028% CTR, 73% lower CPA
- **Rule:** Send Facebook ad traffic to a fanpage tab (using Woobox), not an external URL.

### Test 72 — Ad Frequency Caps
- **No frequency cap:** 1.27% conversion, 0.01% CTR
- **2 per 24 hours cap:** 2.13% conversion, 0.05% CTR
- **Lift:** +68% conversion
- **Rule:** Cap ad frequency at 2 impressions per 24 hours per person.

### Test 73 — Desktop-Only vs. All Devices (Facebook Ads)
- **Targeting all devices:** $6.11 cost per lead
- **Targeting desktop only:** $3.62 cost per lead
- **Plus:** 5% higher conversion rate on desktop
- **Rule:** Exclude mobile from Facebook ad campaigns unless the offer is specifically mobile-optimised.

### Test 74 — CPM vs. CPC Bidding on Facebook
- **CPM bidding:** 500% lower cost per lead than CPC
- **Rule:** Test CPM bidding. Even with lower CTR, CPM often delivers cheaper leads.

### Test 75 — Fancy vs. Basic Banner Ads
- **Fancy design:** 0.11% CTR
- **Plain/basic design:** 0.34% CTR
- **Lift:** +209%
- **Principle:** Plain ads that look like editorial content bypass ad blindness. Fancy ads are instantly recognised as advertisements.
- **Rule:** Use plain, editorial-looking banner ads. Make them look like featured articles, not advertisements.

### Test 76 — Black vs. White Banner Background
- **White background:** 0.34% CTR
- **Black background:** 0.48% CTR
- **Lift:** +41%
- **Rule:** Test dark/black background banners. They stand out on pages and do not look like typical ads.

### Test 77 — Story-Based vs. Feature-Based Ad Copy
- **Ad A (Feature):** "Easier Than MLM? Forget about downlines..." — 0.038% CTR, $0.81 CPC
- **Ad B (Story):** "MLM'ers Hate Him: His simple trick with postcards beats MLM methods. See proof..." — 0.079% CTR, $0.40 CPC
- **Winner:** B — 108% more clicks, 51% lower cost
- **Principle:** Stories and curiosity gaps outperform feature lists. "See proof" creates a compelling reason to click.
- **Rule:** Write ad copy as a story hook with curiosity gap + promised revelation ("see proof," "see how," "watch what happens").

### Test 78 — Viral Coupon Posts with Funny Images on Facebook
- **Basic coupon post:** 2 likes, 0 shares
- **Same coupon + funny image:** 350 likes, 146 shares
- **Revenue lift:** +20% across the board
- **Rule:** Attach humorous, engaging images to any Facebook promotional post.

### Test 79 — Email Subject Lines: Blatant Pitch vs. Curiosity
- **"Keep Calm and Practice Yoga?" (curiosity):** 13.4% open rate, $4,125 revenue
- **"Bolster Special: Buy One Get One Free!" (blatant pitch):** 12.5% open rate, $9,029 revenue
- **Winner:** Blatant pitch by 119% in revenue despite 7% fewer opens
- **Principle:** Curiosity generates opens but not buyers. Clear offers attract people ready to buy.
- **Rule:** Test direct, offer-first subject lines against curiosity-based ones. For promotional emails, blatant pitches often convert better despite lower open rates.

### Test 80 — Command vs. Statement CTAs
- **"I'm on Twitter.":** 4.70% CTR
- **"Follow me on twitter.":** 7.31% (+55%)
- **"You should follow me on twitter.":** 10.09% (+38%)
- **"You should follow me on twitter here.":** 12.81% (+27%)
- **Combined lift from statement to full command:** +173%
- **Rule:** Use direct commands + the word "you" + the word "here" for all link CTAs.

### Test 81 — Email Sender Name: Company vs. Personal Name
- **"HubSpot" sender:** 0.73% CTR, 6.57% open rate
- **"Maggie Georgieva, HubSpot" sender:** 0.96% CTR, 7.1% open rate
- **Lift:** +32% CTR, +8% open rate
- **Rule:** Send emails from a personal name (e.g. "Peter from CompanyName"), not a brand name.

### Test 82 — Images vs. Text in Emails
- **75% of email clients do not load images**
- **Image-heavy emails:** +20% unsubscribe rate, +5% spam rate, -15% revenue
- **Rule:** Use text-dominant emails. Images that do not load deliver zero message and harm sender reputation.

### Test 83 — Email Value by Social Network Connection
- **Email connected to LinkedIn:** $75/year value
- **Email connected to Facebook:** $72/year value
- **Email connected to Google+:** $52/year value
- **Email connected to Twitter:** $38/year value
- **Email with no social connection:** ~$1/year (effectively throwaway)
- **Rule:** Social-connected emails are 72-80x more valuable. Design opt-in flows to capture social authentication where possible.

### Test 84 — Classified Ad Headline: Condition vs. Symptom
- **"NEUROPATHY TREATMENT?!?"** (condition): 11 trials, 1 upsell
- **"SHARP PAIN?!?"** (symptom): 1 trial, 0 upsells
- **"Diabetic Pains?!?"** (general): 0 trials, 0 upsells
- **Rule:** In direct response ads, name the condition/diagnosis the prospect already self-identifies with, not just the symptom they feel.

### Test 85 — Tearsheet vs. Postcard (Direct Mail)
- **Postcard:** 20 trial signups
- **Tearsheet (newspaper article format):** 30 trial signups (+50%)
- **Rule:** Use tear sheet direct mail format over postcard format. The newspaper article appearance creates credibility and bypasses the "junk mail" filter.

### Test 86 — Specific vs. Generic Facebook Ads
- **Generic ad:** 0.24% CTR, $0.29 CPC, 0% ROI, CPA 71% higher
- **Specific ad (mentions a real person + their result):** 0.24% CTR, $0.26 CPC, 200% ROI
- **Rule:** Be specific in ad copy. Name a real outcome from a real person. Curiosity alone does not convert — pair it with specificity and a stated benefit.

## TRIAL CLOSES (The Two Words That Added 74.61% to DPL)

- "Do you see the power of what I'm showing you?"
- "Isn't that awesome?"

**Rule:** Write 20-30 trial closes on note cards. Post them at your desk during webinars. Insert minimum 4-5 after every testimonial. Measure DPL before and after implementation.

## STEP 1: NICHE SELECTION

### The Niche Scoring System
Score your niche on these criteria. Highest-scoring niche wins.

**Criterion 1 — Search Volume (3 points):**
At least 1,000 exact-match searches per month for your target keyword (use Google Keyword Planner with [brackets] for exact match). If fewer than 1,000, find a different keyword or choose another niche.

**Criterion 2 — Backlink Competition (2 points):**
Average backlinks to the top 5 ranking pages must be fewer than 50. You are trying to beat individual pages, not entire domains — so backlinks to the specific page matter more than domain-level links.

**Criterion 3 — PageRank of Competitors (2 points):**
No more than half of the first-page results should have PageRank above 3. New sites are essentially guaranteed to be PR0-PR2. It is very difficult to beat PR4+ pages.

**Criterion 4 — Personal interest/knowledge (1 point):**
You will need to write or review content. Easier to stay engaged and create better content in a niche you know.

**Click-Through Rate by Position (why top 3 matters):**
- Position 1: 37% CTR
- Position 2: 12.5% CTR
- Positions 4+: Essentially irrelevant
- Page 2+: Less than 1% of all searches
- **Implication:** Targeting rankable keywords where you can reach the top 3 is critical. Being on page 2 is commercially worthless.

### Niche Identification Methods
1. Browse ClickBank.com, JVZoo.com, Amazon.com for products that already sell
2. Check Google Trends for trending products — then find sub-niches within those trends
3. Strategy: Take a popular product and target accessories, guides, or comparisons (e.g. instead of "Moto X phone" target "Moto X accessories")
