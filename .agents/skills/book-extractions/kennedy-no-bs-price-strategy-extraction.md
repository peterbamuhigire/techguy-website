# Book Extraction: Dan S. Kennedy & Jason Marrs — No B.S. Price Strategy

**Source:** Kennedy, Dan S. and Marrs, Jason. *No B.S. Price Strategy: The Ultimate No Holds Barred, Kick Butt, Take No Prisoners Guide to Profits, Power, and Prosperity* (Entrepreneur Press, 2011). 248 pages.

**Why this matters:** Price is the single highest-leverage decision in any business. Kennedy and Marrs argue — and demonstrate with before/after case evidence including Kennedy's own $100k-on-the-table cheque story (+225% price lift, $7M cumulative over 5 years) — that most business owners materially under-price. This book is the canonical No-B.S. treatment of pricing as a strategic system, not a formula.

> "Your prosperity begins with your price strategy. Price = Profit = Power = Prosperity." — Kennedy

---

## 1. THE 9 ULTIMATE PRICE AND FEE FAILURES

Kennedy's opening inventory — any one of these is enough to suppress profit permanently:

1. Pricing by textbook formulas, industry norms, or other "standard" means
2. Excess concern about competitors' lower prices
3. Attracting customers who buy by price (wrong customers = permanent margin disease)
4. Pre-determined belief system about "what they'll pay"
5. Permitting apples-to-apples comparisons
6. Insufficient differentiation
7. Not offering premium price options (no ceiling = no signal)
8. Ignorance or stupidity about "business math"
9. Poor self-esteem / business-esteem; feelings of not deserving more

The root: **most price failure is attitudinal, not tactical.** The chain binding product to price is thicker in the owner's mind than in the customer's.

## 2. DISCOUNTING WITHOUT DAMAGE (Marrs)

Discounts are powerful but degrade price integrity. Core rules:

- **Every discount must have a reason.** Volume, prepay, seasonal, pre-order, behaviour-modification, charity-tied. Never "just because."
- **Discounts reduce perceived product performance.** MRI/brain-scan studies: same wine rated higher when priced higher; resort guests paying full fare gave better reviews than discount guests.
- **Predictability is poison.** If you always run a half-price sale in July, customers sit on their hands in May and June.
- **Isolate the damage.** If discounts must be used for new-customer acquisition, designate *one* promotional product for that purpose — don't discount the whole catalogue.

### Two NEVERs

1. **Never give away a prestige offering for free.** There is nothing prestigious about free. Add value with warranties/bonuses/financing/events — don't cut price.
2. **Never be predictable with discounts.** Rotate timing, reason, and magnitude.

### Quid Pro Quo Discounting (Kennedy)

If you must discount, **always get something in exchange.** Options:

- Early registration / deadline compliance
- Referrals (2+ appointments with friends/neighbours)
- Multi-engagement booking (consecutive days, pre-book year)
- Bulk purchase
- Charitable donation attached (canned food to pantry → split-tested and outperformed the no-donation version)

### Money at a Discount

Frame your fee as the client *buying money at a discount*. A $188,000 copywriting fee that produces $2.5M over 5 years is not a fee — it is a 1,400% ROI discount on money.

## 3. THE NASTY CANCER OF FREE (Kennedy)

Cultural shift: from self-reliance → entitlement, from pay-as-you-go → go-now-pay-later → expect-it-free. FREE creates three damage vectors:

1. **Trains buyers to demand more free.** News media, music industry cautionary tales.
2. **Devalues everything adjacent.** Giving away what you previously charged $2,997 for ruins trust with past buyers and anchors the perceived value at zero.
3. **Attracts the wrong list.** Freebie-seekers rarely convert to premium buyers.

### How to Compete with Free (Marrs' playbook)

Jason's wife competes with free government-funded speech therapy at $180–$250/hour. The rules:

- **Surface the hidden cost of free.** Red tape, wasted time, loss of control, uncertain quality, permanent-record privacy loss. Make the hidden cost explicit in every ad.
- **Seek buyers willing to pay for value.** Deliberately repel price-sensitive prospects.
- **Educate the market (edu-marketing).** Give away foundational *information* that helps buyers decide, while charging premium for *delivery and results*.
- **Build immunity like a physical immune system** — diet (customer selection), exercise (offer strength), sleep (brand resilience), proximity (niche placement).

## 4. ANTIDOTES TO COMMODITY THINKING (Marrs)

> "There is no such thing as a commodity; there is only commodity thinking."

Case proof: **air is sold** ($60/hour oxygen bars, $444/hour POD devices). Underwear spans $1.33 (Hanes) to $448 (Carine Gilson silk & lace panties) — same cotton. Cat-poo coffee (Kopi Luwak) sells for $85/4oz. Jeans with diamond rivets: $4,000+.

**Anti-commodity levers:**

1. **Target individuality / story / emotion** — customers pay premium for the story, not the thing
2. **Brand ritual and naming** — Republic of Tea's Ministers/Citizens; name the method, the product, the offer
3. **Category-of-one positioning** — make direct comparison impossible
4. **"Radical individualism" segment** — 73% of consumers (Ogilvy 2010) prefer fewer higher-quality items over volume

**Never compete on price against a giant.** There's no glory in second-cheapest; the race-to-the-bottom is a suicide plan. Discounters are pyramid-bottom; the pyramid top is wide open for courageous marketers.

## 5. WHEN HIGHER PRICES SELL MORE

Counter-intuitive evidence Kennedy cites:
- B2B newsletter: $127 pulled 11% MORE than $97 (45% more revenue)
- Book: $24.95 one-payment sold equally vs. three-payments of $8.99 ($26.95 total)
- $49.95 Rolex = fraud signal; $4,999 Rolex = plausible

**Rule:** price signals quality. A too-low price triggers skepticism. "Reassuringly Expensive" is a deliberate positioning choice.

## 6. THE TRIANGLE OF PREEMINENCE (Dr. Barry Lycka)

Price power comes from being *the preeminent* provider in the niche. Three sides:

1. **Expertise and excellent service** — advanced procedures, best technology, trained staff, proprietary methods that defy comparison
2. **High-visibility marketing** — TV/radio/newspaper/internet saturation, event marketing (seminars, open-houses, celebrity guests)
3. **Community service and involvement** — charity, non-profit foundations, activist/thought-leader roles

Any professional or business owner applying the Triangle attracts clients for whom price is the least concern.

### Hawaiian Fisherman Method (bonus from Lycka)

> "I love fish but I hate fishin'. I cast 12 lines at sunrise so I never go hungry at sunset."

The #1 suppressor of premium pricing is **desperate need** — too few lines in the water. Always run more lead-flow than you need so you can say no to wrong-fit clients.

## 7. THE FIVE PROPOSITIONS (Kennedy's core framework)

A customer's reaction to price is coloured by the proposition. Five kinds to build and stack:

| # | Proposition | Core question | Levers |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Unique Selling Proposition (USP)** | Why choose you over any other option? | Positioning, category-of-one, benefit-forward copy |
| 2 | **Unique Value Proposition (UVP)** | Why is this worth far more than the price? | Bundle value vs separate, ROI proof, intangibles |
| 3 | **Irresistible Offer** | What would be impossible to say no to? | Discount + premiums + fast-action bonus + deadline penalty |
| 4 | **Unique Safety Proposition (USP)** | Why is this risk-free to try? | Guarantees, warranties, risk-reversal-plus (double-money-back), social/peer proof |
| 5 | **Unique Experience Proposition (UEP)** | What's the experience before, during, and after? | Theme, ritual, celebrity, events, surprise-and-delight |

**Hybrid build:** lead with the strongest one for your market; hook the others behind it.

The implicit second USP question: *"Why should I choose you regardless of price, be unconcerned about price, and never consider comparison shopping based on price?"* If your copy answers that, you have the foundation for aggressive pricing.

## 8. AVOIDING APPLES-TO-APPLES COMPARISONS

Two roadside apple vendors side-by-side will race each other to the bottom. Escape tactics:

- **Bundle** — three gifts delivered over three days (Calyx Flowers' $199.95 Valentine's bundle)
- **Change ONE little thing** (W. Clement Stone: "little hinges swing big doors") — delivery mechanism, packaging, guarantee, speed
- **Move to category-of-one** — proprietary process, branded method, named system

## 9. PLACE/PRICE STRATEGY — NICHE OVER GENERIC

> "The movement of a product from generic to niche automatically permits a price increase, with no change in manufacture cost."

Same audio programme sells at:
- Generic *Effective Time Management* — $39–$79
- Niche *…for In-Field Sales Professionals* — $179–$379 (400% lift)
- Sub-niche *…for Gardening Center & Hardware Store Industry* — 2× that again

**Every industry, profession, subculture, and sub-subculture supports niche pricing premium.** The deeper the identity attachment (horse-racing owners, pilots, breed-specific dog owners), the more price-elastic.

**Fraud objection: rejected.** If the product genuinely works for both audiences, presenting it specifically-for-each-audience is the most ethical path to getting the audience to actually use it and benefit.

## 10. PRINCIPLE OF ASSOCIATION (Chapter 15)

Price anchors to the company you keep. Allen Brothers steaks command premium because their catalogue features logos of top steakhouses as clients. By association, the home buyer gets "the same steaks Morton's serves." This:

- Differentiates from all other purveyors
- Makes their price feel like a bargain (vs. restaurant mark-up)
- Frees pricing from competitor comparison
- Gives customer pride of ownership
- Delivers a cocktail-party bragging story

**Practical:** add trust-by-association — logos of clients, media appearances, awards, venue affiliation, celebrity endorsers.

## 11. THE FIVE WAYS PRICES GET SET (and four are bad)

Chapter 17 inventory:

1. **WAG** (Wild-Ass Guess) — most common; no rationale
2. **Industry Norm** — following herd; keeps you at parity
3. **Client-Dictated** — letting customer set price; parasitic
4. **Cost-Plus** — add margin to cost; ignores value
5. **Target Return** — start from ROI goal, work backwards

Only #5 is defensible as the *starting point*. Even so, static prices are impossible — prices must evolve with objectives:

- **Sequential Skimming** — launch high, descend over time (Apple, early-adopter pricing)
- **Penetration Pricing** — launch low to capture share (use sparingly; hard to climb out of)

## 12. PRE-DETERMINATION (Chapter 11)

Before you quote a price, the customer has already pre-determined what they'll pay — based on exposure to competitors, category norms, and your signals so far. **Shape pre-determination upstream** via:

- Media placement (WSJ vs Pennysaver)
- Company you keep
- Office / studio / website aesthetic
- Staff demeanour and dress
- Proposal packaging

## 13. THE DEADLY TRAP OF BAD ECONOMICS (Chapter 16)

Under-pricing creates a cascade:
- No margin for good marketing → weak leadflow → desperate pricing → deeper discounting → race to bottom
- No margin for good staff → bad customer experience → complaints → churn → more acquisition cost
- No margin for R&D → no product improvement → commoditization

Bad price economics compound invisibly before they become visible.

## 14. STAFF SABOTAGE OF PRICE STRATEGY (Chapter 20)

Your own people will undercut you unless trained and enforced:

- Remove their authority to negotiate down
- Pay incentives tied to gross margin, not revenue
- Script every price-conversation
- "Get on board or get out" — a staff member who can't defend the price must go

## 15. A JURY OF FOUR, BUT ONLY ONE DECIDES (Chapter 21)

In most household / B2B decisions there are multiple voices (skeptic, penny-pincher, enthusiast, decider). The marketing must speak to each but close with the decider. Know which chair each stakeholder sits in and what they need to hear.

## 16. PRICE IN RECESSIONS (Chapter 22)

Wrong instinct: cut prices. Right instinct in most industries: **raise prices** — and upgrade value, trim wrong customers, market harder to right customers. Recessions clear the market of weak competitors, widening the premium-segment gap.

## SYNTHESIS — THE KENNEDY/MARRS PRICING PLAYBOOK

To move a business to premium pricing:

1. Audit against the 9 Failures; name yours
2. Select a niche/subculture for category-of-one positioning
3. Build the Triangle of Preeminence (expertise + visibility + community)
4. Cast more lines in the ocean than you need (no desperation → no discount drift)
5. Craft a hybrid proposition: USP + UVP + Irresistible Offer + Safety + Experience
6. Destroy apples-to-apples comparisons via bundling, naming, proprietary method
7. Set price from Target Return, not cost; raise to test ceiling; test higher
8. Never discount without quid pro quo
9. Protect prestige offerings from free / sale / discount
10. Train and enforce staff adherence to the price strategy
11. Use Principle of Association to anchor against premium peers
12. Raise — don't cut — in recessions

## Uganda / East Africa application notes

- **Premium positioning is more available in Uganda than most assume.** A 2025 UBOS study shows a growing affluent middle class in Kampala, Entebbe, Mbarara who pay premium for trusted providers (healthcare, education, financial services, food safety).
- **"Compete with free" rules directly apply to private-vs-government services** — private schools, private clinics, private security, private SACCOs. Surface the hidden cost of free (red tape, wait time, queue jumping, quality variance, privacy loss).
- **Niche/subculture pricing** — same coffee sold as "specialty Ugandan single-origin for coffee aficionados" commands 3–10× the price of generic export-grade. Apply to crafts, fashion, beverages, events, training.
- **Price in UGX** — the "reassuringly expensive" effect is amplified where customers equate very-low-UGX with counterfeit risk.
- **Association** — partner with known institutions (universities, hospitals, listed companies, NGOs, banks) and feature their logos on marketing materials.
- **Hawaiian Fisherman** — in Uganda's referral-driven market this means never relying on a single introducer; always run networking, speaking, content, and paid ads in parallel.
