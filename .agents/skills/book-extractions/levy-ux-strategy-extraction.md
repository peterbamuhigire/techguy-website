# UX Strategy — Jaime Levy — Extraction
**Source:** Jaime Levy, *UX Strategy: How to Devise Innovative Digital Products that People Want* (O'Reilly, 2015). Edited by Mary Treseler. Tier: **Full**.
**Coverage:** All 11 chapters. The premium-pricing angle: Levy's framework treats UX strategy as the *bridge between business strategy and design execution* — exactly where customers will pay more if you get it right, and where most failures originate.

---

## CORE FORMULA

> **UX Strategy = Business Strategy + Value Innovation + Validated User Research + Killer UX Design**

These are the **Four Tenets**. They are not phases — they are simultaneously-spinning plates. If any drops, the strategy fails. Every technique in the book serves one or more tenets.

---

## PART I — What UX Strategy Is (and Isn't)

### Definition
> "UX strategy is the process that should be started **first**, before the design or development of a digital product begins. It's the vision of a solution that needs to be validated with real potential customers to prove that it's desired in the marketplace."

UX design = creating the thing. UX strategy = the high-level plan to achieve business goals **under conditions of uncertainty**, before the thing exists.

### Four Common Misinterpretations (correct each one in client kickoffs)

1. **"UX strategy is a North Star."**
   - Reality: A North Star works for slow-moving enterprises. Innovative digital products in fast-moving markets need an *agile, iterative, variable* process with continuous feedback loops. You don't want a fixed star — you want a goal you steer toward through every pivot.

2. **"UX strategy is a strategic way to do UX design."**
   - Reality: Design = creating something. Strategy = the game plan *before* creating something. They are two different disciplines (like product strategist vs product designer).
   - Symptom this is happening: UX designers are working blind beyond a "business requirements document." Lean UX exists to fix this — UX gets a seat at the strategy table.

3. **"UX strategy is just product strategy."**
   - Reality: Product strategy is one product. UX strategy spans dozens of products, services, and platforms — Apple's iMac/iPod/iTunes/iCloud/Mac Store as one ecosystem; Adobe's desktop apps + cloud; Amazon's Prime/AWS/Kindle. UX strategy weaves all touch points into a seamless ecosystem.

4. **"UX strategy is closely tied to brand strategy."**
   - Reality: They overlap but aren't the same. **A poor UX can decrease brand value, but the reverse is rarely true.** Even the brandiest brand can't overcome a poor product UX. Google has a great brand; Google+, Buzz, Wave still failed because they had bad UX.
   - "Marketing can increase awareness for the product, but if the product sucks, that's what the buzz will be." — Brant Cooper & Patrick Vlaskovits

### The "Top 10 Not-UX Strategies" (anti-patterns; reject them in client briefs)
1. A killer idea for a new product
2. A laundry list of features
3. A thoroughly researched game plan with all scenarios considered, no need for customer feedback
4. A creative permutation of trending buzzwords (e.g., "peer-to-peer sharing economies")
5. A generic set of motivational statements ("Go Team Challenge Conquer")
6. An arrogant statement from an expert ("Our product sprung from the genius of Professor I.M. Awesome…")
7. A hypothesis with non-validated risky assumptions ("Well, all women do like pink")
8. A grandiose vision misaligned with company core values/capabilities
9. A vague affirmation that sounds like a Hallmark card ("You can achieve Social Lean Disruption")
10. The North Star

## PART II — Tenet 1: Business Strategy

### Two Paths to Competitive Advantage (Porter)
1. **Cost leadership** — lowest price in the industry. Walmart, Target. The traditional path.
2. **Differentiation** — new/unique aspect for which customers pay a premium because of *perceived value*. **This is where digital products win.**

> "Today, a UX differentiation is the digital-product game changer."

### UX Differentiations That Reinvented Markets
- **Twitter** — 140-char limit (later 280) reinvented news/updates. 20M+ tweets during Hurricane Sandy 2012.
- **Waze** — social traffic + GPS. Acquired by Google for $1.1B in 2013.
- **Facebook** — beat MySpace and Friendster *not by being cheaper* but by (a) differentiated UX and (b) mass adoption, then monetized via targeted ad data.

> "A good business model doesn't just define the revenue stream. Nor does it just rely on a ridiculous number of users adopting it."

### Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder & Pigneur — 9 building blocks)
Used to systematically think through the logic of how the company will make money. The bolded blocks are where UX strategy intersects business strategy:

1. **Customer segments** — Who are the customers? Behaviors, needs, goals?
2. **Value propositions** — What value (qualitative or quantitative) do we promise to deliver?
3. **Channels** — How do we reach the segment? Online or offline?
4. **Customer relationships** — How do we acquire and retain?
5. **Revenue streams** — How does the business earn revenue from the value prop? Customers pay? Other options?
6. **Key resources** — Strategic assets (content, capital, patents). Build or acquire?
7. **Key activities** — Optimizing an outdated business process? Creating a platform for transactions?
8. **Key partnerships** — Suppliers/partners needed to deliver?
9. **Cost structure** — Fixed vs variable. Cutting frills, or premium-cost?

Use the Canvas in the discovery phase. Stakeholder collaboration is required for partnerships and resources; user input is required for segments and value propositions.

### Building strategy ≠ formulating a perfect plan
> "It's about being able to research what's out there, analyze the opportunities, run structured experiments, fail, learn, and iterate until we devise something of value that people truly want."

For a **new product**: strategy is about getting enough product/market fit to raise financing OR grabbing enough market share.
For a **mature product**: strategy is building out the core value prop while preserving infrastructure.

## PART III — Tenet 2: Value Innovation

### Definition (Blue Ocean Strategy — Kim & Mauborgne)
> "Value innovation = the simultaneous pursuit of differentiation and low cost, creating a leap in value for both buyers and the company."

Companies pursue **both** differentiation **and** cost leadership at the same time → high-value low-cost products → blue ocean.

### Red Ocean vs Blue Ocean
- **Red ocean** — sea of competitors with similar products. Sharks, low prices, commoditization.
- **Blue ocean** — uncontested territory. Free for the taking. Built by breaking rules that aren't quite rules yet, or inventing the game.

### Disruptive vs Sustaining Innovation (Christensen)
- **Sustaining innovation** — incumbent does something better for existing customers.
- **Disruptive innovation** — initially serves simple applications at the bottom of a market, then *relentlessly moves up market*, displacing established competitors. Best customer of incumbent often *can't* use the disruptor at first; profit margins lower than incumbent will support.

### Airbnb's Value Innovation (Levy's canonical example)
Airbnb broke the Craig's List rules:
- Added user profiles
- Built verification of host and guest in the transaction
- Wrapped quality and trust into the UX (like Amazon, Yelp, eBay)
- Required users to change their mental model — strangers in your home, you in a stranger's home, both feeling good about it
- 6–18% commission on transaction
- Two-sided market: hosts and guests both have great experiences
- Combined a killer UX with a tantalizing value proposition in a blue ocean

### Lessons for value innovation
- Mental-model innovation IS the goal of UX strategy.
- "What's the point in spending time and energy crafting a digital product that isn't unique? Or, at the very least, a much better alternative to current solutions?"

## PART IV — Tenet 3: Validated User Research

### Why this tenet matters
> "Stakeholders are dreamers — they assume what is valuable to their customers instead of verifying it."

The Field of Dreams fallacy ("if we build it, they will come") is the #1 reason products fail. Levy's opening case study: a software engineer spent millions on a rehab-booking site over 18 months without a single conversion because his assumed customer segment was wrong.

### Validated research vs traditional research
- Traditional methods: ethnographic field studies, contextual inquiries, focus groups, diaries/journals, card sorting, eye-tracking, personas
- **Validated research is different**: it's the *process of confirming that a specific customer segment finds value in your product*. Direct feedback from real interaction. Goes beyond observing and establishing empathy.

### Lean Startup vocabulary (Eric Ries)
- **MVP (Minimum Viable Product)** — learn whether potential customers want your product by building only the *core features of the value proposition*. Not a "prototype to show investors."
- **Build-Measure-Learn** — the feedback loop. Validate decisions; ensure product vision is aligned with end-user needs.
- **Pivot** — if users don't like what they see, switch to a different customer segment OR a different problem your value prop can address.

### Intrapreneurship rule
> "If you happen to find yourself in this familiar position [no validation budget], that's the exact moment that you need to become *intrapreneurial*. Stand up and ask for the extra week or two. If you get a 'no' or are too afraid to ask, it's time to start working off-hours."

### The bottom line
> "Confronting your target customers is non-negotiable. We must learn as quickly as possible if the idea we are working on is stupid and worthless."

## PART V — Tenet 4: Killer UX Design

### What separates novice UX designers from killer UX designers
A killer UX designer:
1. **Works collaboratively from the inception** — designing structured experiments for validation. Decisions based on real evidence, not hunches.
2. **Determines key moments and features absolutely critical to the product** — uses storyboarding, feature poaching from competitors AND non-competitors.
3. **Learns everything about the existing market space** to identify exploitable UX opportunities → leap in value.
4. **Talks directly to potential users or existing power users** to discover and validate primary utility.
5. **Weaves the UX through all touch points — online and offline — frictionless.** Especially for products like Airbnb and Uber where the transaction begins online, fulfills offline, then loops back to interface for reviews.

> "You can't merely 'design think' your way to a killer UX design. It's only when the UX is informed by and affects the other three tenets that mental models are broken."

### Reference exemplars (the killer UX hall of fame)
- **Airbnb** — disrupted travel/housing
- **Uber** — disrupted taxis
- **Waze** — disrupted navigation
- **Tinder** — disrupted dating

These products got there through **experiments, failure, and iterations over months and sometimes years** — not static plans or two-week discovery phases.

## PART VI — Validating the Value Proposition (5-Step Process)

The book's chapter 3 prescribes a 5-step recipe for validating the value proposition. **Use this verbatim in any new-product engagement.**

### Step 1: Define your primary customer segment
- Be specific. Not "everybody who has been ripped off by bad treatment centers." Specific demographics, behaviors, context.
- The startup in chapter 1 failed because their assumed customer segment was wrong; they had built a B2C funnel for a B2B product.

### Step 2: Identify your customer segment's *biggest* problem
- One problem. Not a list. The biggest one.
- "What's keeping them up at night?" framing.

### Step 3: Create provisional personas based on your assumptions
- Provisional = explicitly hypothesis-state, to be validated/invalidated.
- Personas give stakeholders an empathetic sense of end-user needs/goals/motivations.
- Layout: name, demographics, goals, motivations, environment (social/technical/physical), pain points, stress points.

### Step 4: Conduct customer discovery to validate or invalidate
- **The problem interview** — separate from solution interview.
  - **Phase 1: Screener questions** (qualify the participant fits the persona). Example for a wedding-venue product: "Were you married within the last few years?" "Did you happen to get married here in Los Angeles?"
  - **Phase 2: The interview** (open-ended, behavioral): "How did you go about planning your wedding?" "What were the challenges?" "How did you overcome them? Did you compromise?" "Have you ever heard of/tried [closest existing competitor]?"
- The interview is about the *problem*, not your solution. You are not pitching.

### Step 5: Reassess your value proposition based on what you learned
- Iterate until you have product/market fit.
- "Continue to iterate until you have product/market fit" is explicit step-5 phrasing in the book.

## PART VII — Competitive Research (Chapter 4)

### Why
> "The Internet has been around for more than 20 years. Knowing what has actually worked or failed is crucial to identifying a competitive advantage."

You think you're creating a new market — *you almost never are*. Studying current solutions reveals trends, outdated mental models, design best practices, and customer-segment patterns.

### Types of competitors
- **Direct competitors** — same/very similar value proposition to your current/future customers. Your customers are alive *right now* and using a direct competitor's product.
- **Indirect competitors** — similar value prop to a *different* segment, OR your exact segment without the exact same value prop. Often: indirect competitor's *secondary* service is your *primary* value prop.

### Find your competitor list
- During customer discovery, users will name products
- Stakeholder interviews — clients, investors, product owners drop names
- Search keywords your customer would actually use ("Men's ecommerce site", "Personal stylist", "Top brands")
- Crunchbase for startup activity
- Google AdWords "more like this" keyword feature
- Niche media: blogs with "Top 10" / "Best of" articles
- **Target list size: 5 direct + 3 indirect minimum.** Mix established and new competitors.
- Don't stop on page 1 of search results. Look through 5 pages (top 50).

### Competitive Analysis Matrix (the spreadsheet attributes)

Each row = one competitor. Each column = an attribute. Use Google Sheets (free, cloud, shareable). Spend ~30 min per competitor on first pass.

**Columns to capture (in order):**

1. **URL of website / app store** — Apple AND Android URLs if multi-platform; if mobile/desktop differ greatly, give the competitor TWO rows.
2. **Username/password** — create one global throwaway login for the team. Never use real social logins. Buy paid apps; the few dollars are worth team learning.
3. **Purpose of site** — 1–2 sentence value prop description. Sources: About page, Crunchbase, app store first 2 lines, social profiles, online annual report.
4. **Year founded**
5. **Funding rounds** — competitors with funding have advantage; check Crunchbase.
6. **Revenue streams** — transaction fee, advertising, monthly fee, SaaS, data sales. If unclear, click "Advertise with Us"; check annual report.
7. **Monthly traffic** — Compete.com, Quantcast, Alexa, App Annie, AppFigures, Mopapp, Distimo. Triangulate multiple sources.
8. **# of SKUs / listings** (optional) — search a common term ("shoe", "shirt") and use the result count as proxy. For social, count user interactions.
9. **Primary categories** — copy the global navigation. For horizontal marketplaces (Amazon, eBay), list "most popular" / "best selling".
10. **Social networks** — which platforms are they actually using? Just-having-an-account doesn't count; look for active strategy.
11. **Content types** — text/photo/video balance, how organized, how scannable, detail-page depth.
12. **Personalization features** — favorites, watch lists, profiles, wish lists, custom interface, messaging, saved carts. Sticky vs not.
13. **Community / UGC features** — message boards, user posts, comments. Editorial vs user-generated balance.
14. **Competitive advantage** — top 3 differentiators per product. Concrete examples: "Zappos = simple returns + great browsing"; "Vine = touch-anywhere recording".
15. **Heuristic evaluation** — quick A–F grade. Questions:
    - Does the experience let the user accomplish their primary goal? Intuitive?
    - Are nav, layout, and visual design consistent?
    - Easy to find/search/browse?
    - Is feedback (errors, help) satisfactory?
16. **Customer reviews** — recurring complaints from app stores, Quora, etc.
17. **General notes**
18. **Questions/notes to team**
19. **Analysis** (filled in chapter 5)

### Why a UX person should run this (not a generic researcher)
- **Design enhancement** — UX person spots nuance (a slightly better shade, a content message like "Saving…saved")
- **Simplicity** — UX thinks in clicks-to-task; spots interaction-pattern improvements (Tinder's swipe is the classic case)
- **Cost** — same person researches and builds; faster
- **Tag team** — UX lead can mentor a junior researcher
- **UX innovation** — improving design is *almost always possible*

## PART VIII — Competitive Analysis (Chapter 5, 4-step)

The matrix is data. The analysis is intelligence. Steps:

### Step 1: Scan, skim, and color-code each column for highs and lows
- Systems thinking on the raw data
- End goal: distill into a brief or presentation that explains rationale behind recommendations

### Step 2: Create logical groupings for comparison
- Group competitors by similarity for cross-comparison

### Step 3: Analyze each competitor by benchmarking product
- For each competitor, evaluate against the same yardstick

### Step 4: Write the Competitive Analysis Findings Brief
- Distillation document with recommendations to product owners

## PART IX — Storyboarding Value Innovation (Chapter 6)

### Three-step storyboard framework

### Step 1: Create your list of panels
- Show the *most valuable moments* of the customer journey, not all features.
- Some moments are interface; some are offline.
- Show the entire experience regardless of duration (Uber's 20 minutes; Airbnb's 2 months).
- Example panels for "Airbnb for Weddings":
  1. Bride-to-be looking for a beautiful, affordable venue online
  2. Sees a result set of 2–3 listings
  3. Sees detail view of an awesome listing
  4. Selects a package (venue, food, flowers)
  5. Receives confirmation of venue/tour/submission
  6. Gets married on the beach

### Step 2: Decide on visual format
- Sketching, Photoshop mash-ups, screen grabs from other sites — whatever's fastest.
- Don't wireframe a storyboard.
- All panels same aspect ratio.
- Don't design new UI yet — borrow.

### Step 3: Lay out your storyboard on a canvas; add captions below each panel
- Caption ≤ 2 lines, lowercase. "Less is more."
- Review: Does it flow? Concise? Easy to follow?

### Feature poaching
> "Pluck features, interaction patterns, and business-model ideas from other products and mash them up to create something new."
- Look beyond direct competitors — note what works in *non*-competitors.
- The classic case: Tinder's "swipe right/left" was a feature mash-up that inverted the dating-site mental model (only mutual interest unlocks messaging).

## PART X — Creating Prototypes for Experiments (Chapter 7)

### What an experiment is
> "An experiment is a test of a hypothesis. The goal is to discover whether your hypothesis is right or wrong based on measurable results."
- All experiments test a *variable* (any item/factor/condition you can control or change).
- Look for cause-and-effect over a finite time.

### The new rules (after Lean Startup hit)
- No more big-bang 1.0 launches. Plan small incremental pre-releases (MVPs) that articulate different aspects of the UX.
- No more isolation + handoff. Continuous collaboration with stakeholders, devs, designers.
- No more crossing fingers. Insist on validation experiments along the way.

### Two popular experiment types

#### 1. Online campaigns
Drive traffic to a landing page. Measure click-through, signups, conversions. Tools:
- Targeted ad campaigns (Facebook, Google)
- Explainer videos (Drew Houston's 3-minute Dropbox video → 75,000 signups overnight)
- Crowdfunding (Kickstarter, Indiegogo)
- Squeeze pages with email capture

The advertisement / "concierge MVP" tests if users will give you an email address (= "currency"). Successful conversion = customer suspect provides personal info.

#### 2. Concierge MVP
Simulate the experience without backend. Manually facilitate transactions (phone, email, in-person).
- TradeYa: founder personally facilitated trades before automation existed
- Examples cited: Cars Direct, Zappos, Food on the Table, Airbnb, Dropbox

#### Wizard of Oz technique (J.F. Kelley, IBM 1983)
> "An experimental simulation in which participants are given the impression that they are interacting with a program that understands English as well as another human would. In fact, the program is limping along, only partly implemented."

Today: Amazon Mechanical Turk for outsourcing microtasks that mimic backend AI.

### The Solution Prototype — 5-screen framework
For when you don't have a product yet, only a storyboard.

The minimum number of screens necessary to demonstrate:
- The key experience of the interface
- The value proposition
- A sneak peek of the potential business model

**Generic 5-screen template:**
1. **Setup** — landing page or user dashboard
2. **Key UX 1** — 1–3 screens showing crucial interactions that demonstrate value innovation
3. **Key UX 2** — 1–3 screens showing additional crucial interactions
4. **Value proposition** — final result of a successful transaction
5. **Pricing strategy** (if applicable) — cost of the app, monthly fees, package costs, advertising examples

### Three steps to design-hacking a solution prototype
- **Step 1:** Write a simple list/outline of screens.
- **Step 2:** Mash up images. "Make your prototype seem real" — Mechanical Turk principle. Borrow from competitors and influencers (Apple Genius bar appointments, DIRECTV packages, Airbnb listings).
- **Step 3:** Paste screenshots into a presentation tool. **Google Presentation** is best for team collaboration; outputs to PDF; PDF on an iPad is highly effective for guerrilla user research because participants can swipe at their own pace.

### Tools for interactive prototypes
- **Adobe Acrobat** — interactive PDFs from imported images
- **Balsamiq Mock-Up** — rapid wireframing with hundreds of patterns
- **InVision** — high-fidelity comps, mobile sharing, version capture
- **UXPin** — Photoshop import; demo URL
- **Prott** — image stitching, hotspots, multi-device output

> "A prototype that you don't learn from is a waste of time."

## PART XI — Guerrilla User Research (Chapter 8)

### What it is
Cheap, fast, validated user research. Levy's preferred technique.

### The 5-step setup

#### Step 1: Determine the objectives
- What hypothesis are you testing?
- Concrete questions you must answer.

#### Step 2: Prepare the interview questions
- Mix screener (qualify) + behavioral (open-ended).

#### Step 3: Find venue(s) and map team logistics
- Café, public space, target-customer-frequented location.
- Team roles: event coordinator (greets, handles flow), interviewer (UX researcher), client/team-lead seated to observe.

#### Step 4: Advertise for participants
- Craigslist, Facebook ads, flyers in target locales (e.g., Alcoholics Anonymous meetings for treatment-center research — done by Levy's team)
- Offer modest incentive (gift card, coffee).

#### Step 5: Screen participants and schedule time slots
- Screener call/form filters out non-personas.
- Stagger arrivals; one no-show shouldn't waste a session.

The book details a session script: greet at door, walk to interview table, take coffee order, conduct ~30-45 min interview with prototype/MVP, debrief.

## PART XII — Designing for Conversion (Chapter 9)

### The Funnel Matrix tool
Replaces "designing screens" with "designing for conversion at each engagement level."

**Engagement levels (rows):**
- **Suspect** — anyone who might land on your product
- **Lead** — gave you contact info / first interaction
- **Prospect** — engaged with product
- **Customer** — converted (trade, purchase, signup)
- **Reference** — evangelizing on social channels

**Per level (columns):**
- **User process** — what the user is doing
- **Business task** — what the business wants done
- **Required functionality** — features/integrations needed for that level to work
- **Validated learnings** — measurable, answerable questions you must answer at this stage

**Discipline:** Each question at each level must be measurable. "How intrigued were users when they got to the landing page?" is NOT measurable. "How much traffic came from organic search vs social media?" IS.

The Funnel Matrix becomes a Build-Measure-Learn device deployed throughout the product lifecycle.

### Required functionality discipline
Each feature must be evaluated against:
- **User value** — how many people will actually use it / requested it?
- **Level of effort to build**
- **Business value** — gimmick or differentiator?

### Analytics tool categories
- **Enterprise reporting** — Google Analytics, Adobe Analytics. Need a developer/analytics specialist.
- **Dashboards / funnel conversion / A/B testing** — KISSmetrics, Optimizely, Mixpanel, Geckoboard, Totango, Chartbeat. Real-time, cohort segmentation, A/B and multivariate testing.
- **Email delivery / tracking** — SendGrid, MailChimp, iContact, Constant Contact. "Deliverability" = % into inbox.

### Landing page experiments (Suspect-stage validation when no MVP exists)

#### What a landing page is
A web page that is **not** the home page of your product. Designed to elicit **one** key action. Drives traffic from organic search, ads, social.

Other names: lead capture page, squeeze page, destination page.

#### The 5-step framework

1. **Define the experiment** — what part of the customer experience is being tested? How does it correspond to the value proposition? Is it value-prop testing, a pivot, or lead acquisition?

2. **Design/build your landing page** — must convey value prop **as fast as a 30-second commercial**. Distill product into something digestible via text, photos, video. Bring content/brand strategists in. Plan a Thank You page with next steps; consider a phone number for nervous users.

3. **Design/build secondary pages (variants)** — A/B test value props, features, copy, UI patterns.

4. **Run a controlled online campaign** — typically <1 week. Boundaries:
   - Initial budget (e.g., $500)
   - Keywords
   - Ad copy strong enough to drive suspects to landing page
   - Customer demographics (education, city, age)
   - Start date

5. **Measure metrics for validated learnings** — track click-through, conversion, conversion cost, social-sharing rate.

#### Tools for landing pages
- **Unbounce** — robust A/B variant runner; cited extensively in the book.
- Choose tools that allow plug-and-play widgets, custom domain assignment, and conversion tracking.

#### Real numbers from the book
TradeYa Facebook ad campaign:
- $2,000 spent
- Sent to "millions"
- 6,700 clicked → landing page
- 5,000 entered the contest (= 5,000 exposed to the value prop) = **74% conversion**
- $0.33 per click; $0.41 per email captured
- 5%+ of contest entrants started sharing on social → became *references*

## PART XIII — Strategists in the Wild (Chapter 10) — Selected Wisdom

### Holly North (London experience strategist)
- Prefers "experience strategy" to "user experience strategy" — UX strategy shouldn't be set apart from business or product strategy.
- "Strategy comes from this collective vision of the business and its customers."
- Talk to unexpected stakeholders: office manager, mailroom guy, sales assistant.
- Her go-to tool: **Customer Experience Map** (similar to user journey).
  - Phases (left → right): Discover → Evaluate → Buy → Use → Advocate (varies)
  - Per phase: goals, motivations, trigger to next phase, questions users ask, activities, satisfaction rating 1–5, quotes
  - Layered with a *Service Map* (business perspective) over the *Customer Experience Map* (customer perspective)
- Key skills: emotional intelligence, critical thinking, fact-based decisions, listening to disagreement, owning decisions.

### Universal qualities of UX strategists
- Critical thinking — disciplined, clear, rational, open-minded, evidence-informed
- Comfort with risk and failure
- Empathy with stakeholders' motivations, not just users'
- Ability to ask "why" repeatedly without satisfaction
