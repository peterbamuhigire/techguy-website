# BOOK 1: How to Become a Social Media Manager
**Author:** John Johnson (2021)

---

## Core Thesis

Social media management is a multi-disciplinary professional role — not simply posting content. A social media manager is simultaneously a copywriter, photographer, graphic designer, video editor, metric analyst, data analyst, customer service rep, and project manager. The role requires systematic processes, hard skills, and business acumen. Done well, it creates measurable ROI for clients.

---

## Part 1: The Social Media Manager Role

### What a Social Media Manager Actually Does (26 Duties)

**Strategy Layer:**
1. Create and own the social media strategy — right platforms, right objectives
2. Define customer personas and help the team identify strategies toward targeted goals
3. Build budgets for social media operations
4. Plan social media campaigns — from strategy through to day-to-day execution
5. Recruit and manage the communications team for social media
6. Manage change and integrate social media into the organisation's philosophy
7. Leverage staff to build organic reach (counteracting declining organic reach)
8. Collaborate with other marketing divisions so all activities reinforce each other

**Implementation Layer:**
9. Measure outcomes — leads, reach, sales, engagement — and set KPIs
10. Manage social media campaigns and day-to-day events without micro-managing
11. Upload new content, react to comments, share material from influencers/fans/clients
12. Brand advocacy — find blogs, forums, and influencers to collaborate with
13. Build a network of brand ambassadors
14. Community outreach — participate in groups addressing business-relevant problems
15. Manage influencer outreach and partnerships
16. Engage in debates and respond to questions

**Content Creation Layer:**
17. Seed content into social networks — not just owned profiles
18. Write editorial content — minimum 100 words/day, 1,000 words/week, 3 blog posts/month
19. Create videos for TikTok, YouTube, and Instagram
20. Post and comment on relevant blogs
21. Maintain an editorial calendar and posting schedule

**Monitoring Layer:**
22. Establish benchmark metrics and assess outcomes
23. Analyse and test social media campaigns and tactics
24. Conduct effectiveness studies
25. Track social media trends and competitor activity
26. Monitor the brand in social spaces and understand the link between social and search traffic

## Part 2: Eight Role Specialisations Within Social Media Management

A social media manager may focus on one or more of these:

### 1. Copywriter
- Writes platform-specific content (Instagram captions casual, LinkedIn formal)
- Produces posts from 140 characters to 200+ word blog-style posts
- Retains brand voice while adapting to each platform
- Key skills: copywriting, brand voice, adaptability, placement, creativity

### 2. Digital/Multimedia Producer
- Creates photos and graphics to accompany posts
- Takes and curates Instagram photos and videos
- Creates Snapchat mobile-optimised stories
- Produces original visual content from scratch
- Key skills: design software, basic photography and videography, adaptability, creativity
- Data point: Tweets with platform-optimised images produce 55% more leads than text-only tweets

### 3. Social Media Metric Analyst
- Dives into both macro data (total reach, leads generated) and micro data (A/B content tests)
- Sets targets that are just out of reach — then finds a way to achieve them
- Runs experiments including post-frequency studies
- Key skills: data analysis, curiosity, scientific experimentation, presentation
- Note: Continually testing new tactics, content, and promotions is essential

### 4. News Commentator and Curator
- Monitors business news and social media patterns to identify opportunities
- Capitalises tactfully on news items, trends, and hashtags ("newsjacking")
- Acts as an extension of the PR team
- Key skills: content curation, hunger for information, efficient monitoring, fast decision-making, good judgement

### 5. Customer Service Representative
- Serves as the brand's spokesperson on social media
- Navigates multiple purchasing stages and emotional states
- Thoroughly knows the brand, products, and services
- Key skills: strong communication, adaptability, calm under pressure, problem-solving, product knowledge
- Data point: Over 45% of customers have reached out to a brand on social media

### 6. Community Manager and Facilitator
- Builds community where followers interact with each other (not just with the brand)
- Asks seed conversation questions, moderates, enforces community rules
- Key skills: resourcefulness, ability to connect people, stimulates discussion, firm but gentle

### 7. Funnel Marketing Manager
- Uses social media across the full funnel: top (content/reach), middle (lead nurturing), bottom (conversion)
- Stays in touch with sales and customer service departments
- Understands the sales and marketing funnel
- Key skills: funnel comprehension, content matching to funnel stages, basic sales skills, strong communication

### 8. Project Manager and Campaign Coordinator
- Manages requests from across the organisation for social sharing
- Decides which requests fit the brand and audience
- Coordinates with other agencies for campaign launches
- Key skills: strong organisation, strong communication, negotiation, data-based decision-making

## Part 5: Daily Routine of a Social Media Manager

A structured daily workflow:

1. **Identify and list essential tasks for the day** — prioritise what cannot be pushed
2. **Stay on top of daily trends** — monitor trending hashtags, blogs, keywords in real-time (trends can expire within minutes)
3. **Content creation** — blogs, videos, infographics, GIFs; always grounded in current trends
4. **Content curation** — actively seek third-party content to supplement original material
5. **Content scheduling** — post at optimal times using scheduling tools (ManageFlitter, Hootsuite, Buffer)
6. **Contact new followers** — reach new, targeted followers daily regardless of existing follower count; create lists by intent (influencers, contest, campaigns)
7. **Audience engagement** — respond to all comments, messages, and mentions; this builds loyal clients
8. **Brand monitoring** — track all mentions using Hootsuite automated keyword searches
9. **Competitor monitoring** — identify top 5-10 rivals; study their content types, tweet volumes, influencer relationships, and promotions daily
10. **Measure results** — evaluate social media output regularly (not just end of month)

## Part 6: 9 Steps to Start a Social Media Management Career/Business

1. **Ask yourself honestly if this is right for you** — Do you genuinely serve businesses through social media?
2. **Get foundation knowledge** — Learn relationship marketing; treat knowledge as a commodity
3. **Get hands-on experience** — Offer free work to local businesses to build a portfolio
4. **Build watertight personal social media** — Well-structured, daily updates, engaged, quality content (follower count matters less than quality)
5. **Become a LinkedIn expert** — LinkedIn lets you reach decision-makers who control marketing budgets; small operators can appear as the "go-to" expert even against large-budget rivals
6. **Network** — Attend business breakfasts, cocktail evenings, networking bars; first clients come from referrals
7. **Offer free social media "health checks"** — Use tools like Klout and Hubspot to score their social media; this opens doors for paid work
8. **Run local webinars/seminars** — Start with a free seminar on social media for business (30 people in a coffee shop is a great start); use seminars to demonstrate expertise, win clients, and upsell paid workshops (charge $150/delegate for half-day workshops)
9. **Partner with digital marketing agencies** — Offer services as white-label to SEO firms, media buyers, PR firms, web designers; these agencies have clients needing social media

## Part 8: Pricing and Packages

### Salary Benchmarks (2021 data)
- Full-time social media marketer (entry level): $42,000/year
- Mid-level (more experience): $58,000/year
- Senior professional (10+ years): $90,000+/year

### Freelance Hourly Rates
- **New (0-2 years experience):** $15-$80/hour
- **Experienced (3+ years):** $50-$100/hour
- **Expert (10+ years):** $120+/hour minimum

### Instagram Management Estimate (2021)
- Approximately $800/month for full account management (no ad spend included)
- This is the rate for a manager with at least 5 years of experience

### Pricing Models

**Hourly Rate:**
- Best for new freelancers building a client base
- Example: 10 hours/month × $30/hour = $300/client; 3 clients = $900/month
- Drawback: clients may demand unlimited revisions or try to maximise your time

**Monthly Retainer:**
- Provides predictable revenue; better for relationship-building over time
- Allows the manager to develop a strategy that fits their expertise
- Risk: clients may feel they "own" your time and increase workload
- Tip: agree scope clearly in writing before starting

**Per-Project Rate:**
- Best for new client setups, product launches, or one-off projects
- Estimate project weight, then set a package price
- Best for clients who will take over management themselves

### Package Structure (Starter / Essential / Advanced)

**Starter Package:**
- 2 social media profiles managed
- Content development included
- Customer engagement included
- Choose from: Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, YouTube, Snapchat, Instagram, or Yelp

**Essential Package:**
- 4 social media accounts managed
- Blog posts included
- Content creation included
- Customer interaction included

**Advanced Package:**
- 6 social media accounts managed
- Influencer marketing included
- Blog posts included
- Content creation included
- Customer interaction included

### Pricing Decision Process
1. Consult with the client first to understand what they want to accomplish
2. Determine the client's primary interests and what outcomes you've achieved for similar clients
3. Research comparable competitors who serve the same types of clients
4. Listen to how potential clients react to your prices — their responses reveal whether rates are too high or low
5. Focus on packages so new clients can self-select without needing a lengthy explanation

## Part 10: Common Mistakes in Social Media

1. **Opening accounts and abandoning them** — An inactive account is worse than no account; it signals to visitors the brand is unresponsive
2. **Talking about what matters to you, not what matters to the customer** — Posts about sales and promotions only fail; every post must deliver value the customer wants to share
3. **Failing to engage** — Social media requires two-way communication; not responding to comments or questions destroys trust
4. **Ignoring images** — Images are the most frequently viewed content on social media; always accompany posts with visual content
5. **Chasing follower count over engagement rate** — How much followers share your posts is a more significant indicator of success than how many followers you have
6. **Spreading across too many platforms** — Dilutes strategy; better to dominate 2-3 platforms than be mediocre on 8
7. **No strategy** — A social media strategy is a description of what you expect to do and what you intend to accomplish; without it, there is no measure of success or failure
8. **Tracking vanity metrics only** — Beyond follower counts, track leads, connection clicks, and impressions
9. **Not adapting content per platform** — Generic posts on all platforms look out of touch; each platform has its own voice, format, and audience
10. **Not staying on top of trends** — Trends can expire within minutes; real-time monitoring is essential

# BOOK 2: Powerful Social Media Marketing for Beginners
**Author/Publisher:** Sobia Publication (2022)

---

## Core Thesis

Building a successful social media presence for business is fundamentally about personal branding through three interconnected elements: **Purpose** (what you do and the value you bring), **Character** (how you present yourself — your tone, values, and personality), and **Content** (what you create and publish, as shaped by your purpose and character). The book is structured around a series of practical worksheets that guide the reader through defining each of these three elements, then building a channel strategy and posting cadence on top of them.

---

## Framework 1: The Three-Element Personal Brand (Purpose + Character + Content)

### Purpose
- Not about getting your name out there — it is about **controlling the narrative** around what you do
- Gives you guardrails: clarity about what you do so others can quickly understand and refer you
- Based on answering: "What are you known for?" and "What have you achieved that you're proud of?"
- Leads to a **Purpose Statement**: "We are [brand/name], which is [what you do], specifically [how you're different], and what we want to do for people is [impact]."
- Example: "Wit and Delight is a website designed for living well and creating a life well-lived."
- Purpose statements change over time as the brand evolves — revisit annually

### Character
- How you say things; your tone and voice
- The non-verbal dimension of communication — what people read between the lines
- The general impression of the type of person behind the account
- Defined through a character attribute worksheet: rank 8-10 attributes (e.g., creativity, integrity, community, freedom, meaningful work) in order of importance
- Character prevents misreading (e.g., sarcasm being read as arrogance)
- Goal: The online persona should match the in-person persona so there is no jarring transition when you meet someone who follows you

### Content
- Everything you create and publish, shaped by purpose and character
- Defined through three content dimensions: **profession** (your professional successes and attributes), **personality** (your interests and values), and **passions** (things that define you beyond work)
- The best content sits at the intersection of all three (Venn diagram model)
- Engaging content is an **open-ended question** to the reader — it makes them want to react, share, or start a conversation

---

## Framework 2: The 10 Rules of Social Media Character

1. **Be kind** — Kindness builds professional relationships that pay off over time; approach everything diplomatically
2. **Confidence ≠ cockiness** — Cockiness tells people you're amazing; confidence shows your work and lets it speak; always think about the person on the other end
3. **Speak naturally** — Write as you speak; fill the gap where intonation doesn't exist
4. **Be yourself** — Genuineness is an attribute everyone looks for; people can detect inauthenticity online
5. **Listen** — Engage with what others post; show you're reading and interested in the community
6. **Contribute to the conversation** — Share articles, resources, and ideas that help others; the more you give, the more referrals you receive
7. **Don't overshare** — Not everything needs to be shared; some mystique makes you more intriguing; good judgment about what to share shows respect for the audience's time
8. **Support others** — Get genuinely excited for others' successes; be generous; pay it forward
9. **Stay grounded** — Don't believe your own hype; consistency requires humility; great creators need space to create again
10. **Don't compromise your values** — If integrity is a core value, do not create controversial content just for clicks; stay true to what the character worksheet identified

---

## Framework 3: Content Strategy (Content Buckets Model)

**Content Buckets** are categories that give guardrails for what to post:
1. **Finished work** — The end product; completed projects
2. **Process** — Behind-the-scenes; how you created the work; case studies
3. **Inspiration** — Things that inspire your work; articles, images, ideas
4. **Community** — Local events; other people's work; neighbourhood/industry happenings
5. **Personal experience** — Anecdotes; humanity; struggles; mental health; making the brand human

**Application to channels:**
- Home base (blog/website): All buckets — long-form content for loyalists
- Instagram: Personal experiences, process, inspiration — visual gallery
- Twitter: Process, inspiration, community — soapbox/commentary
- Pinterest: Finished work, inspiration, process — viral discovery

---

## Framework 4: Channel Strategy (4-Ring Target)

Choose channels based on the purpose, not based on being everywhere. Structure as a target emanating outward:

**Ring 1 — Home Base:** Where all your content lives. Blog/website, Vimeo page, or portfolio site. Long-form; this is where people go to understand what you do. Role: establish expertise and archive.

**Ring 2 — Gallery/Amplifier:** Where you tell people about what is at home base. Instagram for visual industries. Role: distribute and share. "Look at this cool thing we just did — go to the website for more."

**Ring 3 — Voice/Soapbox:** Where you talk about things that interest you beyond your brand. Twitter. Role: commentary, personality, community building. Builds the human side of the brand.

**Ring 4 — Viral Discovery:** Where your content goes to be found by new people. Pinterest, Reddit. Role: organic reach to audiences who have never heard of you.

**Platform selection criteria:**
- Match platform to purpose (visual industries → Instagram/Pinterest; writers → Twitter/blog)
- Go where your skills are strongest — done is better than perfect
- Don't try to do everything at once — 2 well-executed platforms beat 8 mediocre ones

---

## Framework 5: Purpose Worksheet (Self-Discovery Process)

**Step 1 — Past Successes:** List 3-5 past successes. Can be professional or personal. Starting a social media account counts. Getting your first client counts. The goal is to recognise progress and build confidence.

**Step 2 — Contributions:** List what you've created that impacts more people than just you — mentoring, content advocacy, community building, process improvements. This reveals what type of work environment and business model fits you.

**Step 3 — Goals (3 timelines):**
- Short-term (1 year): Something attainable, within reach
- Mid-range (5 years): Something you can see the action plans for; requires significant effort
- Long-term (10 years): Can be as simple as "I want to be happy" or running a specific type of company; should scare you a little but excite you

**Step 4 — Purpose Statement:** "We/I am [who you are], which is [what you do, specifically how you're different], and what I want to do for people is [impact]."

---

## Framework 6: Posting Cadence Recommendations

**Blog/Website:**
- Minimum: 1 post/week
- Optimal: 5 posts/week (significantly increases traffic)

**Instagram:**
- Goal: Once/day at optimal times (check Insights for when followers are most active)
- Follower growth associated with 2-3 posts/day if content quality is maintained
- Caveat: Never post just for the sake of posting; all content must genuinely align with brand values

**Twitter:**
- Multiple times/day
- Wide variety: process, inspiration, community, commentary, pop culture
- Used as a real-time soapbox

**Pinterest:**
- Multiple times/day
- Best during project work (use as inspiration during lunch hour)
- Focused on inspiration, finished work, tips

---

## Framework 7: Audience Segmentation by Platform

Different audiences exist on different platforms — even for the same brand:

- **Blog/website audience:** Loyalists; consume all content; long-term followers; want deep, personal, long-form content
- **Instagram audience:** Visually driven; interested in how the brand intersects with real life; want process and inspiration
- **Twitter audience:** Voice-driven; want commentary, news, humour; interested in what is happening in culture and the world; smaller and more engaged
- **Pinterest audience:** Less loyal; broader age range; many have never heard of the brand; want tips, shareable solutions, inspiration; interested in "I found this great recipe" type of content

---

## Framework 8: Analytics-Driven Iteration

**Principle:** Post things, observe reactions, and use the data to make better decisions — not to chase follower growth but to align with your values while improving.

**What to watch:**
- When something goes viral, don't feel pressure to replicate it exactly — this breaks creators
- When you lose followers after a post, assess whether the content misaligned with your values — if it did, adjust; if it was intentional and aligned, the loss is acceptable
- Look at when you post more and what happens to engagement — use this to set optimal posting times
- Review analytics to identify which content formats outperform others (e.g., blog art vs. plain text)

**Iteration frequency:** Monthly formal reviews plus ongoing observation; revisit the purpose/character/content worksheets annually

---

## Framework 9: Personal Branding as Business Development

Key insights for freelancers and agencies:

- Your online presence has replaced the resume — employers and clients look at Instagram, Twitter, and LinkedIn before they look at a CV
- "Finding good people is still the hardest thing for employers" — a strong social media presence makes you easy to find and recommend
- Top-of-mind presence: Clients and collaborators remember and recommend people they follow on social media
- A clear purpose statement helps collaborators quickly understand and refer your business
- Doing too many things, or not being able to articulate clearly what you do, costs opportunities
- The process of documenting your work (case studies, process posts) keeps your portfolio current and generates inbound inquiries
- Social currency: People want to recommend people they follow online because the recommendation reflects on them

## Synthesis: Combined Framework for Web Agency Social Media Services

### What a Web Agency Offering Social Media Management Needs

**Before Taking on a Client:**
1. Conduct a free social media "health check" using tools like Klout and Hubspot — score their current presence and show them what is missing
2. Run a discovery consultation to understand their objectives: Is the goal trending posts? Brand awareness? Website traffic? Lead generation? Target audience exposure?
3. Identify which of the eight role specialisations the client most needs (copywriter, multimedia producer, analyst, curator, customer service, community manager, funnel manager, or campaign coordinator)
4. Match pricing model to client type and relationship: hourly for transactional, monthly retainer for ongoing, per-project for launches

**Client Onboarding Process:**
1. Define client's Purpose Statement for social media ("What are we doing on social media, for whom, and to what end?")
2. Define client's Character attributes (what 8-10 words describe how this brand should speak and be perceived?)
3. Map out Content Buckets (finished work, process, inspiration, community, experience — which matter most for this client?)
4. Select 2-4 channels using the 4-Ring Target model (home base → gallery → soapbox → discovery)
5. Assign each channel a clear role (no channel should overlap entirely with another)
6. Set a realistic posting cadence per channel
7. Identify audience segment per channel (who specifically reads each platform?)
8. Establish KPIs: go beyond vanity metrics; track leads, connection clicks, impressions, and engagement rate

- Weekly: Content creation, editorial calendar updates, community management
- Monthly: Analytics review, campaign performance reporting, strategy adjustment

## Agency Business Development: Getting First Clients

Proven tactics from Book 1 (the author's own experience):

1. **Free social media health check** — Use Klout/Hubspot tools; give a percentage score; send a written report identifying improvement areas; this creates an immediate "in" for paid work
2. **White-label partnerships** — Partner with SEO firms, web designers, PR agencies, media buyers; offer your social media services under their brand name ("you scratch an itch for us")
3. **Local business networking events** — Breakfast events, cocktail evenings, networking bars; position yourself as "the local social media manager"
4. **Free seminar on social media for local businesses** — Host in a coffee shop; 30 attendees is excellent for the first event; demonstrate knowledge; provide take-aways; convert attendees into clients
5. **LinkedIn thought leadership** — Join LinkedIn groups relevant to your target industry; participate genuinely; decision-makers are accessible here
6. **Case study documentation** — After every client success, document it as a case study; post it across your own social media channels
