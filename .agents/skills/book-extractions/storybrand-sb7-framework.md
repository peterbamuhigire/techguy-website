# StoryBrand SB7 Framework

## The Framework Overview

The SB7 BrandScript maps every brand message to a 7-element story arc. The customer is always the **hero**; the brand is the **guide**.

| # | Element | Story Role | Key Question |
|---|---------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | Character | Hero | What does the customer want? |
| 2 | Problem | Villain + conflict | What's stopping them? |
| 3 | Guide | Mentor | Why should they trust us? |
| 4 | Plan | The road map | What are the steps? |
| 5 | Call to Action | The invitation | What must they do? |
| 6 | Failure | The stakes | What's at risk if they do nothing? |
| 7 | Success | The resolution | What does winning look like? |
| + | Transformation | Character arc | Who do they become? |

---

## 1. Character — Define a Single Desire

The customer is the hero; the brand is the guide. Never position the brand as the hero.

**Define a single desire** — the one thing the customer wants that relates to your brand. Multiple desires confuse the message. One desire drives action.

**Six survival categories** customers care about:
1. **Financial** — save money, earn more, retire comfortably
2. **Time** — save time, reduce workload, simplify life
3. **Social** — gain status, be liked, belong to a group
4. **Relationships** — find love, connection, community, support
5. **Purpose** — have impact, leave a legacy, do meaningful work
6. **Generosity** — give to others, contribute to something larger

Choose the category your product most naturally resolves. State the desire in one sentence.

---

## 2. Problem — Three Levels + the Villain

Every customer problem has three layers. Brands that only address the external problem miss the real motivator.

**External Problem** — the tangible, surface-level problem ("my lawn is overgrown", "my website doesn't convert").

**Internal Problem** — the emotional frustration caused by the external problem ("I feel like I'm failing", "I feel invisible online"). This is the real motivator. Most purchase decisions are driven here, not at the external level. Copy that names the internal problem creates an instant "they understand me" response.

**Philosophical Problem** — the injustice or principle at stake ("hard-working business owners shouldn't have to lose money to bad marketing"). Copy that touches this level creates a movement, not just a sale.

**The Villain** — every conflict needs an antagonist. The villain is the root source of the problem (the broken system, the inefficient industry, the shady practice). Rules:
- Must be a root cause, not a symptom
- Must be relatable — customers must immediately recognise it
- Must be singular — one villain, not a list
- Must be real — no manufactured enemies

---

## 3. Guide — Empathy + Authority

The brand plays the guide: Yoda to Luke Skywalker, Haymitch to Katniss. The guide never steals the hero's glory.

**Two characteristics every guide must demonstrate:**

**Empathy** — acknowledge the customer's pain before offering the solution.
- Formula: "We understand how it feels to [internal problem]…"
- Never skip this. Customers buy from guides who understand them first.
- Empathy copy appears in the hero section, above the fold, before any product description.
- Use "you" and "your" more than "we" and "our."

**Authority** — demonstrate competence without self-congratulation. Four forms:
1. **Testimonials** — let customers speak for the brand (most powerful)
2. **Statistics** — specific numbers ("helped 200+ businesses", "97% client retention")
3. **Awards / credentials** — logos, certifications, press mentions
4. **Demonstrations** — show the product or process working

**What the guide never does:** position themselves as the hero of the story, talk about their own journey at length, or lead with their own credentials before acknowledging the customer's problem.

---

## 4. Plan — Reduce Decision Risk

A clear plan removes the friction of confusion. Plans answer: "How does this work?"

**Process Plan** (pre-purchase) — 3 steps showing how to engage:
1. Schedule a call
2. We build your strategy
3. Watch your business grow

Maximum 3 steps. More than 3 creates complexity and reduces action.

**Agreement Plan** (post-purchase) — a set of named commitments that address customer fears:
- "We never use hidden fees"
- "We guarantee satisfaction or full refund within 30 days"
- "Our team responds within 24 hours, always"

Name the plan (e.g., "The HomeFirst Guarantee", "The 3-Step Clean Process"). A named plan feels more concrete and more trustworthy than an unnamed list.

---

## 5. Call to Action — Direct and Transitional

Heroes need to be challenged to take action. Passive or unclear requests are ignored.

**Direct CTA** — leads to a sale or first step towards a sale:
- "Buy now" / "Order today" / "Schedule an appointment" / "Register" / "Call today"
- Must appear in top-right of every page header
- Must repeat above the fold in the hero section
- Must repeat again every time the page creates momentum (after proof, after plan, after success vision)
- Use one consistent button colour for direct CTAs throughout the entire site — same colour, same text

**Transitional CTA** — builds trust with customers who are not yet ready to buy:
- Free PDF / Download guide / Watch webinar / Free trial / Free assessment / View case study
- Stakes the brand's claim to expertise in the territory
- Creates reciprocity (give generously; customers give back when they are ready)
- Positions the brand as the guide before purchase is made
- Appears alongside every direct CTA: "Ready? [Buy Now]   Not sure? [Get the Free Guide]"

**Transitional CTA: 3 strategic benefits:**
1. Stakes a claim to territory — signals expertise before the sale
2. Creates reciprocity — generosity is remembered and repaid
3. Positions the brand as guide — when the customer is ready, they return

**Rule:** every marketing touchpoint (website, email, print) should offer both a direct CTA and a transitional CTA.

## 6. Failure — Use Loss Aversion Lightly

Stakes make a story compelling. Without stakes, there is no story. Without urgency, customers defer.

**Loss aversion principle** (Kahneman, Prospect Theory, 1979): people are 2–3× more motivated to avoid a loss than to achieve an equivalent gain. Naming what the customer loses by not acting is more powerful than naming what they gain.

**Fear appeal formula** (Infante, Rancer, Womack):
1. Establish vulnerability: "30% of small businesses fail within 2 years from poor cash flow management."
2. Show it applies to them: "If cash flow is a concern, you should act now."
3. Present the solution: "Our system flags warning signs before they become crises."
4. Issue the CTA: "Schedule your free review today."

**"Pinch of salt" rule:** use failure content sparingly — 2–3 points per page is enough. High fear levels cause customers to disengage and block the message. Moderate fear creates urgency. Low fear has no effect. The purpose is urgency, not fear-mongering.

**Failure copy formats:**
- 3 bullet points answering "Are you struggling with…?" (homepage)
- Problem-agitation paragraph in email or blog post
- "Don't let X happen" as a gentle stake in a sales section

## 7. Success — Paint the Resolution

Never assume customers understand how their lives improve after purchase. Tell them explicitly. Show them specifically.

**Three proven story endings** (the three dominant human desires):

**1. Power and Position (Status)**
Customers want to be more respected, admired, or capable.
- Offer access (membership tiers, priority service)
- Create scarcity (limited edition, exclusive cohort)
- Offer a title or identity association ("Preferred Partner", "Diamond Member", "Certified")
- Use aspirational identity language in copy and on-boarding

**2. Union / Wholeness (External Completion)**
Customers feel incomplete; the product provides what is missing.
- Reduced anxiety: "Imagine finishing every project on time, every time."
- Reduced workload: "Free up 10 hours a week. Focus on what you love."
- More time: "Get back to your family by 5pm."

**3. Self-Realization (Reaching Potential)**
Customers want to prove themselves or become the person they aspire to be.
- Inspiration: associate the brand with achievement and accomplishment
- Acceptance: celebrate customers exactly as they are
- Transcendence: invite participation in a larger mission or movement

**Before/After grid** (Ryan Deiss — DigitalMarketer) — fill in for every key page:

| Dimension | Before (without us) | After (with us) |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Have | [external problem] | [external resolution] |
| Feel | [internal frustration] | [emotional resolution] |
| Average day | [current pain] | [improved daily life] |
| Status | [current identity] | [aspirational identity] |

**Rule:** always show photos of happy customers using the product. Images of success are as powerful as success copy. Never use abstract graphics or empty office stock photos.

---

## 8. Transformation — Aspirational Identity

The most powerful motivator is the desire to become somebody different, better, or more self-accepting.

**Define the aspirational identity:** who does the customer want to become as a result of engaging this brand?

**From → To format:**
- From: Confused and ill-equipped → To: Competent and confident
- From: Passive dog owner → To: Every dog's hero
- From: Overwhelmed entrepreneur → To: Focused business leader

**How to identify the aspirational identity:**
- Ask: "How does the customer want to be described by their friends?"
- Ask: "What kind of person do they want to become?"
- The aspirational identity answers WHO the hero becomes — not where they are going.

**Affirm the transformation:** after the customer achieves their goal (purchase, onboarding complete, first result), the guide acknowledges the change. A follow-up email, success page, or celebration moment:
"You've just done something most people never do. You're now the kind of person who…"

Brands that affirm transformation create brand evangelists, not just customers.

## Website Implementation — 5 Rules (Ch12)

Directly from Donald Miller's chapter on building a better website:

**Rule 1: Above-fold offer**
A single line (10 words or fewer) communicating one of: the aspirational identity you offer, the problem you solve, or what you do. Never lead with the company name, tagline, or product category. The hero must know within 5 seconds what you offer and why it matters to them.

**Rule 2: Obvious CTAs**
One direct CTA button in the top-right header. The same button repeated center-page in the hero section. The same button colour used consistently throughout every page. Never bury the CTA. Repeat it after every moment of conviction the page creates.

**Rule 3: Images of success**
Photos of happy, satisfied customers using the product or experiencing the result. Never use abstract graphics, geometric shapes, or stock photography of anonymous offices. Show the transformation visually — people in the "after" state.

**Rule 4: Bite-sized revenue streams**
Present offerings simply: one primary offer + up to two alternatives. Overwhelming options reduce conversion. Three maximum. If the brand has more than three offerings, choose the most important and lead with it.

**Rule 5: Very few words**
Websites are scanned, not read. 10 sentences or fewer above the fold is ideal. Use headlines, subheads, and short bullets. Every sentence must earn its place. If a line doesn't serve the BrandScript, cut it.
