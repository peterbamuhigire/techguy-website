## OPENING STATISTICS (use these to justify UX/UI investment)

- **70%** of online businesses fail due to bad usability
- **75%** of users judge within seconds whether a page is right for them
- **70%** of small business websites have no call to action
- A bad UX doesn't just frustrate users — it creates distrust, causing them to go off-script, ignore error messages, and become unpredictable; once they deviate, you lose all control over their actions

## PART 1: UX DESIGN

---

## 1. THE FUNDAMENTAL DEFINITION

**UX = the study of the relationship between a person and a product or service.**

Three key words:
- **Relationship** — bad UX makes users angry, mistrustful, and desperate to leave; they feel they are wrong, not the software
- **Person** — a real human with goals, emotions, and limited patience
- **Product/Service** — anything, digital or physical; a poorly designed door has bad UX

**Core principle:** When the user goes their own way, you have lost them. Navigation becomes unpredictable and conversion becomes impossible.

---

## 2. THE CLIENT BRIEFING — 5 MANDATORY QUESTIONS

Every client meeting must answer these five things, in this order:

### 2.1 Business Goal
Ask:
- Why did the client come to me today?
- What is the result they want to achieve?
- What is their problem?
- What do they want to achieve at the end of this project?
- Why does this project exist?

**Rule:** Never assume the client knows how to explain their objective precisely. They know their problem; your job is to identify the solution. Like a dentist: the patient says "pull the tooth"; the dentist decides the actual treatment.

**Examples of business goals:**
- Web agency: receive phone calls from potential clients, or collect emails
- Airbnb: get users to book overnight stays
- Amazon: become the first place users think of when they want to buy anything
- Eventbrite: get users to attend events

### 2.2 User Goal
- Business goal = rational (earn from a transaction)
- User goal = emotional (impress a date, try a new experience, feel competent)
- Both must be identified; they intersect but are never identical
- Duolingo example: the user goal is "learning a language can be fun" — every design decision (mascot, cartoon style, buttons) serves this emotional promise

### 2.3 Target & Personas
- **Target** = quantitative demographic/behavioral data (from Google Analytics)
- **Personas** = qualitative profile of a likely user (invented by Alan Cooper)

**Target data from Google Analytics:**
- Age and gender splits
- Screen resolution and mobile vs desktop percentage
- New vs returning users (loyalty indicator)
- Navigation flow and traffic sources (social media traffic = more superficial; newsletter/direct = more engaged)
- Preferred time of day (4am users: less attention to text, more to images/buttons)

**Persona profile — required fields:**
- Average age
- Gender
- Education level
- Professional background
- Technology skill level
- Why they arrived on the site
- How important is the information they seek; what happens if they don't find it
- Other possible sources for the same information
- Time of day and context of access
- Preferred tech devices (PC, Mac, tablet, smartphone)
- Other software/apps they use regularly
- Daily internet browsing time

**Rule:** The more specific the persona, the more the design can speak to them and only them. Goals and problems of users have enormous impact on content choices.

### 2.4 Competition Analysis
- A competitor is anyone who addresses the same user need (not just identical products)
- Scooters compete with buses, bikes, cars, subscription mobility services, and walking
- The status quo — doing nothing — is always a competitor

**Three angles to analyze competitors:**

**Communication:**
- What is their communication style?
- What are they taking for granted?
- What qualities/features are they emphasizing and why?
- Example: Jira targets formal product managers at structured companies; Basecamp targets informal startup founders — same product category, radically different personas

**Target:**
- Is their target identical to yours?
- What advantages does each target have?
- Jira → longer buying cycles, multi-stakeholder decisions; Basecamp → impulsive buying by small teams

**Business Model:**
- Do they charge once or by subscription?
- How much per user per month?
- Is your client priced higher or lower, and is that good or bad?

### 2.5 Metrics — Make the Goal Measurable
**Rule:** Every client meeting must end with a measurable business objective written down.

**Not measurable:** "so the client can get rich", "receive many emails", "be seen as cutting-edge", "make competitors jealous"

**Measurable:**
- Get X more customers than last year
- Increase average browsing time
- Increase emails received vs previous year
- Increase social shares
- Reduce service support tickets
- Reduce e-commerce product returns
- Number of compliments sent via email or support calls

**UX designers are evaluated on numbers, not on whether the client likes the colors.** Yes or no. Did it improve? Yes or no.

## 3. THE MESSY MIDDLE & CUSTOMER JOURNEY

### 3.1 Google's Messy Middle
Two phases of the decision-making process before purchase:

**Exploration phase:** User doesn't have a clear goal yet. Needs to expand their list of possibilities. Ask friends, search online, read articles.

**Evaluation phase:** User has enough options; now needs to narrow down. Compares features. Asks: does this solution match my goal?

These phases are not linear — users cycle back and forth between them many times.

**Three key takeaways:**
1. **User experience starts before your website** — shaped by word-of-mouth, forums, price comparison tools, prior brand experience; a single site has limited short-term impact
2. **Users don't know all alternatives** — your product must be present in the channels users are already searching: social media, Amazon, Google
3. **Users seek objective data in evaluation** — "market leader" is meaningless; "500,000 customers" is an objective characteristic

### 3.2 Customer Journey Framework
Map the path from state A (need arises) to state B (action taken).

**Three columns:** Exploration phase | Evaluation phase | Decision-making phase

**Four rows:**
1. What are their actions, and where are they?
2. What are their thoughts? What are they saying to themselves?
3. What are their feelings?
4. What opportunities do we have in this transition?

**Optional additional phases:**
- **Post-decision phase:** User has purchased; small effort here cements the relationship. Celebrate with them, confirm the choice was right, provide contact info for issues.
- **Post-experiential phase:** User has completed the experience; opportunity to re-engage, offer value, plant the seed for the next purchase (Evaneos example: free photo album → brand presence in the home for years).

**Design principle derived from customer journey:** The moment the user decides to go their own way, you have lost them. Every element on every page must either advance the user to the next step or be removed.

## 4. INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE (IA)

IA = the organization of site content and navigation flow, taking into account both the client's business objective and the user's goal.

**Bookstore analogy:** A bookstore organizes books not alphabetically alone but by: featured/trending titles near entrance (exploration phase), sponsored/highlighted titles, genre categories, then alphabetical within genre. Websites mirror this exact structure.

### 4.1 Three IA Questions

**Question 1: How do we organize the content?**
- What priority does each piece of information have?
- What does the client want included?
- What is the user actually looking for?
- Organization must anticipate the user's reasoning during navigation

**Question 2: What do we name our content?**
- What terms does the user expect to find?
- Menu items, categories, tags — must match user vocabulary, not internal jargon
- Example: a property listing page must show price, rooms, surface area, bathrooms, floor — in the order the user asks these questions

**Question 3: Which navigation?**
- What menu items are relevant to the user?
- Where is the menu?
- Is there a free search bar? Why or why not?
- This leads directly to the sitemap

---

## 5. SITEMAP

**Definition:** A block-based visual representation where each block = a web page, and each arrow = a user navigation path. The skeleton of the site.

**Why build a sitemap:**
- Truly understand the complexity of the project
- Ensure you know and control every section
- Estimate design and development hours
- Create a visual overview for yourself and the client
- Show the client all pages included in scope

**Tools:** Octopus.do (sitemap + wireframe hybrid), Figma, pen and paper

---

## 6. WIREFRAMING

**Definition:** The skeleton of a page — an elementary visual representation of where elements will be positioned. NOT the final design.

**Rules for wireframing:**
- Do NOT include brand graphics (colors, photos, fonts, logo) — too early; judging on aesthetics derails structural thinking
- Draw only bulky/structural items: image placeholder, text block, two columns, title, video
- Focus only on structure and information priority

### 6.1 Three Wireframe Questions

**1. How does the user interact between and within pages?**
- What are the clickable areas?
- What happens when you click the title of an item?
- What does the user see on mobile? On tablet?

**2. How do we group the information?**
- How many columns?
- Is there a main column?
- Does the sidebar follow as the user scrolls?
- How do I differentiate results from main content?
- How do I differentiate title from subtitle?
- How do I make links visually clear?

**3. What is the priority with which we display information?**
- What is the main content of this page?
- How important is it that the user reads the title?
- When is the right moment to show the call to action?
- What must the user read before clicking it?

### 6.2 Three Rules for Better Wireframes

**Rule 1: Base everything on the customer journey**
- Ask: which phase is the user in when they arrive on this page? Exploration or evaluation?
- Exploration phase → maximize options, inspire, show possibilities (Tripadvisor, Amazon homepage)
- Evaluation phase → provide features, proof, reasons to proceed (Dropbox, Spotify homepage)
- For every element added, ask: does this advance the user to the next step? If no, remove it.

**Rule 2: Keep the client's business goal in mind**
- The business goal must appear as a visible button/area/CTA on every relevant page
- **Rule: each page should have only one objective** — competing objectives create confusion
- Ryanair example: 32 buy buttons in one purchase flow = UX disaster
- Mobile apps: even stricter — one CTA per screen, due to screen size and single-handed use

**Rule 3: Validate your hypotheses**
- Show wireframes to team members and representative users before presenting to client
- Simulate navigation with printed sheets or on-screen
- Ask them to think aloud; capture their problems, thoughts, concerns

### 6.3 The Trunk Test (Steve Krug)
Put a wireframe in front of anyone with basic browser familiarity and ask:
1. Where are we? What is this page?
2. What actions can you take?
3. Where can you go from here?
4. How did we get here?

If they can answer these four questions, the wireframe communicates correctly.

### 6.4 How to Start Wireframing
**Start by copying.** Browse 10+ professional sites in the same category. Analyze how they structure pages. Ask yourself: what am I looking for here? What am I thinking? Why is this button here?

**Exploration site wireframe patterns:**
- Homepage: header (logo left, menu right), title stating what site does, search bar near top, featured/highlighted items, feature roundup or articles, footer (3–5 column menu)
- Detail page: two-column layout — main column (title + photos + description), sidebar (actions: reservation, purchase, contact request). On mobile: sidebar goes above main photo or fixed at bottom on scroll. Reviews section below, then related items, then footer.

**Evaluation site wireframe patterns:**
- Homepage: header (logo left, menu right), hero image + business card text (every word matters here), CTA button, feature roundup, more detail for thorough users, repeat CTA, footer
- One or two columns max — mobile transition is easy
- After CTA click → acquisition funnel: one-way path to a single action (purchase, email capture, trial download)

## PART 2: UI DESIGN

## 7. WHAT IS UI DESIGN?

**UI = a visual language that controls, manipulates, and directs the user's gaze, helps them understand page content, and spurs them to take action.**

UI is NOT:
- Drawing pictures
- Decoration or "making the graphics"
- Expressing personal creativity
- Changing a template's logo and colors

UI IS:
- Problem-solving tool
- Conversion maximization
- Carrying forward all strategic UX decisions through visual execution

**Questions UI design must answer:**
- What colors do we use?
- Which font do we choose?
- What are the images?
- How do we visually define clickable areas?
- How do we ensure the user reads the page in the order we defined?
- How do we emphasize the business objective?
- How do we characterize buttons, text, titles so they are clearly differentiated?
- How do we visually differentiate the main column from the sidebar?
- How are elements aligned with each other?
- What is the dominant element of the page?
- How do we achieve a good reading pace?
- How do we incorporate the client's brand visual elements?

**The generic interface problem:**
A page with structure seen a million times, colors seen elsewhere, text that looks the same as everything else. Clean and linear but not memorable. Doesn't create a unique, sincere, or honest relationship with the user. Avoid it at all costs.

---

## 8. MENTAL MODEL VS IMPLEMENTATION MODEL (Alan Cooper)

**Implementation model:** Design that starts from how the technology works, then builds the UI. Rational but fails to match the user's way of reasoning.

**Mental model:** Design that starts from how the user thinks — simpler, more basic, more instinctive. How even a child can use an iPad.

**Representative model:** What designers actually build — a bridge between the two worlds. The closer it approximates the mental model, the easier the software feels.

**Practical applications:**
- Date picker: instead of a calendar with infinite dates, offer "today / this week / this weekend / this month" — matches how users actually think about scheduling
- Airport + city: at a mental model level, "JFK" and "New York" are interchangeable for a user booking a flight; the system must accept both even if technically they differ
- Pre-select round-trip + economy class in flight booking (most users choose these defaults) — the software takes care of the user like a waiter who refills your wine
- Airbnb search wizard: broken into multiple steps not for technical reasons but to follow the user's natural thought process when planning a stay

**Wizards** = step-by-step guides through complex processes. Technically a complication that aims to simplify user life. Use them for any multi-step process that would otherwise overwhelm.

**Key question to always ask:** "How would my user think about this?"

---

## 9. SIMPLICITY

**Three reasons we fail to simplify:**

1. **Simplifying is hard** — things naturally grow complex over time (Mailchimp: newsletter tool → CRM with predictive automation; Ryanair: ticket seller → adds 10+ buy buttons in one page; Google Maps: show map → add 8 more modes)
2. **Our work is objectively difficult** — users don't know what a browser is; they don't know the difference between Facebook and Google; never assume technical literacy
3. **We are too immersed** — we forget to explain what we do; we assume users already know

**Rules:**
- Never assume the user, when they arrive, knows everything and just needs a buy button
- Always explain what the page/service/software is — even Stripe says "payment infrastructure for the Internet"
- The user is replaceable by a competitor in one click; you have a moral duty to make their life easier and be explicit about why that page exists

## 10. PROGRESSIVE DISCLOSURE

**Definition:** Any information presented to a user who is not interested in or ready to evaluate it is irrelevant and must be eliminated.

**Corollary:** Every piece of information the average user looks for and cannot find is a UX problem.

### 10.1 Three-Level Information Hierarchy

**Level 1 — Essential information:**
- Without it, the user cannot proceed at all
- Must be immediately visible, always
- No margin for error: user must always be able to see it
- Example: current balance + payment button in N26 banking app (nothing else on first screen)

**Level 2 — Important information:**
- Conditions the user's decision to act (click a button, submit email)
- Its absence doesn't block navigation entirely but weakens decision-making
- Explains why the user should do something
- Example: today's weather in a weather app (takes up half the screen — user is at the door deciding whether to take an umbrella)

**Level 3 — Unimportant information:**
- The majority of all information
- Hidden behind a click or two
- Never forced into the primary view
- Example: hourly weather breakdown, license plate in a parking app, less-used menu items in Asana

**Rule for weather app design:** The primary question the user is asking is: "Should I take an umbrella right now?" Everything else is secondary and should be one tap away.

**Progressive disclosure checklist:**
- Show only the most relevant information to the user
- Prioritize according to the user's objective
- Put everything not needed for decision-making at second or third level of depth

---

## 11. DOMINANCE

**Definition:** The dominant element is the one noticed first. It sets the reading order for the entire page.

**Three tools to create dominance:**

1. **Size** — larger elements grab attention first; titles are larger than paragraphs for this reason; warn clients: a large logo is not the user's first need when visiting an unknown company's site
2. **Contrast** — a small element with strong contrast dominates over larger, lower-contrast elements; buttons must use contrast to stand out from surrounding content
3. **Negative space** — surrounding any element with lots of empty space puts it center stage regardless of size or position

**Rule:** When a layout has no dominant element, the user has no reading order. Every element competes equally. Navigation becomes unpredictable. The user gets impatient and leaves.

**Practical application:** Every time you create a layout, ask:
- What elements does the user need to make their choice?
- How do I make those elements dominant on this page?

**Client battle:** Clients always want a large logo. Resist this. The user rarely visits a page to admire the logo of an unknown company. The dominant element should be the headline or CTA — whatever drives the business objective.

---

## 12. ALIGNMENT

**Definition:** That which is aligned makes it easier and faster for the user to read and interpret the space.

**Why left-aligned text beats centered text:**
- Left-aligned text allows the eye to start every line from the same point
- Centered text forces the brain to reset at every line break to find where to start reading again
- Rule: never use centered text for body copy; use it only for short headlines

**Alignment creates:**
- Easier, faster scanning
- A sense of relationship between elements
- Trust, optimism, and calm — the ideal pre-conversion emotional state
- Perception that the elements belong to the same context

**Practical applications:**
- Medium.com homepage: minimalist alignment makes the page effortlessly scannable
- Amazon vs Alibaba product listing: Amazon's perfectly aligned product photos and text make it more persuasive and readable
- Alignment is an **optical effect**, not a mathematical result — visual weight of elements varies, so manual adjustment is sometimes required even when the grid says "aligned"

---

## 13. ERROR MESSAGES

**Core principle:** The word "error" causes estrangement, disgust, anger. Users believe it is always the software's fault, never theirs.

**Rules for error messages:**
1. **Never use the word "error" or "mistake"** — it makes users feel stupid and blamed
2. **Start with empathy** — "I'm sorry, the password you entered is incorrect"
3. **Be specific** — don't say "there is a problem on this page"; name exactly what went wrong
4. **Highlight the problem visually** — underline the specific field where the issue occurred
5. **Suggest the solution explicitly** — "Maybe we can help you find your username again?" + direct link; "Maybe you forgot your password?" + direct link

**Who should write error messages:** Communication manager, copywriter, or designer — NOT the developer/programmer.

**Principle:** The moments of difficulty are where the most lasting relationships are created. A compassionate error message turns a frustration point into a trust-building moment.

## 14. ICONS

**Why icons work:**
- Catch attention better than text
- User understands function without reading
- Faster path to action than parsing a sentence
- Universal — no translation required
- Space-saving (app name + icon → users stop reading names, recognize icons by color)

**When icons fail:** When they don't unequivocally convey their meaning to your target audience. If users don't know what will happen when they click, you have a UX problem.

**Best practice:** Text + icon together is always the best combination.

### 14.1 Four Icon Rules

**Rule 1 — Consistent perceived size:**
- Two icons can be the same pixel size but different visual weight (due to balance of empty/full space, or edge thickness)
- Inconsistent visual weight draws unintended attention to one icon over others
- Normalize perceived size, not just pixel dimensions

**Rule 2 — Consistent detail level:**
- More detailed, denser icons attract more attention
- If one icon in a set is significantly more complex than the others, it will dominate regardless of intent
- Choose a single complexity level and stick to it across all icons

**Rule 3 — Don't mix icon styles:**
- Once you choose a style (pastel, rounded corners, illustrative, outlined), maintain it throughout
- Mixing styles is visually jarring and breaks consistency

**Rule 4 — Respect conventions:**
- Very few icons are truly universal (print, save, home, back arrow)
- For these universals, don't reinvent the wheel with novel styles
- User should recognize them instantly without reading any label

## 15. RHYTHM

**Definition:** When the interval between elements composing a page is always the same, the mind learns the rule instinctively. The eye moves between elements predicting where one ends and the next begins.

**Effect:** Creates an invisible reading metronome that carries the user forward while reading — without conscious effort.

**How rhythm is created:**
- Same shape, same font, same size, same box for elements that belong to the same logical group
- Example: Facebook notifications — each one identical in structure → brain locks into a rhythm → can process thousands of them without fatigue
- Example: Amazon category page — product cards share identical grid proportions → user scans fluidly

**Practical rule:** Group elements that belong to the same logical category and give them identical visual treatment (size, spacing, font, color). Different logical groups get different visual treatment.

---

## 16. PREDICTABILITY

**Definition:** Predictable design means the user never has to ask what page they are on or what will happen next.

**Five types of predictability:**

1. **Navigation predictability** — user can always answer: where am I? What happens next?
   Example: YouTube playlist shows what's currently playing and every song that follows

2. **Interaction predictability** — user can identify clickable vs non-clickable areas without hovering
   Rule: **never design text that looks like a button, and never design a button that looks like text**
   Buttons must look like buttons; links must look like links

3. **Convention predictability** — using established design conventions correctly
   Example: a dropdown arrow always means a dropdown menu; every time a user sees it, they expect the same behavior regardless of site

4. **Consistency predictability** — the same navigation rules apply across all pages
   Breaking a self-established rule even once confuses the user and makes the whole site unpredictable from that point forward

5. **Copy predictability** — using microcopy to explain what will happen after an action
   Examples:
   - Facebook below "Publish": explains exactly who will see the post
   - Ryanair: explains in 3 bullet points why you should log in
   - The Fork: explains 3 key advantages of clicking the book button

**Core principle:** Users who are in predictable environments feel welcomed, calm, and open — the emotional precondition for conversion.

## 17. AFFORDANCE

**Definition:** Design elements that visually suggest how they are used, without any instruction text.

**Rule:** We no longer need to tell users "Click here to do X". If we feel we must explain what to click, the design is too difficult to understand.

**How affordance works in practice:**
- Acrobat Reader sidebar: small arrows that look like drawer handles → cursor darkens on hover → click opens "drawers" → natural drawer metaphor throughout
- Easypark parking app: an animation on the main element suggests the user should interact with it (animated meter near the thumb's natural resting position)
- Slack: unavoidable on-screen notification floats over content when you have an unread message — impossible to miss
- Momondo price graph: interactive graph with handles the user has never seen before, yet instinctively tries to drag

**Negative affordance:** Inactive buttons must look inactive (grayed out, dimmed) — like an LED that is off. Toggle states (on/off) must always be obviously and unambiguously clear.

**Rule:** Use visual language to invite users to interactive areas and discourage them from non-interactive areas — without writing a single instruction.

---

## 18. CONSISTENCY

**Definition:** The visual language of a site must remain the same from page to page and within every page. When it changes, the user must unlearn one rule and decode a new one.

**What consistency applies to:**
- Font choice (same font family throughout)
- Color palette (same colors always mean the same things)
- Title sizes (H1 always looks like H1 everywhere)
- Button sizes and styles (a set of 3–4 buttons all the same size)
- Icon style (same line weight, same fill treatment, same corner radius)

**Rule:** Choose in advance the size and colors of titles, subtitles, buttons, and links. Ensure they are all clearly differentiated from each other. Then never deviate.

**Cross-platform consistency:**
- Duolingo: same mascot, same bright green buttons, same font across app and website → user instantly recognizes they are "in the same place" regardless of platform
- Consistency = choosing in advance + never making exceptions

**The cost of inconsistency:**
- Example: a train booking app with different font sizes, colors (light gray, dark gray alternating), and weights (bold/not bold) across three consecutive screens — the result is navigation that feels difficult, unpredictable, and frustrating

**Pattern:** When consistency extends across multiple sites/platforms/products, it becomes a design pattern — a shared visual language the entire web recognizes and trusts.

---

## 19. PROXIMITY

**Definition:** Elements close to each other are perceived as belonging to the same logical group.

**Core principle:** Proximity signals relationship. Elements far apart are perceived as belonging to different logical categories.

**Proximity is powerful enough to override other variables:**
- Even if you add different colors to elements that are close together, they are still perceived as one group
- Even if you add a dividing line, proximity still signals grouping more strongly than the line

**Practical applications:**

- **Titles and content:** A title placed far from the top and bottom of a card guides the eye and allows the user to scan headlines without reading body text — enabling fast decision-making about whether to read further
- **Samsung navigation:** Two navigation groups spaced apart → left nav = product categories (primary), right nav = generic sections/support (secondary) — spacing communicates hierarchy without labels
- **Amazon:** Correct distances between product items substitute for box borders — cards appear card-like through proximity alone, not through visual outlines
- **Advanced search filters:** Filter elements always clustered very tightly together — because they all belong to the same logical operation
- **Complex layouts (4+ columns):** Proximity creates the column structure — no dividing lines needed when spacing is precise

**When proximity breaks down:**
- On very complex layouts (Alibaba example), spacing alone is insufficient; visual boxes/borders become necessary to separate items

**Rule for every element placement:** Ask: how does this element relate to everything around it? That answer determines its position.

## 20. COLOR

**Function 1 — Attention and mood:**
- Red = urgency, emergency, required attention (notifications, error messages, high-attention buttons)
- Green = safety, completion, tranquility (thank-you pages, success states, completion confirmation)
- These are now ingrained conventions; attempting to use red for calm or green for urgency will confuse users

**Color associations:**
- Black = authority, power
- White = innocence, purity, peace
- Pink = romanticism, passion, compassion, feminine
- Blue = tranquility; used heavily in business and technology
- Yellow = optimism, positivity, joy
- Purple = change, sophistication, creativity, feminine
- Brown = nature, earth, stability, solidity

**Function 2 — Interactive area indicator:**
- Scanning for color allows users to identify all clickable areas on the page
- Twitter example: remove all neutral-colored (white/black) elements; what remains colored = all main actions available
- WhatsApp: green = interactive active areas; grey = inactive/viewed areas; blue = message read receipt (no action needed, just status)
- Slack: blue = active conversation; green = user online status
- Google Drive: light blue = current state; grey hover = clickable area indicator

**Function 3 — Brand identity:**
- Mailchimp yellow, Soundcloud orange, Coca-Cola red, Mastercard red+orange
- Colors become inseparable from brands when used with extreme consistency

**The 10% rule:**
- ~10% of the population has a color perception deficiency
- **Never rely solely on color to convey meaning**
- Errors in forms cannot be signaled only by turning fields red — must also add icon and text

### 20.1 Four Color Questions (mandatory before finalizing any design)
1. Am I using colors sparingly? (aim for 3–5 max; every new color needs a defined meaning)
2. Does the chosen color reinforce the message I want to convey, or interfere with it?
3. Am I using the color palette consistently? (same color = same function, same mood, everywhere)
4. Does a functional meaning depend on that color? If yes, add text or icon support to ensure colorblind users are not excluded

## 21. CONTRAST

**Definition:** High-contrast elements are noticed first. The eye scans from highest contrast to lowest contrast.

**Contrast and readability:**
- Black text on white background = maximum contrast = easiest, smoothest reading
- Low contrast text on background = user must exert more effort = fatigue = abandonment
- Complementary colors (e.g., red on green) can have high contrast but remain unreadable — high contrast ≠ readable

**Using one color with multiple gradations to set reading order:**
- Dark background → lighter header → even lighter main content area
- Three shades of one color = three reading priority levels, created before a single word is written
- Most important buttons use the highest-contrast color treatment on the page

**Practical test:** Stand 2–3 meters from your monitor and look at the page. Whatever you can still see clearly is your highest-contrast element — that should be your primary CTA or most critical message.

**Rule:** The next time you choose a color tone for a text or button, ask: how important is it that the user reads this text or button compared to all other elements on the page? Set contrast accordingly.

## FRAMEWORKS & CHECKLISTS SUMMARY

### Wireframe Checklist
- [ ] Does each page serve one (and only one) objective?
- [ ] Is the user in exploration or evaluation phase on this page?
- [ ] Is the CTA visible and is there enough supporting content before it to justify clicking?
- [ ] Does every element advance the user to the next step?
- [ ] Has the wireframe passed the Trunk Test (4 questions)?

### UI Design Checklist
- [ ] Is there a dominant element that sets the reading order?
- [ ] Is left alignment used for all body text?
- [ ] Do elements that belong together sit close together?
- [ ] Do elements that are unrelated have clear space between them?
- [ ] Are clickable areas obviously clickable?
- [ ] Are inactive elements visually grayed out?
- [ ] Is the same visual language used consistently across all pages?
- [ ] Does the page have a reading rhythm (consistent spacing between repeated elements)?
- [ ] Is color used with a defined system (max 3–5 colors, each with a clear meaning)?
- [ ] Does contrast lead the eye to the most important element first?
- [ ] Are all error messages empathetic, specific, and solution-oriented?
- [ ] Are icons consistent in style, perceived size, and detail level?
- [ ] Are icons accompanied by text labels when targeting less tech-savvy users?

### Color Checklist
- [ ] Using max 3–5 colors?
- [ ] Does each color reinforce the message?
- [ ] Is the palette used consistently (same color = same meaning, always)?
- [ ] Have you added icon/text support for any meaning conveyed by color alone?

### Progressive Disclosure Checklist
- [ ] Identified the three tiers: essential / important / unimportant information?
- [ ] Essential information is always visible at first view?
- [ ] Important information is above the fold or within the natural scroll?
- [ ] Unimportant information is 1–2 clicks away?
- [ ] Every element on the page — can you justify its presence at this level?

### Customer Journey Checklist
- [ ] Defined exploration, evaluation, decision, post-decision, and post-experiential phases?
- [ ] Mapped user actions, thoughts, feelings, and opportunities at each phase?
- [ ] Used post-decision phase to cement the relationship?
- [ ] Used post-experiential phase to plant seeds for the next purchase?

## KEY PRINCIPLES INDEX (Quick Reference)

| Principle | Core Rule |
|---|---|
| Mental Model | Design for how users think, not how the system works |
| Simplicity | Always explain what you do; never assume user knowledge |
| Progressive Disclosure | Show only what is needed at this moment; hide the rest |
| Dominance | Every page must have one element that is noticed first |
| Alignment | Aligned elements = faster reading + trust; always left-align body text |
| Rhythm | Identical spacing for identical categories = effortless scanning |
| Predictability | User must always know where they are and what happens next |
| Affordance | Visual design tells users what to click without instructions |
| Consistency | Same visual language = same rule = no relearning required |
| Proximity | Close = related; distant = different category |
| Color | Max 5 colors; each with a defined meaning; never rely on color alone |
| Contrast | Highest contrast = first read; use it to lead the eye to the CTA |
| Error Messages | Empathetic, specific, solution-oriented; never use the word "error" |
| Icons | Text + icon together; consistent style, size, and detail level |
| Each page has ONE objective | Multiple competing objectives create confusion and reduce conversion |

## SITE PATTERN LIBRARY

### Homepage — Exploration Site (Tripadvisor, Airbnb, Amazon, Eventbrite pattern)
- Header: logo left, menu right, user account/login at end of menu
- Hero: title stating what site does + search bar (dominant element)
- Featured/highlighted content rows
- Category/feature summaries
- Footer: 3–5 column menu

### Detail Page — Exploration Site
- Two columns: main column (title + photos + description text) + sidebar (primary actions)
- Sidebar: reservation / purchase / contact request — convention users expect
- Mobile: sidebar above main photo OR fixed at bottom as overlay on scroll
- Reviews section below main content
- Related products/items
- Footer

### Homepage — Evaluation Site (SaaS, product landing, Dropbox/Spotify pattern)
- Header: logo left, menu right
- Hero: large image/video + business card text (every word matters) + CTA button
- Feature roundup (the product's key differentiators)
- More detailed information section for thorough evaluators
- Repeat CTA
- Footer
- Mobile: 1–2 columns → easy transition

### Acquisition Funnel Page (post-CTA click)
- One-way path with single exit = the target action
- Remove all navigation (user should not be able to leave easily)
- Price + purchase mechanics OR email capture OR trial initiation
- No competing CTAs
