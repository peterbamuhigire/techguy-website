# Legal Sector Ethics and Bar Advertising Constraints

## Why Ethics Rules Matter for Law Firm Websites

Law firm websites are subject to state bar advertising rules in every jurisdiction where the firm practices. These rules vary significantly by state. Violations can result in disciplinary action against the attorney. **The website developer is not responsible for compliance — the attorney is.**

Build a pre-launch ethics review step into every law firm project handoff. Before any law firm website goes live, the attorney must review all content for compliance with their jurisdiction's rules. A developer who writes "expert in personal injury law" may not know that claiming "expert" is prohibited in most jurisdictions.

---

## Universal Prohibitions (Apply in Most Jurisdictions)

### No "Expert" or "Specialist" Claims

Unless the attorney holds a specialty certification issued by their state bar's approved certification program, do not use any of the following:

- "Expert in [practice area]"
- "Specialist in [practice area]"
- "Leading expert"
- "Nationally recognized expert"
- "Top specialist"

**What to use instead**: "Experienced in," "focused practice in," "extensive background in," "dedicated to [practice area]," "concentrates in."

**How to verify**: Check whether the state bar has a board certification program for this practice area. If the attorney holds that certification, the claim is permissible. If not, it is not.

### No Outcome Guarantees

Never write or imply guaranteed results anywhere on the site — in headlines, in body copy, in CTAs, or in case result presentations:

- "We win cases" — implies a guarantee
- "Get the maximum settlement" — implies a guarantee
- "We'll get you what you deserve" — implies a guarantee if used as an outcome promise
- "100% success rate" — almost certainly false and a guarantee
- "Guaranteed results"

**What to use instead**: Specific past case results, where permitted by jurisdiction, with the required disclaimer.

**Required disclaimer for case results**:
> "Past results do not guarantee a similar outcome. Every case is different."

### No Misleading Comparisons

Cannot claim superiority over other attorneys without a verifiable basis:

- "Best law firm in [city]" — only permissible if supported by a verified, independent award
- "Top attorney" — only permissible from a verifiable peer-review source (Super Lawyers, Best Lawyers, etc.)
- "Better results than [competitor]" — prohibited without substantiation

**What to use instead**: Specific, verifiable claims — "Rated AV Preeminent by Martindale-Hubbell," "Named to Super Lawyers 2024," "Recipient of the [specific award] from [organization]."

### Testimonial Rules

Most jurisdictions allow client testimonials with restrictions. Before publishing any testimonial:

- Check the state bar's advertising rules for the specific jurisdiction where the firm practices
- Many jurisdictions require a disclaimer near testimonials
- Some jurisdictions require testimonials be from actual clients — no composites
- Some jurisdictions prohibit testimonials entirely
- Never publish testimonials that create unjustified expectations of specific results

**Required disclaimer for testimonials**:
> "Client testimonials do not guarantee, warrant, or predict results in your case."

## Domain Name Ethics Rules

Some state bars regulate law firm domain names. Issues to check before finalizing:

- Some jurisdictions require the domain to include the name of at least one partner
- Domain names that are misleading or imply guaranteed results may be prohibited
- Examples of potentially impermissible domains: `www.WeWinEveryCase.com`, `www.GuaranteedResults.com`, `www.TheBestLawyerInChicago.com`

Always verify with state bar rules before registering a domain for a new firm.

## Domain of Safe vs. Risky Content

### Informational Content (Safe)

Website content must remain on the informational side of the line between information and legal advice.

- "In most personal injury cases, victims have two years from the date of injury to file a claim in Illinois."
- "A DUI conviction in Texas can result in a fine, license suspension, and possible jail time depending on prior offenses."
- "Probate in California typically takes 9 to 18 months for an average estate."

### Legal Advice (Risky on Public Web Content)

Avoid content that applies law to a specific fact pattern or tells a reader what they should do:

- "You should file your claim immediately because the two-year window expires soon." (risky)
- "Based on what you've described, you have a strong case." (never put this in written web content)
- "In your situation, you should choose a trust over a will." (advice, not information)

**The test**: Does the content give information about how the law generally works, or does it tell a specific reader what to do? Information is acceptable. Advice to a specific (even hypothetical) reader creates liability.

### Case Results and Settlement Amounts

When publishing case results:

- Include the required disclaimer about past results on the same page
- Be accurate — inflating amounts creates ethics and fraud exposure
- Some jurisdictions require disclosing that the result was exceptional or atypical
- Some jurisdictions prohibit publishing settlement amounts entirely — check before including

## State-Specific Rule Variations

Bar advertising rules differ significantly across states. The categories below are where rules most commonly vary. Always verify local rules for the specific jurisdiction before finalizing the site.

| Category | What Varies by State |
|----------|---------------------|
| Testimonials | Permitted, permitted with disclaimer, or prohibited entirely |
| Case results | Permitted, permitted with disclaimer, or prohibited entirely |
| "Best" / "Top" claims | Standard for what counts as verifiable varies |
| Domain name content | Some states have explicit restrictions |
| Expert/specialist claims | Certification programs vary; some states have no bar-certified specialties at all |
| Paid client referrals | Most states prohibit fee-splitting with non-attorneys |
| Live chat with accident victims | Some states restrict real-time solicitation of potential clients |
| Response time promises | Most allow "we respond within X hours"; avoid any phrasing that implies a legal obligation |

**Where to check state rules**:
- State bar's website — search "advertising rules" or "attorney marketing rules"
- ABA Center for Professional Responsibility: `americanbar.org/cprc`
- State bar ethics opinions — many bars publish advisory opinions on specific situations

## Required Disclaimers (Build Into Every Template)

These three disclaimers are non-negotiable. Build them into the law firm site template so they appear automatically on every deployment.

### 1. Contact Form Disclaimer

Place immediately adjacent to the form submit button:

```
Submitting this form does not create an attorney-client relationship.
Please do not include confidential information in your initial message.
```

**Why this matters**: Protects the firm from conflict of interest claims from prospective clients who disclosed information about opposing parties, from inadvertent privilege claims, and from potential malpractice exposure from advice given before formal retention.

### 2. Footer Informational Disclaimer

Place in the footer of every page:

```
The information on this website is for general informational purposes only.
Nothing on this site should be taken as legal advice for any individual case
or situation. This information is not intended to create, and receipt or
viewing does not constitute, an attorney-client relationship.
```

**Why this matters**: Establishes that the website content is informational, not legal advice. Reduces liability from readers who act on web content without consulting the attorney.

### 3. Case Results Disclaimer

Place immediately adjacent to any case result, settlement amount, or verdict figure:

```
Past results do not guarantee a similar outcome. Every case is different.
```

Some jurisdictions require additional language noting that the result was exceptional. Check local rules.

## The Developer's Role in Ethics Compliance

As a website builder working with a law firm client, your responsibilities are limited but specific:

1. **Flag potential issues, do not enforce** — note in writing any content that may raise ethics concerns; the attorney makes the final call
2. **Build disclaimers in by default** — the three required disclaimers above must appear in every law firm template automatically
3. **Avoid outcome language in any copy you draft** — never draft headlines or body copy that implies guaranteed results
4. **Recommend pre-launch attorney review** — communicate in writing that all content requires bar rule compliance review before going live
5. **Do not make compliance determinations** — telling a client "this is fine under bar rules" is outside your role and could expose you to liability

**What developers should never do**: Make final determinations about what does or does not comply with bar rules. This is the attorney's professional and ethical responsibility.

## Pre-Launch Ethics Checklist for Attorney Review

Deliver this checklist to the attorney as a signed handoff document. The attorney should review and confirm each item before approving the site for launch.

### Claims and Language

- [ ] No "expert" or "specialist" language appears anywhere on the site without a verified bar certification
- [ ] No outcome guarantees appear anywhere — headlines, body copy, CTAs, image captions
- [ ] No unsupported superlatives ("best," "top," "most experienced") without a verifiable, independent source
- [ ] All "best" or "award" claims are backed by a current, verifiable recognition
- [ ] No misleading comparisons to other attorneys or firms

### Credentials and Bar Admissions

- [ ] Attorney credentials stated accurately — no inflation or omission
- [ ] All bar admissions listed accurately — no states listed where attorney is not currently admitted
- [ ] Law school and graduation year accurate
- [ ] Certifications listed are current and from bar-approved programs

### Case Results

- [ ] Case results disclaimer ("Past results do not guarantee...") appears adjacent to every result
- [ ] Settlement and verdict amounts are accurate
- [ ] Jurisdiction rules permit publication of settlement amounts — confirmed
- [ ] No results described as "typical" or "expected" unless substantiated

### Testimonials

- [ ] Jurisdiction rules permit client testimonials — confirmed
- [ ] Testimonial disclaimer appears adjacent to all testimonials
- [ ] All testimonials are from actual clients who provided written permission
- [ ] No testimonials imply a guaranteed result
- [ ] No composite or anonymized testimonials in jurisdictions that prohibit them

### Disclaimers

- [ ] Contact form includes attorney-client relationship disclaimer
- [ ] Footer includes general informational disclaimer
- [ ] Case results disclaimer appears wherever case results appear
- [ ] Testimonial disclaimer appears wherever testimonials appear

### Domain and Identity

- [ ] Domain name complies with state bar rules — confirmed
- [ ] Firm name on the site matches the name registered with the state bar
- [ ] All contact information is accurate (address, phone, email)

**Signature block for attorney sign-off**:

> I, [Attorney Name], have reviewed the above checklist and confirm that the website content complies with the advertising rules of [State Bar(s)]. I accept responsibility for the content published on [website URL].
>
> Signature: ________________  Date: ________________
