# Cultural Patterns

**Read this when**: shaping tone, photography, testimony, and
conversion copy for an African-market site; or auditing an existing
site's cultural fit with its primary market.

**Purpose**: African markets are not culturally interchangeable. A
template that works in Kampala will not always work in Lagos, let
alone Johannesburg or Cairo. This reference catalogues the market-
by-market patterns that shape how trust, conversion, and voice land.

## Treat this as guidance, not stereotype

- Markets are heterogeneous. Any generalisation below has exceptions.
- The guidance is a default starting point; client-specific research
  refines it. Never use this as a substitute for talking to real
  buyers in the target market.
- Decisions that lean on patterns here are recorded in the strategy
  brief and revisited quarterly.

## Testimony hierarchy

"Whose word counts?" varies by market. The engine adjusts which
testimonials lead.

| Market | Primary trust source | Secondary | Supporting |
|---|---|---|---|
| Uganda, Kenya | Named individual with photo and title | Business endorsement with logo | Community / church leader |
| Nigeria | Named business (buyer recognises) | Named individual with title | Public figure endorsement (used sparingly) |
| Ghana | Named business / institution | Named individual | Community elder / pastor |
| Rwanda | Government or institutional endorsement | Named business | Individual |
| South Africa | Named business with B-BBEE context | Named individual | Sector-body endorsement |
| Francophone West Africa (Senegal, CI) | Community or religious endorsement | Named business | Individual |
| Ethiopia | Institutional endorsement | Named business | Individual |

Implication: a site that leads with "★★★★★ from Sarah G." underperforms
in markets where institutional endorsement carries weight.

## Community framing vs individual framing

In most of East and West Africa, conversion copy works harder when
framed in community terms:

- "Join 1,200 Ugandan pharmacy owners using our platform" > "Take
  control of your pharmacy stock."
- "Built for Ghanaian SMEs" > "Built for business."
- "Trusted by MTN, Airtel, Total" (when true) > "Trusted by industry
  leaders."

South African, Egyptian, and Moroccan urban B2B markets are more
receptive to individual-agency framing; test per project.

## Formality vs warmth

A two-axis read per market.

| Market | Register | Voice |
|---|---|---|
| Uganda | Mid-formal with warmth | "We" + named team; service-hearted |
| Kenya | Formal-urban, warm-small-business | Varies sharply by audience |
| Nigeria | Confident, direct | Assertive; "We build X" beats "X might help you" |
| Ghana | Warm, respectful | Community-rooted; elder-respect cues |
| Rwanda | Formal, ordered | Institutional; government-partner tone reads positively |
| South Africa (urban) | Professional, crisp | Global B2B register acceptable |
| Francophone West Africa | Respectful, elder-aware | Community framing, proverb-aware |
| North Africa | Formal, authority-signalled | Institutional voice; avoid too-casual |
| Ethiopia | Formal, status-aware | Senior-voice leadership signals trust |

Do not assume "friendly American SaaS" voice travels. It often reads
as unserious in markets that expect weight.

## Photography conventions

- **Named faces over stock.** In every market the engine serves,
  stock photography reads as foreign. Budget for real photography.
- **Owner and team.** A named founder or manager photo near trust
  signals outperforms abstract "team" shots.
- **Real customers where consent permits.** A named customer using
  the product in context outperforms a studio portrait.
- **Setting and context matter.** Shoot in the user's real
  environment — shop interior, field, clinic — not in a generic
  co-working space.
- **Dress codes vary.** Formal business attire reads positively in
  Lagos, Cairo, Johannesburg enterprise contexts; relaxed-smart
  reads positively in Kigali, Kampala SME contexts.

Banned:
- Generic diverse-office-stock with laptops and whiteboards.
- AI-generated portraits.
- Stock photography repurposed across multiple client sites.

## Religious and cultural sensitivity

- **Religion is present.** Most markets in the region are religious-
  majority. Neutral, respectful framing is the default; religious
  signalling is client-dependent (e.g. a church-aligned business may
  prominently display faith cues; a health NGO may not).
- **Imagery considerations.** Avoid imagery that conflicts with local
  norms (dress, consumption, gestures). Test with local reviewers.
- **Time and calendar.** Ramadan in Muslim-majority markets affects
  business hours, campaign timing, and launch windows. Schedule
  accordingly and mention consideration in client proposals.

## Price and pricing-display patterns

- **Show the price.** In many African B2C markets, sites that hide
  prices behind "Contact us" read as scammy. B2B is more flexible.
- **Payment cadence matters.** Monthly subscriptions are common;
  annual-only pricing narrows the market.
- **Family / group pricing.** Some categories (insurance, health)
  respond to family-tier pricing patterns more than individual-only.

## Language switcher placement

- **Top-right desktop, accessible in mobile header.** Not buried in
  footer.
- **Language labels in their own script.** "English / Kiswahili /
  Luganda", not "EN / SW / LG".
- **Persistent choice.** Cookie the user's language; do not re-
  greet them in English when they return.

## Testimonial format preferences by market

| Market | Preferred format |
|---|---|
| Uganda, Kenya, Rwanda | Video (30–60 s) with named speaker; photo + quote second |
| Nigeria | Short quote + logo grid of known buyers |
| Ghana | Photo + quote; community-context matters |
| South Africa (urban B2B) | Logo grid + case studies with numeric outcomes |
| Francophone WA | Short audio / radio-quote format reads well; community focus |

## Humour, proverbs, and local references

- **Proverbs land well when used sparingly.** Overuse is performative.
- **Humour travels poorly.** A joke that works in Lagos may read as
  rude in Addis Ababa.
- **Local references build trust.** A pharmacy site that mentions
  Nakawa or Owino market by name reads local. A generic "city of
  Kampala" phrasing reads foreign.

## Visual hierarchy of trust

For most African markets, the engine's default trust order is:

1. A recognisable human face (owner, founder, named customer).
2. A concrete, dated specific outcome ("reduced stockouts by 30%
   across 200 pharmacies since 2024").
3. A regulator or association badge that is linked.
4. A community or institutional endorsement.
5. Certifications and standards marks.

South African and North African enterprise B2B often reorder 3 and
1; verify per project.

## Verification in-market

Ship-readiness check for cultural fit:

- A native-speaker reviewer walks the site and annotates any phrase,
  image, or pattern that reads wrong.
- A customer-proxy interview: "What does this site make you think the
  business is?" Answers that diverge significantly from the client's
  intent are the actionable gaps.

## Cross-market projects

A single site serving multiple African markets needs:

- Language switcher per market.
- Currency display per market (the localized pricing where relevant).
- Trust signals per market (see `africa-trust-signals.md`).
- Testimonials from at least one named speaker per market — not one
  set recycled.
- A way for the user to self-identify their market on entry without
  pushiness (a subtle top-bar, not a modal).
