# Hybrid Agency Operating Model

The internal operating model for a premium hybrid website agency (strategy +
design + development + content + SEO + analytics under one roof), drawn from
Paul Roetzer's *The Marketing Agency Blueprint* (Wiley, 2012).

The book's tooling is dated; the operating model is remarkably durable and
maps almost exactly onto a $12k–$45k build agency selling $750–$3,000/month
retainers in 2026. This file extracts the durable principles and discards
the dated tooling.

This file pairs with `retainer-package-catalog.md` (what we sell),
`expansion-offers-matrix.md` (how we expand accounts),
`subscription-economy-model.md` (the recurring-revenue financial model),
and `okrs-and-client-reporting.md` (the measurement layer).

## Part 1 — The hybrid agency thesis

Roetzer's central claim: the marketing-services industry is being
disrupted (Christensen's sense) and the winning firm is the **hybrid** —
one roof covering PR + SEO + content + web + advertising + social +
email + analytics, not silo'd specialists federated together.

He classifies the ecosystem into 6 firm types:

| Type | Role | Trajectory |
|------|------|-----------|
| **Disruptors** | Tech-savvy, integrated, versatile talent, value-based pricing | Dominant |
| **Traditionalists** | Strong legacy brand, top-heavy staffing, struggle to integrate digital | Declining |
| **Softservers** | Software companies with service arms (HubSpot, etc.) | Rising, structural advantage |
| **Specialists** | Niche (SEO-only, PR-only, video-only) | Project-based, acquisition targets |
| **Connectors** | Networkers/thinkers with distributed subcontractor networks | Lifestyle |
| **Soloists** | Freelancers | Capacity-limited |

We are a Disruptor. The operating model below is what makes that work.

### What "hybrid" structurally means

1. **Immersion** — leadership obsessively tracks tech-industry news and
   adopts tooling early.
2. **Integration** — digital and traditional services are not divisions;
   they're woven through every engagement, led by generalists who blend
   strategies.
3. **Outsourcing plus partnership** — rather than build every competency
   in-house, form trusted partnerships with complementary firms (soloists
   and specialists) using compatible PM tooling so workflows mesh.

Three catalysts drive the shift (Roetzer's analytical frame): **change
velocity** (tech innovation rate), **selective consumption** (buyers
choose when and where to engage; inbound beats interruption), and
**success factors** (measurable outcomes replace impressions).

## Part 2 — Productized services (Roetzer's structural contribution)

In 2004 Roetzer decided the billable hour was broken and spent **21
months** building a productized service model. In January 2006 he
published the **20/20 Standard** — a public online service menu with **19
categories, 102 standardized services, set prices**.

### The Roetzer productized service template

Every service product is defined by:

- **Description** — one paragraph, benefit-oriented.
- **What makes a good [X]?** — qualifying criteria so clients self-assess
  fit.
- **What's included?** — concrete deliverable spec (e.g. "Approximately
  700 words. Optimised with priority keywords and written with buyer
  personas' key needs in mind.")
- **Turnaround** — e.g. "15–20 business days."
- **Set price** — value-based, not hourly.
- **Add-on fees** clearly stated (graphic design, extra strategic
  planning).

Key principle:

> "If you can define the scope, you can standardize the service and
> assign a set price. The burden is on the agency to build systems and
> processes, and put the right talent in place, to profitably deliver
> services at set prices."

Our entire skill library (this repository) is the documented playbook
that makes productisation possible. Without it, every engagement
re-invents itself and margin collapses.

### What stays variable

Not everything productises. Roetzer keeps hourly billing for **genuinely
variable scope** (open-ended consulting, specific media-relations work).
For us, the equivalent is the strategic discovery work *before*
productised tier selection — that's intellectual labour where the
deliverable is judgement, not a defined output.

## Part 3 — Pricing model and KPIs

### The 7-Variable Value-Based Pricing Formula

Not hours × rate. Roetzer explicitly lists seven variables for any
priced engagement:

1. **Estimated Hours** — at *peak efficiency*, not actual. Client does
   not pay for agency inefficiency.
2. **Hourly Revenue Target (HRT)** — the per-hour revenue your agency
   needs to hit profit goals. Worked example: 5-person agency, $600,000
   revenue goal, 5,880 client-service hours/year → **HRT = $102/hour**.
3. **Costs** — pass-through (designer, wire service, licensing).
4. **Perceived Value** — fair market: what will buyers actually pay.
5. **Builder vs. Driver** — foundation work vs. short-term results.
   Drivers command premium pricing because they produce measurable
   outcomes.
6. **Loss Leader** — only acceptable with a 12-month contract attached.
   "If prospects or clients want plans for free, they will never value
   your agency's services and knowledge."
7. **Service Level** — basic / intermediate / advanced talent required.

### Revenue-Efficiency Rate (RER) — the central pricing KPI

> **RER = [(Price − Costs) / Hours] / HRT**

A 100% RER means one service hour produces HRT dollars.

Worked examples:

- Website project — $12,000 gross profit / 120 hours / $100 HRT = **100%
  RER** (healthy).
- Marketing plan — $5,000 / 120 hours / $100 HRT = **42% RER** (bad
  unless attached to a 12-month contract, in which case it's a justified
  loss-leader).

### Roetzer's recurring-revenue rules

- **80%+ of annual revenue from 12-month+ contracts.**
- **No single client > 20% of annual revenue** (concentration risk).
- Project-based work is for soloists/specialists; agency growth requires
  campaign/retainer book.
- Target **35% of revenue for base salaries, 50% for total labour
  costs**; net profit margin **10–25%** depending on size/growth stage.

### Time tracking (even in value-based pricing)

This is critical and often misunderstood. "Accurate time tracking
actually becomes *more* essential in order to monitor efficiency and
productivity, evaluate employee performance, produce activity reports,
and evolve pricing."

Every lead also goes in the time-tracker so you can assess business-
development efficiency.

## Part 4 — Capacity math and hiring discipline

### One hire per 120–140 client-service hours/month sustained demand

Per-person monthly client-hour targets:

| Role | Client hours/month |
|------|-------------------|
| Associate consultant (0–3 yrs) | 120–140 |
| Consultant (3–7 yrs) | 100–120 |
| Senior consultant (7+ yrs) | 80–100 |
| Founder/CEO | ~50 |

The senior person carries fewer client hours because they own pricing,
positioning, hiring, business development, and quality oversight.

### Soft and absolute capacity

- **Soft capacity** = 100% utilisation of stated client-hour targets.
- **Absolute capacity** = soft + 20%.

If forecasts exceed absolute capacity for 3+ months, hire. Don't hire on
one-month spikes.

### Defined hiring triggers (when to hire ahead of need)

- Major growth initiative launching.
- New partnership demand incoming.
- Deep pipeline (3+ months of forecasted demand at absolute capacity).
- Preparing to transition an underperformer.
- Understaffed core account requiring rebalance.
- Chance to grab a high-potential A-player.

### Retention — model agencies recruit to retain

AAAA data Roetzer cites: **30% of agency workforce turns over in 12
months; 70% would return a recruiter's call.**

> "Model agencies recruit to retain. They seek to hire career
> professionals who view the agency as a destination rather than a
> steppingstone."

Retention drivers (in order of leverage):

1. Intrinsic motivation (Pink: autonomy, mastery, purpose).
2. Clear career path documented.
3. Purpose — the agency stands for something.
4. Culture — daily rhythm, productivity blocks, shared rituals.
5. Compensation — necessary but not primary.

## Part 5 — The 17 A-player traits + 9-step recruiting system

### A-player competencies (the trait rubric)

Analytical; balanced (work-life); confident (not arrogant); creative;
detail-oriented; focused (no multitasking); intrinsically motivated;
"it factor"; listener; positive; relationship-builder; risk-taker;
social-web savvy; strategic; team player; tech-savvy; **writer**.

Roetzer: *"Copywriting is one of the most valuable competencies in a
marketing-agency professional."*

### The 9-step recruiting system

1. Start with brand and culture clearly defined.
2. Define career path on the careers page.
3. Strong online presence (the agency's authority engine doubles as
   talent magnet).
4. Capture inbound candidates via careers page + qualifier questionnaire.
5. Conduct informational interviews even when not hiring.
6. Be selective — declining a hire is cheaper than firing one.
7. Monitor social signals on candidates.
8. Offline networking.
9. Standard grading rubric (1–5 on the 17 traits + yes/no "it factor"
   field). *If unsure on "it factor," answer is no.*

### The hybrid training programme

No college produces hybrid marketing professionals. Build them:

- Webinars (curated required viewing).
- Books — Roetzer's list includes *Good to Great*, *Innovator's
  Dilemma*, *Rework*, *Drive*, *Topgrading*, *Mastering the Rockefeller
  Habits*. We add: *Subscribed* (Tzuo), *The Automatic Customer*
  (Warrillow), *Measure What Matters* (Doerr), *They Ask You Answer*
  (Sheridan), *Show Your Work* (Kleon), *Content Inc.* (Pulizzi).
- Real-time sharing on internal channel (Slack).
- Knowledge transfer via written playbooks (this skill repository).
- Role-playing exercises.
- Conferences.
- Online courses — every consultant required to pass Google AdWords
  Fundamentals + Google Analytics IQ (we add: a sector-specific
  certification per practice area).
- Blog-post writing exercises.
- Group book-study exercises.

### Career path (default 3 titles)

- **Associate Consultant** (0–3 yrs) — producer, 120–140 client
  hrs/month.
- **Consultant** (3–7 yrs) — primary day-to-day client contact, 100–120.
- **Senior Consultant** (7+ yrs) — builder/innovator/manager, 80–100.

Plus optional overlay roles: Group Manager, AVP, VP. Roetzer
deliberately chose "consultant" over "account executive" to frame the
team as strategists, not practitioners.

## Part 6 — Internal operating rhythm

### Daily, weekly, monthly cadence

| When | What | Length |
|------|------|--------|
| **Daily huddle** (Tue–Fri, 8:15 AM) | Trends, account issues, each person's top 3 priorities, day's events | 15 min |
| **Monday Morning Meeting (M3)** | Good news / numbers / agency / project center / loyalty / pipeline / priorities | 30–45 min |
| **Group meetings** (Monday after M3) | 3–5 people per group, up to 12 clients per group reviewed | 15–30 min |
| **Quarterly** | Campaign reviews, budget reviews, OKR setting | 2 hours |
| **Monthly** | Forecasts, scorecards, P&L review, retainer churn review | 90 min |

### The Agency Manager Dashboard (the founder's spreadsheet)

Roetzer built this in Excel because no product fit at the time. Modern
equivalent: a Notion/Coda/Airtable workspace with the same fields.
Contents:

- Monthly client-hour forecasts by client × employee.
- Account status (project vs. campaign) for every client.
- Income potential rank 1–5 by forecasted annual revenue.
- Capacity: soft (100%) and absolute (120%).
- Revenue-efficiency rates per account.
- Forecast vs actual.
- Sortable by priority.

### The KPIs the founder reads

- Revenue per employee — industry benchmark $120,000 (Roetzer); we
  target $150k+ given the premium tier.
- RER per account (100% target).
- % revenue from 12-month contracts (target: 50%+ Year 1, 80%+ Year 3).
- Largest client as % of revenue (<20%).
- Soft/absolute capacity utilisation.
- Client hours logged by professional vs forecast.
- Monthly forecasts vs actuals.
- Sales pipeline: lead volume, quality distribution, conversion rate,
  cycle time.

Plus the subscription-business dashboard from
`subscription-economy-model.md` (MRR, churn, NRR, GEI, etc.).

### Cash-flow discipline

Revenue ≠ cash. Roetzer's worked example: $84k invoices, $82k expenses
looks fine until two clients pay late and an unplanned $3k IT bill hits
— suddenly drawing reserves.

Keep a daily pulse on both sides. The Care & Maintenance retainer book
(per `retainer-package-catalog.md`) is the cash-flow stabiliser — it
collects in advance, is highest gross margin, and pays the rent while
project work pulses.

## Part 7 — Sales and lead-management system

### MAD-R lead qualification

A prospect must have:

- **M**oney (budget).
- **A**uthority (decision power).
- **D**esire (wants the services and you specifically).
- **R**esponse (actually engaged with your marketing).

Leads without all four are "suspects" — noise. Pairs with our existing
`agency-positioning/references/qualification-guide.md` and
`lead-qualification-scorecard.md`.

### The 5-Factor Discovery Survey

15–20 minute lead questionnaire covering:

1. **Foundation** — existing brand/site/infra strength.
2. **Platform** — reach and influence.
3. **Expectations** — priority needs and goals.
4. **Potential** — can the agency have sustained impact.
5. **GamePlan strategy** — what strategies fit.

### The 6-Part Proposal Template

Roetzer's structure (also reflected in our existing `proposal-skills`
submodule):

1. **Snapshot** — executive summary, "where accounts are won," 1–2
   pages, narrative not jargon.
2. **Discovery** — keyword analysis, competitor audit, social audit,
   content gap analysis.
3. **Objectives** — measurable, benchmarked.
4. **Activity Center** — services, pricing, timeline; standardised
   package customised.
5. **Appendix** — team profiles, experience, testimonials.
6. **Terms of Agreement** — plain-language contract: pricing structure,
   payment terms, reporting, expenses, add-on billing.

### Buying cycle (5 stages)

Awareness → Search → Evaluation → Decision → Postpurchase. Match
content and outreach to the stage.

### Client references policy

Only provide references when (a) you're 100% sure you want the client,
AND (b) they've confirmed references are the *final step* before
signing. Otherwise — direct prospects to case studies, online reviews,
and deep team/methodology content on the site.

This protects existing clients from being beaten up for reference calls
that go nowhere.

### Sales-team reality check

Kurlan & Associates' evaluations of 100+ agency professionals showed
**none could run a consultative sales process consistently; <3% could
qualify a prospect adequately; 100% had no sales process; 92% wouldn't
hunt new opportunities.**

Conclusion: sales requires systems + training; don't assume your account
people can sell. The senior strategist + founder are the sales team
until the agency is past 12 people.

## Part 8 — Client relationship management

### Onboarding

30-day GamePlan development:

- Discovery (week 1).
- Benchmarks (week 2).
- 12-month approach (week 3).
- Q1 activities approved (week 4).

### Monthly scorecard (3 sections)

Per Roetzer (and aligned with our existing `monthly-report-template.md`):

1. **Snapshot dashboard** — top metrics, comparisons, RAG flags.
2. **Analysis** — website grade, traffic, keywords, inbound links, lead
   sources/quality, blog stats, social reach/engagement, conversions.
3. **Updated GamePlan** for next month with reallocated time/budget.

### The Account Team Dashboard (Excel/Notion, per campaign client)

Ownership of every standard activity. Visible to the team. Updated
weekly. Replaces the "who was supposed to do what" chaos that kills
retention.

### The Client-in-Residence (CIR) — Roetzer's structural innovation

A senior agency leader whose full-time job is to assess accounts from
the *client's* perspective.

- Sits in on client calls.
- Reviews monthly analytics.
- Challenges account-team strategy.
- Questions invoices.
- Advocates for client interests.

For small agencies (< 12 people): the founder plays this role part-time.
For larger agencies: full-time hire. **This is Roetzer's structural
answer to how quality scales without founder bandwidth.**

### Account prioritisation and churn signals

All clients are NOT equal. Ranking factors:

- **Efficiency (RER)** — anchor accounts (Roetzer's term: "$100k/yr
  billings consuming 2,000 hours") are unprofitable regardless of
  top-line.
- **Financial stability** — 15+ days past due = red flag, often a sign
  of trouble. "You have to be willing to walk away."
- **Realistic expectations.**
- **Leadership support** — engaged client decision-maker.
- **Shared values and working fit.**
- **Commitment length and budget history.**
- **Growth opportunities.**
- **Profile and platform** — credibility halo.
- **Experience** — niche depth.
- **Innovation** — willing to try things → case-study material.

### Long-Term Locke (the prototype buyer persona)

Roetzer publishes his ideal-client profile explicitly. Adapted for our
agency:

- Budget: $12k+ build + $1.5k+/month retainer.
- 12-month contracts.
- Moderate growth potential.
- Integrated services (web + content + SEO + analytics, not à la carte).
- Service-level mix 30/60/10 (basic/moderate/advanced).
- Sectors: financial services, healthcare, professional services,
  technology B2B.
- Title: founder, partner, or marketing director.
- Low-risk profile, moderate-to-high profitability potential.

**Target portfolio allocation: 60–75% of clients fitting the Long-Term
Locke profile.**

### Relationship diversification

Never have a single point of contact in a client organisation. Multiple
relationships = survivable when your champion leaves.

For every retainer client: maintain active working relationships with at
least 2 people on the client side (typically the day-to-day contact +
the decision-maker).

## Part 9 — Service categories (the hybrid agency mix)

Roetzer's full hybrid mix (every competency either in-house or
partnered):

- Brand marketing.
- Website development.
- SEO (on-page + link-building + long-tail content).
- Online advertising / PPC.
- Local search.
- Email marketing & lead nurturing.
- Social media marketing.
- Copywriting.
- Video production.
- PR.
- Graphic design.
- Mobile.
- Content marketing.
- Analytics / measurement.

Our agency's actual service mix (Phase 1 & 7 decisions): brand strategy,
website strategy + design + development, SEO foundation, content
engine, conversion architecture, multilingual rollout, technical
support. We **partner** for: paid media, video production, PR, mobile
apps. We **decline**: black-hat SEO, pay-for-performance, generic
"social media management."

### Partner-evaluation criteria

When choosing soloists/specialists to partner with:

- Services complementary (limited crossover with our in-house).
- Pricing transparency and range compatibility.
- Matching process tooling (so workflows mesh).
- Performance track record.
- Financial stability.

## Part 10 — Scalability — growing past founder bandwidth

### The 2×–3× scale rule

At every infrastructure decision, ask "will this solution work at 2× to
3× our current size?" Roetzer at 5 employees built office/phone/server
for 15 employees, with option on adjacent space for 25.

### Infrastructure time horizons

- **Current** (12 months) — plan firmly.
- **Short term** (1–3 years) — plan firmly.
- **Mid term** (3–5 years) — plan contingencies.
- **Long term** (5+ years) — vision only; no infrastructure bets.

### The 5 infrastructure lessons

1. **Prepare for perpetual change** — iterative; agile.
2. **Build through trusted solution providers** — test startups, deploy
   on stable.
3. **Understand your limits** — HR capacity is the real ceiling.
4. **Find reliable advisors and mentors** — advisory board covering
   finance, tech, HR, legal, accounting.
5. **Create a funding runway** — before you need it.

### The documented playbook is the scalability lever

Roetzer's entire book is essentially his agency's playbook written out.
Every system documented: pricing formula, grading rubric, meeting
cadence, talent-evaluation framework, proposal template, buyer persona
templates, change-velocity assessment format, client-onboarding flow.

For us: this skill repository is the documented playbook. Every SKILL.md
file is a productised internal capability. New hires onboard against the
repo, not against tribal knowledge. This is what makes scaling beyond
founder bandwidth possible.

## Part 11 — Revenue diversification (multiple streams)

The hybrid doesn't only sell services. Roetzer names 5 revenue streams:

1. **Services** (campaign retainers — the core).
2. **VAR / reseller commissions** (HubSpot's 20% lifetime margin was
   the canonical example; modern equivalents: tooling partnerships).
3. **Affiliate programs.**
4. **Educator / publisher** (speaking, online courses, webinars,
   ebooks, books, teaching).
5. **Software** (higher margins but dangerous — Mark Suster's warning
   that service companies chasing "product envy" usually damage their
   core).

For our agency:

- Year 1–3: services only (engagements + retainers + expansion offers).
- Year 3+: educator/publisher (paid roundtable, annual benchmark
  report, signature-talk derivatives).
- Year 5+: consider VAR for our preferred tooling stack.
- Software: explicitly avoided. We are a services firm with a media
  function, not a SaaS startup.

## Part 12 — The 12 pitfalls Roetzer names

Failure modes to avoid:

1. **Product envy** — services firm sets up a skunkworks product
   division, resources drain from core, both fail.
2. **Selling digital before you can do digital** — overpromise to win
   business, can't deliver, reputation dies.
3. **Hiring ahead of sustained demand** (or on one-month spikes) → cash
   crunch; alternatively, hiring too late → overwork, quality
   collapse.
4. **Black-hat shortcuts** — JCPenney/Google Panda case: short-term
   wins, long-term catastrophe.
5. **Plans as loss leaders** — comprehensive strategic plans given
   cheaply, client uses them to hire in-house and walks away.
6. **Single-point-of-contact relationships** — champion leaves, account
   dies.
7. **Over-reliance on one big client** (>20%) — overexposed.
8. **Top-heavy staffing** — traditionalists' curse; makes value-based
   pricing mathematically impossible.
9. **RFP addiction** — "archaic process that devalues agency experience
   and expertise."
10. **Growth for growth's sake without a profitable model** — "little
    more than an entrepreneurial ego boost."
11. **"Always-wired" professional culture** — multitasking kills
    productivity; every interruption costs ~45 min of refocus time.
    Remedy: mandatory 4-hour-minimum unplugged blocks (Roetzer's PR
    20/20 used 9–11 AM and 1–3 PM productivity blocks).
12. **LeBron parable** — leaders who delegate when they should be taking
    the shot themselves. Leaders must lead when it's all on the line.

## Part 13 — What's now dated vs durable

### Use the principles, skip the tools

**Specific tools Roetzer recommends that are dated or gone:**

- HubSpot VAR program — still exists but commoditised; first-mover
  advantage gone.
- TimeFox, Highrise, Yammer, GoToMeeting, Website Grader, SEOmoz — all
  superseded.

**Dated tactical content:**

- SEO ranking factor breakdowns from 2012 — modern SEO centres on
  E-E-A-T, Core Web Vitals, semantic search, LLM-era GEO signals.
- Social-network list (Twitter/Facebook/Google+/LinkedIn/YouTube) —
  Google+ dead; X, TikTok, Threads, Bluesky added.
- Specific Panda/spam references — period pieces.
- Press releases on the wire as a primary strategy — largely theatre
  now.

### What's enduringly true (carry forward)

- Value-based productized services with set prices and defined SLAs.
- The 7-variable pricing model and RER formula.
- 80%/20% recurring-revenue and concentration rules.
- One-hire-per-120-140-hours capacity math.
- Soft/absolute capacity + 3-month trigger hiring discipline.
- Buyer-persona-driven sales process.
- MAD-R qualification.
- 6-part proposal template.
- Client-in-Residence role.
- Monthly scorecard structure.
- Builder-vs-driver service taxonomy.
- Daily huddle + M3 + group meeting + productivity-block rhythm.
- A-player trait rubric + grading system.
- Documented playbook as the scalability lever.
- Revenue diversification into education/publishing.
- "Draft-first, free-agents-second" talent model.
- Partner-ecosystem discipline.
