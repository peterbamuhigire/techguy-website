# Post-launch Retention Playbook

The 12 months after launch are the agency's largest commercial opportunity. This playbook turns one-off launches into multi-year relationships.

## Retention mindset

A launched site is not a delivered project. It is a starting point. Premium agencies hold themselves accountable for outcome, not output. Outcome takes 90 days to begin showing and 12 months to compound.

## Operating cadence

- Day 0–7: hypercare window via `post-launch-support-runbook`.
- Day 8–30: stabilisation; first quick wins.
- Day 31–90: first experiment cycle; first quantitative results.
- Quarter 2: first quarterly business review; renewal conversation begins.
- Quarter 3: scope expansion conversations where evidence supports it.
- Quarter 4: case study refresh, public proof, multi-year renewal.

## Retention triggers (positive)

Watch for and act on these signals:

- KPI movement at or above target.
- Client introducing the agency to peers or sister organisations.
- Inbound from the client's customers asking who built the site.
- Client asking "what next" without a fee discussion.
- Client speaking publicly about the engagement.

For each trigger, prepare a conversation: scope expansion, referral programme, public case study, advisory retainer.

## Retention triggers (negative)

Watch for and act on these:

- Quiet retainer (no inbound for two weeks). Schedule a proactive review.
- Late client decisions delaying experiments. Surface the cost in the next monthly report.
- Repeated escalations on similar issues. Run a structural review.
- Decision maker change on the client side. Re-establish trust early.
- Budget pressure in the client's wider business. Discuss scope, do not panic-discount.

## Relationship rituals

- Weekly: short written update.
- Monthly: full report via `monthly-report`.
- Quarterly: business review via `quarterly-business-review.md`.
- Twice a year: in-person or video session with the agency principal.
- Annual: renewal and roadmap meeting.

## Loyalty through value, not lock-in

Premium retention does not depend on switching cost. It depends on visible value and trust.

- Open documentation. The client's team can take the site over without the agency at any time.
- Open data. The client owns analytics, KPIs, and reporting.
- Honest reporting, including bad numbers.
- Public-facing proof points the client benefits from (case study, sector visibility).
- A referral path that benefits the client when they refer.

A relationship the client could walk out of cleanly tomorrow but does not is the goal.

## Scope expansion conversations

Expand scope when:

- The data justifies it (a metric is plateauing without further investment).
- The client's business is changing (new product, new market, new region).
- The agency has earned the right (KPI movement, on-time delivery, low drama).

The conversation pattern:

1. Show the current trajectory and the next plateau.
2. Show three options with cost and outcome.
3. Recommend one with rationale.
4. Allow the client to decline without damage to the existing retainer.

## Case study and public proof

With consent, every retained client becomes a public proof asset over time.

- Quarter 2: anonymised case study draft.
- Quarter 4: named case study with KPI movement and quotes.
- Year 2: refreshed case study with retention story, not just launch story.

Public proof is a long-term commercial multiplier. It also reinforces the loyalty story to the client themselves: they see their progress documented.

## Exit cleanly when needed

Some retainers end. Make exit a clean process, not a slow drift:

- Three months before: notify in writing if the agency proposes to end.
- One month before: deliver the handover plan.
- Final month: handover sessions, documentation, credentials.
- After exit: 14-day courtesy support window.
- Six months later: courtesy check-in if both sides remain in good standing.

A clean exit becomes a future referral or a future re-engagement. A messy exit becomes a public liability.

## Anti-patterns

- "Set and forget" retainers that bill without obvious value.
- Hidden lock-in via undocumented systems or proprietary frameworks the client cannot exit.
- Discount-led renewals that erode margin and signal weakness.
- Loyalty programmes that benefit nobody but the agency's reporting.
- Treating retention as marketing instead of as service quality.
