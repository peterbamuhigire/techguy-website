# Agency Narrative

The one-paragraph internal story that anchors every downstream artefact — sales pages,
proposals, case studies, content, onboarding, retainer pitches. If a sentence does not
serve the narrative, it does not ship.

Grounded in the Phase 1 offer architecture and in the reading set for this phase
(Warrillow, Enns, Weiss, Robinson, Block).

Companion files: `premium-website-offer-playbook.md`, `pricing-rules.md`,
`scope-matrix.md`, `qualification-guide.md`, `proposal-positioning.md`.

---

## 1. The agency category

> **Authority Website Systems for high-trust businesses.**

Full phrasing for first-contact contexts:

> **We build authority website systems for businesses whose reputation is their primary
> asset.**

Compressed phrasing for repeat contexts:

> **Authority websites for high-trust firms.**

The category name is deliberate and should not be rewritten without cause:

- *Authority* signals expertise-led work, diagnosis before prescription, strategy before
  design. It separates us from execution shops.
- *Website systems* signals a productized deliverable — a named process, a repeatable
  system, a transferable asset — not a custom-every-time artefact. It separates us from
  bespoke agencies that trade margin for novelty.
- *High-trust businesses* signals the niche: firms for whom a generic website is a
  commercial liability. It separates us from general web agencies serving every sector.

## 2. The agency narrative (canonical paragraph)

> We build high-trust, conversion-ready website systems for businesses that cannot afford
> to look generic. Every engagement combines positioning, messaging, premium design, SEO
> foundations, and rigorous delivery standards so the website becomes a working business
> asset — visible, measurable, and operable without the founder's personal presence. We
> work with professional services, legal, healthcare, education, and premium local
> firms where reputation is the primary asset and trust is the buying criterion. We are
> not cheaper than a freelancer or faster than a template shop. We are the firm a trust-
> heavy business hires when it can no longer afford a generic website.

Use this paragraph verbatim in internal onboarding. Every team member should be able to
say it from memory within a week of joining.

## 3. The one-sentence versions

Three registers. Pick the one that matches the context.

- **Expert posture (first-call opener).** *"We build authority website systems for
  high-trust firms — the kind where the site carries the reputation, not just the
  contact form."*
- **Commercial posture (proposal and sales page).** *"We build the websites that
  professional services, legal, healthcare, and education firms use to turn reputation
  into revenue."*
- **Peer posture (networking, referral introductions).** *"We do authority websites —
  mostly consulting firms, law firms, clinics, institutions. Strategy-led, productized
  delivery, $12k and up."*

## 4. What the agency is, restated

Four propositions that distinguish the work commercially:

1. **Strategy first.** We do not design until positioning is agreed. We do not build
   until strategy is signed. We do not sell without the value conversation.
2. **Premium execution.** Modern technical standards, distinctive design, performance
   budgets, accessibility conformance, documented systems.
3. **Measurable outcomes.** Every engagement ships with analytics, conversion events,
   and a measurement plan. The client can tell whether the site is working; so can we.
4. **Delivered as a system, not a file.** Documentation, training, and a handover kit
   are part of the deliverable. The site survives the launch, staff turnover, and
   agency handoff.

These four propositions are the commercial logic behind the $12,000 floor and the
$85,000 ceiling. Each of them is labour-intensive enough that the floor must exist, and
each is valuable enough that the ceiling is defensible.

## 5. What the agency is not, restated

Negative positioning is positioning. The category holds only when these lines are held:

- We are not a web design agency. We are not a design firm. Design is one of our tools.
- We are not developers for hire. We do not take pair-of-hands work.
- We are not a full-service marketing agency. We do not run ads, produce social content,
  or sell email campaigns.
- We are not an SEO agency. SEO is embedded in our work; it is not the offer.
- We are not a brand identity studio. We align brand; we do not reinvent it.
- We are not an e-commerce agency. We do not build carts.
- We are not the cheapest option. If price is the buying criterion, we are not the firm.
- We are not the fastest option. If "next week" is the deadline, we are not the firm.
- We are not a subcontractor for other agencies. We do not white-label.

## 6. The client the agency is built for

The agency is built around one buyer profile, stated explicitly so every sales and
delivery decision can check against it:

**The principal-led firm, 10–250 people, revenue $1M–$30M, where reputation is the
primary commercial asset and the current website materially understates the firm.**

Signals that this is the buyer in the room:

- Referrals are the dominant lead channel
- Enterprise buyers decide in weeks, not minutes
- Trust is the buying criterion — the prospect is vetting the firm, not shopping
- The principal(s) are the expertise; staff extend it
- The firm has outgrown its current positioning and knows it
- There is a dated event (funding, launch, repositioning, expansion, partner change)
  creating commercial pressure to act

Every other signal is noise. Every other buyer is a potential referral, a potential
pass-through, or a polite no.

## 7. The promise the narrative makes

A premium engagement makes a specific commercial promise to the client:

> We will build a website system that carries the firm's reputation credibly to
> referral traffic, new-market traffic, and enterprise buyers — in a way your current
> site does not — and that can be operated, measured, and maintained without our
> ongoing presence.

This is a promise of positioning made operational, not a promise of traffic or leads.
Traffic and leads are downstream consequences of positioning, content, and trust signals
landing correctly. We do not promise outcomes we do not control.

---

## 8. The commitments the narrative requires us to make

Holding the narrative means holding these behaviours, without exception:

- We state the minimum engagement ($12,000) in every first conversation
- We diagnose before we prescribe
- We recommend one direction, not three
- We present proposals in conversation, not by email
- We say the price out loud before the document is shown
- We get paid before work begins
- We write change orders before extra work begins
- We fire clients who cannot be led
- We refuse work outside the sector and scope, and refer it elsewhere
- We log every waived minimum, every scope change, every lost pitch — and review them
  quarterly

These are not tactics. They are the behavioural expression of the category. If we do not
behave this way, we cannot claim the category.

## 9. Downstream usage

Every artefact downstream of this file should be able to point back to a specific line in
this narrative. The test:

- *Agency sales page.* Does the hero make the one-sentence promise in section 3? Does
  the body explain the four propositions in section 4? Does it name the buyer in
  section 6?
- *Proposal.* Do the situation appraisal and value sections reflect the narrative's
  commercial promise (section 7)? Do the joint accountabilities reflect the commitments
  (section 8)?
- *Onboarding.* Can a new team member recite the paragraph in section 2 and explain what
  it means?
- *Content and thought leadership.* Every post should make the reader more certain they
  are hearing from a specialist in this niche — not a general web agency.
- *Pricing conversation.* Does every price quoted map to the floor, tier, and
  value-first logic in `pricing-rules.md`?
- *Qualification.* Does every "no" come from the rules in `qualification-guide.md`?

If an artefact drifts from the narrative, the artefact is wrong. The narrative does not
flex to the artefact; the artefact re-aligns to the narrative.

---

## 10. Review

The narrative is reviewed every 12 months, or whenever the market position shifts.
Changes are:

- made in writing
- dated
- circulated to everyone who sells or delivers
- paired with a short rationale — why the change, what the change implies downstream

Small drift without a review is the beginning of generic positioning. A review is
mandatory before any substantive change lands in a sales page, proposal template, or
public statement.
