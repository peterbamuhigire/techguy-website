# Case Study Template

Standardised case study format for a premium website agency. Every completed
engagement at Growth tier or above must produce a case study using this structure.
Foundation-tier work may produce a short "project snapshot" instead (see the end of
this file).

A case study is a sales asset, not a portfolio screenshot. It must stand on business
context, strategic decisions, and measurable outcomes. Pretty pictures without
business logic are worthless to a premium buyer.

---

## The 7-part case study structure

Use this exact structure every time. The reader should be able to land on any heading
and understand what decision was made and why.

### 1. Headline and subhead

**Headline** — 6 to 10 words, benefit-led and outcome-specific. Not "Website for
AcmeCorp." Instead: "How AcmeCorp cut proposal time by 60% with a sector-focused
authority site."

**Subhead** — 15 to 25 words. State the sector, the tier of engagement, and the single
most compelling outcome. Example: "A $22,000 Growth engagement for a regulated
financial services firm in Kampala, delivered in 11 weeks, now ranking #1 for three
high-intent queries."

### 2. The client in one paragraph

- Who they are (2 sentences: sector, size, geography, years in business).
- Who they serve (1 sentence: their ideal customer).
- Why a reader of this case study should care (1 sentence: what makes this engagement
  a relevant example).

No logos, no superlatives, no adjectives borrowed from their marketing. A factual,
quiet introduction.

### 3. The situation before (the problem)

State the commercial pressure that drove the engagement. Not "their website was old."
Instead: the revenue consequence of the old website.

Structure:

- **Commercial trigger** — what business event forced the decision (lost pitch,
  competitor launch, rebrand, expansion, funding round, leadership change).
- **Symptoms the client named** — in their own words, quoted where possible.
- **Deeper diagnosis we surfaced** — what we found during discovery that the client
  had not articulated. This is where the agency's expertise shows.
- **The risk of not acting** — what was about to break if the project did not happen.

Four short paragraphs. Each paragraph one thought.

### 4. The strategic frame (what we decided to build)

This is the doctor's diagnosis moment. Before describing the build, describe the
decision.

- **The single strategic bet** — stated in one sentence. "We decided to position them
  as the only firm in Uganda specialising in X, and build the entire site around that
  category claim."
- **Why this bet over the alternatives** — what we rejected and why.
- **Category we positioned them in** — use the client's category name, not ours.
- **Primary buyer the site is built for** — one named persona with their
  decision-making context.
- **Success criteria we agreed with the client** — 3 to 5 specific measurable
  outcomes, agreed before the build started.

This section proves the agency thinks before it builds. Without it, the case study
reads like a portfolio entry.

### 5. What we built (the delivery story)

Describe the build in terms of strategic decisions, not deliverable lists.

Cover:

- **Site architecture** — why this IA, not another (1 paragraph, optionally with a
  small diagram).
- **Message hierarchy** — the headline, the promise, the proof stack, the offer.
- **Design decisions** — the specific design choices that enforce the positioning
  (type, palette, photography style, motion tone). Two or three choices, each with a
  reason.
- **Conversion mechanics** — the CTA ladder, form design, trust signals, FAQ strategy.
- **Content engine** — what the client will now publish (the Big 5 pillar topics, the
  cornerstone articles, the editorial rhythm).
- **Technical foundations** — performance, SEO schema, analytics event map, hosting
  topology. One paragraph, quiet, competent.
- **Languages delivered** — English / French / Kiswahili, where relevant.

Two to four short subsections with small screenshots inline. Never a long image
gallery with no captions.

### 6. The outcome (with numbers)

The hardest section to fake and the easiest to skip. Do not skip it.

Required numbers (at least four of the seven):

- **Traffic / search** — impressions, clicks, rankings for target queries, before/after.
- **Lead volume** — number of qualified enquiries per month, before/after.
- **Conversion rate** — form completions per visitor, before/after.
- **Proposal / sales efficiency** — proposals sent, proposals won, average cycle length.
- **Revenue attributable** — deals closed traced back to the website.
- **Performance** — Lighthouse score, Core Web Vitals pass rate.
- **Qualitative win** — a specific quote from the client about what changed.

Each number needs a date range. "3x increase in leads" is not a number. "Qualified
enquiries rose from 4/month (Jan–Mar 2026) to 12/month (Jul–Sep 2026)" is a number.

Always state measurement method (GA4, Search Console, CRM export) and the
measurement window. Do not cherry-pick a one-week spike.

If numbers are not yet available (because launch is recent), write a "First 30 days"
section and commit to a follow-up at 90 days and 12 months.

### 7. Client testimonial

End every case study with a 3 to 6 sentence client quote. It must name:

- What was broken before.
- What changed after.
- Why they would recommend us.

Attributed to a named person with role and company. No "CEO, Firm A" anonymisation —
that reads as fabricated.

Follow the testimonial with one sentence on what happens next (retainer, phase 2,
referral, etc.) to signal ongoing relationship.

## Supporting blocks (optional, use when available)

### Sidebar: Engagement facts

A pull-out card with:

- Tier: Foundation / Growth / Authority
- Fee band: (state a band, not exact dollar — "$20k–$25k")
- Timeline: weeks from kickoff to launch
- Team: named roles (e.g. "strategist, designer, developer, SEO lead")
- Languages: list
- Primary platform: Astro / Tailwind / hosting

### Sidebar: The strategic shift in one line

One sentence. "AcmeCorp stopped looking like a generalist firm and started looking
like the regional specialist they actually are."

### Process snapshot (1 image)

A single annotated diagram of the build process. Never a 12-step flowchart with no
legend. One diagram, clearly labelled.

### Before/after comparison

Two screenshots, identical page section, labelled with dates. Only where the
difference is dramatic enough to communicate in 2 seconds.

## Tone and voice

- Third-person past tense for the narrative. "The firm had been losing 40% of
  qualified pitches to a younger competitor." (Not "We worked with a firm that...")
- First-person plural ("we") only in the strategic frame and delivery sections,
  where agency agency is the subject.
- Quiet, specific, commercial. No adjective stacks ("beautifully crafted, thoughtful,
  strategic"). No exclamation points. No hero's-journey breathlessness.
- Respect the client's language. If they say "advocates" not "lawyers," we say
  "advocates."
- Zero jargon unless the buyer uses it. "Schema" is fine in a law-firm case study if
  the buyer is technical; otherwise explain or remove.

---

## What not to put in a case study

- Logo walls (they belong on the home page, not in the study).
- Deliverable checklists ("we delivered X pages, Y components, Z forms").
- Design process buzzwords ("we held a discovery workshop, then iterated").
- Generic industry statistics ("79% of users form an opinion in 50ms").
- "Challenges we faced" in a self-congratulatory tone.
- Client names or faces without written permission.
- Screenshots of internal Slack messages, Notion boards, or Figma comments.
- AI-generated imagery to fill gaps. If the photo does not exist, use a wordmark.

## Case study formats by tier

| Tier | Format | Length | Purpose |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| Foundation ($12k–$15k) | Project snapshot | 300–500 words, 2 images | Proof of craft and launch discipline. Not a strategic narrative. |
| Growth ($18k–$28k) | Full case study | 1,200–1,800 words, 4–7 images, 1 diagram | Full 7-part structure. Strategic frame is non-negotiable. |
| Authority ($30k+) | Extended case study | 2,000–3,000 words, 8–12 images, diagrams, testimonial video | Full structure plus a mini-video (60–90s) with the client on camera. |

Foundation-tier "project snapshots" have 4 sections only:

1. Client, sector, and the single job.
2. What we built (one paragraph).
3. One outcome number.
4. A one-line client quote.

No strategic frame, no deep diagnosis — Foundation engagements did not justify that
depth of strategic work, so pretending otherwise is misleading.

## Case study production process

Every engagement produces its case study within 90 days of launch. The process:

1. **Week 0 (kickoff)** — client signs a case study release in the contract. Agree
   what can be named (client name, specific numbers, client quote) and what must be
   anonymised (financials, employee names).
2. **Week 1 (strategy complete)** — capture the strategic frame in writing. This is
   the hardest section to reconstruct later; write it when the decisions are fresh.
3. **Week 4 (design direction set)** — capture the design rationale. Screenshots of
   the three directions considered, and why we chose one.
4. **Week 8 (build complete)** — capture the technical decisions.
5. **Launch + 30 days** — first data pull. Write the "early signals" paragraph.
6. **Launch + 60 days** — client check-in, testimonial recording, first numbers
   review.
7. **Launch + 90 days** — full case study published on agency site. All numbers
   verified with the client. Case study emailed to client for approval before
   publication.

Put the case study assignment on the engagement schedule from day one. Cases written
retroactively are always weaker than cases captured in real time.

## Reuse in proposals

Every case study feeds the proof library (`proof-library.md`). Extract from each
case study:

- 2 to 4 pull-quote testimonial snippets.
- 3 to 5 numeric proof points.
- 1 diagram (annotated).
- 1 before/after comparison.
- 1 strategic-frame sentence.

These extracts become the reusable components used in proposals, sales pages, and
authority content. The full case study is never pasted into a proposal — only the
relevant snippets.

---

## Quality gate before publishing

Before any case study goes live on the agency site, verify:

- [ ] All numbers have a measurement method and date range.
- [ ] The client has approved the text in writing.
- [ ] No confidential data (fees, contracts, financials) is exposed.
- [ ] The testimonial is from a named person with title and company.
- [ ] Every claim can be proven from analytics, CRM data, or client email.
- [ ] The strategic frame answers "what did you decide, and why this over the
      alternatives?"
- [ ] No AI-generated imagery, no stock photos stitched in to fill gaps.
- [ ] Links to the live client site work and open in a new tab.
- [ ] The study is discoverable: linked from the home page, the services page, and
      the relevant sector landing page.
- [ ] Meta description, OG image, and JSON-LD Article schema are set.
- [ ] The study is referenced from the proof library index.
