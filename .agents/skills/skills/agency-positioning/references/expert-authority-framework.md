# Expert Authority Framework

The structural frameworks from Russell Brunson's *Expert Secrets*, filtered and
translated for a premium B2B website agency ($12k–$45k engagements, high-trust
buyers: principals of law firms, financial services firms, clinics, B2B services).

Brunson's frameworks are strong. His *tone* is wrong for our market. We keep the
structures and discard the bro-marketing register, fake scarcity, countdown
timers, hype language, and launch-formula theatre. Senior buyers detect that
tone within 2 seconds and disqualify us on the spot.

Use this file when:

- Designing the founder's public voice and bio.
- Writing the agency's About page, signature talk, or long-form sales page.
- Structuring the first sales conversation or discovery call.
- Building the case-study narrative structure.
- Diagnosing a page, email, or asset that isn't converting.

---

## 1. The Attractive Character (for a senior professional)

Brunson's claim: every expert leading a market has a defined public persona. Four
identities available:

- **The Leader** — has walked the path; is 1–3 years ahead of the audience. *Our default.*
- **The Reluctant Hero** — doesn't want the spotlight; shares because the pattern must be named. *Our alternate.*
- The Adventurer — publicly discovering in real time. *Too amateur for our tier. Avoid.*
- The Reporter — curator of others' expertise. *Cedes the authority we need. Avoid.*

Four content storylines:

- Loss and redemption
- Us versus them (shared enemy — e.g. the cheap-template industry, the
  pay-for-performance SEO shop, the agency that builds without strategy)
- Before and after
- Amazing discovery

Character elements to define:

- **Backstory** — origin of the mission in one paragraph.
- **Parables** — 5 to 8 teaching stories drawn from real client engagements.
- **One visible flaw** — the specific weakness we lean into, not hide.
- **Polarity** — what we publicly stand against.

### Our polarity stack (what we refuse)

- The cheap-template agency — sub-$5k "web design" that produces brochure sites.
- Pay-for-performance, equity, or revenue-share structures.
- The "we just build what the client tells us" order-taker posture.
- Day-rate and hourly billing.
- Starter packages that violate the $12k floor.
- Engagements under $12k full stop.

### Our visible flaw (the one we lean into)

> "Our engagements take 10 to 14 weeks. We're not the fastest. We're not the
> cheapest. We're the firm you call when the last two attempts failed."

The flaw is the positioning. Speed and price are competitors' ground; strategic
rigour and engagement depth are ours.

### How to render this as a professional — not an influencer

- No selfies, no hustle-culture language, no vulnerability theatre.
- No "let me tell you a story" openers.
- British/professional register at every turn.
- Flaw admitted factually, never performed.
- Opinion stated with evidence, never with exclamation.

The Attractive Character for a $22k buyer reads like a senior partner at a
boutique firm, not a YouTuber. Keep the frameworks, change the register.

## 2. The Epiphany Bridge (the 8-beat story structure)

Brunson's strongest framework. People don't buy from logic; they buy because they
had an emotional epiphany that made the purchase obvious. Your job is to walk
them across the same bridge you walked across.

The 8 beats:

1. **Backstory** — who I was, where I was, what I wanted.
2. **Desire** — what I wanted to achieve or escape.
3. **External wall** — what stopped me; the old way failed.
4. **Internal struggle** — what I thought it meant about me.
5. **Epiphany** — the realisation that a new opportunity existed.
6. **Plan** — what I decided to do with the epiphany.
7. **Conflict** — what went wrong on the way.
8. **Achievement → transformation** — what changed externally and internally.

### Use 1 — The founder story (About page, signature talk opening)

Compressed into 3 paragraphs:

- Paragraph 1 (beats 1–3): the work we did before the agency, the pattern we kept
  seeing, the wall.
- Paragraph 2 (beats 4–6): the realisation that changed how we worked, and the
  decision to build differently.
- Paragraph 3 (beats 7–8): the early engagement that failed, the one after that
  worked, and the principle we've built on since.

### Use 2 — Client case studies

Every Growth+ case study uses the 8 beats as its narrative spine. The strategic
frame section (see `case-study-template.md`) is beats 4–6 — the diagnosis and
the decision. The delivery story is beat 7. The outcome is beat 8.

Opening example, written in the agency's register:

> "When we met Kampala Legal Partners, they had just finished a $28,000 rebrand
> with a London agency. The site looked beautiful. In six months it had generated
> two inbound enquiries, both from recruiters. The managing partner assumed the
> problem was traffic and was about to commit another $40,000 to SEO. In our first
> call we looked at the three enquiry forms on the site — each asked nine
> questions before a prospect could say hello. The problem was never traffic. It
> was that the site made it harder to become a client than to avoid becoming one.
> Here is what we changed, and what happened in the 90 days that followed."

That paragraph holds beats 1–5. The rest of the case study handles 6–8.

### Use 3 — The "why we do this work" paragraph

A single 4-sentence block on the home page that compresses the full 8-beat arc
into a mission statement.

## 3. New Opportunity vs Improvement Offer

The commercial core of premium positioning. Buyers resist *improvement* offers
because accepting one implies the previous decision was wrong (status threat).
Buyers accept *new opportunity* offers because the new category preserves their
identity and reframes past failures.

### Test for every page, offer, and headline

Can a prospect finish this sentence while reading our copy?

> "Oh, this is like [competitor X], but [better/faster/cheaper]."

If yes, we wrote an improvement offer. Rewrite.

Can they finish this sentence instead?

> "I have never seen an offer structured quite like this before."

If yes, we wrote a new opportunity. Keep it.

### Our new opportunity category

The **Authority Website System** — a strategy-first engagement that transforms
the website from a brochure into the highest-leverage sales asset in the firm.

- Not "better websites."
- Not "premium web design."
- Not "web design + SEO + copy."

A category that doesn't sit next to "web design $5k–$50k." It sits next to
"management consulting," "brand strategy," "business transformation."

### The practical test

A prospect must not be able to ask us:

- "How much do you charge per page?"
- "What's your turnaround on a 10-page site?"
- "Can we drop the homepage from scope?"

If they can, we haven't established a new category — we've just given an
improvement offer a fancy name. Rewrite until the questions don't fit.

### Headline examples

- "Better websites for law firms" — improvement offer. ❌
- "Faster, higher-converting websites for law firms" — improvement offer. ❌
- "The Authority Website System for law firms generating $2M+ in fees" — new
  opportunity. ✅

The third headline implies a category the prospect didn't know existed,
positions around revenue not deliverables, and disqualifies the bottom of the
market automatically.

## 4. The Big Domino

In every sale, one belief — if held — makes all other objections fall. Everything
we publish should advance that one belief. It becomes the editorial filter for
the entire content engine.

### Our Big Domino

> **If the prospect believes that a website is a revenue-generation *system* —
> not a marketing deliverable — and that the system's design requires a
> specialist engagement rather than a vendor, then the $12k–$45k price is not
> only justified, it is the only sane option.**

Every asset, every page, every discovery-call question, every case study
advances that single belief. If a piece of content doesn't advance it, it
doesn't get published.

### Editorial filter test

Before publishing any cornerstone piece, ask:

- Does this advance the belief that a website is a revenue-generation system?
- Does this advance the belief that the design of that system requires a
  specialist engagement?
- Does this advance the belief that the outcome justifies the fee?

If a piece advances two of three, it can ship. If it advances none, rewrite
until it does or abandon it.

### Example Big Domino post

A blog post titled *"Why your agency-built website hasn't generated a single
qualified lead in 18 months"* does three things in one piece:

1. Names the status quo (website = marketing deliverable).
2. Frames the failure as systemic, not cosmetic (so design refresh won't fix
   it).
3. Positions the solution as a different kind of engagement.

One post; one domino push.

## 5. The Three Secrets (discovery-call and talk structure)

Every sales conversation and every long-form presentation must address three
categories of belief, **in this order**:

1. **Vehicle belief** — does this kind of solution actually work?
2. **Internal belief** — can I do this / justify this / pull this off?
3. **External belief** — will my circumstances permit it?

Fixing internal belief before vehicle belief does not work — the prospect still
doesn't believe the vehicle is real.

### Applied to the discovery call

This maps directly onto the four-conversation arc in `proposal-positioning.md`.
Within conversation 1:

**Secret 1 — Vehicle (first 15 min).** "Let me show you three firms that went
from X to Y by changing their website from a deliverable into a system. Here's
what they changed, here's what happened, here's why the mechanism works."

**Secret 2 — Internal (next 15 min).** "The concern I hear most from managing
partners is 'I couldn't justify $30k on a website to the partnership.' Here's
the framing that works: this isn't a marketing spend, it's business development
infrastructure, with an expected payback inside 12 months at typical conversion
rates. Here's how one partner walked his board through it."

**Secret 3 — External (final 15 min).** "Our engagements take 10–14 weeks,
require 4 hours of partner time in weeks 1–3, and work best alongside your
existing BD efforts rather than replacing them. For firms of your size the
budget sits within the normal range for a year of marketing headcount."

Never start with price (secret 3). Never start with "here's how you'll feel"
(secret 2). Always start with the case for the vehicle.

### Applied to the signature talk

See section 8 below.

## 6. The Stack (scope as value, not deliverables list)

Brunson's principle: present the offer as a stack of value components, not a
list of things. The list invites line-item negotiation. The stack reads as an
integrated system.

**We do not put numeric dollar values next to components.** That's mail-order
infomercial behaviour and it shreds credibility with principals. The *structural
idea* — scope as stack, not deliverables list — is what we keep.

### Wrong — deliverables list

> 10 pages, responsive design, CMS, hosting, 2 rounds of revisions — $22,000

### Right — the stack

> The Authority Website System (Growth tier) includes:
>
> - **Positioning sprint** — the 4-session engagement that produces the
>   messaging hierarchy, category narrative, and Big Domino before a single page
>   is designed.
> - **Conversion architecture** — the page-by-page content and design logic
>   engineered against defined buyer journeys.
> - **The site itself** — built on a performance-tuned, accessibility-audited,
>   on-brand stack.
> - **Measurement infrastructure** — analytics, event map, attribution, monthly
>   review cadence that keeps the system earning.
> - **Editorial runway** — the first 3 long-form pieces that anchor the
>   authority layer.
> - **Founder access during build** — direct partner-level involvement through
>   strategy, not handoff to a junior.
> - **90-day post-launch optimisation** — we don't launch and leave.
>
> Investment: $22,000.

Every component has a reason. The word "includes" does the work — none is
optional, none is negotiable. Each answers a specific objection before it's
raised.

### The stack's defensive function

When a prospect tries to reduce scope — "Can we skip the positioning sprint?" —
the answer writes itself: *"The positioning sprint is why the rest works.
Removing it converts the engagement back into a deliverable, and we don't sell
deliverables."*

The stack defends itself.

## 7. False Belief Patterns (and the stories that reframe them)

Three layers of false belief, each requiring a reframe delivered through an
Epiphany Bridge story. You cannot argue someone out of a false belief. You can
only tell them a story that gives them a new one.

### False belief 1 (vehicle) — "Websites don't drive revenue."

**Reframe.** Websites as currently built don't drive revenue. An Authority
Website System does, because it's engineered against buyer journey rather than
assembled from templates.

**Canonical story.** A case study where the previous $30k website generated
nothing and the rebuilt system generated measurable pipeline. Walk the 8-beat
bridge.

**Where it lives.** The "Why Most Websites Fail" blog post; the vehicle block
of the signature talk; the proof library under `proof/rationale/`.

### False belief 2 (internal) — "I couldn't justify that investment to my partners."

**Reframe.** You're not justifying a website expense. You're proposing business
development infrastructure with a documented payback model.

**Canonical story.** The managing partner who built the board paper. What he
said, what the CFO pushed back on, how he handled it, what happened at the
vote. Make the prospect see a peer doing the justification.

**Where it lives.** The "How to Get Partnership Approval for a Website
Investment" guide; the internal block of the signature talk; the proposal
section on fees.

### False belief 3 (external) — "My market isn't sophisticated enough" or "my industry is different."

**Reframe.** Every industry says this. In every industry, the first firm to
build a category-leading digital presence compounds an advantage that takes
competitors years to close.

**Canonical story.** A client in an unglamorous niche (logistics, regional law,
specialist manufacturing) who assumed their market wouldn't notice. The enquiry
pattern shift in the first 90 days. The first client they won who they would
never have reached otherwise.

**Where it lives.** The "Why Your Industry Is Not As Unsophisticated As You
Think" essay; the external block of the signature talk; the sector landing
pages.

These three stories live permanently in the authority content stack. Each is
re-told on podcasts, in proposals, at talks, in emails, on LinkedIn. Repetition
is the point.

## 8. The Signature Talk (the Perfect Webinar structure, cleaned up)

A 45-minute talk delivered 6 to 10 times per year — livestream, conference
keynote, private webinar, partner-firm lunch-and-learn. Same structure, very
different tone from Brunson's launch webinars.

### Structure

1. **Hook (2 min).** "In the last decade I've watched firms spend over
   $40 million cumulatively on websites that generated no measurable revenue.
   Tonight I'll show you why that happens, why it's accelerating, and the three
   things that have to change before the next one works."

2. **Origin (5 min).** The founder story — 8-beat Epiphany Bridge, compressed.
   Delivered as a senior professional would tell it at a partner dinner, not as
   a keynote speaker's origin story.

3. **The One Thing (5 min).** The Big Domino stated explicitly as a principle.
   "A website is a revenue-generation system or it is wallpaper. The firms
   winning the next decade are the ones treating it as the former."

4. **Three Secrets (25 min).**
   - Vehicle — how the system works, 3 case studies.
   - Internal — how partners justify it, the financial framing.
   - External — the operational reality, the timelines, what it requires.

5. **Offer (7 min).** Here's what we do, here's who we do it for (narrower
   than they expect), here's what happens if you're interested. One CTA.

### What the signature talk does not contain

- No urgency, no countdown timers, no "limited spots."
- No stack-value theatrics ("normally $5,000 — yours today free!").
- No pain-amplification ("imagine 5 years from now, still stuck").
- No open-loop cliffhangers.
- No invitation to join a "movement" or "tribe."

A single CTA: "If this is interesting, book a call." The talk's job is to
qualify the audience down to the 2 to 5 per cent who are a fit, not to convert
the room.

### The Authority Website Roundtable

The signature talk is delivered monthly as the "Authority Website Roundtable"
— gated signup, livestreamed to the Dream 100 (section 10), recording sent to
attendees, refined twice a year. This is the anchor asset of the entire
publishing engine.

## 9. Publishing Cadence (Soap Opera and Seinfeld, B2B version)

Brunson advocates daily publishing — correct for a $47 info-product audience,
wrong for $22k B2B buyers. The principle (publish consistently enough to stay
present without burning out the founder) is correct; the cadence needs to match
the audience.

### The four tiers of our publishing cadence

**Tier 1 — Weekly anchor (non-negotiable).** One long-form piece per week. Blog
post, teardown, case study, or essay. 1,200 to 2,500 words. Published on the
site, emailed to the list, distributed to LinkedIn. Story-driven, lesson-
embedded, no hard pitch. This is our Seinfeld substitute.

**Tier 2 — 5-part onboarding sequence (our Soap Opera).** New subscribers
receive 5 emails over 10 days:

1. The story of why we do this work (Attractive Character + Epiphany Bridge).
2. The Big Domino stated, with the vehicle-belief case.
3. The internal-belief case (the financial/justification framing).
4. The external-belief case (the operational reality).
5. The single invitation — here's what we offer, here's who it's for.

**Tier 3 — Monthly roundtable.** Signature talk recorded and distributed.

**Tier 4 — Quarterly cornerstone.** One deep asset per quarter — original
research, benchmark report, long teardown. The authority anchors.

### What we don't do

- No daily emails.
- No "pattern-interrupt" subject lines ("You won't believe what happened
  next...").
- No open-loop cliffhangers.
- No urgency theatre.
- No countdown timers.

The register must match a senior-partner audience that deletes anything that
smells like direct-response bait within 2 seconds.

### Weekly anchor title examples

- "The five objections we hear from managing partners — and why three of them
  are right."
- "What I learned tearing down 30 law-firm websites in July."
- "Why we refuse pay-for-performance engagements."

Each is story-led, lesson-embedded, no CTA beyond "reply if this applies to
you."

## 10. Dream 100

Make a list of 100 people whose yes would change the business. Work the list
patiently for 12 months.

### Two tracks

**Dream 100 Clients.** 100 specifically named firms — not roles, not segments,
named firms with named principals. Each entry holds: firm name, principal's
name, public trigger events (new hire, funding, rebrand signals), mutual
connections, current warmth level (cold → following → engaged → conversation →
proposal → client).

**Dream 100 Influencers.** 100 partners — lawyers who refer to us, consultants
adjacent to our work, authors whose audiences trust them, podcast hosts,
journalists covering our sectors. Same warmth tracking.

### The weekly ritual

Every Friday, 60 minutes. The founder works through one segment of the list.
One touch per contact:

- A thoughtful comment on something they published.
- A useful article sent with one line of context.
- An introduction made on their behalf.
- A private teardown of something they shipped (sent privately, not published).

Not spray-and-pray. Genuine relational compounding. Logged in the agency's own
`project-log/`.

### The expected outcome

After 12 months, a well-worked Dream 100 yields 5 to 15 closed engagements at
full price. At our tier, that is the year's revenue.

### What not to do

- Mass-message sequences using LinkedIn automation tools.
- "Quick question" templates.
- Lead-magnet drops at unknown contacts.
- Pitching on first touch.
- Measuring by impression or connection count.

The Dream 100 is not a cold-outreach list. It is a relational asset worked with
patience.

## 11. A Published Point of View (the sane "movement")

Brunson calls this a tribe or a movement. We translate the *mechanic* without
the *theatre*. Buyers prefer firms that stand for something specific over firms
that stand for general excellence.

### Our published point of view (partial list)

- Websites are revenue systems or they are wallpaper.
- The best time to engage us is 90 days before you need the result, not 30.
- We will turn down engagements under $12k — and we'll tell you who to call
  instead.
- We do not accept pay-for-performance engagements.
- A principal who cannot spend 4 hours in weeks 1 to 3 is not ready for us yet.
- Strategy precedes design; design precedes build; build precedes measurement.
  Always, in that order.

### Our shared vocabulary

- **Authority Website System** — the category.
- **Big Domino** — the core belief we advance.
- **The value ratio** — the 10:1 / 20:1 pricing rule from `pricing-rules.md`.
- **The three false beliefs** — vehicle, internal, external.
- **The four conversations** — the sales arc.

### How it renders

A public "Our Principles" page — not buried in About, deliberately prominent.
Each principle has 2 to 3 sentences with a link to the long-form argument.

Prospects start referencing the principles back to us in discovery calls
("We read your piece on pay-for-performance — that's why we called you"). That
is when we know the mechanic is working.

### What we never do

- "Tribe," "movement," "cult-ure" language used literally.
- "Join us," "become one of us," "the community."
- Bracelets, merch, branded hashtags.
- Fan-service performance of any kind.

Just: a firm with a published position, a consistent vocabulary, and a small
number of defended principles. The 2 to 5 per cent of the market who share
that worldview self-select in. Everyone else self-selects out.

## 12. Hook, Story, Offer (the diagnostic lens)

The unit of every asset: hook, story, offer. If the asset isn't converting, one
of the three is broken. Diagnose which.

### Applied to our assets

**Home page.**
- Hook: the H1 states the new-opportunity category and the buyer in one
  sentence.
- Story: 4 to 6 sections — why most websites fail, how the system is
  different, 3 case study proofs, the principles, the team.
- Offer: book the discovery call. One CTA. Repeated twice.

**Case study page.**
- Hook: the specific before-and-after outcome in the H1 and the opening
  paragraph.
- Story: the 8-beat Epiphany Bridge applied to the client.
- Offer: "If this looks familiar, here's the next step" — one CTA.

**Long-form blog post.**
- Hook: the first sentence names a belief the reader holds and challenges it.
- Story: the teaching, grounded in one or two client examples.
- Offer: sometimes the discovery call, sometimes "subscribe to the weekly,"
  sometimes "join the next roundtable," sometimes nothing beyond "reply if this
  resonates."

**LinkedIn post.**
- Hook: first line names the false belief or the pattern.
- Story: 4 to 8 sentences of story or teaching.
- Offer: often none. The post is the offer to exist in someone's awareness.

### Diagnostic workflow

Home page gets 2,000 visits per month, zero discovery calls. Run the test.

- Hook — is the H1 generic ("Websites that work")? Fix the hook first.
- Story — is there no proof, no case studies above the fold? Fix next.
- Offer — is the CTA passive ("contact us")? Replace with specific: "Book a
  30-minute diagnostic call — no pitch, we'll tell you whether a system like
  this would even help."

Each fix is a discrete test. Hook-story-offer is a diagnostic lens, not just a
writing formula.

## Use and avoid summary

### Use (keep, adapt, deploy across Phase 7)

- The Attractive Character — identity, storylines, polarity — rendered as a
  senior professional's bio, not an influencer's persona.
- The Epiphany Bridge (8 beats) — founder story, case studies, objection
  stories.
- New Opportunity vs Improvement — the core positioning mechanic.
- The Big Domino — editorial filter for the entire content engine.
- Three Secrets — discovery-call structure, talk structure, proposal structure.
- The Stack (structurally) — scope as value components, each answering an
  objection.
- False Belief Patterns — three agency objections, three canonical stories.
- The Perfect Webinar skeleton — the 45-minute signature talk.
- Soap Opera sequence — 5-email onboarding, not daily emails.
- Dream 100 — named clients and named influencers, worked weekly.
- A published point of view — shared vocabulary, defended principles,
  self-selecting audience.
- Hook, Story, Offer — diagnostic lens for every asset.

### Avoid (explicitly reject)

- Fake scarcity. Countdown timers, "only 3 spots left," disappearing bonuses.
  Toxic at our tier.
- Stacked dollar-value theatrics. "$2,000 value!" beside each component
  destroys credibility with principals instantly.
- Pain-amplification theatre. "Imagine 5 years from now, still stuck."
  Manipulative and off-register.
- Bro-marketing tone. "What's up, rockstars," hustle culture, "7-figure"
  anything. Entire register wrong.
- Daily email cadence. Wrong frequency; signals low-ticket positioning.
- "Tribe," "movement," "cult-ure" framing used literally. Translate to "point
  of view" and "shared principles."
- Open-loop cliffhanger subject lines.
- Launch-formula mechanics. Our sales cycle is consultative and continuous,
  not event-driven.
- Guru-positioning of the founder. Selfies at conferences, "my journey to 7
  figures," vulnerability-as-marketing.
- Webinar-as-pitch. Our signature talk qualifies down to 2 to 5 per cent, not
  converts the majority.
