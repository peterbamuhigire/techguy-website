# Premium Website Offer Playbook

The commercial logic for selling websites at $10,000 or more as a repeatable, productized
offer. Grounded in Warrillow (*Built to Sell*), Enns (*Win Without Pitching*, *The Four
Conversations*), Weiss (*Value-Based Fees*), and Robinson (*Secrets of Business Value*).

Use when: defining what the agency sells, writing sales pages, onboarding new staff,
structuring a proposal, deciding whether to accept a project.

## 1. Agency category

We are not a web design agency. We are not a developer for hire. We are not a marketing
shop. The commercial category is:

> **Authority Website Systems for high-trust businesses.**

Every word earns its place:

- **Authority** — the work is expertise-led. We diagnose before we prescribe. Strategy,
  messaging, and positioning precede design. Clients buy our judgement, not our keystrokes.
- **Website Systems** — the deliverable is a system, not a file. Content architecture,
  design system, SEO foundations, analytics, and conversion mechanics are built together,
  documented, and operable without us.
- **High-trust businesses** — professional services, healthcare, education, legal,
  consulting, and premium local businesses where reputation is the primary asset and a
  generic website is a commercial liability.

What this category excludes on purpose:

- e-commerce and DTC unless the trust layer is the core problem
- low-trust commodity brochure sites
- "just build what we spec'd" pair-of-hands work
- agency-of-record relationships, ad buying, general marketing
- logo-only engagements, copywriting-only engagements, SEO-only engagements

The category promise, compressed: **we build the websites that trust-heavy businesses cannot
afford to get generic.**

## 2. Why the offer is structured as a system, not a site

Warrillow's verdict on bespoke agency work is the starting premise: custom service
businesses are structurally fragile because every project is different, which forces
generalist hiring, lumpy margins, founder dependency, and low transferable value. Every
hour of undocumented custom work is value leaking out of the business.

The counter-move is to productize — one named process, one audience, one price format, one
way to buy. Warrillow's agency in the story collapses its offering to a five-step logo
process billed as a flat fee on signing. We do the equivalent: a named, documented website
system delivered through a fixed sequence of phases, priced as a flat project fee.

Robinson reinforces the same logic from the buyer's side: in a knowledge business, the
website is where the buyer completes about 60% of the purchase decision before any
conversation happens. The client is not paying for pages — they are paying for the
lead-generating system, brand equity, documented positioning, and intellectual property
the site encapsulates. That is why enterprise buyers of service businesses pay for
documented marketing systems and refuse to pay for founder-dependent ones.

Enns closes the argument: when the agency sells its thinking rather than its time, margin
ceases to be coupled to labour. The proposal is never a menu of hours; it is a committed
outcome sold at a fixed fee.

## 3. The offer ladder

Three tiers. Each tier is a complete system, not a reduced version of the tier above. The
Foundation offer is the minimum we sell — never a pages-only "starter" package.

### Tier 1 — Foundation Website Offer

**Target buyer.** Established business with clear service offering and at least one
principal willing to be the expert. Revenue $500k–$2M typical. Current website is a
liability, or there is no website.

**The commercial promise.** A site that looks as credible as the principals sound when
they speak to a qualified referral. Conversion-ready from launch. Indexable, measurable,
owned by the client.

**Included system components.**

- Positioning and messaging workshop (half-day or equivalent async intake)
- Brand direction alignment (existing brand consolidated, not redesigned from zero)
- Content architecture for the core buyer journey (home, about, services, contact, one
  proof page)
- Custom design direction within our design system
- Up to 6 unique page templates, 6–10 finished pages total
- On-page SEO foundation: titles, descriptions, schema, sitemap, robots, OG images
- Analytics + conversion event setup (GA4 or equivalent, goal events on the primary CTAs)
- Launch operations: DNS cutover, staging → production, 301 map, post-launch scan
- 30-day post-launch optimisation window

**Excluded on purpose.**

- Full brand identity work (logo, palette, wordmark) — we align, we do not redesign
- Custom photography or illustration
- Long-form content production beyond core pages (blog, case studies, landing pages)
- Multilingual delivery
- Applications or integrations beyond a standard contact form + CRM webhook
- Ongoing management

**Fee floor.** No project below $12,000. This is the minimum level of engagement (Enns's
MLE principle) — stated in every first conversation, waived only as the last concession in
a close, and waived less than once per quarter.

**Typical price range.** $12,000–$18,000.

**What makes this tier premium.** It is not cheaper-than-custom — it is custom-enough, in
a system, delivered at a price that forces client commitment. The Foundation tier is the
smallest engagement we will do without dropping the expert posture.

---

### Tier 2 — Growth Website Offer

**Target buyer.** Established firm with multiple services, multiple buyer segments, or a
clear lead-generation objective that the current site fails to meet. Revenue $2M–$10M
typical. The buyer already knows a website is a revenue lever; they need the lever built.

**The commercial promise.** A site architected around the actual buyer journey, measurable
against a business outcome, designed to compound over 24–36 months without a rebuild.

**Included system components (everything in Foundation, plus):**

- Extended discovery: interviews with 3–5 existing customers, buyer-journey mapping,
  decision-process reconstruction
- Messaging hierarchy across segments and service lines
- Content architecture for 15–25 pages including service deep-dives, segment pages, and a
  proof system (case studies, testimonials, logos)
- Custom design direction with distinct segment treatments
- 10–14 unique page templates, finished pages as scoped
- Core-page copywriting (up to 8 pages written by us against interview evidence, not
  fabricated)
- SEO: topical hub planning, internal linking architecture, technical audit,
  Core-Web-Vitals hardening
- Conversion: lead magnet integration, CRM/marketing-automation handoff, event schema for
  3–5 conversion paths
- Accessibility conformance to WCAG 2.2 AA (documented)
- Launch operations + 60-day optimisation window with two review cycles

**Excluded on purpose.**

- Full brand identity reinvention
- Custom application development, portals, dashboards, authenticated areas
- E-commerce (Stripe/PayPal links for deposits are fine; full cart is not)
- Long-term content production on retainer (that is Phase 8 territory)
- SEO outranking guarantees or traffic quotas

**Fee floor.** $22,000.

**Typical price range.** $22,000–$40,000.

**What makes this tier premium.** The discovery is deep enough to change the client's
understanding of their own buyers. The site is built to carry revenue, not to look good.

### Tier 3 — Authority Website System

**Target buyer.** Firm whose website is central to the commercial proposition: category
leader, specialist practice, institution, or firm attempting to reposition upward. Revenue
$5M+ typical, but the fit signal is commercial weight of the site, not headcount.

**The commercial promise.** A documented website system — positioning, narrative,
architecture, design, content, SEO, conversion, and operations — that raises the firm's
enterprise value by making its expertise visible, transferable, and lead-generating
without the founder's personal presence. Robinson's frame, applied directly: the system
becomes an intangible asset on the balance sheet.

**Included system components (everything in Growth, plus):**

- Positioning engagement: category definition, point of view, proof architecture
- Brand narrative system: founder story, mission, manifesto-grade copy, voice guide
- Full content architecture for 30–60 pages: service, segment, industry, proof, content
  hub, resource library, policy pages
- Original copywriting on all cornerstone pages, derived from customer interviews and
  principal interviews
- Content system: editorial calendar template, content ops playbook, internal
  publishing guide
- Authority asset production: one long-form pillar piece (e.g., diagnostic, white paper,
  research report) built into the site as an authority anchor
- SEO system: entity modelling, topical authority map, structured data strategy, technical
  audit + fixes, analytics attribution model
- Conversion system: multi-path lead architecture, segmented CTAs, sales-enablement handoff
- Measurement: KPI dashboard (leads, pipeline influence, organic growth, conversion
  quality), quarterly review template
- Launch operations + 90-day optimisation window with three review cycles
- Documentation kit: brand style guide, content playbook, SEO plan, measurement plan,
  admin manual

**Excluded on purpose.** Same as Growth, plus we do not take on general marketing,
advertising, PR, or long-term content creation under this tier — those sit on separate
retainer agreements.

**Fee floor.** $45,000.

**Typical price range.** $45,000–$85,000.

**What makes this tier premium.** The deliverable is not a website. It is the firm's
positioning made operational through a website. The documentation means the system
survives staff turnover on both sides. Robinson's enterprise-value argument is the closing
hook.

## 4. Commercial logic behind the ladder

### Why three tiers, not five

Enns's three-option rule: one anchor, one safe middle, one smaller option sized to an
already-stated budget. Extremeness aversion pulls most buyers to the middle; the anchor
raises the overall fee distribution; the smaller option exists so "no" is not the only
response from a constrained buyer. More than three options dilutes the choice and invites
feature comparison.

### Why the minimum is $12,000

Four converging reasons:

1. **Cost of thinking.** Diagnosis, positioning, content architecture, and design
   direction are labour-intensive even in the smallest engagement. Under $12k, the firm is
   doing client work below cost.
2. **Client commitment.** Weiss: the fee must create enough "pain" to ensure the client
   shows up and decides. Under $10k, the project is a line item; over $12k, it is a
   decision the client owns.
3. **Substitution barrier.** Below $10k, the buyer's mental substitute is a freelancer or
   template shop. Above $12k, the comparison moves to other strategic-tier firms.
4. **Positioning integrity.** Enns: "if we are not losing business on price occasionally,
   we are not charging enough." A floor below $12k invites the wrong clients, who then
   expect the wrong behaviour in delivery.

### Why each tier is a system, not a bigger site

Warrillow: the productized service ships a named process, not a variable deliverable. Each
tier is a complete system with a documented delivery sequence. A Growth project does not
contain 60 pages; a Foundation project does not contain 12. Pages are the consequence of
the system, not the definition of the offer.

### Why strategy is always included

Enns: "practitioners do not present; stars do not audition." Every tier opens with
diagnosis — positioning workshop in Foundation, discovery in Growth, positioning
engagement in Authority. The agency never skips the diagnostic phase to hit a lower price
point. A site without strategy is the product a template shop sells; if we strip strategy,
we become that shop.

### Why there is no "pages-only" tier

It is the most common client request and the fastest route to commodity pricing. The
policy: we do not sell page-only engagements. If a prospect wants one, they are not the
right client; refer to a freelancer and move on.

### Why hosting and maintenance are separate

Project fees buy the system. Hosting, maintenance, and ongoing optimisation sit on
separate retainer contracts (Phase 8). Bundling creates scope confusion, weakens the
retainer motion, and couples cash flow to delivery velocity.

## 5. The sales-to-delivery handshake

The offer ladder only holds if the sales motion matches the delivery motion. Keep these
five rules explicit in every sales conversation so the promise survives the project.

1. **Strategy first, always.** No creative, content, or code before the positioning or
   discovery step is complete and signed off.
2. **Fewer options, better quality.** We recommend one direction. We do not present
   three colour schemes and ask which one the client prefers.
3. **Only we present our work.** Deliverables are never forwarded to internal stakeholders
   without us in the room or on the call.
4. **Revisions are structured.** Each tier has a defined number of review cycles; extra
   cycles are billed, not absorbed (scope matrix handles specifics).
5. **Launch is an event, not a date.** Go-live is staged, QA'd, and only released when the
   system passes the launch checklist (Phase 6).

Write these five rules into the proposal. Read them aloud in the kickoff call.

## 6. How to talk about the offer in a sales call

The conversation follows Enns's four-conversation arc (Phase 2 work). For the offer
specifically, stay inside these speech patterns:

- **Do not** lead with tiers or prices. Lead with the diagnostic question: *"Tell me about
  the business problem the current website is creating."*
- **Do not** name a tier until after value has been stipulated. The tier is the answer to
  the value conversation, not the opening.
- **Do** introduce the minimum level of engagement early: *"Our engagements start at
  $12,000 — is that in the range you were prepared to invest?"* This disqualifies on cost
  before time is wasted on fit.
- **Do** present tiers as three options, anchored high. Authority first, Growth second,
  Foundation third. The anchor sets the ceiling even when the client picks the middle.
- **Do** say the price out loud before the proposal is read. Silence follows. Do not fill it.

## 7. Indicators that the offer ladder is working

Track quarterly:

- **Mix.** Growth and Authority tiers should account for at least 60% of new engagements
  by revenue within 12 months. If Foundation dominates, the sales motion is anchoring low.
- **Minimum violations.** No more than one waived minimum per quarter. Every waiver is
  logged with the reason.
- **Win rate at full fee.** 50–70% is healthy. Above 85% means we are underpricing. Below
  35% means the offer is not landing in the right conversations.
- **Post-sale scope drift.** Time spent on out-of-scope work should be under 10% of
  project hours. Above that, scope matrix needs enforcement, not the offer ladder.
- **Referral quality.** Referrals from Authority-tier clients should skew to Authority or
  Growth buyers. If Authority clients send Foundation-size leads, the positioning is
  leaking downmarket.

## 8. Review cadence

The offer ladder is reviewed every 12 months:

- Fee floors adjusted for inflation and win-rate data
- Tier inclusions tightened or expanded based on scope-drift logs
- Exclusions re-affirmed or revised
- New tier or sub-offer considered only if three consecutive quarters of demand signal
  justify it

Changes are made in writing, dated, and circulated to anyone who sells or delivers.
