# Pricing Rules and Calculator

Value-based pricing logic for premium website engagements. The goal is not to estimate
labour — it is to set a fee that reflects the client's future value, leaves adequate
margin, and anchors the agency as an expert rather than a vendor.

Source material: Alan Weiss (*Value-Based Fees*), Blair Enns (*Win Without Pitching*, *The
Four Conversations*), Peter Robinson (*Secrets of Business Value*), John Warrillow
(*Built to Sell*).

Companion files: `premium-website-offer-playbook.md`, `scope-matrix.md`,
`proposal-positioning.md`, `qualification-guide.md`.

---

## 1. Pricing principles

These are the rules. They are not situational.

1. **Price is set against the client's future value, not our cost.** We never sum hours to
   a fee. We never expose a day rate. We never quote a fee before the value conversation
   is complete.
2. **The first fee quoted anchors every fee that follows.** Anchor high. The ceiling set
   in the first price is the ceiling for the engagement. Raising a fee after quoting it is
   not available to us.
3. **Leaving money on the table is equivalent to losing it.** An engagement priced below
   its value ceiling cannot be repriced mid-delivery. Under-pricing is not conservative;
   it is a one-way error.
4. **Never reduce a fee without reducing value.** Discount requests are scope requests in
   disguise. If the client wants a lower fee, the response is to remove scope, not to
   keep the scope and lower the price.
5. **Leave the conversation if fees are discussed before value.** The earliest a fee is
   quoted, the lower it lands. If a buyer pushes for a number before the value
   conversation, redirect — up to two times. On the third time, treat it as resistance
   and name it.
6. **We never quote time-unit prices.** No hourly, no daily, no weekly. No "blended
   rate." Every fee is a fixed project fee or a retainer fee.
7. **We lose projects on price sometimes. That is the signal the floor is correct.** If
   the win rate sits above 85% at full fee, the ladder is too cheap. Raise it.
8. **The minimum level of engagement is stated in every first conversation.** "Our
   engagements start at $12,000" is said, not implied. Waiving the minimum is always a
   closing-stage concession, never an opener.

## 2. The value-first logic

Weiss's "good deal" logic is the spine of the model: value received divided by investment
required. Our conservative target ratio is **10:1 minimum, 20:1 preferred**. If the
client's stipulated annual value from the project is $500,000, a $25,000–$50,000 fee sits
inside the defensible band. A $10,000 fee on the same project leaves $25,000+ on the
table.

Value is never imagined by us. It is **stipulated by the buyer** in the value
conversation, in plain numbers or plain-language outcomes. Our job in that conversation is
to ask, not to prove. The four value inputs we always collect:

1. **Tangible outcomes.** Revenue, leads, close rate, average deal size, customer
   retention, organic traffic, search position, cost-per-acquisition, speed of onboarding,
   founder time reclaimed.
2. **Intangible outcomes.** Reputation, peace of mind, category authority, recruitment
   advantage, board credibility, investor confidence.
3. **Emotional weight.** Personal stake the buyer carries in the outcome. Fee tolerance
   rises with emotional stake.
4. **Duration of benefit.** Premium websites carry benefit 24–36+ months. Annualised
   value, multiplied by expected duration, is the correct denominator for ROI framing.

If the buyer cannot or will not stipulate value, do not move to pricing. Either the wrong
buyer is in the room or the opportunity is not real. Both are disqualification signals.

## 3. Fee floors by tier

The floor is absolute. Below it, we do not work.

| Tier       | Minimum | Typical band   | Anchor option in proposal |
|------------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Foundation | $12,000 | $12,000–18,000 | —                         |
| Growth     | $22,000 | $22,000–40,000 | 1.5× the mid              |
| Authority  | $45,000 | $45,000–85,000 | 1.6–1.8× the mid          |

Fee floors are reviewed annually and adjusted for inflation and win-rate data.

## 4. The pricing calculator

The calculator produces a **defensible price range**, not a single number. The proposal
will carry three options drawn from the range.

### Step 1 — Base fee by tier

Start with the tier floor. If the tier is ambiguous, price to the higher tier and let the
value conversation move it if needed.

### Step 2 — Apply the value multiplier

Convert stipulated annual value × expected duration into a target fee at the 10:1 and
20:1 ratios. Use the lower of (a) tier floor, (b) 10:1 fee as the minimum defensible
price. Use the 20:1 fee as the mid-anchor.

### Step 3 — Apply commercial modifiers

Each modifier adjusts the fee upward. Modifiers are cumulative but capped at 2.0× the
base tier. Use the score sheet below — each point adds 5% to the base, up to the cap.

**Modifier score sheet.** Score 0, 1, or 2 per row.

| Factor                                   | 0 points              | 1 point                       | 2 points                          |
|------------------------------------------|-----------------------|-------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Commercial weight of the site            | Support asset         | Primary marketing channel     | Central to revenue proposition    |
| Buyer's decision complexity              | Single decision-maker | 2–3 stakeholders              | Committee or board involvement    |
| Trust burden (regulated, health, legal)  | None                  | Professional norms            | Regulated compliance stakes       |
| Content complexity                       | <15 pages             | 15–40 pages                   | 40+ pages, multiple segments      |
| Technical integration                    | None                  | CRM + analytics               | Bespoke data, portals, APIs       |
| SEO and conversion depth                 | On-page only          | Topical hubs, schema, events  | Full topical authority system     |
| Timeline pressure                        | Standard 10–14 weeks  | Compressed to 6–8 weeks       | Under 6 weeks                     |
| Multi-language or multi-region           | Single market, single lang | Bilingual                 | 3+ languages or multi-region      |
| Proof-system requirements                | None                  | 3–5 case studies              | Full proof library + research     |
| Strategic repositioning in scope         | No                    | Messaging refresh             | Full category repositioning       |

Each point = +5%. Maximum +100% over the tier floor, capped by the tier band ceiling.

### Step 4 — Sanity check against the value ratio

Run the candidate mid-price through the value ratio:

- Candidate fee ÷ annual value × expected duration = ROI ratio
- Target: 10:1 minimum, 20:1 preferred
- Below 10:1: we are overpriced for the stipulated value — either surface more value or
  adjust tier
- Above 50:1: we are underpriced — raise the anchor

### Step 5 — Build the three options

Options are drawn from the range, not invented. Each option includes the full system of
its tier; options differ in scope of inclusions, not quality. Never vary quality by tier.

- **Option 1 (anchor — most expensive).** One tier above the natural fit, or the natural
  fit at the top of its band + an adjacent strategic addition (e.g. authority asset
  production, proof-system production, deeper measurement).
- **Option 2 (recommended).** The natural fit at the mid of its band.
- **Option 3 (budget yes).** The natural fit at the floor of its band, with reduced but
  still complete scope (fewer pages, shorter optimisation window, fewer interviews).

Options must stand at least **20% apart** by fee — Weiss's rule. Smaller gaps stop the
client from feeling a meaningful choice.

## 5. Worked example

**Scenario.** Regional consulting firm, revenue $6M, current site outdated. Stipulated
outcomes from the value conversation:

- Raise lead quality to unlock enterprise accounts worth $200,000 average
- Target 6 additional enterprise engagements per year (conservative)
- Annual value stated: $1,200,000
- Expected duration of benefit: 3 years
- 3-year stipulated value: $3,600,000
- Emotional stake: founder-stage firm, credibility in boardroom meetings

**Step 1.** Growth tier fit (multi-service, multi-segment, enterprise buyer). Tier floor:
$22,000.

**Step 2.** 10:1 fee = $360,000 (unrealistic upper bound). 20:1 fee = $180,000. Our cap is
the tier band ceiling: $40,000. The value comfortably supports the full Growth band.

**Step 3.** Modifier scoring.

- Commercial weight: 2 (central to revenue proposition)
- Decision complexity: 1 (2 stakeholders)
- Trust burden: 1 (professional norms)
- Content complexity: 1 (~25 pages)
- Integration: 1 (CRM + analytics)
- SEO/conversion depth: 2 (full topical authority system)
- Timeline: 0
- Multi-language: 0
- Proof system: 2 (full proof library)
- Repositioning: 1 (messaging refresh)

Total: 11 points × 5% = +55% over $22,000 = $34,100.

**Step 4.** ROI check: $34,100 ÷ $3,600,000 = roughly 1:106. Well above the 20:1 target,
so the pricing is generous to the client. This is an Authority-tier candidate, not a
Growth-tier candidate. Promote the tier.

**Repricing at Authority floor:** $45,000. Modifier score holds, +55% → $69,750. ROI =
roughly 1:52. Still above 20:1. Landing zone: $55,000–$75,000.

**Three options.**

- Option 1 (anchor): $75,000 — Authority system + authority asset production (diagnostic
  research report)
- Option 2 (recommended): $62,000 — Authority system, core scope
- Option 3 (budget yes): $48,000 — Authority system at the floor, scaled proof library,
  single-pass optimisation window

Each option is 20%+ apart. The anchor is not absurd — it sits comfortably inside the
stipulated value. The budget option is still above Growth pricing, which protects the
category.

## 6. Fee-defence playbook

When the buyer objects to the fee, the objection is almost always about trust, need, or
urgency — not price. Weiss's four-zone model:

1. **"I don't trust you."** Solved with proof: case studies, testimonials, published work,
   referrals. Not solved with a discount.
2. **"I don't need you."** Surface the cost of the status quo. Every month of delay is a
   lost-opportunity number.
3. **"I don't feel urgency."** Connect to a dated event: a funding round, a product
   launch, a competitor move, a board meeting. If no date exists, the opportunity is
   intent-less and should be disqualified.
4. **"I don't have the money."** Almost always a mask for one of the three above. When
   real, help the buyer stage payments or split fiscal years — do not reduce the fee.

Tactical responses to premature fee questions:

- *"It would be unfair to put a number on the table before we understand what you
  need — give me two more questions before I do."*
- *"Our engagements start at $12,000 and range upward based on the scope. Is that in the
  range you expected?"*
- *"The mid-tier projects we run sit around $30,000–$45,000. The work we just described
  sounds closer to the top of that band. Would that be workable?"*

Tactical responses to discount requests:

- *"I can move to a $40,000 engagement, but that means removing X and Y from the scope.
  Which would you like removed?"*
- *"I can offer a 7% courtesy discount for payment in full on signing. That is the only
  discount structure we offer."*
- *"If Option 2 is outside the budget, Option 3 is the right engagement. We should not
  cut Option 2 to fit — we should move to the option that matches."*

Silence is a tool. After stating a number, stop talking. The first person to speak after a
price is quoted loses a round.

## 7. Payment terms

Payment terms are part of the fee. Weaker terms are effectively a discount.

**Standard terms.**

- 50% on signing, 50% at mid-point milestone (typically design sign-off)
- Project does not start until the first payment clears
- Late payment triggers a pause; work resumes when payment lands

**Preferred terms (offer a 7–10% courtesy discount).**

- 100% on signing

**Never-acceptable terms.**

- Net 60+ on any tranche
- Payment contingent on launch
- Payment contingent on outcome (leads generated, revenue earned)
- Payment contingent on third-party approvals

Pay-for-performance, revenue share, and equity deals are declined. Too many variables
sit outside our control.

## 8. Retainers and post-launch work

Project fees buy the project. Retainers buy ongoing access. They are priced, structured,
and contracted separately.

**Retainer design rules (Weiss).**

- Minimum term: 3 months. Preferred: 6–12 months with a single annual payment.
- Scope is access, not presence. The retainer does not buy hours; it buys the agency's
  availability to think with the client on defined topics.
- Paid at the start of the period. Annual prepayment earns a 10% discount.
- New discrete projects inside a retainer are separate engagements, priced value-based.
  Retainer clients receive 20% off discrete projects on quarterly retainers, up to 33%
  off on annual retainers.

Retainer tiers will be defined in Phase 8. Do not invent them inside a project sale.

## 9. Review and governance

- Fee floors reviewed every 12 months. Adjustments documented and dated.
- Modifier score sheet reviewed every 12 months against win-rate and scope-drift data.
- Every waived minimum is logged with reason, client, fee, and outcome. Target: fewer
  than four waivers per year.
- Every closed-lost on price is logged. If lost on price to a premium competitor, the
  floor is correct. If lost to a freelancer or template shop, the qualification step
  failed upstream — review, not adjust the floor.
- Win rate at full fee reviewed quarterly. Target band: 50–70%.
