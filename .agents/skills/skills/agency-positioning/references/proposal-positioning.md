# Proposal Positioning for $10k+ Projects

How we present the offer to a qualified prospect. The proposal is not a sales document —
it is the written record of a decision already reached in conversation.

Grounded in Weiss's proposal structure (situation appraisal, objectives, measures, value,
methodology, timing, joint accountabilities, terms, acceptance), Enns's closing
conversation (three-option one-page proposal, anchor high, say the price out loud), and
Block's contracting framework (explicit wants and offers from both sides, named
boundaries, authentic language).

Companion files: `premium-website-offer-playbook.md`, `pricing-rules.md`, `scope-matrix.md`,
`qualification-guide.md`.

## 0. When this file ends and `proposal-skills/` begins

This file covers the short, 3–6 page sales proposal used in the closing conversation for
a direct-to-principal engagement. That proposal is the words coming out of our mouths,
written down — it is not a formal RFP response.

When the prospect is an institutional buyer — government, NGO, foundation, enterprise
procurement, multi-stakeholder tender, formal Expression of Interest — reach for the
submoduled **`proposal-skills/`** repository instead (at
`https://github.com/peterbamuhigire/proposal-skills`). That skill set covers the ten
numbered sections expected in formal proposals (cover letter, executive summary,
understanding of assignment, firm profile, relevant experience, methodology, team
composition, work plan, expression of interest, financial proposal) plus the supporting
domain skills those proposals draw on.

Rule of thumb:

- Direct principal buyer, private firm, value conversation has happened → this file.
- RFP, tender, institutional buyer, multi-section submission mandated → `proposal-skills/`.
- Hybrid (institutional buyer asking for a short proposal) → this file for the
  commercial logic + pricing; `proposal-skills/` for the formal sections they require.

The commercial logic — offer ladder, pricing, qualification, scope — stays in
`agency-positioning/` regardless of which proposal format is used.

## 1. What the proposal is and is not

**The proposal is:**

- The written record of an agreement reached in the value conversation and the closing
  conversation
- A commitment document — when signed, it is the contract
- The shortest possible expression of scope, value, options, fee, and terms

**The proposal is not:**

- A sales pitch
- A "capability overview" with pages of past work
- A place to introduce new ideas the buyer has not already heard
- A menu of every service we offer
- A budget estimate with pricing at the bottom
- Boilerplate legal language (the legal department reads boilerplate; the economic buyer
  does not)

The sale is lost or won in conversation. The proposal records it. If the proposal is
carrying the persuasion, something upstream has failed.

---

## 2. The conversations that precede the proposal

A proposal is only written after four conversations, in order. Skip any of them and the
proposal becomes a guess.

1. **Probative conversation.** Before the first meeting: the prospect finds us through
   expert content, a referral, or a named specialisation. They arrive already seeing us
   as the expert. If they do not, the flip has not happened yet — do not proceed.
2. **Qualifying conversation.** First person-to-person call. Disqualify or advance on
   fit, decision makers, timeframe, and budget. See `qualification-guide.md`.
3. **Value conversation.** A scheduled working call — typically 60–90 minutes. Four
   steps, in order: confirm the desired future state, identify measures of success,
   quantify the value to be created, set pricing guidance (an anchor range). Do not
   discuss solutions yet. The conversation ends with client agreement on a broad price
   range and permission to think expansively.
4. **Closing conversation.** A scheduled meeting with all decision makers present. The
   proposal is presented, not emailed. The proposal is the words that come out of our
   mouths; the document is the record.

The proposal document is delivered inside the closing conversation, not before.

## 3. Proposal structure (the Weiss skeleton, adapted)

Nine sections, in order. One document. Ideally 3–6 pages. Never boilerplate.

### 1. Situation appraisal

One paragraph. What is true about the client's current condition and what prompted the
project. Written in their words from the qualifying and value conversations.

> *"ACME Consulting is a 12-person advisory firm whose enterprise accounts have grown
> 40% year-on-year but whose digital presence still reflects the five-person firm it was
> three years ago. Incoming enterprise prospects arrive from referrals already primed,
> but then encounter a website that undermines the premium narrative — and, based on
> what [buyer] shared, roughly one in three prospects stalls at this point."*

### 2. Objectives

Business outcomes, not tasks. Three to five items. Each expressed as a result, not an
activity.

> - Support enterprise-tier lead conversion at the rate achieved by current top-of-funnel
>   volume (estimated 6–8 enterprise deals per year vs. 2 today)
> - Reposition the firm against the three named regional competitors as the category
>   authority in [specialty]
> - Reduce the time [principal] spends on first-meeting qualification by 40%
> - Establish a credible platform for the [dated expansion event]

### 3. Measures of success

How we will know progress is being made. Mix quantitative and qualitative.

> - Lead-to-meeting conversion rate
> - Organic-search visibility against the five named target keywords
> - Qualitative feedback from three named enterprise buyers, recorded at 90 days
> - [Principal]'s own judgement that the site is carrying its weight in sales
>   conversations

### 4. Value

The value the client stipulated in the value conversation, repeated here. This section
is their language, not ours.

> - **Stipulated tangible value:** six additional enterprise engagements per year at
>   $180,000 average revenue = $1,080,000 additional annual revenue
> - **Stipulated intangible value:** credibility in boardroom introductions; reduced
>   discomfort in price conversations; recruiting leverage for senior hires
> - **Stipulated duration:** 3 years before a next rebuild
> - **Stipulated emotional weight:** "we want to stop feeling like we outgrew our own
>   website two years ago"

### 5. Methodology and options

Three options, anchored high. Each option describes what is delivered, not how many
hours it takes. Fees are **not** attached here; they appear in section 8.

> **Option 1 — Authority Website System (anchor)**
>
> Full positioning engagement, brand narrative system, buyer-journey mapping across three
> segments, 30+ finished pages, full topical authority SEO system, attribution model,
> KPI dashboard, 90-day optimisation window with three review cycles, full
> documentation kit. Includes the cornerstone diagnostic asset — an original research
> piece published under [firm]'s name that functions as a lead magnet for the next
> 24 months.
>
> **Option 2 — Authority Website System, core scope (recommended)**
>
> Full positioning engagement, brand narrative system, buyer-journey mapping, 25–30
> pages, topical authority SEO system, 90-day optimisation window with two review
> cycles, full documentation kit. No cornerstone asset; content hub is scaffolded and
> ready for the client to populate.
>
> **Option 3 — Growth Website Project**
>
> Discovery and messaging hierarchy, buyer-journey mapping for the two primary segments,
> 15–20 pages, full on-page SEO, 60-day optimisation window, admin documentation.
> Delivers the site. Does not deliver the authority positioning system.

Options must be meaningfully different in value — not in hours, not in page counts, not
in deliverable volume. Each option is a complete engagement at a different altitude.

### 6. Timing

Start date, major milestones, end date. Written explicitly so the client can plan against
it.

> - Start: [date]
> - Strategy phase complete: [+4 weeks]
> - Design direction sign-off: [+8 weeks]
> - Build and content complete: [+12 weeks]
> - Launch: [+14 weeks]
> - Optimisation window ends: [+17 weeks]

### 7. Joint accountabilities

Explicit wants and offers from both sides. Block's contracting ground rule: we name what
*we* need, not just what we will do.

> **Our accountabilities**
>
> - Deliver the system described under the selected option
> - Name one account director as single point of contact
> - Respond to feedback within two business days
> - Host weekly working calls during active phases
> - Maintain the project plan as the single source of truth
>
> **Your accountabilities**
>
> - [Economic buyer] attends kickoff, strategy sign-off, mid-point review, and launch
>   sign-off
> - One named decision maker collates feedback from internal stakeholders
> - Feedback is returned within five business days of request
> - Access to [3–5 named existing customers] for discovery interviews
> - Access to analytics, CRM, and existing brand assets within week one
> - Payment terms as described in section 8

### 8. Terms and investment

Fees live here. One price per option, stated in a single figure. Payment terms stated
explicitly.

> - Option 1: $75,000
> - Option 2: $62,000
> - Option 3: $48,000
>
> **Payment terms.** 50% on signing, 50% at design sign-off. A 7% courtesy discount
> applies if the full fee is paid on signing.
>
> Fees are fixed. Out-of-scope work is agreed via written change order, priced and
> scheduled before work begins.

### 9. Acceptance

A single signature block. No "please contact us to proceed" language — the client signs
the proposal and returns it. The signed proposal is the contract.

> **Accepted by**
>
> Name: ___________________________
>
> Title: ___________________________
>
> Option selected: ___________________________
>
> Date: ___________________________

## 4. The closing conversation choreography

The proposal is presented in a meeting with all decision makers present. The
seven-point agenda:

1. **Timecheck.** "We have 45 minutes. Does that still work?"
2. **Roll call.** Confirm everyone who needs to be on the call is on the call. If the
   economic buyer is missing, pause and reschedule — do not present to a partial room.
3. **State the objective.** "The purpose of today is to agree on a path forward and the
   terms of working together. I expect we'll either land on an option we both feel
   good about, or we'll both leave clear about why this isn't the right fit."
4. **Recap the value conversation.** 3–5 minutes. "Here's what we agreed is at stake —
   confirm this is still accurate."
5. **Share the options.** Anchor first. Say the price out loud before revealing the
   document. Walk through each option in 60–90 seconds. Recommend Option 2 explicitly.
6. **Facilitate discussion.** Ask: "Which of these feels right?" Then be quiet.
7. **Drive the next step.** Signed acceptance today, or a defined next action with a
   date — *"Friday by 5pm, you'll let me know which option and send the signed
   proposal."* Never leave without a named next step and a date.

## 5. The principles behind the document

**Anchor high.** The first price quoted sets the ceiling. Option 1 is deliberately sized
above the natural fit. The middle option is recommended. The smallest option exists so a
budget-constrained client can still say yes without us dropping the floor.

**Say it out loud before they see it.** Humans process spoken numbers differently from
written ones. When we say "Option 2 is sixty-two thousand dollars" before the document is
opened, the number is absorbed relationally, not analytically.

**Embrace silence.** After quoting a price, stop talking. The first person to speak
concedes. This is a discipline, not a tactic.

**Let policy answer objections.** When a request pushes against our model — "can you
just do an hourly estimate?" — the answer is a clear policy. "Our engagements are priced
on value, not hours. We don't provide hourly estimates." Policy is harder to negotiate
against than preference.

**Recommend one.** We present three options, but we recommend one. A client who asks
"which would you choose?" and hears "they're all good options" is being abandoned by
their expert. The recommended option is Option 2 by default; state which it is and why.

**Reduce value to reduce fee.** If the client wants a lower fee, we remove scope. We do
not absorb scope at a reduced fee.

**Get paid before work begins.** The first payment must clear before any work starts.
Not "the contract is signed" — *the money has moved*. Commitment is measured in cleared
funds.

## 6. Writing voice for the proposal

The proposal reads like the buyer's voice on their best day. Three rules:

1. **Use their words, not ours.** "Enterprise accounts," "category authority,"
   "boardroom credibility" — take phrases directly from the value conversation. If you
   did not hear it from them, do not put it in the proposal.
2. **No agency jargon.** No "bespoke," "journey," "ecosystem," "digital-first,"
   "holistic," "synergy." No copy that could appear on any other agency's proposal.
3. **No hedging.** Do not say "we aim to" or "we will endeavour to." Say "we will" or
   "we deliver."

The proposal should feel like the client's own thinking, organised and priced. It is not
the place for the agency's voice. The agency's voice lives in the website we will build
for them.

## 7. Handling the common objections

**"We need to think about it."**
*"Of course. What specifically are you thinking about — is it scope, fee, timeline, or
fit? Let's cover that now so you have what you need to decide."*

**"We need to compare this to another quote."**
*"Understood. I'd rather you didn't compare on price — each firm's scope will look
different. If it's useful, I'll send you four questions to ask each proposer so you can
compare the answers rather than the numbers."*

**"Can we start with just the homepage?"**
*"We don't split projects by page. The engagement is priced on the positioning outcome,
not the page count — a homepage alone doesn't deliver the outcome we discussed. The
nearest fit would be Option 3 or a smaller Foundation engagement."*

**"Can you match [competitor's] price?"**
*"Probably not — our pricing is set against the value we agreed in the last
conversation, not against a competitor's quote. If another firm is significantly
cheaper, they're likely delivering a different scope or operating at a different
level. We can talk about what the difference would be, if it's useful."*

**"Can we reduce the fee by 20%?"**
*"Yes — by removing [X] or moving to Option 3. Which would you prefer?"*

**"Why is it $75,000? That feels high."**
*"Based on what you stipulated, the engagement is worth $3.4M to you over three years.
At $75,000, the ratio is roughly 45:1 — a ratio of 20:1 would be a strong investment.
We can talk about whether Option 2 or Option 3 fits better, or we can talk about the
scope assumptions behind Option 1."*

**"Can you guarantee results?"**
*"Not results in the sense of revenue — too many variables sit with you, not with me.
I can guarantee the quality of the work and the delivery of the system. If you're not
happy with the strategy phase, I refund that phase and we part ways — that's the only
guarantee I offer, and I stand by it."*

**"We want to pay on completion."**
*"Our terms are 50% on signing and 50% at design sign-off. That's non-negotiable."*

## 8. After the close

Buyer's remorse starts within hours of the yes. Enns calls this the reassurance phase.
Counter it with small acts of escalation:

- A same-day written confirmation of the decision, start date, and first milestone
- A welcome-to-the-project packet (one page, the kickoff agenda, the first request for
  materials)
- A scheduled kickoff within 5 working days — the sooner, the more the yes holds
- First payment instruction sent with clear, friction-free payment methods
- A small, symbolic first step completed within the first week — a meeting, a named
  document, a visible artefact — so the engagement feels real

The sequence is: private yes → verbal yes → signed yes → money moved → work begun.
Only the last step is true commitment. Drive the sequence deliberately.

## 9. Quarterly review of proposals

Every quarter:

- Review every proposal sent. What was the win rate? Where were we lost?
- Count proposals that were signed without edits vs. proposals that required
  back-and-forth. Edits usually mean a conversation was skipped upstream.
- Count proposals where the client chose Option 1, Option 2, Option 3, or none. A
  distribution skewed toward Option 3 signals that Options 1–2 are out of reach or
  unconvincing.
- Log every objection that showed up in the closing conversation. Patterns of the same
  objection mean the value conversation is not landing the same point every time.

The proposal is a mirror of the sales motion. Its data reveals where the sales motion
needs repair.
