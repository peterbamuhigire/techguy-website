# Qualification Guide — Who We Serve, Who We Don't

Qualification is the earliest and cheapest place to protect the agency's economics. A
bad-fit client is expensive long before it is obvious, and the cost shows up in margin
erosion, delivery trauma, founder exhaustion, and downstream reputational drift.

Grounded in Enns (selectivity, prospect hierarchy, the expert's posture), Weiss (the
economic buyer, four zones of resistance), Warrillow (firing misfit clients, the
productized "no"), and Block (50/50 responsibility, naming the political terrain).

Companion files: `premium-website-offer-playbook.md`, `pricing-rules.md`,
`proposal-positioning.md`, `scope-matrix.md`.

---

## 1. The qualification principle

> We do not seek clients. We selectively and respectfully pursue fits.

A premium agency's pipeline is a filter, not a funnel. Every conversation qualifies or
disqualifies; nothing sits in limbo. Interest is not intent. A lead that is "still
thinking" after the first conversation is not a warm lead — it is a cold lead who has not
been told no yet.

Four working rules:

1. **Disqualification is the default.** A lead is assumed to be the wrong fit until
   evidence says otherwise. Evidence comes from what the prospect *does*, not what they
   say.
2. **No means save time.** An early no is always better than a late no. Ask the
   disqualifying questions first.
3. **The right-fit client grants behavioural concessions.** They take the meeting, they
   bring the economic buyer, they answer the diagnostic questions, they agree to the
   minimum engagement level. A prospect who will not do these is not right-fit.
4. **We walk before we bend.** If the terms that make the engagement succeed are not on
   the table, we leave politely. Saying no makes the next yes possible.

## 2. Who we serve

Two tests, in order. A prospect must pass **both**.

### Test 1 — Sector fit

We specialise in high-trust businesses: firms whose primary asset is reputation and whose
buyers are making a judgement call before a transactional one.

**Primary sectors.**

- Professional services firms (management consulting, accounting, financial advisory,
  specialist consulting practices)
- Legal firms (full-service and specialist practices, see `sectors/legal/`)
- Healthcare and allied health (clinics, specialists, health-tech with a clinical
  interface)
- Education and institutional organisations (schools, universities, professional bodies,
  certifying institutions, research institutes)
- Premium local businesses where trust is the buying criterion (architects, boutique
  hotels, specialist retailers, high-end home services)
- Consulting-adjacent operators (coaches, advisors, speakers with institutional buyers)

**Adjacent sectors we will consider.** Fintech with an advisory layer, climate/ESG
advisory, research and policy organisations, foundations and grant-making bodies,
specialist software vendors selling into regulated industries.

### Test 2 — Commercial weight

The website must be commercially load-bearing for the firm. We do not build for firms
where the site is an afterthought.

Signals of commercial weight:

- Revenue at least $500k annually; ideally $1M+
- Buyer journey measured in weeks or months, not minutes
- Enterprise value, book value, or partner value attached to the firm's market position
- Specific, dated business pressure driving the project (funding round, rebrand,
  repositioning, new practice launch, merger, competitor pressure, founder transition)
- Decision buyer willing to be interviewed and to invest time in the work

If revenue is below $500k and no dated pressure exists, we refer out. The fee floor does
not match the project's business weight.

## 3. Who we do not serve

These are sector and situational disqualifiers. We refuse, politely, and refer where
possible.

**Sector disqualifiers.**

- Pure e-commerce, DTC brands, and product-led businesses where conversion is
  transactional and trust is commoditised
- Adult industries
- Gambling, speculative trading platforms
- High-risk financial products (binary options, crypto pump-and-dump, multi-level
  marketing)
- Political campaigns, partisan advocacy organisations (case-by-case)
- Products or services whose core promise we cannot verify and would not personally
  recommend

**Situational disqualifiers.**

- The prospect wants to control the process, the design, or the content beyond sign-off —
  "we just need someone to build what we've already decided"
- The prospect refuses to engage with diagnosis ("we don't need a strategy phase, just
  build the thing")
- The economic buyer refuses to be in the room
- The prospect shops aggressively on price, compares quotes line-by-line, or leads with
  "what's your best rate?"
- The prospect has used "inexpensive and inexperienced" providers repeatedly and has been
  mis-educated about what premium work involves (Weiss)
- The prospect attempts to compress the engagement below its minimum size ("can you just
  do the homepage?")
- The prospect insists on pay-for-performance, equity, revenue share, or contingency
  terms
- The budget is materially below the tier minimum and the prospect is unwilling or unable
  to expand
- The timeline is ludicrous (full Authority build in 4 weeks) and unmovable

Each of these is a hard disqualifier. None is negotiable during the sales conversation;
attempting to negotiate them *is* the mistake.

## 4. The fit signals we watch

Signals that a prospect *is* a right fit. Watch for these actively.

- They **describe the problem in business terms**, not design terms. "Our lead quality is
  dropping" is a fit signal. "We want it to look like Stripe's website" is not.
- They **name an economic buyer** and that buyer is accessible.
- They **grant behavioural concessions** — take the meeting at the inconvenient time,
  bring the decision maker, complete the intake questionnaire, show up prepared.
- They **answer the value question**. When asked "what would it be worth to solve this?",
  they can quantify or at least describe the answer.
- They have a **dated event** driving action — a launch, a funding round, a partner
  review, a competitor move.
- They **retreat-follow**. When we raise an objection on their behalf ("I'm not sure we
  can hit that timeline — are you open to a later start?"), they follow. When we retreat
  slightly, they lean in.
- They **respect the minimum**. When we quote the floor, they engage with it rather than
  flinching or disappearing.
- They **want to be led**. They ask "what would you do?" more than "can you do X?"

A prospect hitting four or more fit signals in the first conversation is a strong
candidate. Fewer than three signals is a slow-to-no.

## 5. The red flags

Signals to actively watch for. Any one red flag is grounds to probe. Two red flags is
grounds to disqualify.

- **Self-diagnosed and self-prescribed.** "We've done the research, we just need you to
  execute." The client has closed the diagnostic door before it opened.
- **Process dictated by them.** "Here's our RFP process, please respond by Friday." A
  client who will not allow the expert to lead in the sale will not allow it in delivery.
- **No access to the economic buyer.** We are routed through a marketing coordinator, a
  procurement officer, or an agency intermediary.
- **Aggressive price shopping.** "What's your best rate?" "We have three quotes." "Can
  you beat $8,000?" Fee objections before value is discussed.
- **Revision anxiety.** The prospect telegraphs endless revision expectations. "We're
  quite particular." "Our CEO likes to be very involved." "We'll want to see lots of
  options."
- **Scope creep signalled early.** "And could you also do our pitch deck / brochure /
  emails / Linkedin banners?"
- **Timeline pressure without reason.** A 6-week deadline for no stated commercial
  reason.
- **Non-commercial decision-making.** Decisions routed through a family member, a
  board spouse, or an unnamed advisor.
- **Evasiveness on budget.** When asked about the range they expected to invest, the
  answer is "we don't have a budget yet" combined with a reluctance to discuss the
  question at all.
- **Past agency carnage.** The prospect tells a story of multiple failed agency
  relationships with a villain narrative. They are either genuinely unlucky or they are
  the villain.
- **The request is too small.** "We just need a quick one-pager." Under the floor; not
  for us regardless of sector.
- **The request is too adjacent.** They want primarily paid advertising, brand identity,
  or ongoing content. We are not the right agency.

## 6. The qualification conversation

The first call has one job: disqualify quickly or advance deliberately. Enns's BANT
variant is the structure: Context, Desired Future State, Decision makers, Timeframe,
Budget. In that order.

A practical question map. Use what is useful; skip what is not.

### Context

- *"Before I ask anything about a website, tell me about the business. What do you do,
  who do you serve, how did you get to the position you're in today?"*
- *"What prompted this conversation now — why a website project now rather than six
  months ago or six months from now?"*
- *"What is the current site doing or failing to do?"*

### Desired future state (Dan Sullivan's three-year question, adapted)

- *"If we're having this conversation three years from now and you're looking back, what
  needs to have happened with the website for you to feel it was worth it?"*
- *"What does the business look like in three years if the website is genuinely
  working?"*
- *"What changes about your working life if this project succeeds?"*

### Decision makers

- *"Who else needs to be part of the decision to move forward?"*
- *"Who signs the cheque?"*
- *"Who will be the lead stakeholder on your side during delivery?"*

### Timeframe

- *"Is there a specific event or date this needs to align with?"*
- *"What happens if the project starts six months later?"*

### Budget

- *"Has a budget been allocated for this work?"*
- *"Our engagements start at $12,000 and typical Growth-tier projects run $25,000–$40,000,
  Authority-tier $50,000 and up. Is that in the range you were prepared to invest?"*

## 7. The disqualification script

A polite, clean no saves everyone's time. Three patterns:

**Pattern A — Wrong sector or wrong scale.**

> "Based on what you've shared, this looks like it sits outside the work we take on —
> we specialise in [category] for [sector], where the work runs at $12,000 and up. I
> don't want to waste your time. Here are two firms that would be a better fit — [X]
> and [Y]."

**Pattern B — Wrong posture.**

> "We work in a specific way: strategy first, one recommended direction, options priced
> on value rather than hours. It sounds like you're looking for a partner who'll execute
> a plan you've already built. That's a legitimate way to buy — it just isn't how we
> work. Let me suggest [X] instead."

**Pattern C — Budget below the floor.**

> "Our minimum engagement is $12,000. I hear you're looking at $5,000–$8,000, which is
> genuinely a sensible budget for what you've described — just not for us. I'd recommend
> [freelancer referral] or [template-shop referral]. Both do strong work at that level."

Say these lines clearly. Do not hedge. Hedging invites negotiation.

## 8. Firing misfit clients

Some bad-fit clients are identified after the engagement starts. A bad-fit client we did
not disqualify in the sales stage will not become a good-fit client during delivery.

**Triggers for ending an engagement.**

- Repeated contract breaches (non-payment, silent scope expansion, abusive communication)
- Persistent refusal to engage with the process (missing meetings, ignoring feedback
  requests, stalling sign-offs indefinitely)
- Decision paralysis beyond two structured interventions
- Personal conduct that makes the engagement unsafe or toxic for staff

**The firing process.**

1. **Name the pattern.** A direct conversation with the economic buyer. Block's
   authentic-language frame — describe what is happening in neutral terms, ask for the
   specific change needed.
2. **Offer the last chance.** A written warning with a 14-day window for the specific
   behaviours to change. Documented.
3. **Exit cleanly.** If the behaviour does not change, terminate per the contract.
   Deliver what has been paid for to date. Return any unused fees pro-rata. Send a final
   summary and walk away.

Fire the bottom 10–15% of the client base on an annual review. Clients who refer, who
recognise the value of the work, who pay on time, who grant the agency behavioural
latitude — these are the clients to keep. Everyone else is a slot better filled by a
right-fit client.

## 9. Referral network

A qualified no needs a place to send the lead. Build and maintain:

- 2–3 **freelancer referrals** for sub-$10k projects (named individuals, with quality we
  stake our name on)
- 2 **template-shop referrals** for DIY-adjacent prospects
- 2 **peer-agency referrals** for work genuinely outside our sector or scope (e-commerce
  specialists, brand identity firms, ad agencies)
- 1 **pair-of-hands developer** for execute-the-spec work
- 1 **SEO-only practice** for prospects who want only SEO work
- 1 **PR / content-only agency** for prospects who want only content work

Make the referrals real — co-create with them, exchange introductions, share positioning
so their work matches the pass-through expectations. A referral network is part of the
qualification infrastructure; without it, the "no" costs the prospect a second search.

## 10. Quarterly qualification review

Every quarter, review:

- **Conversion rate by entry source.** Where are fit leads coming from?
- **Disqualification rate at first call.** Target: 40–60%. Lower means the inbound is too
  narrow or we are over-qualifying. Higher means upstream positioning is attracting the
  wrong leads.
- **Win rate of qualified leads.** Target: 50–70% at full fee.
- **Post-launch NPS / feedback quality.** Misfit patterns often show up here, not in the
  initial qualification.
- **Project margin by client sector.** Systematic margin erosion in a sector is a
  qualification signal, not a pricing signal.

Adjust inbound channels, language, and filters based on the data. The goal: fewer
conversations, better ones.
