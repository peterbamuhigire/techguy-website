# Project Scope Matrix

Scope is defended by structure, not by negotiation. Every tier has a documented scope
definition; anything outside that definition is either a separate engagement or a
declined request.

Grounded in Warrillow's productized-service discipline (*Built to Sell*), Enns's rules of
collaboration (*Win Without Pitching*), Weiss's scope-for-fee rule (*Value-Based Fees*),
and Block's contracting framework (*Flawless Consulting*).

Companion files: `premium-website-offer-playbook.md`, `pricing-rules.md`,
`proposal-positioning.md`.

---

## 1. Scope principles

1. **Scope is the product.** Scope is what the fee pays for. Nothing else.
2. **Every inclusion is named. Every exclusion is named.** If it is not named in the
   proposal, it is not included. "Intent to include" is not a scope item.
3. **Scope varies between tiers, not between projects.** Once a tier is chosen, scope is
   fixed to that tier's definition. Custom scope is a new engagement.
4. **Reductions change scope, not fee.** A discount request is a scope-reduction request.
5. **Additions are priced separately.** Change orders are written before work begins, not
   after.
6. **Time is part of scope.** Timeline, revision count, and review cycles are scope items
   as much as pages or features.

## 3. Hard exclusions across all tiers

Explicitly excluded — not for negotiation. Listed in every proposal.

- Full brand identity redesign (logos, wordmarks, palette from zero). We align, we do not
  reinvent. Full brand identity is a separate engagement or referred out.
- Custom photography and illustration. We source from the client library, licensed stock,
  or advise on a photography brief.
- Video production, motion graphics beyond basic UI motion, animation beyond the design
  system's motion tokens.
- E-commerce carts, subscription billing systems, product catalogues. Stripe/PayPal links
  for deposits or event fees are fine.
- Custom applications, portals, authenticated user areas, member dashboards, community
  platforms.
- Integrations beyond a standard contact form + CRM webhook (custom API builds, OAuth
  flows, ETL pipelines, middleware).
- Copy for documents outside the website (brochures, sales decks, pitch materials,
  investor packs).
- Paid advertising, ad creative, media buying, landing page A/B test operations.
- General marketing, PR, email campaigns, social media content production.
- SEO outside the website (backlink outreach, digital PR, guest posting).
- Ongoing maintenance, security patching, content updates, monitoring — these sit on
  separate retainer agreements (Phase 8).
- Hosting management beyond launch configuration. Client owns hosting relationship
  post-launch.
- Outranking guarantees, traffic guarantees, lead-volume guarantees.

## 4. Handoff boundaries

The day the post-launch optimisation window ends, the agency's delivery obligations end.
From that day forward, the client owns the site, the code, the content, and the ongoing
operations.

The handoff package includes:

- Admin credentials transferred to the client's owner
- Hosting account transferred or documented for transfer
- Code repository either transferred or granted permanent client access
- DNS records documented
- CMS training completed
- Documentation kit delivered (tier-specific)
- A final handover meeting with written next-steps

After handoff:

- Ad-hoc support requests are billed at the ad-hoc rate (tier-specific) or routed to a
  retainer agreement
- We do not perform silent maintenance. If a client messages about a bug two weeks
  post-handoff, the response is either a retainer proposal or a billable ticket
- Agency-side brand promotion (case study write-up, portfolio feature) is negotiated in
  the handoff, not assumed

## 5. Hosting, maintenance, and ongoing responsibility

**Hosting.** The client owns their hosting relationship. During delivery we operate in the
client's account or in a staging environment we manage temporarily. At launch, hosting is
either transferred back or documented for client takeover.

**Maintenance.** Not included in any project fee. Options:

- **Care plan retainer** (Phase 8): monthly fee for security patching, backups, uptime
  monitoring, minor content edits, analytics reports
- **Growth retainer** (Phase 8): strategic quarterly reviews, iteration work, content
  additions, SEO operations
- **Ad-hoc billing**: client submits tickets, each billed against the ad-hoc rate

Silent maintenance is not offered. A site without a maintenance agreement is an
unsupported site.

**Content updates.** Not included post-handover. The client is trained to update their own
content during handoff. If the client does not want to update content, they sign a care
plan or a growth retainer.

## 6. Scope-creep defence playbook

Every project attracts requests that sit outside scope. The playbook for handling them:

1. **Name the request.** "That's a new piece of scope — an additional case study / a
   second language / a custom integration."
2. **Check it against the matrix.** If it is in the tier scope, do it. If it is not,
   continue.
3. **Present the options.** Either: (a) swap it into scope by removing something else of
   equivalent effort, or (b) add it as a change order with a defined fee and timeline
   impact.
4. **Write the change order.** No verbal agreements. A one-paragraph written confirmation
   with fee, scope change, and timeline impact. Client signs or emails approval.
5. **Do not start the new work before the change order is accepted.** This is the rule
   that defends every other rule.

Common scope-creep patterns and the response:

- *"Can we just add a blog?"* → Growth and Authority include blog scaffolding. Foundation
  does not. Change order for Foundation: $1,500–$3,000.
- *"Can we add a second language?"* → Change order. Fee depends on content volume:
  $4,000–$12,000 per language.
- *"Can you also migrate our old blog posts?"* → Change order. Content migration priced
  per 25-post batch.
- *"Can you design the launch email?"* → Out of scope. Referred to a copy contractor or
  billed as ad-hoc.
- *"We want to rewrite the homepage after launch."* → Covered if inside the optimisation
  window. Billed as ad-hoc after.
- *"Our CEO wants to see three more design directions."* → Covered by the design revision
  allowance. Additional directions billed per `pricing-rules.md`.

## 7. Revision discipline

Revisions are structured. Each tier carries a defined number of cycles per artefact.

- **A revision cycle is a single round of consolidated, written feedback.** One email, one
  document, one meeting — not a trickle of messages over five days.
- **Feedback must be consolidated across stakeholders.** We do not accept contradictory
  feedback from different stakeholders. The client's sponsor collates.
- **Revisions address the current deliverable, not new scope.** "Make the blue bolder" is
  a revision. "Add a pricing page" is scope.
- **Signed-off artefacts are not re-opened.** Once strategy is signed, design proceeds on
  that strategy. Rolling back an earlier phase is a change order.

## 8. Timeline discipline

Timeline is a scope item. It is part of the fee.

- **Standard timelines are stated in the proposal and reiterated at kickoff.**
- **Client-caused delay.** A client who holds up feedback, sign-off, or content delivery
  past the agreed date extends the project. After 2 weeks of client-caused hold, the
  timeline is re-set. After 4 weeks, the project is paused and a re-entry fee applies
  (typically $2,000–$5,000).
- **Rush projects carry a 30% surcharge.** Rush is defined per tier in the scope matrix.
  The surcharge is non-negotiable — it compensates for the premium allocation of agency
  time.
- **No launches on Fridays or before holidays.** This is a delivery rule, not a
  negotiation point.

## 9. Multi-stakeholder and triangular contracts

Block's rule: we cannot contract with someone who is not in the room. If the project has
stakeholders who influence decisions but do not attend meetings, they are named in the
contract and held to the same approval cadence.

- **Economic buyer must attend kickoff, mid-point review, and launch sign-off.** Optional
  for working reviews. If the economic buyer is routinely absent, escalate before it
  becomes a delivery risk.
- **Decision ambiguity is a red flag.** If it is unclear who has final sign-off, we pause
  and clarify in writing before continuing.
- **We do not accept feedback routed through a non-participant.** Feedback from "the CEO,
  via email" is not accepted as final until the CEO has reviewed the work with us
  present.

## 10. What the matrix buys the agency

A scope matrix is only valuable if it is enforced. Enforced, it delivers:

- predictable margin per project
- predictable delivery timeline
- ability to staff and schedule without slack
- defensible pricing in the sales conversation
- a basis for case-study metrics (consistent scope, consistent outcomes)
- the discipline Warrillow identifies as the difference between a service business and a
  product business

Scope discipline is positioning. Every "yes" to off-matrix work is a "no" to the position.
