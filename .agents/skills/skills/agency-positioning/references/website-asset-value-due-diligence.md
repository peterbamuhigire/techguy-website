# Website Asset Value Due Diligence

Use this reference when selling, scoping, or defending premium website work by explaining
the site as a business asset. It is especially useful for proposals, audits, ecommerce
projects, lead-generation businesses, and clients who need to justify a serious fee.

## Positioning Principle

A cheap website is an expense. A premium website system is an owned commercial asset. It
can create value by increasing qualified traffic, improving conversion, shortening the
sales cycle, reducing founder dependency, documenting the offer, improving trust, and
making marketing performance measurable.

## Asset-Value Questions

Ask these in discovery and sales conversations:

- What revenue, leads, bookings, applications, donations, or subscriptions does the site
  influence today?
- Which traffic sources does the business depend on, and which one would be dangerous to
  lose?
- What is the current conversion rate by channel, device, and key page?
- What proof does the website own: case studies, reviews, testimonials, credentials,
  data, media, awards, or client logos?
- Does the business own its domain, hosting, analytics, CRM, email, payment, ad, and
  social accounts?
- Could a new manager operate the website and marketing system from documentation?
- Where does the site rely on the founder or one staff member to explain what should be
  obvious online?
- What would an investor, acquirer, lender, or board member consider fragile?

## Due Diligence Categories

Traffic:

- Organic trend and ranking concentration.
- Paid dependence and campaign economics.
- Referral, partner, social, local, and email contribution.
- Mobile share and mobile revenue or lead quality.

Revenue and conversion:

- Lead or sale attribution.
- Funnel drop-off.
- Average order value or lead value.
- Repeat purchase, retention, or lifecycle revenue.
- Quality of conversion events and CRM handoff.

Trust and proof:

- Specific claims tied to proof.
- Case studies, testimonials, reviews, guarantees, policies, and credentials.
- Pricing or fee logic clear enough for serious buyers.

Technical:

- Speed, mobile usability, accessibility, security headers, dependency health, backup,
  uptime, forms, search crawlability, redirects, and schema.

Operations:

- Account ownership.
- Documentation.
- Content update process.
- Analytics access.
- Maintenance cadence.
- Supplier or founder dependency.

Risk:

- One-channel traffic dependence.
- Thin or outdated content.
- Weak email capture.
- Unmeasured conversion.
- Broken checkout or lead capture.
- No clear post-launch optimization rhythm.

## Proposal Language

Use direct commercial language:

- "The project is priced as an asset build, not a page build."
- "The deliverable is a measurable system: traffic paths, conversion paths, proof,
  analytics, and handoff."
- "The fee is justified by reducing leakage in a channel the business already depends on."
- "The site must be operable by the client's team after launch; undocumented custom work
  reduces value."
- "The post-launch window is part of the asset build because the first data after launch
  determines the optimization backlog."

Avoid claiming guaranteed traffic, rankings, revenue, or valuation increases. Tie the
argument to controllable improvements and measurable leading indicators.

## Premium Audit Output

A premium audit or proposal should identify:

- Current asset strengths.
- Current asset risks.
- Highest-value leakage points.
- What must be built, fixed, measured, or documented.
- Which risks are inside scope, outside scope, or retainer candidates.
- The metric baseline and review cadence.
