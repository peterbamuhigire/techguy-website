# Editorial Standards Reference — Blog Writer Skill

Rules distilled from *The Chicago Manual of Style* (17th edition) and *Developing Quality Technical Information* (Hargis/Carey). Apply these during the proofing pass (Step 7 of the writing process).

---

## Punctuation

### Serial Comma (Always Use)
Use a comma before "and" or "or" in a series of three or more items:
- CORRECT: "We deployed the system in Kampala, Nairobi, and Dar es Salaam."
- INCORRECT: "We deployed the system in Kampala, Nairobi and Dar es Salaam."

### Commas with Independent Clauses
When two independent clauses are joined by a conjunction (and, but, or, so, yet), place a comma before the conjunction:
- "We finished the testing phase, but the client requested additional features."
- SHORT EXCEPTION: "She coded and he tested." (very short, closely connected)

### Commas with Introductory Elements
A dependent clause or long phrase before the main clause takes a comma:
- "If you accept our proposal, we can begin development next week."
- "After three months of testing, the system went live."
- SHORT PHRASE (optional): "In 2024 we expanded to West Africa."

### Restrictive vs Nonrestrictive Clauses
- **That** = restrictive (defines which one, no commas): "The software that we built for Dynapharm handled multiple currencies."
- **Which** = nonrestrictive (adds extra info, commas): "The ERP system, which took six months to build, is now used daily."

### Semicolons
Join two related independent clauses without a conjunction:
- "The system handled payroll accurately; the staff trusted it within weeks."

With conjunctive adverbs (however, therefore, moreover): semicolon before, comma after:
- "The budget was tight; however, we delivered on schedule."

In complex series (items with internal commas):
- "We worked in Dakar, Senegal; Freetown, Sierra Leone; and Conakry, Guinea."

### Colons
Introduce a list or explanation. The words BEFORE the colon must form a complete sentence:
- CORRECT: "The project required three things: a reliable server, trained staff, and management buy-in."
- INCORRECT: "The project required: a reliable server, trained staff, and management buy-in."

After a colon: lowercase unless introducing two or more complete sentences.

### Em Dashes
Set off amplifying or explanatory elements — stronger than commas, less formal than parentheses:
- "The biggest challenge — and no one predicted this — was internet connectivity."
- Never use more than one pair per sentence. Never nest them.
- No spaces around em dashes in published text.

### En Dashes
Connect number ranges: "pages 14-16", "2020-2025", "chapters 3-7".
Never use with "from" or "between": "from 2020 to 2025" (NOT "from 2020-2025").

### Quotation Marks and Punctuation
Periods and commas ALWAYS go inside closing quotation marks:
- "The client called it 'transformative.'"
Colons and semicolons go OUTSIDE:
- He described what he called "digital transformation": a complete overhaul of processes.

### Exclamation Points
Use sparingly. One per article at most. Overuse dilutes impact and sounds unprofessional.

## Capitalisation

### Headline-Style (Title Case) for Article Titles

1. Capitalise the first and last words
2. Capitalise all major words: nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs
3. Lowercase articles: the, a, an
4. Lowercase prepositions: in, on, at, from, through, between, for, with, of
5. Lowercase conjunctions: and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet
6. Lowercase "to" (including in infinitives) and "as"

**Examples:**
- "Why African Businesses Need Custom-Built Software Solutions"
- "How to Choose an IT Consultant in East Africa"
- "What a Website Actually Costs in Uganda"
- "The Case for Offline-First Mobile Apps in African Markets"

### Sentence Case for h2 and h3 Headings Within Articles
Only capitalise the first word and proper nouns:
- "When custom software makes sense"
- "What to look for in a developer"
- "The Kampala market is different"

### Titles and Job Roles
Capitalise only when directly preceding a name:
- "Chief Technology Officer Jane Mutesi" but "Jane Mutesi, chief technology officer"
- "Director of IT" but "the director of IT"

### Technology Terms
- internet (lowercase)
- email (no hyphen, lowercase)
- website, web page, the web (lowercase)
- Wi-Fi (capitalised — trademark)
- ERP, API, SaaS, CRM (acronyms stay capitalised)

## Number Formatting

### The Blog Rule: Spell Out Zero Through Nine
Use numerals for 10 and above. This is the journalistic convention appropriate for blogs:
- "We worked with three companies across five countries."
- "The system processed 250 transactions daily."
- "She managed a team of 12 developers."

### Exceptions (Always Use Numerals)
- Percentages: "3 percent" or "3%" (use "per cent" in British English)
- Money: "UGX 5 million", "$1,200", "€500"
- Technical specifications: "4GB RAM", "99.9% uptime"
- Ages: "a 10-year-old company"

### Never Begin a Sentence with a Numeral
- CORRECT: "Fifteen companies adopted the system in 2025."
- BETTER: "In 2025, 15 companies adopted the system." (reword to avoid awkwardness)

### Large Numbers
Mix numerals with words: "2.3 million users", "14 billion transactions"

### Dates (British Format)
Day-month-year, no ordinal suffixes:
- "1 March 2026" (NOT "March 1, 2026" or "1st March 2026")
- Decades: "the 1990s" (no apostrophe), or "the nineties"
- Centuries: "the twenty-first century" (spelled out, lowercase)

### Percentages
- Nontechnical text: "12 per cent" (British English)
- Technical context or tables: "12%"
- No space before the % symbol

## Hyphenation

### Compound Modifiers Before a Noun
Hyphenate when two or more words jointly modify a noun:
- "a well-designed system" but "the system was well designed"
- "a decision-making process" but "her decision making was sound"
- "a three-year project" but "the project lasted three years"
- "a custom-built application" but "the application was custom built"

### Never Hyphenate -ly Adverbs
- "a highly effective system" (NOT "highly-effective")
- "a newly deployed application"
- "a fully integrated platform"

### Prefixes (Usually Closed)
- offline, online, ongoing, override
- preinstall, postlaunch, nontechnical, multiuser
- HYPHENATE before capitals or to prevent misreading: "pre-COVID", "co-opt", "re-create" (to create again)

## List Formatting

### Bulleted Lists
- Introduce with a complete sentence followed by a colon
- Each item: lowercase start, no end punctuation (unless complete sentences)
- Parallel structure — every item must be the same grammatical form

**Parallel (correct):**
- installing the server software
- configuring the database
- training the staff

**Not parallel (incorrect):**
- install the server software
- the database needs configuring
- staff training

### Numbered Lists
- Use when order matters (steps, rankings, priority)
- Each item capitalised, period at end if complete sentences
- Keep to 9 or fewer items (subdivide if more)

## Commonly Confused Words

| Word | Correct Usage |
|------|--------------|
| affect / effect | Affect (verb) = to influence. Effect (noun) = result. |
| fewer / less | Fewer = countable. Less = uncountable. "Fewer bugs, less downtime." |
| ensure / insure | Ensure = make certain. Insure = buy insurance. |
| complement / compliment | Complement = complete. Compliment = praise. |
| principal / principle | Principal = main/chief. Principle = a rule. |
| discreet / discrete | Discreet = tactful. Discrete = separate. |
| comprise / compose | The whole comprises the parts. The parts compose the whole. Never "comprised of." |
| imply / infer | The writer implies; the reader infers. |
| unique | Means "one of a kind." Never "very unique" or "quite unique." |
| use / utilise | "Use" is almost always better. |

## Progressive Tense Preference

Prefer simple present or past tense over continuous (-ing) forms:

| Weaker (continuous) | Stronger (simple) |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| The company is planning to expand | The company plans to expand |
| We are hoping to launch in Q3 | We aim to launch in Q3 |
| The team is working on a solution | The team is developing a solution |
| She was thinking about resigning | She considered resigning |

**Why:** Simple tenses are more direct and authoritative. The progressive tense sounds tentative and adds syllables.

## Attribution Format

Every statistic, research finding, and quoted claim needs a source. During the proofing pass, verify that every number has attribution.

**Inline attribution in body text:**
- "Post-harvest losses exceed 40% in East Africa — IFPRI, 2023"
- "According to the World Bank, mobile money accounts in sub-Saharan Africa grew by 28% in 2024"

**Block quotation attribution:**
> "The biggest constraint is not capital — it is management capacity." — Dr Maggie Kigozi, Uganda Investment Authority

**What to verify before publishing:**
- [ ] Every statistic has a named source and approximate date
- [ ] Every quotation is verbatim and correctly attributed
- [ ] Linked sources open correctly (test all external links)
- [ ] Proper nouns (names of people, companies, organisations) spelled consistently throughout

## Words to Avoid in Professional Writing

### Condescending Words (Hargis/Carey)
Never use: "simply", "just", "easy", "obviously", "of course", "as everyone knows". These belittle readers who are struggling with the topic.

### Hyperbolic Marketing Words
Never use: "revolutionary", "game-changing", "cutting-edge", "world-class", "best-in-class", "next-generation", "leverage", "synergy", "disrupt"

### Vague Intensifiers
Delete unless they add measurable meaning: "very", "really", "quite", "rather", "somewhat", "basically", "actually", "literally"

## Possessives

### Singular Nouns (Including Those Ending in S)
Add apostrophe-s: "the business's growth", "James's proposal", "the boss's decision"

### Plural Nouns Ending in S
Add only an apostrophe: "the clients' feedback", "the developers' tools"

### Its vs It's
- "its" = possessive: "The system proved its value."
- "it's" = contraction of "it is": "It's ready to deploy."

## Formatting Web Content

### Link Text
Link text must describe the destination. Never use "click here":
- CORRECT: "Read our [guide to ERP implementation](/en/blog/erp-implementation/)."
- INCORRECT: "For more information, [click here](/en/blog/erp-implementation/)."

### One Space After Periods
Always one space, never two.

### Smart Quotation Marks
Use directional ("curly") quotes, not straight quotes. Astro handles this automatically in rendered HTML.
