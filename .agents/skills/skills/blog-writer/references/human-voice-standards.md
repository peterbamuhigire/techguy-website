# Human Voice Standards — Anti-AI Writing Reference

Every piece of content must read as if a specific human wrote it. Not a generic professional. Not a language model. A person with opinions, experience, and a way of seeing the world that shows in their sentences.

This reference applies to all content-generating skills: blog-writer, page-builder, language-standards, brand-alignment.

## AI Phrase Blacklist

These phrases are dead giveaways. **Never use them.**

### Openings to Ban

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In today's ever-evolving..."
- "In the dynamic world of..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "Are you struggling with..."
- "What if I told you..."
- "In an era where..."
- "As [industry] continues to evolve..."
- "In the world of [topic]..."

### Filler Phrases to Ban

- "It's important to note that..."
- "It's worth mentioning that..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "In summary / In conclusion / In essence..."
- "Without further ado..."
- "Let's dive in..."
- "Let's explore..."
- "This is where [X] comes in..."
- "That said..." (overused as transition)

### Hype Phrases to Ban

- "Embark on a journey..."
- "Treasure trove of information"
- "Game-changer"
- "Golden ticket"
- "Reaching new heights"
- "Uncharted waters"
- "Digital realm / digital landscape"
- "In the realm of..."
- "Well-crafted"
- "Captivating narrative"
- "Ever-evolving"

### Structural Phrases to Ban

- "Not only X, but also Y" (AI uses this constantly)
- "X isn't just Y; it's Z"
- "From X to Y, [subject] has..." (listicle pattern)
- "Whether you're [X] or [Y]..." (false inclusivity)

## AI Structural Pattern Bans

### Sentence Rhythm

AI produces uniform sentence lengths — typically 15-25 words, one after another, steady as a metronome. Humans don't write this way.

**Rule: Burstiness is mandatory.**
- Every 500-word section must contain at least 3 sentences under 8 words
- Every 500-word section must contain at least 1 sentence over 25 words
- Average sentence length should vary between sections (one section might average 12 words, the next 20)
- One-sentence paragraphs are powerful. Use them.

**Rhythm examples:**

Bad (AI-uniform):
> The implementation process requires careful planning and execution. Businesses should consider their specific needs before selecting a system. A thorough evaluation of available options will help ensure the best outcome. Training and support are essential components of successful deployment.

Good (human-varied):
> Start with the problem, not the software. Too many businesses buy first and plan later — I've watched it happen in Kampala, Nairobi, and Dakar. The result is always the same: an expensive system that nobody uses. Planning takes weeks. Recovery takes months.

### Paragraph Structure

AI paragraphs are uniform: 3-4 sentences, each about the same length, neatly self-contained. Humans write messier.

**Rules:**
- Vary paragraph length deliberately: 1 sentence, then 4, then 2, then 3
- Some paragraphs should be a single punchy sentence
- A paragraph can trail off into the next with a bridge word
- Not every paragraph needs a clean topic sentence

### Lists and Formatting

AI loves three-item lists. Everything comes in threes. Then another three. Then three more.

**Rules:**
- Vary list lengths: 2, 4, 5, 7 items — not always 3
- Not every point needs a parallel structure
- Some lists should have one item longer than the others
- Don't bullet-point what works better as a sentence

### Transitions

AI uses robotic transitions: Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Additionally, Consequently.

**Rules:**
- Use these words rarely (max 1 per 1,000 words: furthermore, moreover, additionally)
- Prefer invisible transitions: the logical flow between paragraphs should be obvious from context
- When you need a connector, use conversational ones: "But here's the thing", "The real question is", "And that changes everything"
- Start sentences with "And" or "But" when it adds punch

### Em Dashes

AI overuses em dashes — often two or three per paragraph — creating a predictable cadence. Limit to 2 per article. Use parentheses or restructure the sentence instead.

## Human Voice Techniques

### 1. Take Positions

AI hedges. Humans commit. The difference between generic content and trusted content is willingness to say what you actually think.

| AI (hedging) | Human (committed) |
|-------------|-------------------|
| "One might consider..." | "I recommend..." |
| "It could be argued that..." | "Here's what I've seen..." |
| "There are several approaches..." | "Two approaches work. The rest waste time." |
| "Some experts suggest..." | "After 15 years, I can tell you..." |
| "This can potentially..." | "This works. We proved it in Dakar." |

**Rule:** Every article must contain at least 3 clear opinions the author stands behind.

### 2. Mine Client Language

Read every file in `docs/` and extract the exact words the client uses. Their vocabulary is authentic by definition.

- How do they describe their business? Use those words.
- How do they describe their customers? Mirror that language.
- What problems do they mention? Name them the way they name them.
- What industry jargon do they use naturally? Keep it.

The website should sound like the business owner talking about their work — not like a copywriter describing it from outside.

### 3. Strategic Contractions

Zero contractions = academic or AI. Too many = casual blog. Calibrate for semi-formal:

- **Use contractions:** don't, can't, won't, it's, we're, that's, there's, you'll
- **Frequency:** 2-4 per 500 words in body text
- **Where:** Mid-paragraph, in flowing prose. Never in headlines or CTAs.
- **Never contract in:** Formal passages, legal language, section headings
- **East African calibration:** East African business English uses fewer contractions than American English but more than academic writing. Match the register: a senior consultant at a business conference.

### 4. Sensory and Physical Detail

AI describes concepts. Humans describe experiences. The difference is sensory texture.

| AI (conceptual) | Human (sensory) |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| "The office was recently renovated" | "Fresh paint, new carpet, and a reception desk that still had its plastic wrap" |
| "The training session was well-received" | "By the third exercise, people stopped looking at their phones" |
| "Internet connectivity was unreliable" | "The connection dropped twice during our demo — once mid-sentence with the CEO watching" |
| "The implementation was challenging" | "We spent three nights in the server room, eating rolex from the vendor outside the gate" |

**Rule:** Every story must include at least one sensory or physical detail that only someone who was there would know.

### 5. Imperfection Markers

Polished-to-a-shine text reads as AI. Real writing has deliberate roughness:

- Start a sentence with "And" or "But" (2-3 times per article)
- Use a sentence fragment for emphasis. Like this.
- Ask a real question mid-paragraph — one you actually want the reader to think about
- Admit uncertainty: "I'm not sure this works everywhere, but in East Africa..."
- Use colloquial bridges: "Here's the thing." "Look." "The truth is."

### 6. Write Then Cut

Don't try to write perfectly on the first pass. Write 30% more than you need, then cut ruthlessly.

- First draft: get everything out. Don't edit. Don't polish.
- Second pass: cut every sentence that doesn't earn its place
- Third pass: tighten. Replace 5-word phrases with 2-word phrases. Delete modifiers.

The best writing happens in the cutting. What you remove matters as much as what you keep.

## Voice DNA Extraction

Generic "write like a human" instructions don't work. What works: structured, data-backed voice profiles from the author's actual writing.

### Before First Article: Build a Voice Profile

1. **Gather existing writing** — read the client's company profile, any existing blog posts, LinkedIn articles, social media posts, email newsletters, or proposal documents referenced in `docs/`
2. **Analyse patterns** — identify:
   - Sentence length tendencies (do they write short and punchy, or long and detailed?)
   - Favourite transitions and connectors
   - How they open paragraphs (anecdote? question? direct statement?)
   - Vocabulary level (plain? technical? industry-specific?)
   - How they express opinions (direct? hedged? rhetorical questions?)
   - Unique phrases or expressions they repeat
3. **Note the register** — are they formal-warm (conference speaker), casual-expert (podcast guest), or academic-precise (report writer)?
4. **Extract 5-10 characteristic sentences** — sentences that sound most like the author. Use these as rhythm and tone anchors.

### Why This Matters

Traditional approaches fail:
- Generic "humanising" prompts produce generic output
- Custom instructions alone are too thin to capture voice
- Feeding writing samples without structured analysis wastes them
- Asking AI to "sound more like you" without a profile produces imitation, not voice

The voice profile turns vague "write naturally" into specific, measurable patterns.

### Per-Article Voice Check

Before publishing, read the article and ask: "Could someone who knows the author identify them from this writing alone?" If the answer is no, the voice profile isn't showing through. Rewrite the sections that sound generic.

## The Human Voice Self-Check

Before publishing any content, ask these 5 questions:

1. **Could you identify which human wrote this?** If it could be anyone — or any AI — the voice isn't strong enough. Add a specific opinion, story, or cultural reference.

2. **Does it contain something only this author would know?** A detail from experience, a named place, a specific outcome. Generic advice fails this test.

3. **Read three consecutive paragraphs aloud. Do they sound the same?** If the rhythm is uniform — same length, same structure, same cadence — rewrite for variety.

4. **Count the Tier 1 blacklist words.** Any present? Replace them. Count Tier 2 words. More than 3 in the whole piece? Thin them out.

5. **Does the opening avoid every banned phrase?** Check the first two sentences against the phrase blacklist. If they match any pattern, rewrite from scratch.

## Quick Reference Card

**Always do:**
- Vary sentence length (4 to 30 words in one section)
- Take clear positions with "I recommend" / "I've seen"
- Use the client's own words from their docs
- Include sensory details in stories
- Use contractions sparingly (2-4 per 500 words)
- Start sentences with "And" or "But" occasionally
- Write one-sentence paragraphs for emphasis

**Never do:**
- Use any Tier 1 blacklist word
- Open with any banned phrase
- Write 3+ consecutive sentences of similar length
- Use "Furthermore" / "Moreover" / "Additionally" as paragraph openers
- Hedge where you should commit
- Let every paragraph be 3-4 sentences
- Use more than 2 em dashes per article
