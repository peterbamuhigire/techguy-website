# Blog Ideation & Research Reference

Techniques for generating blog topic ideas and researching articles, distilled from Lima (*Fundamentals of Writing*), Bly (*How to Write and Sell Simple Information*), and Graves (*Writing for Profit*).

---

## Ideation Techniques

### 1. Clustering (Mind Mapping)

Place a keyword in the centre of a blank page. Draw short lines outward, writing every associated word or phrase. Work fast without censoring. When one branch dries up, return to the centre and start another.

**How to use for blog ideation:**
- Cluster the client's industry keyword to discover subtopics
- Cluster a target audience pain point to find angles
- Cluster a competitor's headline to find counter-angles
- Run multiple clusters per project: business name, ideal customer problem, product category, competitor landscape — each yields different angles

After clustering, flip into freewriting for 5 minutes using the richest branch as a prompt. The combo produces both topic ideas and usable phrases.

### 2. The Spin Technique

Read 10 published articles on the same topic. Identify what everyone says. Then find a legitimate way to say something different.

**Steps:**
1. Search the target keyword, read the top 10 results
2. Note the consensus advice
3. Look for contradictions, surprises, or patterns others missed
4. Build your article around that gap

**Example:** Researching CRO best practices, a writer noticed many A/B test case studies contradicted the "rules." Article angle: "Are you sabotaging your conversion rate by following everyone's best CRO advice?"

The spin is not clickbait contrarianism — it requires actual supporting evidence.

### 3. W5 Topic Discovery

For any potential topic, ask six questions. Each answer can surface a distinct article angle:

| Question | Angle it surfaces |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Who** is affected? | Profile/case study angle |
| **What** is happening? | Explainer/guide angle |
| **Where** is it happening? | Local/regional angle |
| **When** is it happening? | Timeliness/trend angle |
| **Why** is it happening? | Analysis/opinion angle |
| **How** is it happening? | How-to/process angle |

One topic → six potential articles.

### 4. Nine Value-Adding Angles (Bly/Davidson)

Any topic can be approached from nine distinct angles. Use this as a checklist when generating ideas:

| Angle | What it means | Example |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Immediacy** | Be first. Cover new developments before competitors. | "New Uganda tax rules: what SMEs must change by July" |
| **Personalisation** | Tailor to a specific audience segment. | "ERP for pharmacies" beats "ERP tips" |
| **Interpretation** | Don't just report facts — explain what they mean. | "What the new data protection law actually means for your business" |
| **Trust** | Use credentials, case studies, proof. | "How we cut stock-outs by 60% at 3 Kampala retail chains" |
| **Accessibility** | Aggregate scattered info into one convenient resource. | "The complete guide to business registration in East Africa" |
| **Embodiment** | Repackage into a preferred format. | Turn a long guide into a checklist or decision tree |
| **Reciprocity** | Give away free, useful content. | Downloadable templates, calculators, checklists |
| **Findability** | Compile resources others must hunt for. | "12 free tools for African SMEs: the only list you need" |
| **New ideas** | Share something the reader hasn't heard elsewhere. | Original research, contrarian takes, novel frameworks |

### 5. High-Value Goal Filter

Topics that generate the most reader interest help people achieve high-value goals. Prioritise topics that help readers:
- Make or save money
- Build a business
- Reduce risk or debt
- Save time or effort
- Gain a competitive advantage
- Learn a high-value skill

**Rule:** The more money your article helps people make (or save), the more valuable it is.

### 6. Unique Expertise Filter

The best topics sit at the intersection of two criteria:
1. They deal with achieving a high-value goal
2. They require expertise that is not commonly available

If the topic is both valuable AND hard to find elsewhere, it commands attention.

### 7. The 90/10 Content Originality Rule

Articles don't need 100% original ideas. **90% familiar content** convinces the reader you know what you're talking about. **10% new insight** creates the value that makes the article worth reading.

The fresh 10% can be: a new framework, a contrarian data point, a case study nobody else has, or a connection between two ideas nobody has made.

### 8. Content Hierarchy

Every topic can be approached at three levels:

| Level | What it is | Value |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| **Information** | Raw facts, statistics, dates | Lowest — Google provides this freely |
| **Knowledge** | Understanding what facts mean and why they matter | Medium — requires expertise |
| **Wisdom** | Guidance on what the reader should DO | Highest — based on years of experience |

The best articles mix all three but lean toward knowledge and wisdom.

### 9. Adjacent Niche Expansion

When you've exhausted obvious topics in a niche, look for adjacent topics that share something in common. A software company can write about project management (adjacent to development), business processes (adjacent to ERP), or industry trends (adjacent to their sector).

### 10. Customer Question Mining

Extract topic ideas from the questions customers actually ask:
- Sales enquiry emails
- Support tickets
- FAQ pages
- Social media comments
- "People Also Ask" boxes in Google
- Forum threads in the industry

Each real question is a proven topic — someone already wants the answer.

## Research Methods

### The 2X Research Rule

Gather approximately twice as much material as you think you need. Having surplus lets you be selective — use only the research that best supports your points. When you collect the bare minimum, the piece sounds thin.

### Research Workflow

1. **Internal research first** — cluster the topic to externalise everything already known. This reveals what you know AND what gaps remain.
2. **Identify gaps** — what questions does the cluster leave unanswered?
3. **Targeted external research** — fill only the identified gaps. Sources: industry reports, case studies, expert interviews, authoritative websites.
4. **Cluster again** — after research, cluster all material to organise it.
5. **Outline from the second cluster** — now write from outline, point to point.

### Types of Supporting Evidence

Back up claims with evidence. The highest-quality articles are full of proof:

| Evidence type | Strength | Example |
|---------------|----------|---------|
| Case study with specific numbers | Strongest | "Reduced stock-outs by 60% across 3 branches" |
| Expert quote or opinion | Strong | "As the Uganda Revenue Authority notes..." |
| Data or statistics from authority sources | Strong | "According to the World Bank, 43% of..." |
| Link to supporting article/research | Medium | "A 2025 McKinsey report found..." |
| Real-world example (named) | Medium | "At Café Javas in Kampala..." |
| Logical argument | Baseline | "If X costs $500 and saves $2,000..." |

**Rule:** Every major claim should be backed by at least one piece of evidence. Use authority sources when possible, but anything that says "this isn't just my opinion" adds credibility.

### Content Selectivity Principle

In each section, have points you want to make and include only facts that prove, illustrate, or support those points. Omit everything else. Don't fall in love with research materials — know when not to use something and don't be afraid to throw it away.

Avoid "content overload" — cramming every fact you found into the article. The goal is a curated, purposeful argument, not an information dump.

## Competitor Content Analysis

### Article Analysis Framework

When analysing what competitors have written on the same topic, read each competing article three times:

1. **First read:** For interest — what is the overall impression?
2. **Second read:** For understanding — write a 75-word synopsis
3. **Third read:** Analytical — answer these questions:

| Question | Why it matters |
|----------|---------------|
| What type of article is it? | Identifies format gaps |
| What is the topic and angle? | Reveals the consensus angle |
| What is the purpose? | Inform? Persuade? Sell? |
| How does it start (the lead)? | Shows what openings are overused |
| How does it keep you reading? | Identifies effective techniques |
| How does it end? | Shows ending patterns |
| What is missing? | **This is your opportunity** |

### Content Gap Methods

1. **The missing angle:** After analysing 10 competitors, ask: what angle has nobody taken?
2. **The depth gap:** Most articles are surface-level ("what to do"). Write the deeper article ("how to do it" with step-by-step detail).
3. **The local gap:** Generic advice abounds. Apply it specifically to the client's market (East Africa, SMEs, a specific industry).
4. **The freshness gap:** If top-ranking articles are 2+ years old, a current version with updated data wins.
5. **The resource gap:** Identify resources you frequently link to but don't have on your own site — then create them.

## Headline Generation

### Three-Headline Method

Always generate 3 headline options for every article. Each headline should incorporate:
1. **The topic** — what it's about
2. **The unique angle** — why this one is different
3. **The audience appeal** — why the reader will care

Present all 3 to the user. The best one wins.

### Headline Patterns

| Pattern | Formula | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Rule-based** | "The Top N Rules of [Topic] Every [Audience] Should Know" | "The 7 Rules of ERP Implementation Every CFO Should Know" |
| **Mistake-based** | "Common Mistakes [Audience] Make That Sabotage [Goal]" | "5 Inventory Mistakes That Cost East African Retailers Millions" |
| **Contrarian** | "Are You [Doing Thing] by Following Everyone's [Advice]?" | "Are You Sabotaging Your POS by Following Generic Setup Guides?" |
| **How-to** | "How to [Achieve Goal] in [Timeframe/Context]" | "How to Choose an ERP System in 30 Days" |
| **Direct promise** | "[Specific Outcome] for [Specific Audience]" | "Cut Monthly Reconciliation from 3 Days to 3 Hours" |
| **Question** | "What Happens When [Scenario]?" | "What Happens When Your Pharmacy Runs Out of Essential Medicines?" |

### The AIAA Headline Test

Every headline must pass the AIAA framework:
- **Attention** — does it stop the scroll?
- **Interest** — does it promise value worth the reader's time?
- **Attitude** — does it challenge or reinforce a belief the reader holds?
- **Action** — does it make the reader want to click/read?
