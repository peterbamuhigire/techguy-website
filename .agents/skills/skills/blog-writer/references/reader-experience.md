# Reader Experience — Touchpoints, Clarity, and Value Delivery

How to treat every article as a product experience. Synthesised from Webb (What Customers Crave), Rowse (Problogger), Hargis et al. (Developing Quality Technical Information), and Whitmell (Business Writing Essentials).

---

## The Article as a Customer Experience

Every blog article is a touchpoint in the reader's journey with the brand. Webb's framework maps directly:

| Touchpoint | Blog Equivalent | What It Must Do |
|------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Pre-touch** | Social share, search result snippet, newsletter teaser | Create curiosity. The title + meta description are your shopfront window. |
| **First touch** | Hero image + opening paragraph | Confirm the promise. Reader decides in 8 seconds whether to stay. |
| **Core touch** | Article body (sections, images, stories) | Deliver value. Educate, surprise, equip. Every section earns the next scroll. |
| **Last touch** | Conclusion + CTA | Leave them wanting more. Clear next step, not a hard sell. |
| **In-touch** | Related articles, newsletter signup, social follow | Keep the relationship alive beyond this single article. |

### The Standing Ovation Rule (Webb)

"Always leave your audience wanting more." The conclusion must not fizzle. It should:
1. Summarise the single most important takeaway (not rehash everything)
2. Give a concrete next action the reader can take today
3. Offer a natural bridge to deeper engagement (consultation, related article, service page)

### Customer Types in Blog Readers

Readers are not a monolith. Identify which type each article primarily serves:

| Reader Type | What They Love | What They Hate | Content Strategy |
|-------------|---------------|----------------|------------------|
| **The Scanner** | Bullet points, bold text, clear headings, numbered lists | Walls of text, buried conclusions | Make every section independently valuable. Front-load key insights. |
| **The Deep Diver** | Comprehensive analysis, data, nuance, expert depth | Shallow listicles, clickbait, surface-level advice | Provide layers — scannable structure with depth underneath. |
| **The Sceptic** | Evidence, real examples, honest limitations, credentials | Hype, unsubstantiated claims, "trust me" | Lead with proof. Show the working. Name specific projects and outcomes. |
| **The Action-Taker** | Step-by-step guidance, templates, checklists, tools | Theory without application, academic tone | End every section with "what to do with this." Include actionable takeaways. |

**Design for all four.** The best articles satisfy the Scanner with structure, the Deep Diver with substance, the Sceptic with evidence, and the Action-Taker with next steps.

## Pillar Content Strategy (Rowse)

### What Is Pillar Content?

Pillar content is foundational, evergreen material that:
- Answers questions your target audience searches for repeatedly
- Remains relevant months and years after publication
- Serves as a reference that other articles link back to
- Demonstrates deep expertise in your core service areas

### Content Types by Purpose

| Type | Purpose | Lifespan | Example |
|------|---------|----------|---------|
| **Pillar** | Foundational knowledge, SEO anchor | Years | "What an ERP System Actually Does for Your Business" |
| **Series** | Comprehensive coverage through connected posts | Months-years | "Website Costs in Uganda" (Part 1: Planning, Part 2: Development, Part 3: Maintenance) |
| **Opinion** | Thought leadership, differentiation | Months | "Why Most IT Consultants in Africa Underprice Their Services" |
| **Story** | Relationship building, trust | Evergreen | "How We Migrated Dynapharm's Operations Across Three Countries" |
| **News-adjacent** | Timeliness, relevance | Weeks-months | "Uganda's New Data Protection Law: What Your Business Needs to Do" |

### Series Planning (Rowse Mindmap Method)

When a topic has 3+ natural subtopics, plan it as a series:

1. **Mindmap the core topic** — break it into component parts
2. **Order logically** — what must the reader know first?
3. **Each post stands alone** — a reader arriving at Part 3 should understand it without Part 1
4. **Cross-link the series** — every post links to the others
5. **Create a series landing section** — the blog index groups series posts together

### Publishing Rhythm

Consistency matters more than frequency:
- Set a cadence you can maintain for months (Rowse: "choose a frequency you feel comfortable committing to for the foreseeable future")
- Two excellent articles per month outperform eight mediocre ones
- Each article should be planned at least a week before publishing (write → rest → review → publish)

## Clarity Principles (Hargis et al.)

The IBM quality framework defines three dimensions of quality information. Apply all three to every article:

### Easy to Use (Task Orientation)

- **Write for the intended audience.** Adjust detail level to what the reader already knows. Business owners need outcomes; IT managers need specifics.
- **Present information from the reader's point of view.** Use "you" and "your." Make the reader the protagonist.
- **Indicate a practical reason.** For every claim, answer "why does this matter to the reader?"
- **Focus on real tasks, not features.** Don't describe what a tool does — describe what the reader can accomplish with it.
- **Provide clear steps.** When advising action, use numbered steps. Each step = one action.

### Easy to Understand (Clarity + Concreteness)

- **Focus on the meaning.** Every sentence should pass the "so what?" test.
- **Avoid ambiguity.** Use "because" instead of "since" (which can mean time or cause). Use "although" instead of "while" (which can mean time or contrast).
- **Keep elements short.** Sentences: 15-20 words average. Paragraphs: 2-4 sentences. Sections: screen-height chunks.
- **Use concrete examples.** "ERP implementation for a 50-person manufacturer in Kampala" beats "ERP implementation for medium-sized enterprises."
- **Set context before detail.** Tell the reader what they're about to learn before diving into specifics.

#### Ambiguous Words to Replace (Hargis)

| Ambiguous | Clear Alternative |
|-----------|------------------|
| since | because (for cause) / since (for time only) |
| while | although (for contrast) / while (for simultaneous only) |
| may | can (ability) / might (possibility) |
| once | after / when |
| as | because |

#### Filler Words to Cut (Hargis + Whitmell)

These weaken rather than strengthen: absolutely, actually, basically, certainly, completely, definitely, fairly, just, of course, particularly, perfectly, quite, really, significantly, simply, some, specifically, totally, very.

**Test:** Remove the word. If the sentence means the same thing, delete it.

### Easy to Find (Organisation + Retrievability)

- **Organise by reader need, not by your outline.** What will they look for first?
- **Use headings that reveal content.** "How to Choose an ERP Vendor" not "Considerations."
- **Front-load key information.** The first sentence of every section should tell the reader what they'll get.
- **Link helpfully.** Internal links should use descriptive text ("see our ERP consulting services") not "click here."

## Concise Writing (Whitmell)

### Three Ways to Write Concisely

1. **Replace wordy phrases with single words:**
   - "at this point in time" → "now"
   - "in order to" → "to"
   - "due to the fact that" → "because"
   - "it is important to note that" → (delete — just state the thing)
   - "a large number of" → "many"
   - "in the event that" → "if"
   - "with regard to" → "about"

2. **Delete unnecessary qualifiers:**
   - "quite difficult" → "difficult"
   - "very important" → "important" (or better: "critical" / "essential")
   - "somewhat complex" → "complex"

3. **Prefer active constructions:**
   - "The system was implemented by the team" → "The team implemented the system"
   - "It was decided that the migration would proceed" → "We decided to proceed with the migration"

### Linking Words That Improve Flow (Whitmell)

Use linking words to connect ideas so the reader follows your logic:

| Purpose | Words |
|---------|-------|
| **Adding** | furthermore, moreover, in addition, also, besides |
| **Contrasting** | however, nevertheless, on the other hand, although, yet |
| **Giving reason** | because, since, as a result of, due to |
| **Showing result** | therefore, consequently, as a result, thus |
| **Giving examples** | for instance, for example, such as, in particular |
| **Sequencing** | firstly, next, then, finally, meanwhile |
| **Summarising** | in short, to summarise, in conclusion, overall |

**Rule:** Every paragraph should connect to the previous one. If you can't identify the logical link, the structure needs work.

## Tone Calibration (Whitmell + Hargis)

### The Formality Spectrum

| Factor | Informal | Semi-formal (Blog Target) | Formal |
|--------|----------|---------------------------|--------|
| Pronouns | I, you, we | I, you, we (with restraint) | The organisation, one |
| Contractions | don't, can't | don't, can't (sparingly) | do not, cannot |
| Sentence length | Short, fragmented | Mixed (15-25 avg) | Long, complex |
| Vocabulary | everyday words | plain + occasional technical | specialised, Latinate |
| Voice | conversational | authoritative but warm | impersonal |

**Our target:** Semi-formal. The author is a senior professional sharing expertise — not a professor lecturing, not a friend chatting. Think "respected colleague explaining something over coffee in a good hotel lobby."

### Politeness Without Weakness

In business writing, polite tone does not mean hedging:
- "I would suggest" → Use when genuinely offering options
- "I recommend" → Use when giving professional advice (stronger, preferred)
- "You should" → Use for clear best practices
- "You must" → Use only for legal/compliance requirements

Avoid false humility: "I'm no expert, but..." undermines the authority the article is building.

## The Quality Gate

Before any article publishes, it must pass these experience checks:

### The 8-Second Test
Read only the title, hero image, and first paragraph. Would you keep reading? If not, rewrite the opening.

### The Scanner Test
Read only headings, bold text, and first sentence of each section. Do you get the article's key points? If not, restructure.

### The So-What Test
After every major claim, ask "so what does the reader do with this?" If there's no answer, add one or cut the claim.

### The Ambiguity Test (Hargis)
Read every sentence imagining a non-native English speaker. Is there any word that could mean two things? Replace it.

### The Fresh Eyes Test (Rowse)
Write the article, then wait at least a few hours before final review. Review with fresh perspective — you'll catch errors and awkward phrasing that were invisible during writing.

### The Touchpoint Test (Webb)
Map the reader's journey through the article. Does every transition feel natural? Is there a clear "what's next" at the end? Would the reader recommend this article to a colleague?
