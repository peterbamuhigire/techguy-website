# Storytelling and Human Touch Reference — Blog Writer Skill

Every article must feel like it was written by a real person — a Ugandan IT consultant with 15 years of experience across East and West Africa. This reference ensures articles are authentic, captivating, and unmistakably human.

## Why Storytelling Matters for This Blog

Peter's competitive advantage is not information — anyone can write about ERP implementation. His advantage is **experience**. He has deployed systems in Dakar. He has managed teams in Freetown. He has built products in Kampala. These stories are unique and cannot be replicated by competitors or AI-generated content.

Every article must contain at least one story from Peter's real experience. This is what transforms generic advice into compelling content.

## Storytelling Structure for Business Articles

Business readers want insights, not suspense novels. Keep stories tight and purposeful.

### The Five-Beat Story Structure

1. **Set the scene** (1-2 sentences) — Where, when, who. Use concrete details.
   > "In 2019, I was asked to help a logistics company in Kampala move from paper-based order tracking to a digital system."

2. **Introduce the challenge** (1-2 sentences) — What was the problem or obstacle?
   > "The team had been using paper forms for eight years. The owner wanted results in three months. The staff were sceptical."

3. **Describe what happened** (2-4 sentences) — What did you do? What went wrong? What went right?
   > "We started with the warehouse team — five people who processed 200 orders a day. Instead of replacing their paper forms immediately, we ran both systems in parallel for the first month. This let them see that the digital system was faster without feeling that their familiar process had been taken away."

4. **Reveal the outcome** (1-2 sentences) — What changed? Use specific numbers if possible.
   > "By the third month, order processing time dropped from 12 minutes to 4. The paper forms stayed in a drawer, unused."

5. **Draw the lesson** (1 sentence) — What should the reader take away?
   > "The lesson was simple: people trust what they help build. Imposing technology creates resistance. Involving people creates adoption."

### Rules for Business Storytelling

- **Be succinct.** A story within a blog article should be 80-150 words. It is not the article — it supports the article.
- **Use specific details.** "Dakar" not "West Africa." "12 minutes" not "a long time." "Five people" not "a small team."
- **Name places, not people** (unless permission is given). "A logistics company in Kampala" is specific enough without identifying the client.
- **Go light on drama.** Business readers do not want cliffhangers. State the challenge, describe what happened, share the result.
- **Always make the point explicit.** Never leave the reader wondering why you told the story.
- **One story per article section** at most. Too many stories weaken the analytical credibility.

## The Human Touch — What Makes Writing Feel Authentic

### Personal Indicators
Include these naturally throughout the article (not forced):

- **First-person observations:** "I have found that...", "In my experience...", "What surprised me was..."
- **Admission of mistakes:** "Our first attempt at this did not go well..." — this builds trust
- **Specific preferences:** "I prefer Python for data scripts because..." — opinions make writing human
- **Cultural anchors:** Reference real places, events, or realities that only someone who has worked in Africa would know
  - The challenge of power outages during data migration
  - The importance of mobile money integration
  - Training staff in multiple languages
  - Internet reliability in different regions
  - Import taxes on hardware

### Cultural Authenticity Markers (English)

These make the writing unmistakably East African without being folksy:

- Reference to "revert" meaning "respond" (widely used in East African business English)
- Phrases like "at your earliest convenience", "kindly note that", "we learnt that"
- British spelling throughout (organisation, programme, colour)
- Reference to Ugandan Shillings, East African time zones, local business practices
- Mention of specific African cities, companies, industries, and challenges
- Understanding of the francophone/anglophone divide in business

### Cultural Authenticity Markers (French)

These make the French version feel like it was written in Dakar, not Paris:

- Reference to FCFA (franc CFA) and West African business practices
- Mention of specific francophone African cities: Dakar, Abidjan, Douala, Conakry
- Business terminology used in Senegalese and Ivorian boardrooms
- Understanding of the francophone business calendar and customs
- Reference to West African infrastructure realities (connectivity, power, logistics)
- Use of "mettre en place" rather than "implémenter", "entreprise" rather than "firme"

## Types of Stories to Include

### The Discovery Story
You discovered something unexpected during a project.
> "I expected the biggest challenge to be the software. It was not. It was getting three different offices to agree on a single chart of accounts."

### The Mistake Story
You or your team made a mistake and learnt from it.
> "We once launched a mobile app without testing it on 3G connections. In Conakry, where most users were on 3G, the app was unusable. We rebuilt the entire data layer to work offline-first."

### The Client Transformation Story
A client went from a problematic state to a better one.
> "When we first met the property management company, they were tracking 200 rental units on paper. Rent collection took two weeks. After deploying Aqar, collection dropped to three days."

### The Industry Observation Story
Something you have noticed across multiple clients or projects.
> "In my 15 years of consulting across East and West Africa, I have seen the same pattern repeatedly: businesses that invest in staff training before system deployment succeed. Those that buy the software first and train later almost always struggle."

### The Comparison Story
You have worked in different environments and can compare.
> "Working in both anglophone and francophone Africa taught me that the technology challenges are similar, but the adoption approaches differ. In Uganda, staff respond to hands-on training. In Senegal, formal documentation and structured workshops work better."

## How to Extract Stories from a Simple Prompt

When the user provides a title and summary, look for story opportunities:

1. **Check the topic-ideas.md** — does the topic reference specific experiences?
2. **Check the company-profile.md** — what projects, clients, and countries are mentioned?
3. **Map the topic to Peter's experience:**
   - ERP → Dynapharm rollout across West Africa
   - Custom software → Maduuka SaaS, Aqar PropTech
   - Website design → 15+ years of client websites
   - ICT consulting → Multi-country deployments, BIRDC
   - Digital transformation → Staff training, change management
4. **Create at least one concrete story** from this mapping
5. **If the user provides specific details in their summary**, weave those into the storytelling

## The Authenticity Test

Before finalising an article, ask:

1. **Could a reader tell this was written by someone who has actually done this work?** — Generic advice fails this test. Specific details pass it.
2. **Does the article contain at least one "only Peter would know this" moment?** — A detail, an observation, or a lesson that comes from lived experience.
3. **Would Peter be comfortable reading this aloud at a conference?** — It must sound like his voice, not a marketing agency's.
4. **Is the tone warm but measured?** — East African business English is polite and professional. Never aggressive. Never casual.
5. **Are the stories true to Peter's experience?** — Never invent experiences. Adapt and generalise from real projects referenced in the company profile.

## Storytelling in French

French business storytelling differs from English:

- **Slightly more formal framing:** "Permettez-moi de partager une expérience..." rather than just diving into the story
- **More context before the story:** Francophone readers expect the significance to be established before the anecdote
- **Different cultural anchors:** Use West African references (Dakar, Abidjan, FCFA, CEDEAO) rather than translating Ugandan ones
- **Balanced sentence rhythm:** French stories can use longer, more complex sentences than English ones — but each must still be clear
- **The lesson should be stated with authority:** "Cette expérience m'a enseigné que..." — the French reader expects the consultant to draw a firm conclusion

## Sales Storytelling vs Informational Storytelling

**Informational storytelling** (default for blog posts): The story illustrates a point. The reader learns something. The CTA is secondary.

**Sales storytelling** (for landing pages, product pages, conversion copy): The story IS the selling mechanism. Every detail moves the reader toward action.

When writing sales-oriented stories, use the frameworks below. For standard blog articles, the Five-Beat Structure (above) remains your primary tool.

## The Dominant Story Idea (Ray Edwards)

Borrowed from movie trailers. Your product has a story — find it and build your copy around it.

### Three Elements:

1. **The Dominant Story Idea (DSI)** — Sum up your product's story the way a movie trailer sums up a film. One sentence. This becomes your headline and lead.

2. **Sample Feelings** — Show scenes that help the reader feel the feelings they want from the product. These become your bullet points and deck copy.

3. **Proof It Works** — Testimonials, case studies, before/after. Distributed throughout.

### Finding Your DSI:
- Immerse yourself in the product and the client's world
- Read copy from OTHER industries and ask "How might this story apply to my client?"
- Ask: "If this product were a film, what film would it be?"
- Watch the news for current tie-ins

### Using Your DSI:
- Get it into the headline and lead
- Summarise Sample Feelings in bullet points
- Distribute proof throughout the body

## John Forde's Story Construction Process (AWAI)

### 1. Know Your Story
- Study everything about the subject
- Read everything available
- Close your eyes and tell yourself what the story is about
- Write the details different ways
- Pick the most effective approach

### 2. Prepare Your Story
- Ask what message you want to convey
- Identify the key moment that makes it compelling and relates to your promise

### 3. Identify the Arc
- Start by figuring out how the story ENDS
- List inspiring details and order them
- Start in the MIDDLE of the story (where the action is) — not the beginning

### 4. Construct the Story
- Make it tight: use only important details, cut ruthlessly
- Hint at the big benefit to come
- Tell it quickly — don't meander
- Stick to the truth
- Rely on specificity and juicy details
- When finished, tie it all together with your promise, then transition to the rest

## 6 Places to Find Stories (AWAI)

1. **Talk to the creator/founder** — there's almost always an interesting backstory
2. **Discover how the product was developed** — unusual origins, accidental discoveries, pivots
3. **Learn what goes into production** — exotic ingredients, new technology, careful processes
4. **Talk to the team** — customer service, product development, sales staff all have stories
5. **Mine testimonials deeply** — call the person, have them expand, record the conversation. Ask "How did you feel?" not just "What happened?"
6. **Current events and trends** — tie your story to something happening now

## Seth Godin's 9 Qualities of Great Stories

1. **True** — consistent and authentic (not necessarily factual, but always honest)
2. **Bold promise** — fun, safety, or a shortcut; must be exceptional or not worth telling
3. **Trusted** — earned credibility to tell the story
4. **Subtle** — fewer details spelled out = more powerful; let readers draw conclusions
5. **Happen fast** — first impressions are far more powerful than we credit
6. **Appeal to senses, not logic** — create feelings, not arguments
7. **Rarely aimed at everyone** — narrow audience spreads the story; water it down and it appeals to no one
8. **Don't contradict themselves** — consistency across all touchpoints
9. **Agree with the audience's worldview** — best stories don't teach anything new; they make the audience feel smart and right

## Jen Stevens' Authenticity Rules (AWAI)

### 1. Employ specific details
- Bad: "The business was successful, very successful."
- Good: "Within 18 months, they'd opened three locations across Kampala, employed 45 staff, and processed 2,000 orders per week."

### 2. Avoid filler words
Eliminate: fashionable, lovely, charming, upscale, colourful, beautiful, amazing, incredible. These are lazy descriptions that don't paint specific pictures. Replace with concrete detail.

### 3. Use strong verbs
Replace "to be" and "to have" with verbs that describe action or offer visual images.
- "swept through" instead of "was in"
- "tucked into" instead of "had"
- "launched" instead of "started"

### 4. Ferret out details others overlook
- What did you see, smell, hear, feel?
- Offer exact numbers
- When gathering testimonials, don't just ask "what happened" — ask "how did you feel?" and "in what ways did this change how you see things?"
- Draw out specific details: not "business improved" but "we hired two new staff and finally took a holiday for the first time in three years"

## 14 Storytelling Tips for Copywriters (John Forde, AWAI)

1. **Appear spontaneous** — study until the message is second nature, then write naturally
2. **Give hope** — for things to come
3. **Show passion** — necessity, not optional
4. **Overcome obstacles** — drama is about obstacles and how they're overcome
5. **Make it personal** — personal stories prove what logic can't
6. **Name the hero** — show your reader how they'll triumph
7. **Name a villain** — what keeps the prospect up at night? That's your villain
8. **Reward and tease** — reward with progress, tease with more to come
9. **Enlarge and enrich** — remind what's important, raise the bar
10. **Build a relationship** — between brand and customer, beneath the surface
11. **Commute facts to the subconscious** — embed facts painlessly through story
12. **Have a good twist** — unexpected angle that keeps them reading
13. **Make sense** — knowledgeable customers spot fakes immediately
14. **Leave them wanting more** — know the final emotion and write toward it
