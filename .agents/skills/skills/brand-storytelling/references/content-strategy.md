# Content Strategy Reference

Supporting frameworks for the brand-storytelling skill. These tools operate at the content strategy level — they define what the brand talks about, who it is talking to, how it sounds, and how content production is organised over time.

---

## 1. Style Guide Framework (Norris — Story Engine)

The Style Guide is the most important document for consistent brand voice. Complete all seven components before writing any website content. Without a completed Style Guide, every page risks sounding like a different brand.

### Component 1 — Mission Statement for Content

Write one to three sentences that answer:
- Why does this brand's content exist?
- Who is it for specifically?
- What problem does it solve for the reader?

This statement should be specific enough to reject off-topic content ideas. If an article or page idea is consistent with the mission statement, it belongs. If not, it does not.

**Template:**
> "We create content for [specific audience] who are dealing with [specific problem]. Our content exists to help them [specific outcome], so that [why this matters]. We do not create content about [exclusions]."

---

### Component 2 — Reader Profile

Go beyond demographics. Define the full human being who reads this content.

| Dimension | Questions to Answer |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Who they are | Job title, life stage, responsibilities, geography |
| What they read | Blogs, publications, influencers, communities they trust |
| What they aspire to | The version of themselves they are working toward |
| What they fear | The outcome they are most trying to avoid |
| How they describe their problem | Their own words — not the brand's words |
| What has already failed them | Solutions they have tried that did not work |

Source this information from: customer interviews (minimum five), support chat logs, sales call notes, review sites (G2, Trustpilot, Google), and community forums (Reddit, Facebook groups).

---

### Component 3 — Voice and Tone Guidelines

Voice is consistent (it reflects who the brand is). Tone shifts by context (more direct in crisis content, warmer in testimonials). Define both.

**Voice — what never changes:**

Write three to five rules in plain language. Examples of well-written voice rules:
- "We write like we are explaining something to a smart colleague, not presenting to a boardroom."
- "We use short sentences by default. Long sentences for rhythm, not habit."
- "We use contractions. We say 'you'll' not 'you will.'"
- "We say what we think. We do not hedge to avoid offence."
- "We do not use jargon without immediately defining it."

**Include examples:**

2. _______________________________________________
3. _______________________________________________

Three sentences that sound nothing like this brand:
1. _______________________________________________
2. _______________________________________________
3. _______________________________________________

**Tone shifts by context:**

| Content Type | Tone Adjustment |
|-------------|----------------|
| Crisis or complaint response | Slower, steadier, more direct — less wit |
| Celebrating a client win | Warmer, more specific, let the client speak |
| Educational / how-to | Practical and precise; reader's time is valued |
| Vision / mission | Wider aperture; more invitation than instruction |

### Component 6 — Content Pillars (Topics the Brand Owns)

Define three to six content pillars. Each pillar is a core problem that the brand's ideal customer faces. Every blog post, page, and article falls within one pillar. If a content idea falls outside all pillars, defer or reject it.

Each pillar should be:
- A problem statement (not a topic category)
- Specific enough to say no to most ideas
- Broad enough to sustain 10-20 articles each

### Component 7 — Structural and Grammar Rules

Define the standards that apply to all content, regardless of who writes it.

| Rule | Decision |
|------|----------|
| Oxford comma | Use / Do not use |
| Heading capitalisation | Sentence case / Title Case |
| Numbers | Spell out under 10 / Numerals throughout |
| Paragraph length target | 2-3 sentences (default) |
| Subheading frequency | Every 200-250 words |
| Spelling standard | British English (agency default) |
| Dates | DD Month YYYY (e.g., 15 March 2025) |
| Currency | [client's local currency]; use symbol + amount |

## 2. Empathy Map (Norris — Story Engine)

Six quadrants. Fill in from evidence — customer interviews, support logs, reviews, sales notes — not from assumptions. Do not proceed to content creation with any quadrant blank.

```
+---------------------------+---------------------------+
|       THINK & FEEL        |           HEAR            |
| What matters most to them | What do trusted sources   |
| What are their worries    | tell them? What do peers  |
| What are their ambitions  | and colleagues say?       |
+---------------------------+---------------------------+
|           SEE             |       SAY AND DO          |
| What is their environment | What do they say publicly |
| What do they observe      | about their problem?      |
| What brands do they trust | What actions do they take |
+---------------------------+---------------------------+
|          PAINS            |          GAINS            |
| Frustrations and          | Desired outcomes          |
| obstacles they face       | Wants and aspirations     |
| Fears they hold           | Metrics of success        |
+---------------------------+---------------------------+
```

**How to fill in the empathy map:**
- Conduct a minimum of five customer interviews; ten is better
- Review the last 50 customer support conversations
- Read one to three pages of competitor reviews on G2, Trustpilot, or Google
- Search Reddit and Facebook groups for the core problem; read recent threads
- Review the last 20 sales call notes or enquiry form responses

The words customers use in these sources — not the brand's marketing language — are what go into the empathy map.

## 3. Content Narrative Framework (Brito)

The Content Narrative is the master document that defines what story the brand tells across all channels. Build it from seven inputs before any content is produced.

### The Seven Inputs

**Input 1 — Brand Messaging and Product Benefits**
From `docs/brand-brief.md`. Five elements:
- Brand Position: what the brand does, for whom, and why it is valuable
- Brand Promise: the single commitment at every touchpoint
- Brand Personality: three to five traits stakeholders should use to describe the brand
- Brand Story: the origin and human narrative of the company
- Brand Associations: name, logo, colours, taglines, typography, imagery style

**Input 2 — Non-Business Issues Important to the Brand**
Does the brand have a CSR commitment, a sustainability practice, a community involvement, or a social impact mission? If so, document it here. These are often the most emotionally resonant stories a brand can tell.
> _______________________________________________

**Input 3 — Media and Industry Perceptions**
How is the brand described when it appears in industry press, trade publications, or peer community discussions? Note: this is about context and language, not sentiment. What frame do writers use when they include this brand?
> _______________________________________________

**Input 4 — Community and Customer Perceptions**
How do existing customers describe the brand in their own words — on social, in reviews, in referral conversations? The goal: does their language match the brand's intended narrative? Where it does, amplify it. Where it does not, investigate why.
> _______________________________________________

**Input 5 — Audience Interests Beyond the Product**
What else does the brand's ideal customer care about deeply? What are they passionate about outside of the problem this brand solves? The overlap between brand narrative and audience interest is where the richest content lives.
> _______________________________________________

**Input 6 — Historical Content Performance**
What content has this brand already produced that has worked? Which formats (video, long-form, short posts, case studies), which topics, and which lengths have driven the most engagement and conversions? Start from evidence.
> _______________________________________________

**Input 7 — Consumer Search Behaviour**
What does the target customer search for before they find this brand? What search terms represent the core problems and questions this brand can answer? This data shapes how topics are framed — not to manufacture keywords, but to use the vocabulary the customer already uses.
> _______________________________________________

### The Content Narrative Output Statement

After gathering all seven inputs, write the brand's content narrative in one to two sentences. This statement governs every content decision:

> "We tell stories about [theme/narrative territory], for [specific audience], to prove that [core belief or brand promise]."

Example (Red Bull): "We tell stories about human performance, adventure, and the refusal to accept limits, for people who believe that the ordinary world is something to push past, to prove that you can go further than you think."

Every piece of content — every blog post, every page, every social update — is a specific expression of this single narrative.

## 4. Content Supply Chain Stages (Brito)

The Content Supply Chain is the end-to-end workflow for producing and distributing content. All seven stages must be documented and assigned before scaling content production.

| Stage | What Happens | Who Owns It |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| 1. Ideation | Content themes, topics, and keywords are captured centrally | Content strategist |
| 2. Planning | Editorial calendar; assignments to writers; alignment to campaigns and seasonal events | Editorial lead |
| 3. Creation | Writing, design, photography, video production | Writers, designers |
| 4. Review and Approval | Editorial review, brand alignment check, legal review where required | Editor + brand lead |
| 5. Distribution | Publishing to owned channels; syndication to earned channels; amplification via paid | Publisher |
| 6. Optimisation | Performance measurement, A/B testing, content refreshing based on data | Analytics lead |
| 7. Archiving | Evergreen content stored and repurposed; outdated content updated or retired | Content operations |

**Minimum viable version for agency clients:** For clients without large content teams, the Supply Chain reduces to: Ideation → Planning → Creation → Approval → Publishing → Performance review monthly.

## 5. The Three Rs Measurement Framework (Quesenberry & Coolsen)

Use this framework to evaluate whether brand storytelling is working. Measure in sequence — Relevance before Reach before Resonance.

**Relevance (Category Fit)**
Does the story fit the category in which the brand competes? Is it recognisably about the right problem, for the right audience, in the right register?
- If yes: proceed to Reach
- If no: the story is off-code; return to the brand archetype and Content Narrative Statement

**Reach (Right Segment)**
Is the story reaching the intended audience segment? Check: organic search traffic for target keywords, social audience demographics, ad audience match rates, email open rates by segment.
- Measurement: reach numbers, audience match percentage, segment penetration rate

**Resonance (Bull's-Eye)**
Does the story move people enough that they share it? Resonance is the highest indicator of story success — it means the audience has adopted the story as their own.
- Measurement: social shares and saves, unprompted referrals, qualitative language ("this is exactly how I feel"), earned media pickup, unsolicited testimonials

**The goal:** Move from Relevant to Reaching to Resonating. Most brand content achieves Relevance; few achieve Resonance. Resonance requires that the customer sees themselves in the story — not the brand.

## 6. Creative Effectiveness Ladder (Quesenberry & Coolsen)

A six-rung ladder for evaluating the business impact of creative storytelling work. Use this in client conversations to set expectations about what great brand storytelling achieves over time, and to assess where any given campaign or website sits.

| Rung | Level | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Influential Idea | A concept that captures attention and is remembered; changes how people think about a brand |
| 2 | Behaviour Breakthrough | Content that changes what people actually do — they enquire, subscribe, refer, return |
| 3 | Sales Spike | A measurable increase in sales directly attributable to the creative work |
| 4 | Brand Builder | Sustained lift in brand metrics — awareness, consideration, preference — over 12+ months |
| 5 | Commercial Triumph | Creative work that fundamentally shifts the commercial position of the business |
| 6 | Enduring Icon | A brand story that becomes culturally embedded; referenced beyond its original context for years or decades |

**Client-facing explanation:**
Most websites sit at Rung 1 or 2. A well-executed brand storytelling approach, consistently applied across the website, blog, and content, creates a path from Rung 1 toward Rung 3 and 4 within 12–18 months. Enduring Icon status requires years of consistent, on-narrative content production — but Rungs 1-3 are achievable in a first website build when the story is right.

## 7. Editorial Calendar Structure (Norris — Story Engine)

### Classic Calendar Format

Use for clients with predictable content cadence and small teams. Spreadsheet or Google Sheet.

| Column | What It Contains |
|--------|----------------|
| Publish Date | Scheduled live date |
| Title (working) | Draft headline — update when final is confirmed |
| Content Pillar | Which of the brand's 3-6 content pillars does this serve |
| Story Type | Founding / Transformation / Failure / Values-in-Action / Vision |
| Author | Who is writing this |
| Status | Idea / Brief / In Progress / Review / Scheduled / Published |
| Primary Keyword | The one search term this piece serves |
| CTA | What action this piece drives the reader toward |
| Language | EN / FR / SW — which language version is this entry for |

### Kanban Calendar Format

Use for clients with multiple contributors and rolling pipeline. Visual board with columns:

```
IDEAS → BRIEF COMPLETE → IN PROGRESS → EDITING → SCHEDULED → PUBLISHED
```

Each card contains: working title, pillar, author, due date, language, primary keyword.

### Story Type Distribution (recommended starting point)

This mix prevents content from skewing too promotional or too abstract:

| Story Type | Recommended Share | Rationale |
|------------|------------------|-----------|
| Transformation (client/customer stories) | 30% | Strongest trust-builder; shows results |
| Educational (answers core problems) | 25% | Attracts organic search; builds authority |
| Values-in-Action | 15% | Humanises the brand; retains existing customers |
| Vision / Industry leadership | 15% | Positions for premium; attracts referral partners |
| Founding / Origin | 5% | About page-level content; quarterly maximum |
| Failure / Vulnerability | 10% | High-trust content; use deliberately, not constantly |

### Seasonal and Content Calendar Anchors

Map content planning around these recurring anchors each year:

- Q1: New year goals and problems (high search intent; Transformation Stories perform)
- Q2: Mid-year review content; industry reports
- Q3: Planning for peak season; case studies from H1 ready to publish
- Q4: Year-in-review content; Vision Stories for the year ahead
- Client-specific events: product launches, anniversaries, industry conferences
- Local market events (East Africa): key business, agricultural, and cultural calendar dates
