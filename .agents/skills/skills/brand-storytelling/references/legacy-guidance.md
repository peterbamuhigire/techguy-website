# Brand Storytelling

## The Central Rule

The customer is always the hero. The brand is always the mentor.

This is not a preference — it is a structural law of effective brand storytelling. The moment a brand positions itself as the hero of the story ("We are the best, most innovative..."), the audience disconnects. People do not buy from brands that talk about themselves. They buy from brands that see them, understand them, and offer them a path to become who they want to be.

**Apply this rule before writing a single sentence:**
- Who is the hero of this page? (Name the customer type — not the brand.)
- What does the hero want? (State the transformation they seek.)
- What obstacle stands in the way?
- How does the brand, as mentor, equip the hero to win?

If any page draft positions the brand as hero, rewrite it.

## StoryBrand SB7 BrandScript

The SB7 Framework (Donald Miller — *Building a StoryBrand*) is the practical implementation of the central rule. It maps all brand messaging to a 7-element narrative structure. Full details and worksheets: `book-extractions/storybrand-sb7-framework.md`.

### The 7 Elements

| # | Element | Story Role | Rule |
|---|---------|-----------|------|
| 1 | Character | Hero | Define ONE desire the customer wants — financial, time, social, purpose, meaning, or generosity |
| 2 | Problem | Conflict | Three layers: External (surface), Internal (frustration — the real motivator), Philosophical (injustice) |
| 3 | Guide | Mentor | Show Empathy ("We understand…") BEFORE Authority (testimonials, stats, awards, logos) |
| 4 | Plan | Road map | 3 named steps (Process Plan) or 3 named commitments (Agreement Plan) — never more than 3 |
| 5 | CTA | Invitation | BOTH a Direct CTA (buy now) AND a Transitional CTA (free guide, trial) — on every page |
| 6 | Failure | Stakes | Name 2–3 negative consequences of inaction. Loss aversion is 2–3× stronger than gain appeal. Use lightly. |
| 7 | Success | Resolution | Show success explicitly. Three desires: Status/Position, Union/Wholeness, Self-Realization |
| + | Transformation | Character arc | From → To aspirational identity. Affirm it after purchase. |

### BrandScript Before Drafting

Complete this before the Creative Brief. It informs Section 5 (Single Most Persuasive Idea):

1. **Character:** What is the single thing our customer wants?
2. **Problem:** External / Internal / Philosophical?
3. **Guide:** How do we show Empathy? What Authority evidence do we have (3 items)?
4. **Plan:** What are our 3 named steps?
5. **CTA:** Direct CTA button text? Transitional CTA (free offer)?
6. **Failure:** What 2–3 things happen if they do nothing?
7. **Success:** What does life look like specifically after they engage us?
8. **Transformation:** From _____ → To _____

The BrandScript is appended to `docs/brand-brief.md` by the brand-strategy skill (Step 10). It is the primary input for all website hero sections.

## Five-Act Dramatic Arc (Quesenberry & Coolsen)

Freytag's Five-Act Structure maps onto every meaningful brand story. It works because it mirrors the structure of all human experience: a character faces a problem, the problem intensifies, a turning point arrives, consequences unfold, and resolution is reached.

### The Five Acts

| Act | Stage | Function |
|-----|-------|----------|
| 1 | Exposition | Introduce characters, setting, and the inciting moment — the conflict that sets everything in motion |
| 2 | Complication | Interests clash, obstacles accumulate, frustration and stakes build |
| 3 | Climax | The high turning point — victory or defeat; the moment of maximum tension |
| 4 | Reversal | Consequences of the climax unfold; falling action as the new reality sets in |
| 5 | Denouement | Conflict resolved; hero is better off (comedy arc) or worse off (tragedy arc); lesson crystallised |

Brand stories always use the comedy arc: the hero succeeds. The question is what obstacles they faced and how the mentor (brand) helped them through.

### Applied to Website Pages

**About Page — Founding Story in Five Acts:**
- Act 1: Founder sees a problem that shouldn't exist; the inciting moment that made ignoring it impossible
- Act 2: Obstacles — the hard early years, the failures, the moments of doubt
- Act 3: The breakthrough — the insight, the decision, the first client who believed
- Act 4: Building the thing; the consequences of committing
- Act 5: Where the company stands today and who it now serves

**Case Study — Client Transformation in Five Acts:**
- Act 1: Client's situation before; the problem they were living with
- Act 2: What they had already tried; why it hadn't worked; rising cost of inaction
- Act 3: The engagement; the key decision or intervention
- Act 4: Implementation; what changed in practice
- Act 5: Measurable outcomes; where the client is now

**Hero Section — Compressed Three-Act Version:**
For hero sections and short copy blocks, compress to three beats:
1. Name the conflict (the problem the customer lives with right now)
2. Offer the turning point (what changes with this brand as guide)
3. State the resolution (the life or business outcome they gain)

Match story structure to page type before drafting:

| Page | Story Structure | Dominant Story Type |
|------|----------------|---------------------|
| Home hero | Three-act compressed | Vision Story (where we are going together) |
| About | Five-Act dramatic arc | Founding Story |
| Case Study / Portfolio | Five-Act: client as hero | Transformation Story |
| Team / Culture | Values-in-Action story | Values-in-Action |
| Why Us / Mission | Golden Circle + Vision Story | Vision Story |
| Services | Hero's Journey: customer journey to outcome | Transformation Story |
| Testimonials | Story Circle (eight stages condensed) | Transformation Story |
| Blog / Articles | Any structure appropriate to format | Varies by topic |

## The Creative Brief (Quesenberry & Coolsen)

Complete this brief before writing any page. Do not skip sections. The Creative Brief forces clarity before creativity — the most common cause of weak brand stories is not poor writing, it is writing without knowing what you need the story to do.

All eight sections must be answered before drafting begins. See `references/story-templates.md` for the fill-in format.

**Section 1 — Why Are We Communicating?**
What business objective does this page serve? (Lead generation, trust-building, conversion, retention?)

**Section 2 — Who Are We Talking To?**
Specific customer segment, not a general description. Name the person, their situation, their role.

**Section 3 — What Do They Currently Think, Feel, and Do?**
Present state: beliefs they hold, emotions they feel, actions they take (or avoid) right now.

**Section 4 — What Should They Think, Feel, and Do After This Page?**
Desired state: the specific shift this page needs to create. One belief, one feeling, one action.

**Section 5 — The Single Most Persuasive Idea**
One sentence only. The core claim that, if believed, would make everything else follow. This is not a headline — it is the brief's north star.

**Section 6 — Why Should They Believe It?**
Proof: evidence, facts, testimonials, case study data, credentials, demonstrations.

**Section 7 — Tone**
Three adjectives that describe how this page should feel, matched to the brand archetype from `docs/brand-brief.md`. Not "professional and friendly" — be specific. "Steady, direct, and quietly confident."

**Section 8 — Requirements**
Constraints: word count, legal disclaimers, mandatory CTAs, imagery requirements, language count (EN/FR/SW).

## The Golden Circle Order (Simon Sinek, applied)

Homepage copy and About pages must follow this sequence — WHY first, HOW second, WHAT last.

Most brands write in the opposite order: "We are a logistics company (WHAT). We use AI-powered routing software (HOW). We care about efficiency." This is forgettable.

**Correct sequence:**
1. **WHY** — The belief that drives everything. Why does this company exist beyond making money? What injustice, inefficiency, or absence does it exist to correct?
2. **HOW** — The distinctive method. What does this brand do differently from others offering similar things?
3. **WHAT** — The product or service. This confirms the purchase — it does not create the desire.

**Homepage copy sequence mapped to the Golden Circle:**
- Hero section → WHY (the world as it should be; the belief that drives the brand)
- Value propositions → HOW (the distinctive approach that makes the WHY real)
- Services / Products → WHAT (confirming what is available for purchase)
- Social proof → Evidence that the WHY and HOW deliver real results

Never lead with WHAT. If the homepage headline describes a product or service category, rewrite it to lead with belief or outcome.

## The Five Signature Story Types (Leland)

Every brand needs all five story types ready before writing begins. Elicit these from the client during discovery interviews.

**1. The Founding Story**
Why the business was started. The origin and founding purpose. This story belongs on the About page. It must contain an inciting moment — the specific event or realisation that made the founder act. A founding story without a moment of reckoning is a press release, not a story.

**2. The Transformation Story**
A client or customer whose situation changed because of the brand's work. Use real names and specific outcomes wherever possible. This story belongs on case study pages, testimonial sections, and services pages. The client is the hero; the brand is the guide who provided the tools, the method, or the insight.

**3. The Failure Story**
A time things went wrong and what the brand learned. This story builds trust through vulnerability — it signals to prospects that the brand does not over-promise and is honest when things are hard. Place this in About pages (as part of the founding arc) or blog content. Use it strategically, not casually.

**4. The Values-in-Action Story**
A moment when the brand lived its values when it cost something — when doing the right thing meant losing money, a client, or a shortcut. This story belongs on Team, Culture, or Mission pages. It proves that stated values are real, not decorative.

**5. The Vision Story**
Where the brand is going and why it matters. This story invites the customer into a shared future. Use it on Why Us and Mission pages, and in hero sections when the brand is early-stage or positioning for growth. The Vision Story answers: "If we succeed, what does the world look like?"

## Style Guide Elements (Norris — Story Engine)

Every website written using this skill needs a Style Guide before content is drafted. The Style Guide ensures that all content — across all pages and all languages — sounds like the same brand voice.

The Style Guide is not a tone-of-voice paragraph. It is a working document with seven operational components. Complete it before page-builder runs.

**1. Mission Statement for Content**
Why does this brand's content exist? Who is it for? What problem does it solve for the reader? One to three sentences, specific enough to reject off-topic ideas.

**2. Reader Profile (Empathy Map)**
Six quadrants: what they think and feel, what they hear, what they see, what they say and do, their pains, their gains. Filled in from customer interviews, support logs, or sales call notes — not from assumptions. See `references/content-strategy.md` for the full empathy map template.

**3. Voice and Tone Guidelines**
Specific descriptions, not vague adjectives. Not "professional and friendly." Instead: write the actual voice rules. ("We write in short sentences. We use plain words. We never use jargon without defining it. We treat the reader as intelligent but busy.") Include three to five example sentences that sound like the brand, and three that don't.

**4. Banned Words and Phrases**
List every word or phrase that is off-brand, overused, or meaningless. Common additions: "leverage," "synergy," "world-class," "best-in-class," "innovative," "solutions." Every brand has its own additions.

**5. Competitors to Reference (Internal Only)**
Which competitors does the client monitor? Understanding what the brand is positioning against sharpens every content decision. This list stays internal — it does not appear in published content.

**6. Topics the Brand Owns**
The three to six content pillars that map to core problems the brand's ideal customer faces. Every page, blog post, and social update falls within one of these pillars. If a content idea falls outside all pillars, reject or defer it.

**7. Structural and Grammar Rules**
Oxford comma policy, capitalisation rules (sentence case vs. title case for headings), paragraph length target, subheading frequency (every 200-250 words recommended), British English spelling (standard across all sites built by this agency).

## Content Narrative Framework (Brito)

Before deciding where to tell the brand story, define what the story is. The Content Narrative is the hero document that governs all content decisions. It is built from seven inputs.

Gather all seven before writing any website content:

**Input 1 — Brand Messaging and Product Benefits**
The five elements of holistic brand strategy from `docs/brand-brief.md`: Brand Position, Brand Promise, Brand Personality, Brand Story, and Brand Associations (name, logo, colours, taglines). Translate every product feature into a customer outcome. "Features tell, benefits sell."

**Input 2 — Non-Business Issues Important to the Brand**
CSR, sustainability, community involvement, social impact. If present, these belong in the content narrative — they are often the most emotionally resonant stories a brand can tell.

**Input 3 — Media and Industry Perceptions**
How does the industry press or peer community contextualise this brand? Consistent language between how media frames the brand and how the brand frames itself validates the narrative and builds credibility.

**Input 4 — Community and Customer Perceptions**
How do existing customers describe the brand in their own words? The language customers use to talk about results, problems, and experiences is the most valuable copy on the planet. Mine it from reviews, support chats, and sales call recordings.

**Input 5 — Audience Interests Beyond the Product**
What else does the brand's ideal customer care about? The brands with the deepest community connections find the overlap between their brand narrative and their audience's broader interests.

**Input 6 — Historical Content Performance**
What content has already worked? Which formats, topics, and lengths drive engagement? Start from evidence, not assumptions.

**Input 7 — Consumer Search Behaviour**
What does the target customer search for before they find this brand? Use keyword data to shape how topics are framed — not to stuff keywords, but to use the language customers already use when they have the problem being solved.

**Output: The Content Narrative Statement**
After gathering all seven inputs, define a single, simple story that can be told across every channel and every page. It should be simple enough to explain in one sentence and powerful enough to organise everything the brand produces. Red Bull's content narrative is not "energy drink" — it is "gives you wings," expressed through extreme performance, freedom, and adventure in every piece of content they create.

## Quality Gates

A brand story fails when any of these conditions is present. Check every piece of content against this list before approving it.

**1. Off-Code (Rapaille)**
Every brand has an unconscious code — the deep cultural meaning that makes it resonate. Stories told off-code feel wrong even when they are technically correct. If a story feels misaligned despite being well-written, check whether it matches the brand archetype and emotional promise defined in `docs/brand-brief.md`. If not, rewrite from the archetype, not from the brief.

**2. Brand as Hero**
If a draft positions the brand as the protagonist — the one doing brave and important things — it is structurally broken. The brand is always the guide. Rewrite to put the customer in the protagonist's role and the brand in the mentor's role.

**3. No Conflict**
A story without tension is a brochure. If the About page, case study, or hero section contains no obstacle, no struggle, no moment of doubt or difficulty, it will not engage or be remembered. Add the complication. Real conflict builds trust.

**4. Five-Act Structure Absent from Long-Form Pages**
About pages and case studies longer than 300 words must follow the Five-Act structure. If the content moves directly from problem to solution with no rising tension, apply the structure from `references/story-templates.md`.

**5. Missing Creative Brief**
No page may be drafted without a completed Creative Brief. A Creative Brief with any of its eight sections blank is not complete.

**6. Golden Circle Inverted**
If the homepage leads with WHAT (product description) rather than WHY (belief or purpose), the sequence is wrong. Reorder before any other revision.
