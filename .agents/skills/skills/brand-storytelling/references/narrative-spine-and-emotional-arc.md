# Narrative Spine and Emotional Arc

Premium websites that move buyers tell a story across pages, not within a single page. Use this reference to plan the story before the design system or page builder.

## Three-act structure for a buyer journey

- Act 1 — setup. What the buyer's world looks like today and why it is not enough. Pages: homepage hero, awareness content, problem-framing case studies.
- Act 2 — confrontation. The decision moments. What the buyer must reckon with: cost, risk, readiness, internal politics. Pages: comparison, methodology, pricing logic, frequently raised objections.
- Act 3 — resolution. The change. What the future looks like with this solution. Pages: detailed offer, proof, guarantee, contact.

The acts map to the experience map's stages and to the page archetypes from `map-to-sitemap-conversion.md`.

## Plot points

A plot point is a specific moment that turns the story. Each premium site needs at least four:

1. The first specific promise on the homepage that is sharp enough to make a generic buyer pause.
2. The first specific proof the buyer encounters that they cannot dismiss.
3. The first objection the site explicitly names and answers without flinching.
4. The first moment the next step is asked for, with the value of the ask made clear.

Plot points are placed deliberately. They are not "above the fold by default"; they are wherever the buyer's emotional state is ready for them.

## Emotional arc

Premium websites accept that the buyer feels things. Map the expected emotion at each stage:

- Skepticism on arrival. Address with specificity, not enthusiasm.
- Curiosity at the second viewport. Reward with a detail or proof point.
- Anxiety on the comparison page. Reduce with a frank acknowledgement of what is hard.
- Trust on the proof page. Earn with verifiable, specific evidence.
- Confidence on the offer page. Reinforce with a guarantee or a clear next step.
- Relief at confirmation. Affirm with what happens next and who owns it.

A page that ignores the buyer's emotional state at that stage is decorative.

## The red thread

A red thread is the single recurring idea that ties every page together. Without it, the site becomes a brochure of disconnected services.

For each engagement, write the red thread as a single sentence:

> "Every page on this site connects [audience] to [specific outcome] by showing [specific proof]."

The red thread shows up in the homepage promise, the methodology page, the case study openings, the offer page, and the confirmation page. It is not the tagline. It is the editorial backbone.

## Character

Cast the customer as the hero. The brand is the guide who has been there before. Avoid the inverse, where the brand is the hero and the customer is a backdrop.

For each persona on the site, write:

- What they want.
- What stops them.
- What they will be capable of after they engage.
- How the brand makes that possible.

Use this card as a check on every page draft. If the page makes the brand the protagonist of every section, rewrite.

## Setting and context

The buyer's context is not a single moment but a sequence: phone in the morning, laptop in the office, tablet in the evening. The story holds across devices. The same red thread runs through; the same plot points appear; the same emotion is acknowledged.

For multi-locale sites, the story translates without losing its shape. The red thread is universal; the proof points are local.

## Page sequencing rules

- Awareness content is structured so the most natural next page is consideration.
- Consideration pages always offer a low-commitment continuation (download, watch, save) and a higher-commitment one (book, buy).
- Decision pages always offer a fallback (contact a human) for buyers who need it before committing.

A site without intentional sequencing is read as a directory. A premium site is read as a story.

## Modular content blocks

For clients with extensive content needs, design reusable narrative blocks:

- Hero promise + proof + action.
- Methodology in three steps with evidence per step.
- Case story spine: situation, complication, decision, evidence, outcome.
- Pricing logic: what is included, what is not, why this floor.
- Objection block: the objection, the honest answer, the proof.

Combine these blocks across pages without losing the red thread.

## Anti-patterns

- Generic "About us" copy that could fit any agency.
- Case studies that read as brand selfies; the customer is invisible.
- Animated heroes that delay the first specific promise.
- Multiple unrelated promises on the homepage; the buyer cannot remember any of them.
- Story that contradicts the price floor on the pricing page.
