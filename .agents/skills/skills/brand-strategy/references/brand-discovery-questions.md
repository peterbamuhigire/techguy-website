# Brand Discovery Questions

Complete reference for the brand strategy discovery interview. Work through each section in order. Document every answer in the session notes before drafting the brand brief.

---

## Section 1: Foundation Questions (Marcoux)

These open the conversation and surface the client's core intent before any framework is applied.

1. What do you want to be known for? (one thing — not a list)
2. Who do you want to be known by? (describe the person specifically)
3. What is your 5-year vision for this business?
4. What are your genuine strengths — the things you do better than most competitors?
5. What are your honest weaknesses or blind spots? (what do you avoid saying to clients?)
6. How do you want the brand to look and feel visually? (gut instinct, not strategy — what does it feel like?)
7. What brands do you admire, even outside your industry, and why?

## Section 2: The 7 Brand Map Elements (Leland)

Work through each element as a question. The client's first answer is often incomplete — probe for specifics.

### Element 1: Unique Branding Proposition (UBP)

- If your best client was describing you to a colleague who had never heard of you, what would they say that makes you different?
- What do you do or deliver that competitors either cannot or do not?
- What is the emotional or experiential difference a client notices — not just the technical or feature difference?
- Complete this sentence: "We are the only [category] that [specific differentiator] for [specific audience]."

### Element 2: Brand Distinction

- Name your three closest competitors.
- What do you do better than each of them — specifically?
- What do they do that you refuse to do?
- If all brand names were removed from your marketing and your competitors' marketing, could a customer tell the difference? Why?

### Element 3: Brand Promise

- What is the one thing you commit to delivering every single time, without exception?
- If a client worked with you and told one friend about it, what is the one sentence you want them to say?
- Is there anything in your current operation that sometimes contradicts that promise? (honest answer required)

### Element 4: Brand Tone and Temperament

- Describe the brand as a person: what is their personality?
- How does the brand behave at its best? At its worst?
- Choose five adjectives that describe the brand voice. Now eliminate three and keep the two most essential.
- What is the brand NOT? (list adjectives that clearly do not fit)

### Element 5: Target Audience

- Describe your single best client — not a type, a specific person. What do they do? How old are they? What do they worry about?
- What does that person Google at 11pm when they cannot sleep?
- What do they read, watch, or listen to?
- What was happening in their life just before they came to you?
- What result did they want that they had not been able to get before?

### Element 6: Brand Archetype

Present the 12 archetypes (see positioning-frameworks.md). Ask:

- Which archetype feels most like how you already behave — not how you wish you behaved?
- Which archetype do your best clients seem to be drawn to?
- Is there a secondary archetype that adds something the primary misses?
- Look at your last 10 pieces of marketing. Which archetype do they reflect?

### Element 7: Brand Story

- Why did you start this business? What was the moment or situation that made you do it?
- What is the most dramatic result a client has experienced because of your work?
- Tell me about a time things went seriously wrong. What happened and what did you learn?
- Describe a moment when you held to your values even though it cost you — a client, a contract, or a relationship.
- Where is this business going? What future are you building, and why does it matter?

## Section 3: The 7 Why Questions (Start With Why / Quesenberry)

These questions drill beneath stated rationale to the genuine motivation driving the brand.

1. Why does this business exist beyond making money?
2. Why would customers choose you if you cost more than competitors?
3. Why do your best employees stay? What do they tell their friends?
4. Why did you personally choose this industry or problem to work in?
5. Why would a customer trust you before they have experienced your work?
6. Why would someone refer you to a friend — what would they say?
7. Why does this matter — what is worse in the world without this business?

The answer to question 7 is often the core of the brand vision.

## Section 4: The 5 Signature Story Types (Leland)

Elicit at least one story in each category. These become the raw material for About pages, case studies, team content, and brand storytelling across the site.

### Story Type 1: The Founding Story

- What was happening in your life when you decided to start this?
- Was there a specific moment, problem, or frustration that triggered it?
- What did you risk or sacrifice to begin?
- What did you believe then that others in the industry did not?

### Story Type 2: The Transformation Story

- Tell me about one client whose situation changed significantly because of your work.
- What was their situation before? What is it now?
- What specifically did you do that made the difference?
- Would that client be willing to tell that story publicly?

### Story Type 3: The Failure Story

- Tell me about a project or period where things went wrong.
- What did you get wrong?
- What did you change because of it?
- How is the business different today because of that failure?

### Story Type 4: The Values-in-Action Story

- Tell me about a time you turned down work, ended a relationship, or made a costly decision because it conflicted with your values.
- What was at stake?
- Would you make the same choice again?

### Story Type 5: The Vision Story

- Where is this business in 10 years?
- What problem in the world will be less severe because of what you are building?
- Who will benefit beyond your direct clients?
- What does success actually look like — not in revenue, but in impact?

## Section 5: The "What Do You Stand Against?" Exercise (Drew/Williams)

One of the most productive brand positioning exercises. Ask these questions and document the answers exactly as the client states them — their language often becomes the most authentic copy in the brief.

1. What behaviour in your industry makes you angry or embarrassed for the profession?
2. What do competitors say they deliver but routinely fail to actually deliver?
3. What experience do your best clients describe having had with others before finding you?
4. What would you refuse to do even if it cost you a significant contract?
5. What do you wish your industry would stop doing?

**Synthesis:** The pattern across these answers defines the brand's competitive stance. A brand that stands against something specific and genuine becomes magnetic to the clients who share that frustration.

## Section 6: Problem-Solution Foundation Questions (Drew/Williams)

These two questions anchor the entire brand strategy in genuine value creation, not positioning theory.

**Question 1: How do you create value?**
- What specific skills, processes, or resources do you bring to a problem?
- What do you know or do that most people in your category do not?
- What would a client lose if you did not exist?

**Question 2: How can you offer those skills to solve customer problems?**
- Which customer problems are an exact match for what you can deliver?
- Which problems do you encounter that you are not the right solution for?
- What is the most urgent problem your ideal customer faces that your capabilities address?

The intersection of the two answers — what you genuinely create, matched precisely to what customers genuinely need — is the brand foundation. Every positioning statement and messaging decision should trace back to this intersection.

## Section 7: Competitive Colour Analysis

Used to apply the Law of Color. Before selecting a brand colour direction.

1. List the 5 primary competitors.
2. For each, note their dominant brand colour.
3. Identify which colours are unclaimed or underused in the competitive landscape.
4. The brand colour should be the opposite of the dominant colour used by the market leader.

Document the colour map in the brand brief before passing to design-system.

## Section 8: 3 Cs Baseline Assessment (Leland)

Assess where the brand stands today before any work begins.

**Consistency**
- Does the brand look and sound the same across all current touchpoints (website, social, email, in-person)?
- Rate 1–5: how consistently is the brand communicated today?

**Constancy**
- How often does the brand appear to the target audience (weekly, monthly, quarterly)?
- Is the brand showing up in the channels where the ideal customer spends time?
- Rate 1–5: how frequently does the brand appear today?

**Congruency**
- Does what the brand promises match what it actually delivers?
- Would a recent client's description of the experience match the brand's own description of itself?
- Rate 1–5: how well do external promise and internal reality align today?

Document honest ratings. The lowest score identifies where brand-building effort should concentrate first.
