# Brand Strategy

## When to Use

Run this skill before any design or content work begins. The output — `docs/brand-brief.md` — is required input for:

- `design-system` (colours, fonts, and visual identity flow from brand archetype and positioning)
- `brand-alignment` (quality gate checks all pages against the brand brief)
- `page-builder` (headline voice, CTAs, and messaging hierarchy come from the brand mantra and positioning)
- `blog-writer` (brand voice, tone, and content pillars come from the brand map)

**Do not start design-system without a completed brand-brief.md.**

The Marketing Mastery Pyramid principle: most clients start at the bottom with tactics (social media, ads, website). Start at the top. Brand clarity makes every downstream decision faster and cheaper.

## Step 1: Brand Discovery Interview

Run this interview with the client before writing a word of copy or touching a design tool. The goal is to complete the Brand Map — all seven elements documented in `references/brand-discovery-questions.md`.

### The 7 Brand Map Elements (Leland)

Work through each element in order. Do not skip any. Each feeds the next.

1. **Unique Branding Proposition (UBP)** — one sentence, emotionally and experientially differentiated, not just a feature list
2. **Brand Distinction** — the competitive gap; what makes them meaningfully different
3. **Brand Promise** — the single most important commitment delivered at every touchpoint
4. **Brand Tone and Temperament** — personality and emotional character
5. **Target Audience** — demographics + psychographics + buying behaviour
6. **Brand Archetype** — the Jungian energy archetype driving the brand (see Step 5)
7. **Brand Story** — origin, purpose, and journey

### Foundation Questions (Marcoux)

Ask these to surface the core of the brand before touching the 7 elements:

- What do you want to be known for?
- Who do you want to be known by?
- What is your vision for the business in 5 years?
- What are your genuine strengths?
- What are your honest weaknesses or blind spots?
- How do you want the brand to look and feel visually?

### The Be-Do-Have Sequence

Brands built in the wrong order break. The correct sequence:

1. **Be** — who must this brand BE in the mind of the ideal customer?
2. **Do** — what actions flow from that identity?
3. **Have** — what results follow?

Ask the client: "Who do you need to BE in your customer's mind?" Answer that first. Every tactical decision flows from the answer.

### Problem-Solution Foundation (Drew/Williams)

Two questions that anchor the brand in genuine value creation:

- "How do you create value?" (what skills, resources, or processes produce something customers want)
- "How can you offer those skills to solve customer problems?" (the business model)

The intersection of these two answers is the brand foundation. Brands that cannot answer both questions clearly have a positioning problem, not a marketing problem.

## Step 2: Competitive Landscape and Positioning

### What Do You Stand Against? (Drew/Williams)

This exercise often produces the most useful positioning insight in the entire discovery session.

Ask the client:
- "What do you refuse to be?"
- "What behaviour in your industry do you find unacceptable?"
- "What do your best customers complain about with other providers?"

Strong brand positioning is as much about what a brand rejects as what it claims. A brand that stands against something specific becomes magnetic to the customers who share that frustration.

### Frame of Reference

Before identifying competitive differences, establish the frame: what category does this brand compete in? Defining the frame of reference determines who the competitors are and sets the baseline for comparison.

### Points-of-Parity (POPs) and Points-of-Difference (PODs)

**POPs** — associations not unique to the brand but necessary to be taken seriously in the category. You must establish these to be considered credible. Failing to meet category POPs disqualifies a brand before it can compete.

**PODs** — associations consumers strongly link to the brand, positively evaluate, and cannot find to the same extent elsewhere. Must pass three tests:

| Test | Question |
|------|----------|
| Desirable | Does the customer actually want this? |
| Deliverable | Can the company actually provide it consistently? |
| Differentiating | Do competitors lack it or do it noticeably worse? |

A POD that fails any one of the three tests is not a point-of-difference — it is a marketing claim with no foundation.

## Step 3: Keller's CBBE Pyramid

The Brand Resonance Pyramid is the diagnostic and planning tool for understanding where the brand is now and what needs to be built. Work through it bottom to top. The full sub-dimensions are in `references/positioning-frameworks.md`.

```
                   [RESONANCE]
                  /            \
         [JUDGMENTS]        [FEELINGS]
            /                        \
    [PERFORMANCE]              [IMAGERY]
              \                    /
                   [SALIENCE]
```

**Level 1 — Salience:** Does the brand come to mind in the right situations? Goal: correct brand identity established in the right contexts.

**Level 2 — Performance / Imagery:** Performance = functional delivery. Imagery = emotional and symbolic associations (user profiles, personality, heritage). These are built simultaneously — the rational route (left) and emotional route (right).

**Level 3 — Judgments / Feelings:** Judgments are rational evaluations (quality, credibility, superiority). Feelings are emotional responses (warmth, fun, excitement, security, social approval, self-respect). Both must be positive.

**Level 4 — Resonance:** The pinnacle. Deep psychological bond — behavioral loyalty, attitudinal attachment, sense of community, active engagement. Resonance cannot be forced; it is earned by doing the lower levels well over time.

**Key ordering rule:** Identity before meaning. Meaning before response. Response before relationship. Cannot skip levels.

## Step 4: Brand Mantra

The brand mantra is the internal compass. Three to five words. Every employee and partner should be able to state it without looking it up.

### 3-Component Structure (Keller)

| Component | Definition | Nike | Disney |
|-----------|-----------|------|--------|
| Emotional Modifier | How the brand delivers its benefits | Authentic | Fun |
| Descriptive Modifier | Clarifies the nature of the function | Athletic | Family |
| Brand Function | The core product/service/experience | Performance | Entertainment |

### Development Process

1. Complete Steps 1–3 first. The mantra distils what emerges from the discovery, not what the client wants to project.
2. Draft five to ten candidate mantras.
3. Test each against three criteria: Does it Communicate (define the category)? Does it Simplify (is it memorable and crisp)? Does it Inspire (is it meaningful to employees)?
4. Eliminate any candidate that fails any test.
5. Choose the one that acts as a "mental filter" — anything inconsistent with the mantra gets rejected.

The mantra is not the tagline. The tagline is the external expression; the mantra is the internal standard.

## Step 5: Brand Archetype

The archetype is the energetic personality driving the brand. It informs font personality, colour palette energy, image style, and content voice. All 12 archetypes with design and voice implications are in `references/positioning-frameworks.md`.

### Selection Process

1. Present the client with the 12 archetypes and their descriptions.
2. Ask which one feels most authentic to how the brand behaves — not how they wish it behaved.
3. Identify a secondary archetype if the primary alone creates a flat or incomplete picture.
4. Test the selection: "Does our current marketing already behave this way, even if unconsciously?"

### Archetype → Design → Voice → Content

Once the archetype is selected, every downstream decision has a clear reference:

- **Font personality**: Sage/Ruler → authoritative serifs. Creator/Jester → expressive display. Everyman/Caregiver → warm humanist sans.
- **Colour energy**: Hero → bold, high-contrast. Innocent → clean, light pastels. Rebel → dark, unexpected combinations.
- **Image style**: Hero/Explorer → action and movement. Lover/Caregiver → intimate, warm lifestyle. Sage → evidence, documentation, expertise.
- **Content voice**: Sage → educational, measured. Jester → witty, irreverent. Ruler → authoritative, definitive.

## Step 6: The 22 Laws Applied

Seven laws are most directly relevant to agency brand strategy work. The full set is in `references/positioning-frameworks.md`.

**Law of Singularity**: Every brand must stand for one thing and only one thing. If a customer cannot answer "what does this brand stand for?" in one word, the brand lacks power. Every brand brief must name the single word or idea the brand owns.

**Law of Contraction**: A brand becomes stronger when it narrows its focus. The reflex to expand services or audiences to capture more revenue reliably weakens the brand. Narrow scope = stronger position.

**Law of Publicity**: Brands are born through publicity, not advertising. Being first in a new category generates the coverage that advertising cannot buy. Strategy should be developed from a publicity point of view before a media buying point of view.

**Law of the Word**: The objective is to own a word in the prospect's mind. Not a complicated word. A simple, everyday word that no competitor currently owns. Build all messaging around claiming that word.

**Law of Color**: Choose a colour opposite to the dominant competitor. Colour differentiation is one of the fastest paths to visual memorability. Start with competitive colour analysis, not aesthetic preference.

**Law of Consistency**: Brands are measured in decades. Repositioning destroys equity. Once the brand position is established, protect it. Build on it. Resist any pressure to pivot without overwhelming strategic cause.

**Law of Mortality**: Some brands have had their day. If a brand is so tied to a disappearing era or product form that revitalisation would cost more than a new brand launch, say so clearly. Honest counsel here is one of an agency's most valuable services.

## Step 7: Frequency-Consistency-Anchoring (Drew/Williams)

This is the brand-building process in practice — not the strategy, but the mechanism by which strategy becomes a real brand in the mind.

**Frequency**: The brand must appear repeatedly in the customer's world. A message seen once does not build a brand. Repetition is not redundancy; it is foundation. The Mere Exposure Effect (Marcoux) confirms that familiarity alone increases preference, independent of content quality.

**Consistency**: Every appearance of the brand must send the same signal. Inconsistency teaches the customer that the brand means nothing in particular. The consistency rule has one practical form: the "Always/Never" style guide rule.

The "Always/Never" Rule: For every brand, define a list of things it Always does and things it Never does. These are not preferences; they are standards. Examples:

- Always: lead with the customer's problem before the solution
- Always: use the specific brand colour in all digital materials
- Never: discount the core service offering
- Never: use stock photography that could represent any competitor
- Never: describe ourselves as "innovative" or "passionate" (generic claims)

**Anchoring**: The Pavlov's meat paste analogy — through consistent, repeated association, the brand stimulus (name, logo, colour, voice) triggers the desired response automatically. Anchoring takes time and cannot be shortcut. Every brand touchpoint is either building the anchor or eroding it.

**Never discount your brand** (Marcoux): Discounting creates a price anchor that permanently degrades perceived value. Premium brands maintain price integrity. If clients must compete on price, re-examine the brand positioning, not the price list.

**Momentum metaphor**: Consistency is like a wheel. The first push takes enormous effort. Each consistent brand action adds momentum. Inconsistency is friction that requires the wheel to be restarted from zero. Clients who rebrand every two years restart the wheel. Clients who hold the line compound their equity.

**WE Cycle values** (Drew/Williams): Authentic brands reject hype, posturing, and arrogance. The values that build lasting brand equity — authenticity, humility, truth, community — are not soft ideals; they are competitive advantages in markets where customers are overexposed to manufactured brand personas. Audit every message against these four: is this authentic? Is this honest? Does this serve the community or only the brand?

## Step 8: Brand Brief Deliverable

The output of this skill is `docs/brand-brief.md`. This file is the single source of truth for all downstream design and content work.

### Required Sections

```markdown
# Brand Brief: [Client Name]

## 1. Brand Foundation
- Business name and tagline
- What the business does (one sentence)
- The single word/idea the brand owns
- Brand mantra (3 components: emotional modifier, descriptive modifier, brand function)

## 2. Target Audience
- Primary customer profile (demographics + psychographics)
- Secondary audience (if applicable)
- Ideal customer's core problems (3–5)
- What they Google before they find this brand

## 3. Brand Positioning
- Frame of reference (category)
- Points-of-Parity (3–5 category requirements)
- Points-of-Difference (2–3 genuine, deliverable differentiators)
- Formal positioning statement

## 4. Competitive Landscape
- Top 3 competitors with brief assessment
- Competitive colour map (who uses what colour)
- What the brand stands against

## 5. Brand Archetype
- Primary archetype and secondary archetype (if any)
- Design implications: font personality, colour energy, image style
- Voice implications: tone, vocabulary level, register

## 6. Brand Mantra
- Final 3-component mantra
- What it means in practice (2–3 sentences)
- Things this brand ALWAYS does
- Things this brand NEVER does

## 7. Brand Story Portfolio
- Founding story (1 paragraph)
- Transformation story (client result)
- Values-in-action story (when values cost something)
- Vision story (where the brand is going)

## 8. CBBE Pyramid Status
- Current level assessment (where is the brand now?)
- Target level (where should it be after this project?)
- Key associations to build (3–5)

## 9. Brand Architecture
- Structure: Branded House or House of Brands?
- Current product/service lines
- Future extension considerations

## 10. 3 Cs Measurement Baseline
- Consistency: is the brand communicated consistently today?
- Constancy: how frequently does the brand appear?
- Congruency: does external promise match internal reality?
```

## Step 10: SB7 BrandScript Synthesis

After completing Steps 1–9, synthesise the brand brief into a one-page SB7 BrandScript. The BrandScript translates brand strategy into message architecture — the 7 elements that govern all website copy, sales scripts, and marketing collateral. Full SB7 rules: `book-extractions/storybrand-sb7-framework.md`.

Complete each element using information already captured in `docs/brand-brief.md`:

| Element | Source in brand-brief.md | Output |
|---------|--------------------------|--------|
| Character (desire) | Target audience → core problems | Single-sentence desire statement |
| Problem — External | Target audience pains (Section 2) | The surface-level problem |
| Problem — Internal | Target audience frustrations | "They feel…" emotional statement |
| Problem — Philosophical | Brand positioning + values | The injustice or unfairness at stake |
| Guide — Empathy | Brand promise + voice tone | "We understand how it feels to…" opener |
| Guide — Authority | CBBE level, testimonials | 3 authority signals (stats, logos, awards) |
| Plan | Services / delivery process | 3 named steps, ≤10 words each |
| Direct CTA | Primary conversion goal | Button text + action |
| Transitional CTA | Content / lead magnet | Free resource aligned to customer problem |
| Failure | Customer fears (loss aversion) | 2–3 consequences of inaction |
| Success | Brand promise + aspirational identity | Before/After: Have, Feel, Day, Status |
| Transformation | Archetype + aspirational identity | From [current identity] → To [desired identity] |

Add the completed BrandScript to `docs/brand-brief.md` as **Section 11: SB7 BrandScript**. It becomes the primary input for `brand-storytelling`, `sales-copywriting`, and `page-builder`.
