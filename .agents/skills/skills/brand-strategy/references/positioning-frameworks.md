# Positioning Frameworks

Complete reference for brand positioning, the CBBE pyramid, brand mantra development, archetypes, and the 22 Laws most relevant to agency brand strategy work.

---

## Framework 1: The CBBE Pyramid — Full Sub-Dimensions

The Brand Resonance Pyramid (Keller). Work bottom to top. Cannot build a higher level without the lower level established.

```
                   [RESONANCE]
                  /            \
         [JUDGMENTS]        [FEELINGS]
            /                        \
    [PERFORMANCE]              [IMAGERY]
              \                    /
                   [SALIENCE]
```

### Level 1: Salience — Who are you?

**Depth of awareness:** Can customers recall or recognise the brand in purchase and consumption situations?

**Breadth of awareness:** Across how many relevant situations does the brand come to mind?

Sub-dimensions:
- Brand recognition: confirms prior exposure when given the brand name
- Brand recall: retrieves the brand from memory given a category, need, or situation
- Category identification: which category does the brand occupy in memory?
- Need satisfied: which need or usage situation triggers the brand?

**Goal:** The brand comes to mind automatically in the right situations. Not just recognition, but recall at the moment of need.

---

### Level 2 (Left): Performance — What are you?

How the product meets functional needs. Five sub-dimensions:

1. **Primary characteristics and secondary features** — what the product does, and what extra features support the core function
2. **Product reliability, durability, serviceability** — consistency over time; how long it lasts; how easy it is to fix
3. **Service effectiveness, efficiency, empathy** — does support solve problems well, quickly, and caringly?
4. **Style and design** — aesthetic appeal and sensory properties
5. **Price** — value-for-money positioning; what price tier is the brand associated with?

---

### Level 2 (Right): Imagery — What are you?

Extrinsic properties — what the brand represents beyond functional delivery. Four categories:

1. **User profiles** — who is associated with using the brand? (demographics, psychographics, lifestyle)
2. **Purchase and usage situations** — where, when, and how is the brand used?
3. **Personality and values** — if the brand were a person, what are their traits? What do they stand for?
4. **History, heritage, experiences** — nostalgia, tradition, or origin story associations

---

### Level 3 (Left): Judgments — What about you?

Customers' rational evaluations. Four types:

1. **Brand quality** — overall assessment of quality and satisfaction
2. **Brand credibility** — perceived expertise (competence and innovation), trustworthiness (honest and dependable), likability (fun and interesting)
3. **Brand consideration** — likelihood of inclusion in the consideration set; "would I buy this?"
4. **Brand superiority** — the extent to which customers see the brand as uniquely better than alternatives

---

### Level 3 (Right): Feelings — What about you?

Customers' emotional responses. Six types in ascending intensity:

| Feeling | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Warmth | Calm, peaceful, affectionate feelings |
| Fun | Amusement, playfulness, lightness |
| Excitement | Energy, vitality, upbeat arousal |
| Security | Safety, comfort, self-assurance |
| Social approval | Positive perception from others |
| Self-respect | Pride, sense of accomplishment |

---

### Level 4: Resonance — What about you and me?

The nature and depth of the psychological bond. Four dimensions:

1. **Behavioral loyalty** — repeat purchase frequency, share of category requirements ("always buy this brand")
2. **Attitudinal attachment** — the brand is loved; it is "my brand"; viewed as a special possession
3. **Sense of community** — kinship with other users; brand functions as a social identity marker
4. **Active engagement** — investment beyond purchase: brand content consumption, word of mouth, user communities, brand advocacy

Resonance is the objective for consumer brands with long buying cycles. For B2B and service brands, credibility (Level 3) is often the primary target — resonance comes with repeated delivery over years.

## Framework 2: Points-of-Parity and Points-of-Difference

### Points-of-Parity (POPs)

Associations not unique to the brand but necessary to be credible in the category. Three types:

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Category POPs | What consumers require to take a brand seriously as a player | A bank must have online banking |
| Competitive POPs | Associations designed to neutralise a competitor's POD | If Competitor A claims "fastest delivery," you need to match it to stay in contention |
| Correlational POPs | Seemingly contradictory associations that can coexist | "Affordable luxury" — luxury and value appear to conflict but can be designed to coexist |

**Rule:** Establish category POPs before attempting to claim PODs. A brand that lacks category credibility will have its PODs dismissed.

### Points-of-Difference (PODs)

Attributes or benefits strongly associated with the brand, positively evaluated, and not found to the same extent in competitors. Three tests:

| Test | Question to Ask |
|------|----------------|
| Desirable | Does this benefit personally matter to the target customer? |
| Deliverable | Can the company consistently deliver it at scale? |
| Differentiating | Do competitors lack it or do it noticeably worse? |

**Common mistakes:**
- Claiming a POD that competitors also deliver (not differentiating)
- Claiming a POD the company cannot sustain operationally (not deliverable)
- Claiming a POD customers do not care about (not desirable)

## Framework 3: Positioning Statement Template

The formal positioning statement is the internal tool that locks the brand's place in the market. Not copy — strategy. It is used to test all marketing decisions.

```
For [target audience],
[Brand name] is the [frame of reference / category]
that [primary POD — most compelling differentiator]
because [reason to believe — evidence, process, or mechanism].

Unlike [primary competitor or category norm],
[Brand name] [specific contrast that reinforces the POD].
```

**Example (constructed):**
```
For small business owners in East Africa who need professional financial management,
Maduuka is the all-in-one business management platform
that runs your entire operation from a single app,
because it was built specifically for African markets, currencies, and workflows.

Unlike imported accounting software designed for Western markets,
Maduuka works offline, handles multi-currency locally, and requires no accountant to set up.
```

**Instructions:**
- One positioning statement per brand (not per product)
- The frame of reference should be the category customers already understand
- The POD must pass all three tests (desirable, deliverable, differentiating)
- The reason to believe should be specific enough to be checked — avoid vague claims
- The contrast statement sharpens the POD by naming what the brand is not

## Framework 4: Brand Mantra Development

### The 3-Component Structure

| Component | Role | Instruction |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| Emotional Modifier | How the brand delivers its benefits | An adjective describing the emotional quality of the delivery |
| Descriptive Modifier | Clarifies the nature of the function | Narrows the Brand Function to a specific type |
| Brand Function | Core product, service, or experience | The category or nature of what is provided |

### Reference Examples

| Brand | Emotional Modifier | Descriptive Modifier | Brand Function |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Nike | Authentic | Athletic | Performance |
| Disney | Fun | Family | Entertainment |
| BMW | (implied) | Ultimate | Driving Machine |

### Development Instructions

1. The mantra emerges from discovery, not brainstorming. Complete the full brand map first.
2. Write five to ten candidate mantras. Apply no filter during generation.
3. Test each against three criteria:
   - **Communicate:** Does it define the category and clarify what the brand is and is not?
   - **Simplify:** Is it short, vivid, memorable, and crisp?
   - **Inspire:** Is it personally meaningful to employees — not just marketers?
4. Eliminate any candidate that fails a single test.
5. The surviving mantra is the internal standard. It functions as a filter: any marketing action inconsistent with the mantra is rejected.

**The mantra is not the tagline.** The tagline is the external, customer-facing phrase. The mantra is the internal compass that makes brand decisions consistent without requiring a committee.

## Framework 5: The 12 Brand Archetypes

Based on Jungian archetype theory adapted for brand personality. Use this table to select the archetype, determine design direction, and set the voice standard.

| Archetype | Core Drive | Real Brand Examples | Font Personality | Colour Energy | Image Style | Content Voice |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|--------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Hero** | Courage, mastery, transformation | Nike, FedEx, Duracell | Bold, strong sans | Bold primaries: red, black | Action, movement, results | Motivational, direct, challenge-accepting |
| **Ruler** | Control, order, authority | Mercedes, Rolex, Microsoft | Classic serif | Deep navy, gold, charcoal | Power, precision, polish | Authoritative, definitive, no caveats |
| **Caregiver** | Compassion, service, protection | Johnson & Johnson, Toms, UNICEF | Soft humanist sans | Warm greens, warm blues, pastels | Intimate, warm lifestyle, human connection | Nurturing, reassuring, patient |
| **Creator** | Innovation, originality, vision | Apple, Adobe, LEGO | Distinctive display or geometric | Bright accent colours, white space | Product beauty, craft detail, process | Curious, perfectionist, referencing craft |
| **Explorer** | Freedom, discovery, adventure | Jeep, REI, Red Bull | Rugged, condensed | Earth tones, deep teals, forest green | Landscape, movement, wild environments | Independent, expansive, restless |
| **Innocent** | Purity, optimism, simplicity | Dove, Innocent Drinks, Patagonia | Clean rounded sans | White, pale yellow, sky blue | Clean, natural, uncluttered | Simple, honest, direct, unpretentious |
| **Sage** | Knowledge, wisdom, truth | Google, TED, Economist | Authoritative serif or clean sans | Deep neutrals, academic blues | Charts, evidence, documentation | Educational, measured, precise, sourced |
| **Rebel** | Revolution, disruption, change | Harley-Davidson, Diesel, Virgin | Unconventional, rough-edged | Black, red, unexpected combinations | Dark, edgy, provocative | Direct, irreverent, calls out the establishment |
| **Lover** | Intimacy, passion, pleasure | Victoria's Secret, Chanel, Häagen-Dazs | Elegant serif or script | Deep reds, rose, champagne | Sensual, close-up, luxurious | Intimate, seductive, emotionally rich |
| **Jester** | Fun, enjoyment, lightness | Old Spice, Dollar Shave Club, M&Ms | Playful display | Bright, unexpected colour combinations | Absurdist, funny situations | Witty, self-aware, irreverent, surprising |
| **Everyman** | Belonging, equality, community | IKEA, Target, eBay | Friendly rounded sans | Accessible mid-tones, nothing intimidating | Relatable real people, ordinary settings | Conversational, unpretentious, inclusive |
| **Magician** | Transformation, vision, wonder | Disney, Tesla, Dyson | Flowing, visionary | Deep purples, midnight blues, gold | Transformation before/after, awe-inspiring moments | Inspiring, visionary, possibility-focused |

### Application Rules

1. Select one primary archetype. The brand should naturally behave this way already.
2. A secondary archetype (if needed) adds texture — but the primary archetype governs every disputed design or voice decision.
3. Archetypes at opposite ends of the wheel conflict: Ruler and Jester cannot coexist as co-primaries without brand confusion.
4. Test the selection by reviewing the client's last 10 marketing pieces. Which archetype do they actually reflect? That is the brand's current archetype whether or not it was chosen deliberately.

## Framework 6: The 22 Immutable Laws — Agency Application

Selected laws most directly relevant to brand strategy work. The full 22 are documented in the source reference. These seven govern decisions that come up in every client engagement.

### Law 22: The Law of Singularity
**"The most important aspect of a brand is its single-mindedness."**

A brand must stand for one thing and only one thing. When a customer cannot answer "what does this brand stand for?" in a single word or phrase, the brand has lost its power.

**Agency application:** Every brand brief must name the one word or idea the brand owns. If the client insists on three things, the positioning work is not done.

---

### Law 2: The Law of Contraction
**"A brand becomes stronger when you narrow its focus."**

The reflex to add services, audiences, or capabilities to capture more revenue consistently weakens brand position. Category killers — brands that dominate their space — always started by narrowing, not expanding.

**Pattern:** Narrow focus → deep depth → purchase advantage → category dominance.

**Agency application:** Counsel clients to pick one thing they do better than anyone in their market. Own that. Then expand from a position of strength, not from uncertainty.

---

### Law 3: The Law of Publicity
**"The birth of a brand is achieved with publicity, not advertising."**

New brands are born through earned media, not paid media. Being first in a new category generates the coverage that no advertising budget can replicate. What others say about a brand is exponentially more powerful than what the brand says about itself.

**Agency application:** Develop brand strategy from a publicity point of view first. What is newsworthy, first, or genuinely remarkable about this brand? Build that into the positioning before planning a media budget.

---

### Law 5: The Law of the Word
**"A brand should strive to own a word in the mind of the consumer."**

The objective is to own a single, simple, everyday word in the prospect's mind that no competitor currently owns. Once a brand owns a word, competitors cannot use it effectively against them.

| Word Owned | Brand |
|-----------|-------|
| Safety | Volvo |
| Overnight | FedEx |
| Search | Google |
| Innovation | Apple |

**Agency application:** Ask clients: what single word should your brand own in your customers' minds? That word becomes the organising principle for all messaging.

---

### Law 17: The Law of Color
**"A brand should use a color that is the opposite of its major competitor's."**

Colour is a memory trigger that can become a brand asset as powerful as the name. The rule is competitive, not aesthetic: choose the colour that the dominant competitor does not use. This creates immediate visual differentiation.

**Agency application:** Colour selection begins with competitive colour analysis, not client preference. Document who owns what colour in the competitive landscape, then choose the opposite of the dominant player.

---

### Law 19: The Law of Consistency
**"A brand is not built overnight. Success is measured in decades, not years."**

Brands that consistently stand for the same thing for decades build compounding equity. Brands that keep repositioning in response to competitive pressure or trend cycles destroy equity every time they change.

**Agency application:** Once the brand position is established, protect it. The role of the brand brief is to make the position explicit and documented, so that future decisions do not erode it without awareness.

---

### Law 21: The Law of Mortality
**"No brand will live forever. Euthanasia is often the best solution."**

Some brands are so tied to a disappearing era, product form, or audience segment that they cannot be revitalised without spending more than a new brand launch would cost.

**Agency application:** Be honest about this. If revitalising a client's existing brand would require fighting years of accumulated negative or irrelevant associations, the correct recommendation may be a new brand rather than a rebrand. This counsel is one of the most valuable services an agency can provide.

## Framework 7: The 3 Cs Measurement Framework (Leland)

Use this to evaluate whether the brand strategy is working after implementation.

| Dimension | Measurement Question | Low Score Symptom |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Consistency** | Is the brand communicated the same way across all touchpoints? | Logo variants in use; different taglines in different channels; inconsistent voice across platforms |
| **Constancy** | Is the brand showing up regularly in the target audience's world? | Long gaps between brand touchpoints; social media presence is sporadic; no content calendar |
| **Congruency** | Does the external brand promise match the internal delivery reality? | The Blow Dryer Effect: luxury marketing, cheap experience |

**The Blow Dryer Effect warning:** This is named after the hotel that markets luxury but provides a cracked, cheap hairdryer in the room. The brand promise and the actual delivery must match. Any gap destroys trust faster than consistent good marketing can build it. Identify brand promise vs. delivery gaps during discovery and document them honestly in the brand brief.

## Framework 8: Brand Architecture Options

For clients with multiple products, services, or business units.

### Two Primary Strategies

| Strategy | Definition | When to Use | Examples |
|----------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Branded House** | Corporate name dominates; sub-products use corporate brand | When corporate brand has strong equity; consistent quality across all products | Google (Docs, Maps, Drive); Virgin (Atlantic, Money, Media) |
| **House of Brands** | Individual product brands dominate; corporate brand is invisible | When products serve very different audiences; corporate association would limit or confuse | Procter & Gamble (Tide, Pampers, Pantene) |

### Brand Hierarchy Levels (top to bottom)

1. Corporate brand
2. Family brand (a sub-group of related products under a shared name)
3. Individual brand (distinct name for a specific product or service)
4. Modifier (variant label: Premium, Lite, Pro, Express)

**Spandex Rule (Keller):** Just because you can stretch a brand into a new category does not mean you should. Before extending, ask: will this extension strengthen or dilute the parent brand's core associations?

**Sibling Strategy:** When market segmentation requires multiple distinct brands in the same category, build individual sibling brands — each with its own name, positioning, and identity — rather than sub-brands of the parent. Sub-brands dilute; siblings multiply coverage without erosion.
