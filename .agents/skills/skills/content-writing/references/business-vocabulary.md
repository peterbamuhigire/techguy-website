## 2. Six Rules of Formal vs Informal Writing

1. Understate feelings — "rather disappointed", "somewhat surprised" instead of emotional extremes
2. No exclamation marks in formal writing
3. Use the passive to emphasise the action when the person is less important ("Although we were offered an alternative dish, when it was delivered to the table it was cold again")
4. Avoid contractions (use "do not" not "don't")
5. Use formal equivalents of phrasal verbs and idioms
6. Use sentence inversion structures for formal tone: "Although [clause A], [clause B]"

## 5. Persuasive Business Writing

### The 4 Most Persuasive Words in English

1. **"You"** — Addresses the reader directly. Studies show it increases engagement and interest. Use "you" more than "we" on any page.
2. **Names** — People trust and engage more when they see or hear their own name. In email marketing and proposals, use the client's name.
3. **"Free"** — Triggers disproportionate response even when the value difference is minimal (Dan Ariely's research in *Predictably Irrational*).
4. **"Because"** — Triggers compliance even with weak justifications. Robert Cialdini's research: adding "because I'm in a rush" to a request raised compliance from 60% to 94%.

### Principles of Persuasive Writing

**Stick to facts, not opinions.** Facts convince; opinions invite pushback. "People love our product" = weak. "Our Net Promoter Score of 78 puts us in the top 10% of our industry" = persuasive.

**Be specific, not broad.** Vague claims destroy trust. Specific numbers, named clients, verifiable data — these build credibility.

**The Power of Three.** Offer three points, three benefits, or three reasons. One or two feels thin; four or more overwhelms. Three feels complete and convincing.

**Audience-first thinking.** Before every persuasive section, ask: "What does the reader already believe? What do they fear? What do they want?" Then address those things directly.

**Lead with what is, then what could be.** Proposals and sales copy: state current reality neutrally, then introduce the opportunity or problem, then give the solution.

### Expressing Opinions Formally (for copy, proposals, presentations)

| Function | Phrases |
|----------|---------|
| Personal stance | I hold the view that... / In my view... / It is probably true to say that... / There can be no doubt that... |
| Others' views | It has been suggested that... / There are those who believe that... / There are those who argue that... / It is often claimed that... |
| Consensus | It is widely believed that... / Few people would contest that... / Nobody would dispute the fact that... / It is generally agreed that... |
| Evidence-backed | Research suggests that... / All the evidence suggests that... / Recent evidence indicates that... |
| Concession | However / Nevertheless |

### Opening Phrases

| Context | Phrase |

| Response | I am writing in reply/response to... |

### Making Requests (Formal)

| Function | Phrase |
|----------|--------|
| Polite request | I would be grateful if... / I wonder if you could... / Could you...? |
| Information needed | Please find my CV enclosed (letter) / attached (email) |
| Available | I am available/able to come for interview at any time convenient to you |
| Reference | With regard to... / Regarding... / Concerning... / As you stated in your letter... |

### Complaints — Opening Lines

| Tone | Phrase |
|------|--------|
| Standard | I am writing to complain about... / I would like to express my dissatisfaction with... |
| Stronger | I wish to complain in the strongest terms about... |
| Concern | I am writing to express my concern about... |
| Error | I am writing to inform you of an apparent error in your records... |

### Complaints — Topic Phrases

- Poor standard of service / slow service
- Defective / faulty goods / defective item
- Badly scratched / dented wrapping / packaging
- I am asking for / I would like to request a replacement
- To claim / demand a refund
- I am returning [item] for correction of the fault / for inspection / repair / servicing
- The [item] may need replacing
- To restore an item to full working order

### Complaints — Closing Lines

- I do not usually complain, but, as a loyal customer, I hope you will be interested in my comments.
- We look forward to dealing with this matter without delay.
- I feel that your company should consider an appropriate refund.
- I would be grateful if you would send me a complete refund as soon as possible.
- I look forward to a prompt reply and hope that you will take into consideration...
- I look forward to hearing from you at your earliest convenience.
- I wish to draw your attention to...
- I suggest that immediate steps be taken.

### Letters of Reference — Useful Phrases

- I have known [person] for [period]
- I am confident that...
- I have no hesitation in recommending him/her...
- [Name] is sociable, reliable, self-confident, outgoing
- [Name] possesses a thorough grounding in...
- ...which will stand him/her in good stead
- As is shown by the fact that...
- I recommend [Name] without reservation — he/she would be an excellent asset to your company.
- Please do not hesitate to contact me if you have any questions.

### Closing Lines (General)

- If you require any further information, please do not hesitate to contact me.
- I look forward to hearing from you.
- Should you require any further information, please do not hesitate to contact me. *(formal)*
- Let me know if you need anything else. *(informal)*

### Formal Signing Conventions

- Name known → "Yours sincerely,"
- Name unknown (Dear Sir/Madam) → "Yours faithfully,"

## 7. Meeting and Conversation Phrases

### Opinion

- As I see it, ...

### Disagreeing

- I take your point but...

- That's not always the case.
- I beg to differ.
- Isn't it more a case of...?

### Asking for Opinion

- What's your take on...?
- Where do you stand on...?
- What are your thoughts on this?
- In my opinion [X], would you go along with that?

### Referring to Evidence

- Research suggests that...
- All the evidence suggests that...

### Making Conclusions/Decisions

- Let's get down to the nitty gritty.
- The bottom line is we have to choose one...
- It's a tough one, I'm torn between... and...
- Shall we go with...?

### Adding a New Point

- Another point I'd like to add about [X] is...
- It's also worth bearing in mind that...
- Coming back to what [Name] was saying about [X], I'd also like to point out that...
- I think it's important not to forget that...

### Personalising

- Speaking from personal experience, ...
- For me personally, ...
- This is a topic that is particularly close to my heart.
- My initial reaction is...

## 8. Discourse Markers for Cohesion

When writing website copy, blog posts, or proposals, use these to refer back to previous content and maintain logical flow.

**Nouns that work with "this" or "these" to refer back:**

analysis / approach / concept / context / data / definition / environment / evidence / factor / issue / problem / function / measure / method / period / policy / principle / procedure / process / theory / response / sector / structure / interpretation

**Example pattern:**
> "There is no doubt that cash flow is the most important challenge here, because it originates from poor invoicing systems. These factors [...] / This negative environment [...]"

**Pro tip:** In any piece of writing, underline the most important sentence in each paragraph. You should be able to read only those sentences and understand the entire argument. If you can't, rewrite.

## 12. Vocabulary for Describing Market Potential and Business Growth

These phrases appear in proposals, pitch decks, and about/services pages. They signal credibility without the vague hype of AI-generated copy.

**Market evidence phrases:**
- "spearheaded by an emerging middle class in [region]"
- "the [industry] industry is expected to grow to [figure] by [year]"
- "infrastructure is growing to meet market requirements"
- "numerous regions within range of our production bases"
- "high market potential as [evidence]"
- "customers have provided direct feedback requesting [solution]"

**Data-backed impact phrases:**
- "Studies have shown that [market] spend more on [category] than on [category]"
- "A recent study by [body] found that [X]% of [group] regularly [action]"
- "It is estimated that [outcome] will [change] to [figure] in [year]"

## 13. Formal Business Letter Structure

**Greeting:**
- Name unknown: Dear Sir/Madam,
- Name known: Dear Mr [Surname] / Dear Mrs [Surname] / Dear Ms [Surname]

**Reason for writing:**
- I am writing to... / I am writing with regard to... / I am writing on behalf of...

**Asking questions:**
- I would be grateful if... / I wonder if you could...? / Could you...?

**Referring to their letter:**
- As you stated in your letter... / Regarding... / Concerning... / With regard to...

**Closing:**
- If you require any further information, please do not hesitate to contact me.
- I look forward to hearing from you.

**Signing:**
- Dear + Name → Yours sincerely,
- Dear Sir/Madam → Yours faithfully,

**Checklist before sending:**
1. Formal register throughout
2. All necessary information included
3. All questions correctly formulated as indirect questions
4. Letter divided into paragraphs
5. Proofread for errors

## 14. Vocabulary Learning Principles (for content teams)

When building client-specific vocabulary, apply these principles from the book.

**Record sentences, never single words.** A word without context is almost useless. Record: word + definition + example sentence.

**Word families.** For every key term, know the noun, verb, adjective, and adverb forms. Example: tradition (noun) → traditional (adj) → traditionally (adv).

**Suffixes as signals:**
- -tion / -sion → noun (tradition, provision, decision)
- -al / -ical → adjective (traditional, practical)
- -ally / -ly → adverb (traditionally, practically)
- -ise / -ize → verb (organise, modernise)
- -ment → noun from verb (development, management, investment)
- -er / -or → person who does (manager, investor, provider)
- -ness → quality/state (effectiveness, awareness)
- -ity → quality/state (productivity, reliability)

**Learn clusters, not lists.** Learn all phrasal verbs with "put" together, or all verbs for financial change together, rather than random word lists.
