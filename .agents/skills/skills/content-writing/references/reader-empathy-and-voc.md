# Reader Empathy And Voice-Of-Customer

Use this reference when a page, article, email, or CTA feels generic, overpolished, or detached from how real buyers talk.

Primary sources behind this file:

- Ann Handley, *Everybody Writes*
- Joanna Wiebe, *Where Stellar Messages Come From*

## Core rule

Do not start from the brand's preferred wording.
Start from the reader's lived problem, desired outcome, and existing language.

## Required inputs before serious writing

Gather at least three of these before drafting major website copy:

- sales-call notes
- client onboarding answers
- proposal objections
- support tickets or WhatsApp chats
- testimonials and reviews
- competitor reviews, especially 3-star and 4-star reviews
- founder interview notes
- sector language pulled from the market itself

If none exist, state that the copy is inference-led and lower confidence.

## Pathological empathy checklist

Before writing, answer:

1. What is the reader trying to get done right now?
2. What are they worried will go wrong?
3. What words do they use for the problem?
4. What outcome would feel like relief?
5. What proof would make them believe us?
6. What would make the next step feel safe enough?

## Voice-of-customer mining workflow

1. Collect raw phrases from customers and prospects.
2. Split them into four buckets:
   - pains
   - desired outcomes
   - objections
   - proof language
3. Highlight phrases that repeat across multiple sources.
4. Prefer phrases with emotion, specificity, or stakes.
5. Turn those phrases into:
   - headline ingredients
   - subhead language
   - objection sections
   - CTA reassurance lines

## What to look for in raw language

- the job they want done
- the cost of delay
- the hidden fear
- the desired transformation
- the trigger event that made them look
- the phrase they use when describing a win

Examples:

- "We look expensive but don't look credible."
- "Referrals come in warm, then cool off on the site."
- "I need a site that lets me charge serious fees."
- "We do not have time to baby-sit developers."

Those are better than:

- "We need an innovative digital presence."

## Reader-swap pass

After drafting:

1. Replace every `we`-heavy sentence with the reader's reality.
2. Circle broad adjectives.
3. Ask "How would a customer say this?"
4. Rewrite until the line sounds like it belongs in a real conversation.

## Red flags

- the copy sounds like an award submission
- the brand is named before the reader is oriented
- every claim is positive and none are risky or specific
- objections are absent
- the copy uses category clichés instead of market language

## Output standard

Good writing in this repository should feel:

- precise
- grounded in real speech
- specific about stakes
- emotionally intelligent without sounding manipulative
- easy to scan, easy to trust, and easy to act on
