# Ethical Persuasion Gate

Premium conversion architecture is persuasive. It is also honest. This gate keeps the work on the right side of the line.

## Two operating principles

1. The persuasive technique must remain honest if the buyer learned how it worked. If the conversion mechanic depends on the buyer not knowing what is happening, it does not pass.
2. The persuasive technique must produce outcomes the buyer themselves would be glad they accepted, six months later. Premium agencies are paid for return business and referrals; manipulative conversions break both.

## Persuasion principles to use

- Reciprocity. The site gives genuine value (insight, tools, education) before asking for the lead. The exchange is visible and balanced.
- Commitment and consistency. Forms and decisions are sequenced so that small honest commitments naturally lead to larger ones, with the buyer always free to step back.
- Social proof. Real customers, real numbers, real dates. Numbers must be sourced; reviews must be real; logos must be authorised.
- Authority. Credentials, certifications, and standards that are real and verifiable. Authority is signalled, not invented.
- Liking. Real photos of real people; warmth without sycophancy.
- Scarcity. Genuine scarcity only — capacity, deadline, supply. Manufactured scarcity is forbidden.
- Unity. Belonging to a real community, sector, or cohort. Generic "join thousands" claims are forbidden.

## Persuasion techniques NOT permitted

- False scarcity ("only 3 left" when stock is unlimited).
- Fake countdown timers, fake "5 people viewing now" widgets, fake recent-purchase notifications.
- Pre-checked consent boxes for marketing or data sharing.
- Forced account creation as a price for completing checkout.
- Roach-motel patterns where signing up is one click and cancelling is hidden.
- Guilt-trip declines ("No thanks, I do not want to grow my business").
- Fake testimonials, fake reviews, fake before/after photos, AI-generated stock people presented as customers.
- Hidden fees or cost surprises in the final checkout step.
- Confirmshaming, dark patterns, drip pricing.
- Misrepresented certifications, bought logos, or vanity awards displayed as standards.

## Audit questions

For every conversion-driving element, answer:

- Is the claim true?
- Is the source verifiable?
- Is the urgency real?
- Is the consent informed?
- Is the comparison fair?
- Is the small print findable?

Any "no" requires a rewrite or removal before launch.

## High-stakes domains

In financial services, health, education, employment, and public-sector work, the gate tightens:

- All claims need a citation and a date.
- All risks must be disclosed in plain language at the point of decision.
- All "before/after" or "outcome" claims must be representative and dated.
- Regulator-specific disclosure rules must be applied; route to legal counsel where unsure.

## Behaviour change campaigns

When a website is part of a public-sector or social-marketing campaign:

- Use behaviour change frameworks that respect autonomy.
- Avoid fear-based messaging that produces avoidance instead of action.
- Pair every "what to do" with the practical means to do it.
- Measure behaviour change, not just clicks; otherwise the campaign is theatre.

## Diffusion and word-of-mouth

Premium sites earn growth through honest advocacy. Build:

- A clear referral path with a real value exchange.
- Shareable content with credit and attribution.
- Real reviews collected with consent and date.
- Press and partner mentions that link out generously.

A site that depends on tricking traffic to keep growing has a structural problem the marketing layer cannot fix.

## Hand-off

- Real proof gathering → `agency-positioning`, `agency-client-retention`.
- Honest copy → `content-writing`, `sales-copywriting`.
- Real measurement → `marketing-measurement-system`.
- Service recovery when something goes wrong → `customer-service-website-ops`.
