# Recovery and Retention Gestures

Service recovery is a tool, not a reflex. Use it deliberately.

## When recovery is warranted

- The agency missed a clearly stated commitment.
- The customer suffered tangible harm (lost time, lost revenue, reputational risk).
- A pattern of small failures eroded trust, even if no single failure was severe.

## When recovery is NOT warranted

- The customer is dissatisfied with a service the agency never agreed to provide. Route to scope conversation.
- The customer wants a permanent discount on the contract because of a one-time complaint. Route to renewal conversation.
- A loud customer is testing the agency's price discipline. Hold the line.

## Proportional response

Match the gesture to the harm. Cheap-and-quick beats expensive-and-late.

| Severity | Recovery gesture | Cost ceiling | Approval |
|---|---|---|---|
| Minor (small SLA miss, cosmetic issue) | Personal call from account lead + plain apology + structural fix | None | Account lead |
| Moderate (real time loss, single missed deadline) | Above + small credit (one retainer hour or equivalent) + new commitment | One day's retainer | Account lead |
| Significant (failed launch component, customer-visible defect) | Above + meaningful credit (one week's retainer or equivalent) + named owner change | Two weeks' retainer | Agency principal |
| Severe (data loss, prolonged outage, public reputational damage) | Above + retainer credit + offer to walk away if relationship cannot be repaired | Negotiated | Agency principal + leadership |

## Pattern for the gesture

- Acknowledge specifically.
- Name what the agency is doing differently going forward (the structural fix).
- Make the gesture, with no strings.
- Do not turn it into a sales moment in the same message.

## Retention conversation (churn risk)

Trigger when the customer signals departure (cancellation language, comparing alternatives, repeated escalations).

Pattern:

1. Acknowledge the signal openly. "I am hearing that you are considering whether to continue with us."
2. Ask the diagnostic question. "What has changed in the last 60 days that is making you reconsider?"
3. Listen without defending. Take notes.
4. State what the agency hears, in the customer's words.
5. Offer one of three responses, depending on cause:
   - If the cause is solvable inside scope: a written corrective plan with owner and dates.
   - If the cause is scope expansion: a clear scope upgrade conversation.
   - If the cause is misalignment: a clean exit, on the customer's terms, with a referral.

A retention conversation that ends in a clean exit is a win when the alternative is a slow, painful churn.

## Tracking

Log every recovery gesture and retention conversation:

- Date, customer, severity, gesture, cost, structural fix, outcome at 90 days.

Patterns emerge fast. Two clients receiving similar gestures for similar reasons signal a service-design failure, not bad luck.

## Anti-pattern: the apology economy

Avoid the cycle where:

- The agency apologises with a discount.
- The customer learns that complaining gets a discount.
- The discount becomes a baseline expectation.
- Margin erodes; the next breach feels less serious because trust is already low.

Break the cycle by:

- Replacing default discounts with structural fixes plus a small symbolic gesture.
- Investing the would-be discount in the SLA system that prevents the next breach.
- Walking away from chronic complainers whose contract is unprofitable. The relationship is the problem, not the service.
