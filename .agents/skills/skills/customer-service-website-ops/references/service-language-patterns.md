# Service Language Patterns

Plain, accountable language that protects premium reputation when something is wrong.

## The five-part pattern

For every service interaction:

1. Acknowledge the specific issue in the customer's words.
2. State what happened — honestly, without blame-shifting.
3. State the next step — concrete action.
4. Commit to a time — date and time, not "soon".
5. Name a person — who is doing it, who to escalate to.

## First acknowledgement (within SLA)

Before:

> Thank you for your message. We will get back to you as soon as possible.

After:

> Thank you for the message. I have read what you described — [specific issue]. I am the right person to handle this and will reply with next steps by [date and time]. If you need to escalate before then, my account lead is [name].

## Holding message (when work is in progress and SLA is approaching)

Before:

> Just an update — still working on this.

After:

> Quick update on [issue]. I have confirmed [what is true so far] and ruled out [what is not the cause]. The next step is [action] and I expect to have a result by [date and time]. I will write again then.

## Resolution message

Before:

> The issue has been fixed.

After:

> [Issue] has been resolved. The cause was [honest cause]. The fix was [what we did]. To prevent this from recurring, we have changed [structural change]. If you see this again, please reply to this thread and I will personally pick it up.

## Recovery message after a real breach

Use only when the agency missed a clear commitment.

> We missed our [response time / quality standard / launch date] on [specific item]. The reason is [honest reason — not a generic apology]. To make this right, we are doing [proportional gesture] and changing [structural change so it does not recur]. The new commitment is [date and time]. The owner from our side is [name].

Avoid:

- "We sincerely apologise for any inconvenience caused."
- "Our team is working hard to resolve this."
- "Thank you for your patience."

These are templates of avoidance. Use specific accountability instead.

## Escalation message (to senior agency staff)

> Escalating [issue] for [client]. Status: [what has been done]. Risk: [what is at stake — relationship, retainer, public reputation]. What I need from you: [specific decision or call]. Customer expects an update by [date and time].

## Refund or credit message

> After reviewing [interaction history], we agree that [specific commitment] was not kept. We are issuing [amount] as a [credit or refund] to [destination]. This will appear in [time]. We are also changing [structural change] so this will not happen again to you or to other clients.

Refunds without structural change are repeat-offence factories.

## Hostile interaction

For abusive or threatening language:

> I want to help with this and I will. I am not able to do that with the current language in your messages. Please reply with the facts of the situation and I will work the issue with you. If you would prefer a different person on our side, [escalation contact] is available.

Document the message. Brief leadership. Continue to honour the contract.

## Exit message (when the relationship ends)

> Thank you for working with us on [project]. Today is the last day of our retainer. Here is what is in your hands now: [list — repository, credentials, documentation, ongoing access]. Here is what we will do for the next 14 days as a courtesy: [bounded handover]. We wish you success.

Clean exits become future referrals more often than messy ones.

## Local and language considerations

- Use locally natural greetings and closings. Stiff English in a region where warmth is expected reads as cold.
- For multilingual clients, write the service message in the customer's primary language even if the original ticket was in English.
- Match formality to relationship, not to fear.
