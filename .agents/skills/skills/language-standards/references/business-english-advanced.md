## 1. Formal vs. Informal Register

### The Core Distinction
- Formal register is required in letters of recommendation, legal documents, official reports, senior-level emails, and professional presentations.
- Informal register is appropriate in peer-to-peer conversation, casual team chat, and relaxed networking.
- Knowing when to switch register is as important as knowing the vocabulary of each.

Full formal/informal vocabulary pairs: see `content-writing/references/business-vocabulary.md`.

## 4. Business Meeting and Discussion Phrases

### Expressing Views
- I would argue that...
- I firmly believe that...
- It seems to me that...
- I tend to think that...
- People argue that...
- Some people think that...
- Many people feel that...
- In my experience...
- It is undoubtedly / certainly true that...

### Refuting an Argument
- I am unconvinced that...
- I do not believe that...
- It is hard to accept that...
- It is unjustifiable to say that...
- There is little evidence to support that...
- This argument has certain specific logic, but...
- To a certain limited extent, there is some truth in this. However, the implication that... is an oversimplification.

### Providing Support / Evidence
- For example, / For instance, / Indeed, / In fact, / Of course,
- It can be generally observed that...
- Statistics demonstrate...
- If this is / were the case...
- In my experience...
- Let me illustrate...

### Defining and Explaining
- By this I mean...
- In other words...
- This is to say...
- To be more precise...
- Here I am referring to...

### Presenting Proposals and Solutions
- One approach would be...
- A second possibility would be to...
- This could involve...
- The most important point is that...
- Of crucial importance, in my opinion, is...
- This would be particularly beneficial.

### Transition Phrases — Use Sparingly
(High frequency use makes writing feel padded)
- Furthermore, / Moreover, / In addition, / Additionally,
- Nevertheless / Nonetheless,
- On the one / other hand,
- Consequently, / As a result,
- In contrast, / In comparison,

### Transition Phrases — Use Moderately
- While... / Although... / Even though...
- In spite of... / Despite the fact that...
- However, / Since, / Similarly, / Thus, / In turn,
- Meanwhile,

### Building Arguments — Useful Structures
- While I admit that... I would argue that...
- Admittedly, in some ways... Surely... Arguably...
- Either way... / In any case...
- There is no doubt that... However, it is possible to tackle this in a number of ways.
- On the other hand, it cannot be denied that...
- Opponents of... point out that... and argue that... Supporters of... argue that...

### Introducing a False Argument (to Demolish It)
- It could be argued that...
- Some people would argue that...
- There is also the idea implicit in the statement that...
- It is often suggested that...

### Demolishing a False Argument
- This is partly true, but...
- To a certain limited extent, there is some truth in this.
- However, the implication that... is an oversimplification.
- This argument has certain specific logic, but...
- On the contrary... / Obviously... / It is therefore quite wrong to suggest that...

### Conclusion and Summary Phrases
- In conclusion, / To conclude, / In the final analysis, / Ultimately,
- To sum up, / In summary,
- Overall, / On balance,

## 6. Presentation Skills and Memory Science

### The Multi-Modal Technique
- Combining text with visual representation improves audience memory beyond what a single channel achieves (Paivio and Csapo, 1973).
- Multi-modal stimuli activate the superior temporal sulcus and middle temporal gyrus, both responsible for multisensory processing.
- Practical application: combine images or short video clips with clearly expressed text and spoken narration. Shift between modes regularly.
- Even simple techniques — saying the names of objects aloud while viewing them — improve retention (Kirchhoff and Buckner, 2006).

### Chunking Information
- Working memory holds roughly 7 individual pieces of information at once (Pickering, 2006).
- Deliver information in small, digestible chunks. Do not overload.
- External representation (writing things down, bullet lists, diagrams) relieves working memory pressure and allows the audience to process faster — like closing background programs on a computer.

### Using Controlled Stress for Memory
- Low-level stress (a time limit on an activity, a practical simulation) activates memory for the material causing the stress.
- Timing matters: stress must occur simultaneously with the content being presented, not before or after (Joels et al., 2006).
- Psychological stress triggers noradrenaline, which mobilises the brain for action and enhances recall of the trigger event specifically.

### Practical Presentation Rules
- Two failures to prepare for: (1) charging into a question with no answer; (2) using content that is not your own without attribution.
- **Lesson 1 — Be prepared.** Know both your core message AND the questions your audience is likely to ask.
- **Lesson 2 — Take your time answering questions.** You are under no obligation to answer instantly. Ask for the question to be repeated. Pause and think. If you do not know, say so and promise to follow up. People respect honesty and courage.
- Always cite sources. Quoting another person's work with attribution is powerful. Parroting it without attribution destroys credibility.

### Eye Contact
- In Western professional contexts, neglecting eye contact implies lack of confidence or untrustworthiness.
- Eye contact that is too intense (unbroken staring) reads as threatening.
- Practical rule: gaze towards faces rather than making direct eye contact. ECU research found that audiences report equivalent connection and enjoyment when the speaker looks at their mouth versus their eyes.
- Eye contact is so cognitively demanding that even watching a face on video while making eye contact with the viewer reduces the viewer's ability to complete word tasks (Japanese researchers).

## 7. The Anti-Jargon Principle

### Why Jargon Damages Communication
- Business jargon creates the illusion of authority but destroys actual connection.
- Clichés and vague language are signals of not knowing what you are talking about.
- When professionals shift into "business mode", they often morph into what Roche calls "corpobots" — producing meaningless collections of clichés and empty statements.
- The result is boring, vague, disjointed communication that no one trusts.

### The Human-First Rule
- "We're living in a connection economy where people want to connect with real people."
- Good speaking and writing do not hard-sell. They use accessible language, focus on adding value, and solve real problems.
- Every listener or reader is human, whether they are the cleaner or the CEO of the company. Write and speak accordingly.

### Clarity = Confidence
- Jargon and unnecessary complexity make you appear nervous, incompetent, or pretentious — even when none of those is true.
- Less is more. Clarity shows confidence and respect for your audience.
- The Economist style guide: "The first requirement is that it should be readily understandable. Clarity of writing usually follows clarity of thought. Think what you want to say, then say it as simply as possible."
- Example rewrite: "Improving the efficacy of measurable learning outcomes" → "Improving learning efficiency."

### The Jargon Removal Exercise — Before and After
**Original (jargon-heavy):** "The staff at XYZ Media have been consulting successfully and fostering measurable, tailored solutions for their clients for years. The company's highly experienced media consultants understand and appreciate their clients' requirement to launch and implement cost-effective cutting-edge solutions strategically and systematically. Their sensitivity to the client's objectives, together with their profound expertise in marketing strategy and social media bring added-value and synergy to any project they embark upon."

**Rewritten (clear):** "We've been helping our clients make profit off advertising and social media for years. Our experienced specialists understand and appreciate your need to increase profits and brand recognition by implementing cost-effective solutions logically and systematically. Our sensitivity to your objectives, together with our expertise in marketing strategy and social media will help you achieve your objectives."

**What changed:**
1. Simplified — cut all words not adding to the message. Words like "synergy" removed entirely.
2. Reduced jargon — replaced "measurable, tailored solutions" with "profits" and "brand recognition".
3. Switched from third person to personal pronouns — "we", "our", "your".
4. Became more specific — named actual outcomes ("profits", "brand recognition") rather than abstractions.

### Clear Communication Checklist
- Keep sentences simple and direct.
- Remove unnecessary business jargon.
- Avoid the third person where possible — use "we" and "you".
- Be as specific as possible. Name actual outcomes, not vague deliverables.
- Avoid the passive voice. Active sentences are more alive and more interesting. ("Gary launched the project" not "The project was launched by Gary".)
- Remember that every reader or listener is a human being, regardless of their seniority.

## 8. Listener-Centred Communication

### The "What's In It For Them?" Principle
- Every listener asks "What's in it for me?" before deciding to pay attention.
- Communication is Brain-to-Brain marketing. In its truest form, it is two nervous systems exchanging information.
- Grab attention first, then deliver the message. In a world of constant distraction, attention must be earned and held.

### Barriers to Effective Communication
1. **Meaningless language** — hyperboles like "incredible" and "awesome" applied to trivial things dilute impact. If everything is incredible, nothing is.
2. **Ego / fear of being wrong** — distorts interpretation of incoming messages and produces defensive or dishonest responses.
3. **People-pleasing and approval-seeking** — comes across as insincere and manipulative. People do not respect a message from someone they do not respect.
4. **Silence in emotional situations** — staying silent to "not make things worse" causes more damage through misunderstanding. Acknowledge what is happening.

### Focus on Pain Points
- Think about what "personal hell" your audience is experiencing. Think about what misery your idea, product or service saves them from.
- Then show them how it feels to have that misery removed. Do not just list features and benefits — make them feel the relief.
- Example from Gousto UK: "With Gousto you don't need that epic shopping list, soul-crushing queue at the checkout or to take part in the heavyweight grocery bag Olympics. All you need is your weekly box." — empathy first, solution second, simple direct language throughout.

### Always Give Details
- Details separate a boring generic message from a lively, interesting one.
- Be specific wherever possible without being excessive. Say "Pizza Marinara" not "pizza". Say "Northern Pike" not "fish".
- Details are especially important in presentations, testimonials and case studies.

## 9. Apologising in Professional Contexts

### The Right Approach
- When you make a mistake, do not pretend it did not happen and do not argue. Both waste everyone's time and make you look unprofessional.
- Admit the mistake, take ownership, and work to fix it.
- Counter-intuitively, most colleagues, partners and clients will respect you more when you take ownership and focus on resolving the problem.
- In some cultures, admitting mistakes is taboo. In international business contexts, it is a professional strength.

### Language for Apologising
- Use: "I regret...", "I apologise...", "I'm sorry..."
- Give details about what happened, but stay professional — focus on facts, not excessive self-flagellation.
- Keep a formal tone if the relationship requires it, but remain relaxed and confident.

### Sample Professional Apology (phone/email)
"We would like to express regret over the inconvenience caused earlier this week. We will replace your product immediately, and you will receive it by Friday."

### Sample Apology Between Colleagues (warmer register)
"I'm terribly sorry that I missed our appointment early this morning. I inadvertently scheduled a breakfast meeting outside the office for the same time that we had arranged to meet and did not check my schedule before driving across town. Maybe we can get together for lunch later this week if you are available? I have reviewed your proposal for launching the new TIX project and think that it might strengthen our market position with this group. I would like to discuss your proposal in more depth with you. Please let me know if you are available for lunch on Wednesday or Thursday."

## 10. Summarising Data for Meetings, Presentations and Reports

### The Three-Part Report Structure
1. **Background** — brief summary of the project or situation.
2. **Findings** — as detailed as possible; factual; bullet points numbered in order of importance where helpful.
3. **Recommendations** — clear, concise conclusions based on the findings.

### The Planning Process
1. **Brainstorm** — brief notes only. No full sentences at this stage.
2. **Outline** — simple skeleton of Background / Findings / Recommendations.
3. **Rough draft** — write without worrying about perfection; correct later.
4. **Proofread** — check: spelling/punctuation/grammar; relevant data covered; irrelevant material cut; varied sentence structures; vocabulary appropriate to context.

### Summary Writing Transitions
- To introduce a finding: "According to the survey...", "It was found that..."
- To add information: "furthermore", "in addition", "additionally"
- To contrast: "however", "despite"
- To compare: "both", "like", "similarly"
- Cause and effect: "because", "as a result", "due to", "resulting in", "consequently"
- Summary close: "in conclusion", "in summary", "to sum up"

### Proofreading Checklist
- Check spelling, punctuation and grammar errors.
- Did I cover the most relevant data?
- Is there anything less relevant I can cut?
- Is information presented in a variety of sentence structures?
- Did I use conjunctions to combine related sentences, or are there many choppy short sentences?
- Is vocabulary appropriate for the topic and situation?
- Have I used words from the same word family (e.g., "promote" / "promotion") to alternate without repeating?

## 11. Persuasion Language

### The 4 Most Persuasive Words in English
1. **"You"** — immediately engages the reader or listener and signals relevance to them.
2. **[Person's name]** — people trust and engage more when they hear or read their own name. Use it deliberately.
3. **"Free"** — Dan Ariely's research (*Predictably Irrational*) shows that "free" triggers a near-irrational decision shift even when the value proposition is identical to a paid alternative.
4. **"Because"** — Robert Cialdini (*Influence*) demonstrates that the word "because" triggers compliance even when the reason given is weak. A queue-jumping request with "because I'm in a rush" succeeded 94% of the time vs. 60% without any reason.

### Persuasion Principles
- Address the listener's specific problem before offering the solution.
- "What's in it for them?" must be answered in the first sentence of any persuasive communication.
- Show how they will feel after the problem is resolved — do not just list features.
- Simple, direct, human language persuades more effectively than impressive-sounding language.

| Accede to | Initially reject and then agree to a request after negotiation |
| Account for | (1) Explain how/why something happened; (2) be a named portion of something; (3) keep records of resources; (4) take into consideration |

| Amount to | (1) Total / add up to; (2) be equivalent to |

| Break down | (1) Separate information into parts for easier analysis; (2) fail |

## 13. Critical Grammar Rules for Professional Writing (80-Rule Set)

These are the rules most commonly violated by ESL professionals, extracted from the full 80-rule set. Grouped by category.

### People, Everyone, Everything
- **"People" is always plural.** "People are..." not "People is..."
- **"Everything" is singular.** "Everything was broken." not "Everything were broken."
- Use **"everybody"** not "all" when no noun follows. "Everybody was quiet." not "All were quiet."

### Would Rather / Used To
- After "would rather": use infinitive without "to" or past tense. "I'd rather stay." not "I'd rather to stay." / "I'd rather you paid." not "I'd rather you pay."
- After "be/get used to": use -ing form. "I'm used to driving." not "I'm used to drive."

### Could vs. Managed To
- Do not use "could" for a single past achievement. "I managed to run 10 km." not "I could run 10 km yesterday."

### Indirect Questions
- Indirect questions use statement word order. "I asked where her parents were." not "I asked where were her parents."

### Mustn't vs. Don't Have To
- "Mustn't" = forbidden. "Don't have to" / "needn't" = not necessary. These mean different things.
- "You needn't pay now." not "You mustn't pay now" (which means payment is forbidden).

### Say vs. Tell
- "Say something to a person." — "She never says 'hello' to me." not "She never says me 'hello'."
- "Tell" requires a personal object. — "He told us..." not "He told that..."

### Each vs. Every
- Use "each" (not "every") for exactly two. "He was holding a glass in each hand."

### Because / Because of
- "Because" is a conjunction (followed by a clause): "because it rained."
- "Because of" is a preposition (followed by a noun): "because of the rain."
- Never use both together: "Because the train was late, so I missed it" is wrong.

### Lack
- No preposition after "lack." "The soup lacks salt." not "lacks of salt."

### News / Advice / Weather / Travel
- **News** — singular and uncountable. "All the news is bad." "I have some news." not "a news."
- **Advice** — uncountable. "Some advice." / "Three pieces of advice." not "three advices" or "an advice."
- **Weather** — uncountable. "We had terrible weather." not "a terrible weather."
- **Travel** — normally uncountable. "I like travel." "We went on a trip/journey." not "a travel."

### Articles — Common Errors
- After "a" vs "an": depends on pronunciation, not spelling. "A US citizen." / "An MP." / "An hour." / "A unicorn."
- Titles (President, Doctor, King) take no article. "President Obama visited..." not "The President Obama..."
- No article after "amount/number of": "the amount of money" not "the amount of the money."
- No article in common expressions: "at school", "in bed", "at home", "in hospital."

### Adjective Ordering
- Opinion adjectives before descriptive adjectives: "a lovely cool drink" not "a cool lovely drink."
- Descriptive adjectives before classifying adjectives: "an old political idea" not "a political old idea."
- Never use "too" before adjective + noun: "The problem was too difficult." OK. "It was a too difficult problem." Wrong.

### Comparatives and Superlatives
- Never use "more" and "-er" together: "colder" not "more colder."
- Use superlatives to compare within a group: "the most important" — never drop the noun. "The most important thing" not "the most important."

### Active vs. Passive
- Prefer active voice. "Gary launched the project." not "The project was launched by Gary."
- Use passive only when the doer is unknown or unimportant.

### Actually vs. Currently
- "Actually" = really / in fact (contrasting with a false impression). "She said she was 18, but actually she was 15."
- "Currently" = at the present time. "The number of users is currently rising."
- These are not interchangeable.

### Hear vs. Listen To
- "Hear" = something comes to your ears passively. "I suddenly heard a noise."
- "Listen to" = deliberate, active attention. "I listened to the music for hours."

### Eventually vs. Finally
- "Eventually" = after a long time, after some difficulty. "Eventually he finished his studies."
- NOT a synonym for "possibly" or "from time to time."

### Borrow From
- "I borrowed the money from my brother." not "I borrowed my brother the money."

### Crash Into
- "Granny crashed into a tree." not "crashed against a tree."

### Whose vs. Who's
- "Who's" = who is / who has. "Whose" = possessive. Keep these distinct in professional writing.

### Closed vs. Shut
- Before a noun, prefer "closed." "A closed mind." not "a shut mind."

### Come From (origin)
- Use present tense to state origin. "James comes from Liverpool." not "James came from Liverpool."

## 15. Key Principles for East African Business English

These insights are particularly relevant for professionals writing in English as a second or additional language in an East African context:

1. **Register awareness is the most important skill.** The ability to move fluently between formal legal register and warm human-centred language is what separates adequate communicators from outstanding ones.

2. **Avoid over-formality as a default.** ESL professionals often over-formalise to signal intelligence. This produces jargon-heavy, distancing language. Clear and direct is more authoritative than complex and obscure.

3. **Take time before answering questions.** Silence is not a sign of weakness in professional settings. A pause before answering signals careful thought. "Could you repeat the question please?" is entirely professional.

4. **Admit mistakes directly and immediately.** In cultures where admitting error is taboo, this may feel unnatural. In international business, it consistently produces more trust and respect than deflection or silence.

5. **Use personal pronouns in business writing.** "We" and "you" create connection. Third person ("the company", "the client") creates distance. Reserve third person for formal legal documents only.

6. **Specificity signals credibility.** Saying "profits" and "brand recognition" is more credible than "measurable, tailored solutions." Naming the actual outcome, the actual number, or the actual product builds trust.

7. **The word "because" is an authority multiplier.** Even a weak reason after "because" dramatically increases compliance and agreement. Use it deliberately in requests, proposals and follow-up messages.

8. **The word "you" and the person's name are the most engaging words in English.** Use both deliberately in emails and client-facing communication.
