# Authority Content Matrix

The content architecture every premium site ships with. Defines what goes on the site by page type, how much depth each type requires, and how the system connects into a compounding authority asset.

Grounded in Schwartz (*Product-Led SEO*: completeness over selective volume, authority earned through depth), Handley (*Everybody Writes*: editorial discipline, reader-centric content), Bly (landing-page structure, long-form sequencing), and the commercial logic in `agency-positioning/references/` (Authority tier sells the system, not the pages).

Companion files: `page-conversion-checklist.md` (this directory), `website-messaging-framework.md` (in `sales-copywriting/references/`), `premium-seo-package.md` (in `seo/references/`).

---

## 1. Content principles

1. **The site is an asset, not a brochure.** Every page either earns trust, earns rankings, or earns a lead. Pages that do none are cut.
2. **Cornerstone pages over volume.** Five excellent pages outperform fifty thin ones. Invest where it compounds.
3. **Original research beats commentary.** One piece of primary data — a benchmark, survey, case aggregation — outranks 50 summaries of someone else's work.
4. **Depth is a defensive moat.** Thin content is replicable; comprehensive content becomes the reference.
5. **Client-language, not agency-language.** All copy mined from customer interviews, reviews, and recorded sales calls.
6. **Completeness where programmatic applies.** If the offering has a template (every service × every city, every product variant, every condition), build all of it.
7. **No content without measurement.** Every page has a purpose and a way to tell if it is working.

## 2. The content architecture — what every Authority-tier site contains

| Layer | Pages | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Home + navigation** | Home, primary nav | Qualify, orient, route |
| **Services** | One per offering | Convert qualified traffic |
| **Segments / industries** | One per target segment (if multi-segment) | Qualify by reader context |
| **Proof** | Case studies, portfolio pieces, testimonials hub | Earn trust through specifics |
| **About / humanity** | About, team, philosophy | Earn trust through character |
| **Authority content** | Pillar articles, guides, research | Earn rankings, earn links, earn citations |
| **Blog / resources** | Ongoing editorial | Sustain authority, capture long-tail intent |
| **Lead magnets** | Guides, templates, diagnostics | Convert top-of-funnel to list |
| **Operations / trust** | Contact, pricing, policies, FAQ | Handle objections, legal protection |

Foundation tier ships the first three layers lite. Growth tier adds proof, about, and blog scaffolding. Authority ships the full matrix with editorial ops documented.

## 3. Cornerstone service pages

The money pages. One per service line. Each written as a complete, self-contained sales asset.

### Structure

1. Hero: headline, subhead, CTA, hero image
2. Audience qualifier: who this is for
3. Problem statement: the pain in reader's words
4. Offer summary: what you get (feature → benefit)
5. How it works: process, phases, timeline
6. Proof: 2–3 testimonials inline + 1 case excerpt
7. Comparison: how this differs from alternatives
8. Pricing: tier or range with logic
9. Objection handling: 3–5 specific questions
10. Guarantee: risk reversal
11. Final CTA

### Depth and length

- **Word count:** 1,200–2,500 words. Long enough to cover objections; short enough to read.
- **Sections:** 8–12 subheaded blocks.
- **Visuals:** 3–6 images (hero, process diagram, team photo, case shot, outcome chart).
- **Inline CTAs:** 2 minimum (mid-page + final).

### SEO anchoring

- Target one primary keyword per service page (commercial intent).
- 2–3 secondary keywords naturally distributed.
- H1 contains primary keyword; H2s distribute secondaries.
- Internal links from 3+ related articles and 2+ case studies.
- Schema: `Service` + `Offer` (optional) + `FAQPage` (for inline FAQ section).

## 4. Segment / industry pages (multi-segment firms)

When the firm serves multiple segments with different needs, create a page per segment. Each reads as if it were written only for that reader.

### Structure (same spine as service pages, adjusted)

1. Hero framed for the segment
2. "We work with {segment}" — specificity beats reassurance
3. Segment-specific problems and stakes
4. Services adapted to the segment
5. Segment-specific proof (case studies named to the segment)
6. Why this segment picked us (common decision rationale from real sales calls)
7. Segment CTA

### Anti-patterns

- Duplicating a generic services page with one word swapped. Readers notice.
- A "we serve all industries" hub. Commit to segments or don't claim them.
- Segment pages without segment-specific case studies — no proof = no trust.

## 5. Proof pages

### Case studies

**Structure:**

1. Client name, industry, scale (named with permission)
2. Challenge: before-state with specific numbers
3. Approach: what we did (3–6 bullets)
4. Outcome: after-state with specific numbers, year-over-year where possible
5. Direct client quote: named, 10+ words, specific
6. Visuals: before/after, dashboard, product shot, team
7. CTA to related service

**Depth:**

- 600–1,200 words.
- Minimum one specific number per case. "Leads up 4.8×" beats "significant lead increase."
- One case study per significant engagement. Target: 6–12 on an Authority-tier site at launch.

### Testimonials hub

A page exists but is not the primary home of testimonials. Testimonials live inline on relevant pages; the hub collects them for:

- Buyers doing deep due diligence
- SEO (Review schema, FAQ answers)
- Internal reference

Structure: video testimonials first (if available), named quote cards with headshots, client logo wall, by-segment filter.

### Portfolio / work pages

For visual or design-heavy services. Each entry minimum:

- Client name
- 3–6 images (hero + details)
- 1–2 paragraphs context
- Link to the full case study

## 6. About / humanity pages

The About page is not about the firm. See `website-messaging-framework.md` section 9 for the structure.

### Team page

- One named person per card: photo (real, not stock), role, 50–80 word bio, one concrete specific (what they studied, what they worked on before, what they're reading, what they wrote).
- Photographed in context where possible (team group shot + individual photos).
- Link to LinkedIn or equivalent for verification.

### Philosophy / principles page

Optional but valuable for Authority tier. States 5–9 principles the firm operates by, each with one sentence of context. Positions the firm's values as differentiators, not platitudes.

## 7. Authority content — the pillar system

The body of content that earns the firm rankings, links, and citations. This is where Authority-tier engagements justify the fee.

### Pillar pages

**What:** Comprehensive, ~2,000–4,000 word treatments of the head-term intent for each of the firm's top 3 topics.

**Characteristics:**

- Definitive. "The complete guide to X for Y." Not "5 things about X."
- Original framing. Even if the topic is well-trodden, the firm's angle must be specific.
- Data-rich. Charts, stats, tables. Numbers cited with sources.
- Named. Author's name, credentials, date published, date updated.
- Linked. Every cluster article links back; pillar links to every cluster.

**Deliverable:** One pillar piece per top topic (3 at Authority launch). Commissioned and written as part of the engagement.

### Cluster content

**What:** 5–15 supporting pieces per pillar, each targeting a specific long-tail question.

**Characteristics:**

- Specific intent. One question per piece.
- 800–1,500 words typical.
- Internally linked to the pillar and 2–4 sibling clusters.
- Published on an editorial schedule, not all at once.

**Deliverable:** Cluster scaffolding delivered; first 3–5 cluster pieces written as part of the engagement; remainder delivered through the retainer or client content team.

### Original research (the authority asset)

At Authority tier, one cornerstone research piece is produced per engagement. Options:

- **Industry benchmark.** Survey the firm's sector, publish the findings. (The classic Schwartz low-cost-high-impact move.)
- **Aggregate case analysis.** Pull data from the firm's own client work; publish anonymised patterns.
- **Diagnostic tool.** Interactive self-assessment that produces a personalised result.
- **Annual report.** Year-over-year trends on a defined metric the firm is uniquely positioned to measure.

The authority asset:

- Publishes under the firm's name.
- Has a dedicated landing page.
- Has a PDF or long-form download.
- Is submitted for trade press coverage.
- Anchors the firm's link-earning strategy for 12–24 months.

### Programmatic / scalable content (where applicable)

Schwartz's product-led SEO pattern. Only relevant where the firm's offering has an underlying template structure:

- Law firms: page per practice area × per jurisdiction
- Healthcare: page per condition × per treatment × per location
- Consulting: page per problem × per industry
- Education: page per course × per student profile

When it applies, build **completely**, not selectively. Every combination the firm can serve. Quality is preserved through templates, not compromised by volume.

When it does not apply, do not force it. A bespoke professional services firm should not generate 500 thin programmatic pages.

## 8. Blog / resources

The ongoing editorial stream. Feeds the cluster system, captures long-tail intent, provides retainer deliverables.

### Editorial rhythm

- **Foundation tier:** blog scaffolded, not populated. Client fills it post-launch.
- **Growth tier:** 2–3 launch articles to seed the blog; editorial calendar template provided.
- **Authority tier:** 6–12 launch articles; full editorial calendar for the first 12 months; content-ops playbook.

### Article types that earn

1. **Explainers.** "What is X and how does it work?" Head-term intent.
2. **How-tos.** "How to do X." Actionable, numbered, screenshot-heavy where applicable.
3. **Comparisons.** "X vs Y — which do you need?" High commercial intent.
4. **Case narratives.** Story-form case studies for top-of-funnel readers.
5. **Opinion.** Original point-of-view pieces that take a position. Link magnets.
6. **Research.** Original data. Highest-value, lowest-frequency.
7. **Process reveals.** "How we do X" — authority-building by transparency.

### Article types that don't earn

- **Generic listicles.** "10 tips for X" without specifics.
- **Derivative news.** Rehashing what every other outlet already covered.
- **Founder-ego posts.** Thought leadership that nobody asked for and nobody will cite.
- **AI-generated fill.** Reader and engine both detect thin AI content.

### Per-article structure

1. Title: passes 4 U's
2. Lede: reader-in-scene or specific hook
3. Table of contents (for 1,500+ word pieces)
4. Subhead every 2–3 paragraphs
5. One pull quote or callout per 500 words
6. One chart, image, or graphic minimum
7. Author byline with photo and bio
8. Published + updated dates
9. 2–4 internal links to related content
10. 1 mid-article CTA + 1 end-of-article CTA
11. Comments or response mechanism (optional)

See `website-messaging-framework.md` for copy rules.

## 9. Lead magnets

Content that converts top-of-funnel readers to the firm's list without requiring a fit-qualifying call.

### Tier-by-tier

- **Foundation:** newsletter signup only.
- **Growth:** 1 lead magnet (a guide, template, or checklist matched to the primary service).
- **Authority:** 2–3 lead magnets targeted at different funnel stages + 1 authority-asset download.

### Lead magnet types that work

1. **Diagnostic or self-assessment.** 5–10 questions, instant result, email for full report.
2. **Template or swipe file.** Something the reader will actually use ("Our RFP response template").
3. **Mini-course.** 5-email sequence with structured learning.
4. **Benchmark report.** The authority asset, gated.
5. **Checklist.** 1-page, highly specific, tactical.

### Lead magnet rules

- **Earn the email.** The magnet must be worth the exchange.
- **Deliver immediately.** Email arrives within 2 minutes.
- **Follow-up sequence.** 3–5 email sequence that provides further value before making any ask.
- **Track with events.** Signup is a conversion event; each follow-up email has open/click events.
- **Refresh quarterly.** Stale magnets lose conversion rate.

## 10. Operations / trust pages

Pages that exist to handle objections, legal protection, and operational necessity.

### Required

- **Contact.** See `page-conversion-checklist.md` section 3.
- **Pricing.** Where appropriate.
- **FAQ.** Limited to 5–10 leftover questions. See messaging framework.
- **Privacy Policy.** Per jurisdiction. Use `policy-pages/` skill.
- **Terms of Use.** Per jurisdiction. Use `policy-pages/` skill.
- **Cookie Policy.** Where the site uses cookies. Per jurisdiction.
- **Accessibility Statement.** For WCAG-conformant sites. Use `accessibility/` content if present.

### Recommended

- **Process page.** "How we work." Builds trust through transparency.
- **Careers / people.** If the firm hires, this page earns recruiting value.
- **Press.** Coverage, awards, mentions, speaking engagements.
- **Partnerships.** Alliances, affiliations, certifications — proof by association.

## 11. Content depth by tier

| Content type | Foundation | Growth | Authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Home | 1 page, written | 1 page, written | 1 page, written |
| Service pages | 1–2, full | 3–5, full | 5+, full |
| Segment pages | — | 1–2 if relevant | 3+ if multi-segment |
| Case studies | 0 (hub only) | 2–4 | 6–12 |
| About / team | Basic | Full | Full + philosophy |
| Pillar articles | — | 1 | 3 |
| Cluster articles | — | 0–2 | 8–15 launched, scaffolded to 30+ |
| Authority asset | — | — | 1 cornerstone research piece |
| Blog articles | Scaffolded | 2–3 launch | 6–12 launch |
| Lead magnets | 0 (list signup only) | 1 | 2–3 + authority asset |
| FAQ | Short | Full | Full + per-page |
| Trust / policy | Privacy + Terms | Privacy + Terms + Accessibility | Full suite |

**Total cornerstone pages** (excluding blog articles): Foundation ~8, Growth ~20, Authority ~40+.

**Total words delivered (launch)**: Foundation ~6k, Growth ~18k, Authority ~45k–80k.

## 12. Content ops (retainer territory)

The Authority tier delivers the editorial system — not unlimited monthly content.

Post-launch content ops:

- **Editorial calendar maintained.** Updated monthly with new topics from Search Console queries, client feedback, and sales-call patterns.
- **Content briefs.** Each article scoped before it is written: target keyword, intent, length, outline, internal links, CTA.
- **Writing cadence.** Matched to the retainer scope (typically 2–4 articles per month at Authority retainer).
- **Editorial review.** Every piece edited against the messaging framework and voice guide before publishing.
- **Performance review.** Quarterly: what ranked, what didn't, what to update.

## 13. Content governance

### Sources and attribution

- Every stat cited has a source and a date. Broken source links are broken content.
- Every quote is attributed or clearly labelled as paraphrased.
- Client stories are published with explicit permission logged in `project-log/decisions/`.

### Freshness

- Cornerstone pages reviewed every 6 months. Out-of-date numbers trigger updates.
- "Updated on {date}" visible on long-form content.
- Quarterly content audit: identify pages with declining traffic, declining conversions, or outdated information.

### Deletion

- Pages that cannot be made valuable are deleted, not kept as dead weight. 301 to the nearest live page.
- Schwartz's rule: crawl budget is finite; every thin page spent is a valuable page not crawled.

### Ownership

- Every cornerstone page has a named owner.
- Every article has a named author.
- Content without ownership becomes orphaned within 12 months.
