# Professional Writing Quality Gate

Use this reference for final copy QA on premium commercial writing.

## Source Boundary

This file is a distilled operating standard. It uses source books and existing repository extractions as high-level inspiration only. Do not copy source-book text, long paraphrases, examples, or quotes into client work or repository files.

## Premium Writing Standard

Premium commercial writing must be:

- specific enough that a buyer can see whether the offer fits
- useful before it asks for action
- restrained enough to be trusted by serious buyers
- clear enough to survive scanning on mobile
- concrete enough to support SEO, AI answers, and sales conversations
- polished enough that the website itself becomes proof of professional quality

## Pre-Draft Gate

Do not draft major copy until these are stable:

- Audience: the right-fit buyer or segment.
- Job: rank, educate, compare, qualify, sell, reassure, onboard, or retain.
- Offer: what is being advanced now.
- Proof burden: what must be believed before the buyer can act.
- Objection set: price, trust, risk, speed, fit, effort, switching cost, or authority.
- CTA: direct action plus lower-commitment action where needed.

If any item is unknown, state the assumption and write lower-confidence copy.

## Claim Discipline

For every important claim, choose one treatment:

- prove it with a named result, number, process detail, credential, case, demonstration, or authentic testimonial
- qualify it so it is accurate
- attribute it to a credible source
- move it to a hypothesis or positioning note
- remove it

Never use invented metrics, synthetic testimonials, fake scarcity, unsupported rankings, or vague superlatives.

## Premium Tone

Prefer:

- plain confidence
- sharp nouns and verbs
- business outcomes over decoration
- helpful specificity
- frank tradeoffs
- named next steps

Avoid:

- luxury adjectives used as proof
- generic innovation language
- theatrical urgency
- empty empathy
- category cliches
- AI-sounding filler

## Editorial Passes

Run these passes in order:

1. Strategy pass: does the copy advance the page's commercial job?
2. Buyer pass: does it use the reader's problem, language, stakes, and desired result?
3. Proof pass: are proof and reassurance close to the claim or CTA that creates doubt?
4. Structure pass: can a scanner understand the page from headings, lead sentences, bullets, and CTAs?
5. Voice pass: does it sound like a specific expert or brand, not a generic assistant?
6. Search pass: does it answer the real query and support entity clarity without keyword stuffing?
7. Implementation pass: are headings, CTAs, metadata notes, schema notes, and internal links ready for the next skill?

## Acceptance Checklist

- [ ] The right reader can identify the offer and fit quickly.
- [ ] The copy names the real problem, not only the service category.
- [ ] The strongest proof appears before or near the main CTA.
- [ ] The primary CTA names the action or reward.
- [ ] At least one objection is handled before the FAQ.
- [ ] The copy has one dominant idea per section.
- [ ] Claims are proved, qualified, attributed, or removed.
- [ ] The page has a clear internal-link or next-step path.
- [ ] The prose varies sentence and paragraph length naturally.
- [ ] The draft includes content gaps instead of hiding missing evidence.

## Red Flags

- It could fit any competitor.
- The headline is a slogan.
- The first paragraph describes the company before orienting the buyer.
- The page asks for a call before earning trust.
- Proof is quarantined at the bottom.
- The copy talks about "solutions" but never names the actual offer.
- The article answers a keyword but not a buyer question.
- The CTA says only `Submit`, `Learn more`, `Continue`, or `Get started`.
