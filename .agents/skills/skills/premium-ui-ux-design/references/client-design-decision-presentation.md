# Client Design Decision Presentation Playbook

Designs do not sell themselves. The presentation is part of the design. Use this playbook for any client-facing design review, decision meeting, or sign-off conversation.

## Core stance

A great designer is also a great communicator. The presentation defends decisions in business and user terms, not in taste. The client should leave the room able to defend the design to their own boss without the agency in the room.

## Pre-meeting

- Confirm the decision being made today. "We are choosing the homepage hero direction. We are not opening up brand colour today."
- Confirm the decider is in the room. Without the decider, this is a feedback session, not a decision meeting.
- Send the pre-read 24 hours in advance. Include only what is needed for today's decision.
- Prepare three options where appropriate. One option is rare, two collapse to a binary, four overwhelm.

## Meeting design

- 10 minutes context.
- 30 minutes work review.
- 15 minutes structured discussion.
- 5 minutes decision and next steps.

## Articulating the decision

For each design decision, present in this order:

1. The problem the decision solves (in user and business terms).
2. The constraints respected (brand, accessibility, performance, sector).
3. The options considered and rejected, briefly.
4. The recommendation and the rationale.
5. The trade-off accepted.

Trade-offs make the work credible. A decision that has no trade-off is either obvious or hiding something.

## Listening

- Take written notes. Two people on the agency side; one designer presents, one notes.
- Distinguish reaction from feedback. "I do not like it" is reaction. "I do not like it because [reason]" is feedback.
- Ask one diagnostic question on every reaction: "What would change if we kept it as is for two weeks?"
- Repeat back what you heard before responding.

## The right mindset

- Stakeholders are people. They have political constraints, fears, and preferences. Acknowledge them; do not roll your eyes.
- The room is not adversarial. The agency and the client both want the project to succeed.
- The designer's authority is earned by past evidence and current rationale, not by tone.
- Disagreement is data, not insult.

## Forming a response

When the client pushes back on a recommendation:

1. Acknowledge specifically. "You are right that the hero feels heavy on mobile."
2. Confirm or correct. Either the feedback is valid, partially valid, or based on a misread. Say which.
3. Offer a path forward. Either keep, change, or test.
4. Tie the path to the original problem.

If the client wants to change something the agency strongly recommends keeping, surface the trade-off explicitly and note the decision in writing.

## Choosing the message

For different stakeholders, use the right frame:

- Decision makers: business outcome, risk, time-to-value.
- Marketers: conversion, brand consistency, content adaptability.
- Engineers: performance, maintainability, edge cases.
- Operations: training cost, content production cost, ongoing service load.

Lead with the frame the most senior person needs.

## Locking in agreement

- End the meeting by reading back the decisions. "We agreed to A, B, C. We deferred D until next week. E is a parking item."
- Send the written summary within 24 hours.
- Mark every decision with an owner and a date.
- Any decision changed later requires a written amendment, not a hallway "actually...".

## Following up

- Within one week, share the artefact that reflects the decision.
- Within two weeks, share the next iteration with explicit links to which feedback shaped which change.
- If the client surfaces new concerns after sign-off, route via the change-request gate.

## Dealing with changes

- Late-stage change is normal. The cost of late change is also normal; surface it.
- Use a change log: what changed, why, who decided, what it cost.
- Protect the calendar. Late changes that break launch dates require trade-off conversations, not silent overtime.

## When the client is stuck

If a stakeholder consistently blocks the work without giving usable feedback:

- Surface a one-page "what is at stake" summary.
- Offer a 30-minute structured session focused only on the blocker.
- If the block continues, escalate inside the client's organisation. Premium agencies do not absorb organisational dysfunction silently.

## Anti-patterns

- "Trust me, I am the designer."
- Defending decisions with vocabulary the client does not share.
- Surrendering to the loudest voice in the room and silently resenting it later.
- Walking out without a written decision summary.
- Treating client pushback as betrayal. It is information.
