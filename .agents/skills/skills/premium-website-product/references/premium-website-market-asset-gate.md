# Premium Website Market-Asset Gate

Use this gate when a website must be a premium product, a marketing asset for the owner, and proof of the developer's world-class standard.

## 1. Commercial Position

- Name the buyer segment and the website's commercial job: leads, sales, bookings, investor trust, recruitment, partner confidence, retention, support reduction, or brand authority.
- State the owner-facing promise in one sentence: audience, outcome, proof, and next step.
- State the developer-facing promise in one sentence: the site demonstrates strategic thinking, premium UX, technical discipline, and measurable delivery quality.
- Identify the ordinary alternative the site must rise above: template brochure, slow WordPress build, generic agency site, social-media-only presence, marketplace listing, or unmeasured ad landing page.
- Reject briefs that cannot name a business outcome, decision maker, proof source, or launch measurement plan.

## 2. Website Service Packaging

- Package the work as a system, not pages: strategy, content, SEO, UX, design system, implementation, analytics, QA, launch, training, and optimisation.
- Define what is included, excluded, and optional. Premium service needs boundaries.
- Use a named process with visible milestones: discovery, market-message map, content architecture, design direction, build, QA, launch, and 30/60/90-day review.
- Make invisible expertise tangible with sample artefacts: strategy brief, page-goal matrix, wireframes, SEO map, schema plan, analytics event map, performance report, accessibility report, and handover pack.
- Preserve scarcity honestly: limited project slots, qualification, paid discovery, senior review, or application-only engagements only when operationally true.

## 3. World-Class Website Content

- Treat content as a sales and SEO system, not filler.
- Every page must answer: who this is for, what problem it solves, why this provider is credible, what proof supports it, what to do next, and what happens after action.
- Build from buyer language: search queries, sales objections, support questions, reviews, interviews, competitor gaps, and actual service terms.
- Use content layers:
  - Money pages: home, services/products, pricing or quote path, proof, contact/booking.
  - Authority pages: case studies, guides, comparisons, FAQs, diagnostics, resources.
  - Trust pages: about, team, credentials, process, policies, guarantees, locations.
  - Retention pages: onboarding, support, documentation, customer education, updates.
- Use specific CTAs matched to intent: book consultation, request quote, view packages, download guide, compare options, join waitlist, start checkout, call now.
- Every major claim needs proof nearby: metric, testimonial, case detail, credential, demo, screenshot, process artefact, review, guarantee, or transparent limitation.

## 4. SEO And Authority

- SEO starts with business strategy and search intent, not plugins.
- Build a topic and intent map before page production: commercial, transactional, local, comparison, informational, brand, and support intent.
- Map each target topic to one page, one primary CTA, one internal-link cluster, one proof burden, and one measurement event.
- Implement technical SEO as a floor: crawlable routes, metadata, schema, sitemaps, canonicals, hreflang where needed, image alt, OG assets, `llms.txt`, and fast pages.
- Create authority assets worth citing or sharing: original guide, diagnostic, calculator, checklist, benchmark, case study, pricing explainer, or buyer's guide.
- Plan digital PR and distribution where relevant: partner outreach, industry publications, founder posts, email launch, social proof posts, and referral landing pages.
- Track quality metrics: organic sessions, non-brand queries, ranking pages, qualified enquiries, conversion rate, lead quality, content-assisted opportunities, and revenue where available.

## 5. Premium UX And Simplicity

- Simplicity is premium when it removes effort without removing useful proof.
- Prioritise the user's next decision: understand, compare, trust, act, recover, or share.
- Remove or hide complexity before adding new components. Do not automate, animate, or decorate unclear journeys.
- Navigation must match buyer mental models, not internal departments.
- Forms must be short, grouped, clear about next steps, and reassuring about privacy, response time, eligibility, and payment where relevant.
- Mobile must feel designed first, not compressed after desktop.
- Error, empty, loading, success, privacy, and handoff states must feel as polished as the hero.

## 6. Premium Visual And Packaging Signals

- Packaging affects perceived value. A website's packaging is its first viewport, typography, spacing, imagery, microcopy, proof layout, motion restraint, and handover documents.
- Use real images, product views, people, locations, interface states, data, or proof whenever available.
- Make premium tactile through consistency: tokens, ratios, crop logic, icon style, page rhythm, table design, charts, and button behaviour.
- Avoid cheap signals: generic stock, vague gradients, cramped sections, over-rounded cards, random icons, fake scarcity, unsupported awards, and content that could fit any business.
- Show premium service evidence: clear process, named team role, review cadence, quality gates, support route, and measurable launch report.

## 7. Stack Philosophy

- Explain stack choices through client value:
  - Static-first or hybrid frontend for speed, security, SEO, and lower maintenance where content needs allow it.
  - CMS only when the client has a real publishing workflow.
  - Server-side systems only when login, payments, inventory, dashboards, or integrations require them.
  - Minimal JavaScript unless interaction, analytics, or application behaviour justifies it.
  - Self-hosted or first-party assets where performance, privacy, and control matter.
- Default technical commitments: responsive design, accessibility, performance budgets, schema, analytics, security headers, backups/rollback, version control, and documented handover.
- Do not choose a fashionable stack if it increases maintenance cost without improving business outcomes.

## 8. Launch And Growth System

- Launch is a campaign, not a file upload.
- Prepare prelaunch: redirect map, metadata, schema validation, Search Console/Bing, analytics, event tracking, CRM/email routing, social previews, content QA, and backup.
- Prepare launch distribution: owner announcement, email, social posts, partner/referral messages, PR pitch where relevant, Google Business updates, paid retargeting if economics support it.
- Prepare post-launch optimisation: first 30 days for bug and conversion friction, 60 days for SEO/indexing and content gaps, 90 days for CRO and pipeline review.
- Include a retainer recommendation when ongoing content, SEO, analytics, CRO, maintenance, or campaign support is needed.

## 9. Agency Proof And Premium Reputation

- Every delivered site should be able to become a case study unless confidentiality blocks it.
- Capture before/after evidence: old-site issues, strategy decisions, performance scores, accessibility checks, SEO coverage, conversion events, screenshots, and client results.
- Where agreed, include a restrained developer credit that points to a credible agency page, not a distracting sales pitch.
- Maintain an internal proof pack: standards met, screenshots, launch report, client testimonial request, and 30/60/90-day outcomes.
- Do not publish weak work. A public site that looks ordinary lowers the premium ceiling for future projects.

## Blocking Defects

- No clear business result for the owner.
- No content strategy, search intent, proof architecture, or conversion path.
- First viewport could belong to any competitor.
- Site is visually polished but slow, inaccessible, unmeasured, or hard to update.
- Stack choice is unexplained or mismatched to client capability.
- Website cannot credibly support the fee, case-study claim, or premium agency reputation.
