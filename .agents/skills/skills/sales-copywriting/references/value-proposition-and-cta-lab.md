# Value Proposition And CTA Lab

Use this reference when the work involves:

- homepage headline and subhead
- hero section rewrites
- pricing-page positioning
- landing-page CTA treatment
- button text
- testable copy variants

Primary sources:

- Joanna Wiebe, *The Great Value Proposition Test*
- Joanna Wiebe, *Buttons*
- Joanna Wiebe, *Headlines, Subheads & Value Propositions*

## Value proposition rule

State the value proposition fast enough that the right visitor can answer:

1. what this is
2. who it is for
3. why it is better or more desirable
4. what they should do next

If the visitor has to infer all four, the hero is underperforming.

## Value proposition standard

A strong value proposition should be:

- unique
- desirable
- specific
- succinct
- sticky

Do not force it to sound like a slogan.
Clarity beats elegance on first contact.

## Value proposition structure

Default formula:

`[desired outcome] for [specific audience] without [major friction], backed by [distinct mechanism or proof]`

Examples:

- `High-trust websites for East African firms that need premium clients, not more empty traffic.`
- `Launch pages and conversion copy for service businesses that need a booked pipeline before the campaign window closes.`

## Testable headline variants

Generate at least four variants:

1. direct value proposition
2. problem-led
3. audience-qualified
4. proof-backed

Do not stop at one polished option.

When testing, make sure at least one variant:

- says exactly what the offer is
- names the audience directly
- handles one major objection
- uses buyer language instead of internal positioning jargon

## CTA rule

A button is a commitment label, not a decoration.
Button copy should do at least two of these:

- name the reward
- lower anxiety
- clarify the next step
- match buyer readiness
- reinforce the value behind the click

Weak:

- Submit
- Learn more
- Click here
- Get started

Stronger:

- Book your diagnostic
- See plans and pricing
- Join the waitlist
- Get the launch brief
- Start the application

## Anxiety reducers near the CTA

Add a short reassurance line near important CTAs when anxiety is likely:

- `Takes 90 seconds.`
- `No payment required.`
- `We reply within 1 business day.`
- `Best fit for firms spending from UGX X upward.`

Closed-door rule:
if clicking the CTA feels like stepping into an unclear room, add microcopy that reveals what happens next.

Useful clarifiers:

- what they will see
- how long it takes
- whether payment is required
- who the offer is for
- when they will hear back

## CTA ladder by intent

- low intent: `Read the guide`, `See examples`, `Watch the walkthrough`
- medium intent: `Get the brief`, `Join the list`, `Check your fit`
- high intent: `Book your consult`, `See pricing`, `Start the project`

Do not ask for a high-intent action on a page that has only earned low trust.

Match the CTA to offer type:

- lead magnet: sell the free resource, not the whole company
- consultation: stress fit, outcomes, and what happens on the call
- direct purchase: stress the offer, price logic, guarantee, and next step
- destructive or high-risk action: state the consequence explicitly

## Testing discipline

Test value propositions against a downstream action, not against taste.

Good outcome metrics:

- clicks to pricing
- consult bookings
- completed lead forms
- qualified applications
- waitlist joins

## Guardrails

- Do not use fake urgency.
- Do not promise what the offer cannot deliver.
- Do not make the CTA louder than the value proposition.
- Do not make all buttons sound identical across the site.
- Do not use `Submit`, `Send`, `Continue`, or `Learn more` when a more specific outcome can be named.
