# Website Messaging Framework

The agency's default system for writing persuasive copy on any premium website. Every page gets a headline that works, a sub-headline that continues the work, proof that lands in the right place, objections handled where they arise, and a CTA that fits where the reader is in the journey.

Grounded in Bly (*The Copywriter's Handbook*), Sugarman (*The Adweek Copywriting Handbook*), Handley (*Everybody Writes*), and the conversion architecture rules from *Made to Sell*.

## 1. The agency's messaging principles

These are the rules. They are not situational.

1. **Copy is salesmanship, not decoration.** Every line earns its place by advancing the reader toward a decision. Award-worthy cleverness without a measurable outcome is a fail.
2. **Reader first, brand second, product third.** The headline belongs to the reader. The brand gets the subhead. The product gets body copy. Never reverse this order.
3. **Direct beats clever.** A useful promise written plainly outperforms a cute one. "Join the Adventure" is a confession of vagueness; "Book a 30-minute intake with our lead advisor" is an instruction.
4. **One idea per section, one CTA per section.** Readers scan. Give them exactly one thing to understand and exactly one thing to do, per section.
5. **Proof is interleaved, not quarantined.** Claims and the proof for those claims sit together on the page. Testimonials do not live on a "Testimonials" page and nowhere else.
6. **Objections are handled where they arise, not in an FAQ.** If a reader's objection pops up at the pricing section, it is answered at the pricing section. The FAQ handles only the leftovers.
7. **Write in the reader's voice, not the brand's.** Copy is mined from customer interviews, not from the founder's dictionary.
8. **Edit ruthlessly.** The second draft is where the agency earns its fee. The unedited first draft is where freelancers die.
9. **Sell on emotion, justify with logic.** Lead with the feeling, follow with the numbers. Sugarman's rule.
10. **Read it aloud before it ships.** If it sounds like a corporate press release when spoken, rewrite it.

## 2. Headline logic

Every page has one primary headline. Its jobs (all four, every time):

1. Get attention of the right reader.
2. Select the audience (repel wrong-fit, attract right-fit).
3. Communicate a benefit that is useful, ultra-specific, urgent, or unique.
4. Pull the reader into the sub-headline and the first body sentence.

### Eight primary headline types

Use these names in briefs, sales calls, and review meetings so the team can talk about headlines with shared vocabulary.

1. **Direct.** States the offer plainly. "Premium websites for high-trust firms, $12k and up." Works when the reader already understands the category.
2. **Indirect / curiosity.** Pays off in the subhead. "The website quietly costing you enterprise accounts." Works when attention is scarce.
3. **News.** Begins with *new*, *announcing*, *launched*, *introducing*. Works when novelty is real.
4. **How-to.** "How law firms in Kampala attract boardroom-level clients." Reliable; almost never bad.
5. **Question.** Must be a question the reader wants answered. "Is your current site selling you short?" Use sparingly.
6. **Command.** Verb-led. "Stop losing enterprise leads to a website that underwhelms them." Direct and active.
7. **Reason-why.** "7 reasons your law firm's website isn't converting." Pair with a scannable list.
8. **Testimonial.** Customer in quotes. "'We closed three enterprise accounts in the first month after the relaunch.'" High trust.

### The 4 U's rating test — run it on every headline

From Bly, adapted as the house rule:

- **Useful.** Benefit is explicit to the reader's self-interest.
- **Ultra-specific.** Names, numbers, places, outcomes. "£180,000 account" beats "big account."
- **Urgent.** Gives a reason to read now, not later.
- **Unique.** Says something new, or something familiar in a fresh way.

Rate each headline 1–4 per U. Ship only if **at least three of the four U's score 3 or 4**. Below that, rewrite.

### Practical headline rules

- **Use numerals, not spelled numbers.** "7 steps" beats "seven steps." Numerals catch the scanning eye.
- **Front-load the keyword** for SEO and mobile truncation.
- **Odd numbers outperform round ones.** 7, 13, 17, 29 outperform 5, 10, 20.
- **"The" beats "a/an"**; strong modifiers (*those, this, these*) beat weak ones (*some, a*).
- **Write seven headlines in different formats; pick the best.** Never the first one written.
- **Spend as much time on the headline as on the page.** Eight of ten readers read only the headline (Ogilvy). Invest there.

### Avoid

- "Welcome to {company}." A headline that says nothing.
- "Leading provider of…" — category-of-one clichés that every competitor also claims.
- "Join the Adventure," "Experience the Difference," "Elevate Your Brand." Indirect CTAs masquerading as headlines.
- Jargon stacks: *innovative, cutting-edge, best-in-class, synergistic, holistic, end-to-end.* These signal nothing and repel specific readers.

## 3. Sub-headline logic

The sub-headline has two jobs:

1. Deliver the promise the headline implies, in plain words.
2. Hand the reader to the first sentence of body copy.

Formulas that work:

- **Context + promise.** "You run a 12-partner firm in a city of referrals. We build the website that matches the reputation clients already have of you."
- **Problem + solution bridge.** "Your current site underwhelms referrals and converts nothing. Here is what to do about it."
- **Specific outcome + duration.** "In 14 weeks, we turn a weak website into a system that carries your reputation to enterprise buyers."
- **Qualifier + commitment.** "For trust-heavy firms only. We start at $12,000 and work in three tiers."

Length target: one to two lines at desktop (~15–25 words). Sub-headlines longer than two lines collapse the hero.

## 4. The first sentence rule (Sugarman's slippery slide)

The first sentence of any body paragraph has a single job: get the reader to read the second sentence. Not to pitch. Not to describe features. Not to introduce the company.

Rules:

- **Very short.** "It's simpler than you think." / "We only build websites for a specific type of firm." / "Here is what most agencies will not tell you."
- **Never start with a value proposition.** Save that for paragraph 2 or 3.
- **Open with a hook** — a question, a short statement, a contradiction, a number — that makes the next sentence inevitable.

### Seeds of curiosity (Sugarman)

Between paragraphs, use momentum cues to keep reading:

- "But there is more."
- "Here is the part that matters."
- "Let me explain."
- "We did not expect this either."
- "Now the numbers."

These bridge attention across scroll breaks. Use them sparingly — one or two per long page — not as filler.

## 5. Proof placement

Proof never lives alone. Every meaningful claim on a page carries proof within the same section or the one immediately following. Proof that sits on a dedicated testimonials page is proof nobody reads.

### Forms of proof, in priority order

1. **Specific-outcome testimonials.** Customer names the before-state, the action, the after-state, with numbers. "We were converting 2% of referrals. After the rebuild, 11%. Three enterprise accounts in the first quarter." Outranks every other form.
2. **Case studies with receipts.** Named client, defined challenge, methodology, measured outcome. Linked from the section making the related claim.
3. **Numbers and specifics.** "72,000 visits to the old site last year, 4 leads" — specificity is proof. Round numbers read as estimates; odd numbers read as measurements.
4. **Third-party endorsements.** Press, awards, certifications, affiliations ("Goldman Sachs 10kSB alumnus," peer-reviewed study citations).
5. **Named client logos.** Three to seven strong logos beat fifteen weak ones.
6. **Photographs of people, places, and results.** Faces of named clients, the team's photograph, the office building. Photography reads as proof; stock does not.
7. **Demonstrations.** Screenshots, short video walkthroughs, before-and-after comparisons.
8. **Guarantees and risk reversal.** Sugarman's "satisfaction conviction": a guarantee generous enough that the reader thinks *"they must really believe in this."* Doubles response in his tests.

### Placement rules

- **Above-the-fold hero: 1 proof signal** (logo row, award, "built by the team behind X").
- **Immediately after the primary promise: a concrete testimonial or outcome.** Never let a big claim sit unproven for a screen height.
- **Beside every benefit bullet: one specific.** A number, a customer quote fragment, a timeframe.
- **Pricing section: strongest objection-handling proof** — the case-study link, the guarantee, the FAQ addressing "is it worth it?"
- **CTA module: one final proof line above the button.** "27 premium websites shipped. 86% of clients active on the system after 3 years."

### Make testimonials real

- Use the customer's own words. Do not polish. If they offered to let you write it for them, politely refuse — their version will be more specific.
- Name, role, firm, photograph. Anonymous testimonials read as fabricated.
- Video where budget allows. Written + headshot where it does not.
- Pull a 10–20 word excerpt for scanning; link to the full 60–120 word version.

## 6. Objection handling

The reader's objections are a list. Each one appears at a predictable point in the journey. Handle each one in its place.

### Canonical objection map

| Objection | Where it appears | Where to handle it |
|---|---|---|
| "What is this?" | Above the fold | Headline + subhead |
| "Is this for me?" | Scroll screen 1 | Audience qualifier — "We work with…" |
| "Can I trust you?" | Scroll screen 1–2 | Social proof band, logos, founder photo |
| "Does it work?" | Scroll screen 2–3 | Case study excerpt or named outcome |
| "How is it different?" | Scroll screen 3–4 | Comparison section, USP restatement |
| "What exactly do I get?" | Scroll screen 4–5 | Scope / features / what's-included |
| "How does it work?" | Scroll screen 5–6 | Process walkthrough, phase timeline |
| "What does it cost?" | Scroll screen 6–7 | Pricing section, tier structure |
| "What if it goes wrong?" | Scroll screen 7–8 | Guarantee, risk reversal, FAQ |
| "Why now?" | CTA section | Urgency (real, not fake) |
| "What's the next step?" | CTA section | Explicit single CTA |

### Handling rules

1. **Raise objections yourself.** A hidden objection is more damaging than a surfaced one. Sugarman's Magic Baloney ad led with everything wrong with the product and sold over the objection.
2. **Address price before the reader has to ask.** Pricing opacity signals bad faith. State the floor ("from $12,000"); name a range.
3. **Never dangle a raised objection.** If you name a concern, resolve it in the same section.
4. **Use the FAQ only for leftovers.** Real objections handled on the page do not need FAQ duplication. The FAQ covers edge cases (payment methods, geographic coverage, timeline flexibility).
5. **Compare to the "do nothing" alternative.** Robinson's rule: the real competitor is the reader deciding not to act. Quantify the cost of the status quo.

## 7. CTA ladder

CTAs are a ladder, not a single button. Readers arrive at different rungs and want different next actions.

### The five-rung ladder

1. **Explore.** Low-commitment — read more, scroll further, see more work. Placement: soft in-page links.
2. **Learn.** Medium-low — read a guide, download a resource, watch a short video. Placement: content block CTAs, sidebar magnets.
3. **Subscribe.** Medium — join the list, subscribe to the newsletter, follow for updates. Placement: footer, article end, strategic interrupt.
4. **Self-qualify.** Medium-high — book a consult, complete a fit questionnaire, see if you qualify. Placement: home hero, services pages, pricing.
5. **Convert.** Highest — start the project, sign the contract, make the payment. Placement: proposal-linked pages, post-qualifying conversation flows.

Every page has one **primary CTA** (one rung) and zero or one **secondary CTA** (the rung immediately below). No page has more than two CTAs competing for the same reader attention.

### CTA copy rules

- **Verb first.** "Book a consult," "See the work," "Get the report." Not "Consultation Form" or "More Info."
- **Second person.** "Book your consult" beats "Book a consult" in most contexts; "Get your intake sent" beats "Submit."
- **Name the reward, not the action.** "Get the 14-week roadmap" beats "Submit form."
- **Specify what happens next.** "Book a 30-minute diagnostic call" beats "Contact us."
- **Anti-verbs banned:** *Submit, Send, Click here, Learn more* (unless paired with specifics).
- **Reduce risk at the button.** A one-liner above the button: "No credit card. 90 seconds." / "Discovery call — free, no obligation." / "27 firms have taken this step."

### CTA design rules (Made to Sell)

- Static buttons with direction cues can outperform animated buttons.
- One primary button per section.
- Button colour must contrast with the section background.
- Minimum tap target: 44×44px.
- On mobile, primary CTAs sticky at bottom where commerce or high-intent flows.

## 8. Service page structure

The default service-page message stack. Works for any service. Adapt section names to context.

1. **Hero** — headline (benefit) + subhead (qualifier + specifics) + primary CTA + hero image
2. **Who this is for** — audience qualifier section. Names the reader explicitly; repels wrong fit
3. **The problem we solve** — articulates the reader's pain in the reader's own words
4. **What you get** — scope, deliverables, what's included, what's excluded
5. **How it works** — process or phase walkthrough with timeline
6. **Proof in context** — 2–3 named testimonials with outcomes; 1 case study excerpt
7. **Why this way** — USP / methodology / what makes this different
8. **Pricing or fee structure** — honest, specific, with tier logic
9. **Objection handling** — "Common questions at this stage" (~5 items, not a full FAQ)
10. **Guarantee / risk reversal** — what happens if it does not work
11. **Final CTA** — one primary ask, one proof line above it

## 9. About page structure

The About page is not about the firm. It is about the reader, told through the firm's story.

1. **Hero** — one line that connects the firm's purpose to the reader's benefit. "We build websites for the firms our sector quietly depends on."
2. **Who we serve** — the audience qualifier. First, not last.
3. **Why we exist** — the problem we saw, the gap we fill. 2–3 short paragraphs.
4. **How we work** — principles, commitments, the practices that make the work different
5. **Who is here** — the team as named, photographed individuals. No stock, no anonymity.
6. **What we believe** — values stated as positions, not platitudes
7. **How we got here** — the firm's origin story (optional — only if genuinely interesting)
8. **Proof of standards** — awards, affiliations, published work, press
9. **Where we go from here** — a forward statement; what the firm is building toward
10. **CTA** — fit-qualifier ("See if we're right for you" → consult or questionnaire)

### About-page voice rules (Handley)

- Lead with customer benefit, not "founded in 2014."
- Include a photo of real people doing real things.
- One Easter egg — a quirky line, an unexpected story, a concrete human moment — that the reader remembers.
- Ban "best-in-class," "world-class," "industry-leading."
- Name customers in the narrative. "We learned to run discovery calls because Acme's founder told us our intake form was infuriating."

## 10. FAQ strategy

The FAQ is not a dumping ground for objections. It is a structured answer library for leftover questions the page-level objection handling did not cover.

Rules:

- **5–10 questions max.** A 30-question FAQ signals unresolved copy on the main page.
- **Phrase questions in the reader's voice.** "How much?" beats "What is the fee structure?"
- **Answers are short and direct.** 1–3 sentences. If an answer runs long, it belongs on a dedicated page linked from the FAQ.
- **No FAQ as hero content.** The FAQ is a secondary page element, never the backbone of the page.
- **Mirror real inquiry emails.** If the inbox repeatedly sees a question, it earns an FAQ slot. If it doesn't, it doesn't.

FAQ categories that work:

- **Scope and deliverables.** "Do you handle hosting?"
- **Timeline.** "How long does it take?"
- **Pricing.** "What's the cost?" / "Do you offer payment plans?"
- **Process.** "Who's involved on my side?"
- **Outcomes.** "What happens if it does not work?"
- **Boundaries.** "Do you do maintenance after launch?"

## 11. Features → benefits translation

The Features/Benefits Grid (Bly). Run this for every offering before writing a page.

| Feature (what it is) | Benefit (what the user gets) | Proof (how we know) |
|---|---|---|
| Astro static site | Loads in under 1 second, no maintenance burden | Lighthouse 98 on six recent launches |
| Positioning engagement phase | Website that matches the firm's actual market position | Named client testimonial |
| 30-page content architecture | Readers find the right page for their segment without bouncing | Session time up 2.4× on rebuilt Acme site |
| 90-day optimisation window | Launch is not the end; 3 iteration cycles post-launch | Standard practice, documented |

On the page, bullets render as **"Feature — benefit"** paired with dash or colon. Never list features without benefits. Never list benefits without a proof anchor on the same page.

### Feature-dump antidote (Handley's "So what? Because…")

For every line, repeat *"So what?"* until the benefit is unarguable.

- "Custom design."
- *So what?* → "Design built to your brand."
- *So what?* → "A site that looks like nobody else's."
- *So what?* → "Your referrals stop comparing you to generic competitors."

Stop when the chain lands on something the reader cares about in their own language.

## 12. Voice and tone

### Voice (fixed)

Four dimensions (Nielsen Norman), set in the brand voice guide once, never varied per page:

- **Serious ↔ Funny** — high-trust agency default: serious, with occasional dry warmth
- **Formal ↔ Casual** — default: formal but plainspoken; never corporate
- **Respectful ↔ Irreverent** — default: respectful with earned confidence
- **Matter-of-fact ↔ Enthusiastic** — default: matter-of-fact; let the work speak

### Tone (varies)

Voice does not change. Tone flexes for context:

- **Error state / apology:** warm, short, direct. No corporate euphemism.
- **Pricing page:** matter-of-fact, specific, unapologetic.
- **About page:** reflective, human, concrete.
- **Sales page:** confident, benefit-led, specific.
- **FAQ:** helpful, short, peer-to-peer.

### Banned vocabulary (house style)

- *deep-dive, leverage, synergy, ideate, bandwidth, ping, circle back, move the needle, boil the ocean, at the end of the day, best-in-class, cutting-edge, world-class, revolutionary, next-level, game-changing, end-to-end, holistic, seamless, turnkey, bespoke, curated, handcrafted, thought leadership, learnings, solvings, impactful*

Any word that appears on this list is a rewrite trigger, not a stylistic choice.

### Word-bloat swaps (Handley)

- *despite the fact that* → *although*
- *when it comes to* → *when* / *in*
- *in order to* → *to*
- *the majority of* → *most*
- *a number of* → *some*
- *moving forward* → *later* / *from now on*
- *there will be times when* → *sometimes*
- *continues to be* → *remains*

## 13. Editing discipline

The first draft is never the shipped draft. The agency's editing sequence:

1. **The Ugly First Draft (TUFD).** Write without stopping. Grammar, flow, precision — all irrelevant. Target 999 words for a long page.
2. **Walk away.** An hour minimum. Overnight preferred. Fresh eyes do the cutting.
3. **Chainsaw edit.** Cut the first paragraph (running starts). Remove Parrot Sentences (lines that restate their neighbour). Kill throat-clearing. Make every paragraph earn its place.
4. **Surgical edit.** Replace weak verbs with strong ones. Shorten sentences. Swap word-bloat for plain words. Remove -ly adverbs where a strong verb does the job. Kill Frankenwords.
5. **Reader-swap pass.** Re-read every line as "Petunia" — your single named reader. Anywhere the copy talks at Petunia or talks down to Petunia, rewrite.
6. **Voice pass.** Add a tonal beat. Remove one more cliché. Check the logo-mask test: cover the logo and read the page — would anyone recognise it, or does it sound like every competitor?
7. **Read aloud.** If a sentence snags the tongue, it snags the reader.
8. **Headline pass.** Write seven headlines. Run each against the 4 U's. Pick the best.
9. **Scanner pass.** Does the page read in 15 seconds for a scanner? Subheads every 2–3 paragraphs. Bullets for lists of 3+. Bold for the decision-relevant phrase.
10. **Ship.**

## 14. The Motivating Sequence (long pages)

For sales pages, landing pages, and long service pages, Bly's five-step sequence is the default spine:

1. **Get attention.** Headline + hero image + powerful subhead.
2. **Show a need.** Make the reader's problem explicit and urgent. Use their words.
3. **Satisfy the need.** Position the offering as the answer.
4. **Prove it.** Testimonials, case studies, numbers, demonstrations.
5. **Ask for action.** Specific, time-incentivised CTA with risk reversal.

All page-level objection handling and CTA ladder rules plug into this spine. The sequence does not change; the sections within each step flex by page length.
