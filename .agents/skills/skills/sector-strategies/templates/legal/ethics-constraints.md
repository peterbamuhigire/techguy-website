# Legal Sector Ethics & Bar Advertising Constraints

## Why Ethics Rules Matter for Law Firm Websites

Law firm websites are subject to state bar advertising rules in every jurisdiction where the firm practices. These rules vary significantly by state. Violations can result in disciplinary action. **The website developer is not responsible for compliance — the attorney is.**

**Build in a pre-launch ethics review step**: Before any law firm website goes live, the attorney must review all content for compliance with their jurisdiction's rules. A developer who writes "expert in personal injury law" may not know that claiming "expert" is prohibited in most jurisdictions.

---

## Universal Prohibitions (Apply in Most Jurisdictions)

### 1. No "Expert" or "Specialist" Claims

Unless the attorney is certified as a specialist by their state bar's approved certification program, do not use:
- "Expert in [practice area]"
- "Specialist in [practice area]"
- "Leading expert"
- "Nationally recognized expert"

**What to use instead**: "Experienced in," "focused practice in," "extensive background in," "dedicated to [practice area]"

### 2. No Outcome Guarantees

Never write or imply guaranteed results:
- "We win cases" (implies guarantee)
- "Get the maximum settlement" (guarantee)
- "We'll get you what you deserve" (guarantee if used as outcome promise)
- "100% success rate" (almost certainly false and a guarantee)

**What to use instead**: Case results with specific amounts are permitted in many jurisdictions when accompanied by a disclaimer that results vary.

**Standard disclaimer for case results**:
> "Past results do not guarantee a similar outcome. Every case is different."

### 3. No Misleading Comparisons

Cannot claim superiority over other attorneys without verifiable basis:
- "Best law firm in [city]" — unless supported by verified award
- "Top attorney" — unless from verifiable peer-review source
- "Better results than [competitor]"

**What to use instead**: Specific verifiable claims — "Rated AV Preeminent by Martindale-Hubbell," "Named Super Lawyers 2024"

### 4. Testimonial Rules

Most jurisdictions allow client testimonials with restrictions:
- Must not create unjustified expectations of similar results
- Many jurisdictions require a disclaimer near testimonials
- Some jurisdictions require testimonials be from actual clients (not composites)
- Some jurisdictions prohibit testimonials entirely — check local rules

**Standard disclaimer for testimonials**:
> "Client testimonials do not guarantee, warrant, or predict results in your case."

---

## Domain Name Ethics Rules

Some state bars regulate law firm domain names:
- Some jurisdictions require the domain name to include the name of at least one partner
- Domain names that are misleading or imply guaranteed results may be prohibited
- Examples of potentially impermissible domains:
  - `www.TheBestLawyerEver.com`
  - `www.WeWinBigEveryTime.com`
  - `www.GuaranteedResults.com`

**Always check**: State bar rules before finalizing the domain name.

---

## State-Specific Rule Variations

State bar advertising rules differ significantly. Always verify local rules for:

| Category | What Varies by State |
|----------|---------------------|
| Testimonials | Permitted, permitted with disclaimer, or prohibited |
| Case results | Permitted, permitted with disclaimer, or prohibited |
| "Best" / "Top" claims | Verifiable standard varies |
| Domain name content | Some states have explicit restrictions |
| Specialist/expert claims | Certification programs and restrictions vary |
| Paid client referrals | Many states prohibit fee-splitting with non-attorneys |
| Real-time solicitation | Cold calling / live chat with accident victims may be restricted |

**Resources for checking bar rules**:
- State bar's website → "advertising rules" or "marketing rules"
- ABA Center for Professional Responsibility: `americanbar.org/cprc`
- State bar ethics opinions (many bars publish advisory opinions on specific situations)

## Attorney-Client Relationship Disclaimers

### Required on Contact Forms

Every contact form must include a disclaimer that submission does not create an attorney-client relationship and that confidential information should not be included:

```
"Submitting this form does not create an attorney-client relationship.
Please do not include confidential information in your initial message."
```

This protects the firm from:
- Conflict of interest claims from prospective clients who disclosed opposing parties
- Inadvertent privilege claims
- Potential malpractice from advice given before formal retention

### Required in General Website Disclaimers (Footer)

```
"The information on this website is for general informational purposes only.
Nothing on this site should be taken as legal advice for any individual case
or situation. This information is not intended to create, and receipt or viewing
does not constitute, an attorney-client relationship."
```

## Content That Requires Extra Caution

### Specific Legal Advice

Website content must stay on the informational side of the line. Do not publish content that:
- Applies the law to a specific hypothetical fact pattern
- Tells readers what they should do in their specific situation
- Uses "you should" language about specific legal choices

**Informational** (acceptable): "In most personal injury cases, victims have two years from the date of injury to file a claim."

**Advice** (risky on public-facing web content): "You should file your claim immediately because your two-year window expires soon."

### Case Results and Settlement Amounts

When publishing case results:
- Include the required disclaimer about past results
- Be accurate — inflating amounts creates ethics exposure
- Some jurisdictions require disclosing that the result was atypical or exceptional
- Some jurisdictions prohibit publishing settlement amounts entirely

---

## The Developer's Role in Ethics Compliance

As a website builder working with a law firm client:

1. **Flag but don't enforce** — note potential issues in content; the attorney decides
2. **Build in the disclaimer by default** — always include attorney-client disclaimer on contact forms
3. **Avoid outcome language** in any copy you draft for the client
4. **Recommend pre-launch review** — remind the attorney that content needs bar rule compliance review before going live
5. **Include footer disclaimer** — make the general informational disclaimer standard in every law firm template

**What developers should NOT do**: Make final determinations about what does or doesn't comply with bar rules. This is the attorney's responsibility.

---

## Pre-Launch Ethics Checklist for Attorneys

Include this as a handoff checklist item with every law firm website:

- [ ] No "expert" or "specialist" claims without bar certification
- [ ] No outcome guarantees anywhere on the site
- [ ] Case results include appropriate disclaimer
- [ ] Testimonials include appropriate disclaimer (check local rules)
- [ ] Domain name complies with state bar rules
- [ ] Contact form includes attorney-client relationship disclaimer
- [ ] Footer includes general informational disclaimer
- [ ] No statements that create unjustified expectations of specific results
- [ ] Attorney credentials stated accurately
- [ ] All bar admissions listed accurately (no states listed where attorney is not admitted)
- [ ] Review any content written by developer about outcomes, results, or expertise
