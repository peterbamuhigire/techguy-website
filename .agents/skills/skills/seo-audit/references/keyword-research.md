# Keyword Research — Complete Reference Guide

The definitive keyword research reference for this skills library. Built from four source texts: *Keyword Intelligence* (Ron Jones), *Cutting Edge Keyword Research* (Alex Safie), *Keywords: A Vocabulary of Culture and Society* (Raymond Williams), and *Keywords in Creative Writing* (Starkey & Bishop). Applied to African ICT, software, and professional services markets.

---

## Part I: Foundations — What Keywords Really Are

### 1.1 The Definition That Matters

A keyword is not a word. It is a compressed expectation. When a person types "IT consultant Kampala" into a search engine, they have taken a complex mental image — a reliable, experienced professional who can solve their technology problems in their city — and condensed it into three words. The searcher did not choose those words randomly; they chose the words that, in their experience and cultural context, most accurately represent what they are looking for.

Ron Jones (Keyword Intelligence) defines a keyword as "a word or phrase that represents a larger concept or idea." The practical implication: every keyword you target is a hypothesis about how your buyers express their needs. Get the words wrong and you are invisible to them, no matter how good your service is.

Raymond Williams (*Keywords: A Vocabulary of Culture and Society*) spent 30 years studying how words carry meaning beyond their literal definition. His central insight: words belong to communities. The vocabulary of a Ugandan procurement officer searching for an ICT consultant is different from the vocabulary of a Nairobi startup founder, which is different again from a Dakar SME owner searching in French. Keywords are not neutral labels. They are cultural signals that mark belonging, intention, and expectation.

Wendy Bishop and David Starkey (*Keywords in Creative Writing*) apply this to professional vocabulary: expert writers choose words that signal membership in a community. The wrong vocabulary excludes the intended reader. The same principle governs keyword selection — if your page uses the vocabulary of the seller (your service description) rather than the vocabulary of the buyer (their problem description), the page will not rank and will not convert.

**Applied principle:** The primary criterion for any keyword is whether it matches the buyer's own vocabulary for their problem — not your vocabulary for your solution.

---

### 1.2 The Vocabulary Gap: Seller Vocabulary vs. Buyer Vocabulary

The most common keyword mistake is targeting the vocabulary you use to describe your services, rather than the vocabulary buyers use to describe their problems.

| Seller Vocabulary (Your Language) | Buyer Vocabulary (Their Language) |
|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| "ICT advisory services" | "IT help for my business" |
| "Digital transformation consulting" | "How to computerise my company" |
| "Software engineering solutions" | "Someone to build a system for me" |
| "Business process reengineering" | "Fix how my company operates" |
| "PropTech solutions" | "Software for managing my rental properties" |
| "ERP implementation" | "Connecting my accounts and inventory" |

Williams observed that vocabulary reflects power — the dominant group imposes its terminology and calls it "correct." In SEO, the "dominant group" is the searcher, not the seller. Google's algorithm is designed to surface the seller's vocabulary only when it matches the searcher's query. Closing the vocabulary gap is the primary task of keyword research.

**Practical method:** Before researching keywords with tools, interview five buyers (or read five real enquiry emails). Write down every word they use. These are your seed keywords.

## Part II: The Six Principles of Keyword Research (Jones)

### Principle 1: The Secret Formula — Right Keywords + Relevant Destination = Conversions

This is the most important principle. It has two components:

**Component A: Choose keywords that match searcher expectations.** When a searcher types "website developer Uganda", they have a specific mental image of what they will find. Your page must deliver on that expectation. If you target that keyword but your page talks about international web design philosophy rather than Ugandan clients and Uganda-specific pricing, the searcher will bounce. High bounce rates signal to Google that your page is not a good match for that keyword.

**Component B: Match your landing page content to the keyword.** The keyword, the page title, the H1, and the first paragraph must all align. This is called keyword relevance. A page about "website development" that ranks for "IT consultant Uganda" creates a mismatch — even if you get the click, you lose the visitor.

**Formula:** Right Keyword + Relevant Destination Content = Conversions

### Principle 2: Keyword Relevance Across All Touch Points

Every point at which the searcher encounters your content — the search result snippet, the page title, the H1, the opening paragraph, the call to action — must reinforce the same keyword theme. Relevance is the glue that keeps visitors on the page and leads them to a conversion.

This principle also applies in reverse: relevance signals to Google that your page is a genuine answer to the query. Pages that are genuinely relevant on every level — title, meta, headings, body copy, internal links — rank higher and hold their rankings longer.

### Principle 3: Keywords and the Buying Cycle

Searchers use different keywords at different stages of their decision-making process. A person who types "what is an ERP system" is in early research mode — not ready to hire. A person who types "ERP implementation Uganda" is much closer to a buying decision. The keyword reveals where the buyer is in their journey.

Match each keyword to a specific buying cycle stage. Then ensure the content on the page matches that stage. Do not put a consultation CTA on an early-stage informational article; put it on a buying-stage service page.

### Principle 4: Broad Keywords vs. Specific Keywords — Strategic Choice

Broad keywords (head terms) have high search volume and high competition. They attract visitors at the beginning of the buying cycle. Specific keywords (long-tail) have lower volume but higher conversion rates because the searcher knows exactly what they want.

Three keyword strategies:
- **Branding strategy:** broad keywords → many impressions, low conversion rate
- **Conversion strategy:** specific keywords → fewer impressions, high conversion rate
- **Hybrid strategy:** broad + specific → brand awareness AND conversions

For new sites with low domain authority, lead with specific keywords. Build rankings on long-tail terms, then use that authority to compete on broader terms over time.

### Principle 5: The Long Tail

Popularised by Chris Anderson (WIRED magazine), the long tail describes how the majority of search volume comes not from the few high-volume head terms, but from the enormous quantity of specific, lower-volume queries. Each individual long-tail query has low volume, but in aggregate they generate more traffic than head terms.

**Long-tail keyword benefits:**
- Easier to rank — less competition
- Lower cost per click in paid search
- Higher conversion rates — searcher knows what they want
- More relevant — allows precise landing page matching
- Builds topical authority when you cover many related long-tail terms

**For African markets specifically:** head terms like "IT consultant" are dominated by global results. Long-tail geo-modified terms like "IT consultant Kampala small business" have almost no competition and rank quickly.

### Principle 6: Keyword Interpretation — Reading Your Analytics

Once you are ranking and driving traffic, study which keywords are actually bringing visitors and whether those visitors convert. Analytics reveal:

- Which keywords drive qualified traffic (visitors who stay and enquire)
- Which keywords drive unqualified traffic (visitors who bounce immediately)
- Which keywords reveal demand for products/services you have not yet built
- Which unexpected keyword phrases real buyers use (these become new targets)

This feedback loop makes keyword research continuous, not a one-time task. Review keyword performance quarterly. Update your targets based on what the data reveals.

## Part III: The Keyword Research Process

### 3.1 Stage Overview (Jones Framework)

The keyword research process has five stages:

**Stage 1: Brainstorm and Seed Keywords**
Start from the inside: list every service you offer, every problem you solve, every client type you serve. Add geographic modifiers. This produces your seed list — the raw material for research.

Sources for seed keywords:
- Your own service descriptions (then translate to buyer language)
- Customer enquiry emails and calls — verbatim phrases clients use
- Competitor websites — what terms do they target in their page titles?
- Industry associations and publications — what vocabulary do they use?
- Social media conversations — how do buyers describe their challenges in posts?
- Google Suggest — type a seed term and record all autocomplete suggestions
- People Also Ask boxes — question-based long-tail keywords
- Related Searches (bottom of Google results page) — adjacent buyer vocabulary

**Stage 2: Expand and Categorise**
Using keyword tools, expand each seed keyword into a full cluster. Group related keywords by theme, intent, and geography. Each cluster typically corresponds to one page on the site.

**Stage 3: Score and Prioritise**
Apply the seven-step refinement process (see Part IV). Score each keyword for relevance, specificity, competition, and popularity. Prioritise keywords with high relevance + low competition.

**Stage 4: Assign to Pages**
Map each keyword to a specific page. One primary keyword per page. Multiple secondary and long-tail keywords per page. No two pages should compete for the same primary keyword (cannibalisation).

**Stage 5: Measure and Iterate**
Track rankings, traffic, and conversions by keyword. Adjust quarterly based on performance data.

---

### 3.2 The Seven-Step Keyword Refinement Process (Jones, Chapter 5)

**Step 1: Categorisation and Audience Segmentation**
Group keywords by the audience segment they serve. A keyword like "ERP for manufacturing Uganda" targets a different buyer segment than "business plan writer Kampala". Each segment gets its own keyword cluster and, ideally, its own content.

**Step 2: Scoring for Relevance**
Score each keyword 1-5 for how closely it relates to your core services. A keyword must be genuinely relevant — your page must be able to deliver what the keyword promises. Discard keywords where you cannot provide a credible answer or service.

**Step 3: Scoring for Specificity**
Score 1-5 for how specific the keyword is. More specific = higher commercial intent. "ICT consulting Uganda small business ERP implementation" scores 5 on specificity. "IT" scores 1.

**Step 4: Scoring for Competition**
Score 1-5 (5 = low competition, easiest to rank). Check:
- Domain authority of pages currently ranking for this keyword
- On-page optimisation quality of those pages
- Number of real competing pages (use the &start=990 trick to get true count)
- Presence of large authoritative sites in results (Wikipedia, Forbes, etc.)

**Step 5: Scoring for Popularity**
Score 1-5 for estimated search volume. For African markets, use relative volume rather than absolute numbers — 100 monthly searches in Uganda for a high-intent term is more valuable than 10,000 searches in the UK for an irrelevant term.

**Step 6: Overall Scoring and Prioritisation**
Combine scores. Standard formula: (Relevance × 2) + Specificity + (Competition × 1.5) + Popularity = Priority Score. Weight relevance and competition most heavily. A keyword that is highly relevant and has weak competition is your ideal target even if it has lower search volume.

**Step 7: Keyword Interpretation**
For each final keyword, interpret what the searcher is really asking. What stage of the buying cycle are they in? What content would genuinely answer their need? What would make them convert? This interpretation guides content creation.

## Part IV: The Safie Method — Golden Keyword Criteria

Alex Safie (*Cutting Edge Keyword Research*) provides a direct, no-nonsense framework. A keyword is only worth targeting if it has ALL FOUR of the following:

### Criterion 1: Adequate Search Volume
A keyword must have enough real search traffic to justify creating content for it. Safie targets 500-3,000 exact monthly searches for affiliate sites. For professional services in African markets, adjust downward: 50-500 monthly searches for a high-intent geo-modified term is excellent.

**For African markets:** Standard keyword tools undercount African search volume significantly. Google does not provide accurate volume data for Uganda, Ghana, or other lower-traffic markets. Use relative volume trends rather than absolute numbers. A keyword with "low" reported volume in a tool may still generate valuable leads in practice.

### Criterion 2: Commercial Intent
A keyword must attract people who are ready or nearly ready to spend money. Commercial intent signals:
- Words: "hire", "cost", "price", "services", "company", "agency", "consultant", "developer"
- Behaviour: multiple advertisers bidding on the keyword (high CPC = money being made)
- Context: specific product/service name + location + modifier

**How to check commercial intent:**
1. Search the term and count the number of Google Ads appearing. More ads = higher commercial intent.
2. Check the estimated CPC in Google's Keyword Planner. Higher CPC = more advertisers = more money in the market.
3. Check if the term appears naturally in enquiry messages from potential clients.

### Criterion 3: Stable or Rising Trend
A keyword declining in search trend is a declining market signal. Use Google Trends to check the trajectory. For ICT and software services in Africa, trends are broadly rising — but specific terms (e.g., terms referencing specific software that is being phased out) may be declining.

Target keywords with flat or rising trends. Never invest content effort into a declining keyword.

### Criterion 4: Weak Competition
Weak competition means the pages currently ranking for your target keyword have exploitable weaknesses:

**Assessing competition:**
1. **Real competing pages count:** Add `&start=990` to a Google search URL to see the true number of relevant indexed results (much lower than the stated total). Under 400 real competing pages is favourable.
2. **Average Page Rank of top 10:** If the average PR of the first-page sites is 0-3, you can outrank them with good on-page SEO.
3. **On-page SEO quality:** Are the ranking pages well-optimised? Check: keyword in title, URL, H1, description, first sentence. Poorly optimised pages with high rankings are vulnerable.
4. **Backlink quality and quantity:** Analyse the top 5 results. Few backlinks from low-authority sites = beatable.
5. **Bounce rate signals:** If competing pages have poor, thin content, Google will reward a genuinely better page.

**The Safie quick-check sequence:**
1. Does it have adequate volume? (If no, discard)
2. Does it have commercial intent? (If no, discard or deprioritise)
3. Is the trend stable/rising? (If declining, discard)
4. Is competition weak? (If too strong, deprioritise or find longer-tail variant)
5. All four present → Golden keyword. Target it.

## Part V: Keyword Psychology — The Williams-Starkey Contribution

### 5.1 Keywords as Cultural Vocabulary (Williams)

Raymond Williams spent 30 years studying how keywords carry meaning beyond their literal definition. He found that "the significance [of a word] is in the selection" — choosing a word is never neutral; it signals which community you belong to, which values you hold, and what you expect of your audience.

Applied to SEO, this means:
- A buyer who searches "IT consultant" may have a different expectation than one who searches "ICT consultant" — the latter is more common in East African formal/government contexts
- A buyer who searches "website designer" may be looking for aesthetic work; one who searches "web developer" may want technical functionality
- A buyer who searches "logiciel sur mesure" is signalling a sophisticated understanding of custom software — they want to be spoken to as a peer
- A buyer who searches "how do I get a website for my business in Uganda" is a first-time buyer who needs guidance, not technical jargon

**The Williams principle:** Before selecting keywords, analyse what each candidate keyword says about the person who would use it. What is their sophistication level? Their urgency? Their context? Match both the keyword AND the vocabulary of the content to the buyer's actual word-world.

### 5.2 Jargon vs. Professional Vocabulary (Starkey/Bishop)

Bishop and Starkey distinguish between jargon (insider vocabulary that excludes newcomers) and professional vocabulary (that includes the right people while excluding wrong-fit buyers).

**Jargon excludes:** "Microservices architecture leveraging containerised deployment pipelines" — a buyer who does not already know this language will not search for it and will not understand it if they land on it.

**Professional vocabulary includes:** "Custom software built specifically for your business" — any business owner understands this without prior technical education.

**Applied principle:** For awareness-stage keywords (early funnel), use plain buyer language. For decision-stage keywords (late funnel), technical vocabulary is appropriate — by that stage the buyer has learned the vocabulary and is using it to filter providers.

**Example:**
- Awareness: "how to improve my business with technology Uganda" → plain language content
- Consideration: "ERP system vs custom software Africa" → intermediate vocabulary
- Decision: "ERP implementation company Uganda SAGE integration" → technical vocabulary is now correct

### 5.3 Vocabulary and Trust Signals

Williams observed that the "surpassing confidence of any particular use of a word, within a group or within a period, is very difficult to question." Applied to professional services: when your content uses the exact vocabulary your target buyer uses internally, you are communicating professional membership. This creates an immediate trust signal — "this person understands my world."

When Peter's website uses the term "ICT consulting" (the standard East African public sector and NGO term) rather than just "IT consulting", he signals membership in that professional community. When the French pages use "système d'information" rather than just "système informatique", he signals familiarity with Francophone African business vocabulary.

**Vocabulary selection is itself a trust-building exercise.** Get the vocabulary right and buyers feel they have found someone who understands their context. Get it wrong and they sense a mismatch — even if they cannot articulate why.

## Part VI: Search Funnel Mapping (Jones, Chapter 3 & 7)

### 6.1 The Search Funnel — Five Stages

The search funnel maps keyword types to the buyer's journey from no awareness to purchase. Each stage requires different content, different keywords, and different conversion expectations.

| Funnel Stage | Buyer State | Keyword Pattern | Intent | Content Type |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|--------|--------------|
| **Stage 1: Awareness** | Does not know they have a problem, or knows the problem but not the solution category | Industry trends, technology names, broad problem terms | Informational | Blog posts, thought leadership, explainers |
| **Stage 2: Research** | Knows the problem, actively seeking information | "What is...", "How does...", "Why do...", comparison terms | Informational | Guides, comparisons, FAQs |
| **Stage 3: Consideration** | Evaluating solution options | "Best...", "[solution] vs [solution]", "How to choose...", "Cost of..." | Commercial | Service pages, case studies, pricing guides |
| **Stage 4: Intent** | Selecting a provider | "[service] + [city]", "[service] company", "hire [role]" | Transactional | Landing pages, service detail pages |
| **Stage 5: Action** | Ready to contact | "[service] quote", "contact [company]", brand name | Transactional | Contact page, quote form, pricing page |

### 6.2 African Market Nuances

The standard search funnel model assumes linear digital research. East African buyer behaviour includes important departures from this model:

**WhatsApp referrals start searches:** A buyer receives a WhatsApp recommendation — "try Peter Bamuhigire, he's good" — and their first action is a Google search for Peter's name or company. This makes branded/navigational keywords and a strong Google Business Profile critical.

**Mobile-first, often mobile-only:** Most East African business buyers research on smartphones. Voice search usage is higher than in Western markets. Long queries typed on mobile keyboards are truncated; queries asked to voice assistants are full sentences.

**Word-of-mouth validates online:** Unlike Western buyers who may make a decision entirely from online research, East African buyers often use online research to validate a referral. The site must confirm credibility, not generate it from scratch.

**Trust before transaction:** Buyers spend longer in the consideration stage. They read more articles, check the portfolio more carefully, and look for evidence of work in their specific industry or geography. Content that demonstrates specific African market experience is a conversion accelerator.

**Implications for keyword strategy:**
- Branded searches (name, company name) are high-value — optimize the home page to rank for personal brand name
- Local keywords with "Uganda" or "Kampala" are essential for voice search validation
- Industry-specific keywords (ERP for healthcare Uganda, software for NGO Uganda) convert better than generic service terms
- Social proof terms ("reviews", "testimonials", "case studies") are searched by validation-stage buyers — create this content

### 6.3 Keyword-to-Stage Assignment Template

For each keyword in the master list, assign:
- Funnel stage (1-5)
- Intent (informational / commercial / transactional / navigational)
- Content home (which page or article this keyword belongs to)
- Conversion goal for that page (read more / enquire / call / download)

## Part VII: Long-Tail Strategy for African Markets

### 7.1 Why Long-Tail Is Especially Powerful in African Markets

African market characteristics make long-tail keywords exceptionally productive:

1. **Low competition on specifics:** Major SEO players have not built out geo-specific long-tail content for Uganda, Tanzania, or Francophone Africa. A well-structured page targeting "ERP implementation company Kampala" faces almost no real competition.

2. **High intent on geo terms:** A person searching "software developer Kampala" is not academic browsing — they are looking for someone to hire. Conversion rates on geo-modified long-tail terms are high.

3. **Voice search growth:** African mobile users use voice search more than keyboard search for local queries. Voice queries are naturally long-tail ("Who is the best IT consultant in Kampala?"). Targeting these question-form keywords captures voice traffic.

4. **Tool undercount creates opportunity:** Google Keyword Planner systematically underestimates search volume for African markets. Competitors who rely on tool data skip keywords that appear to have "zero volume" but actually generate qualified leads.

### 7.2 Long-Tail Keyword Construction Patterns

**Pattern 1: Service + Specificity + Geography**
- "Custom software development company Kampala Uganda"
- "ERP implementation for SMEs Uganda"
- "Website designer for schools Uganda"
- "Business plan writer Kampala Uganda"

**Pattern 2: Problem + Geography**
- "How to digitise my business in Uganda"
- "Why my website is not getting customers Uganda"
- "How much does software cost in Uganda"
- "Best way to manage properties Uganda"

**Pattern 3: Comparison + Geography**
- "Custom software vs off-the-shelf East Africa"
- "Agency vs freelancer website design Uganda"
- "ERP vs accounting software Uganda small business"

**Pattern 4: Time/Urgency + Geography**
- "IT consultant available Kampala"
- "Website development fast delivery Uganda"
- "Urgent business plan writer Uganda"

**Pattern 5: Industry Vertical + Service + Geography**
- "Property management software Uganda"
- "Software for NGOs Uganda"
- "IT consulting for healthcare companies Uganda"
- "ERP system for manufacturing company East Africa"

**Pattern 6: Voice/Conversational Form**
- "Who is the best IT consultant in Uganda?"
- "Where can I find a website developer in Kampala?"
- "How much does an ERP system cost in Uganda?"
- "Is it worth getting custom software for my business in Africa?"

## Part VIII: Competitive Keyword Intelligence (Jones, Chapter 2 & 4)

### 8.1 The Competitive Intelligence Framework

Competitive keyword intelligence answers three questions:
1. What keywords do my competitors rank for that I do not yet target?
2. What weaknesses exist in my competitors' ranking pages that I can exploit?
3. What keyword clusters are completely uncontested in my market?

### 8.2 Competitor Analysis Process

**Step 1: Identify real competitors**
For Peter's services:
- Local: Ugandan IT companies, freelancers ranking in Kampala
- Regional: East African consultancies ranking for regional terms
- Francophone: French-language ICT firms serving Africa
- Indirect: freelancer marketplaces (Upwork, LinkedIn) that rank for hiring-intent terms

**Step 2: Analyse competitor pages**
For each competitor ranking for a target keyword, assess:
- Page title: does it include the target keyword?
- URL: does the URL include the keyword?
- H1: does it match the title?
- Meta description: is it optimised?
- Content depth: 500 words or 3,000 words?
- On-page SEO score: are keywords in the first sentence? In subheadings?
- Backlink profile: how many links? From where? What anchor text?

**Step 3: Identify exploitable weaknesses**
Common weaknesses in African ICT competitor pages:
- Generic pages with no geo modification (rank for service term but not service + Uganda)
- Thin content (fewer than 800 words) on important service keywords
- No structured data (schema) on service pages
- No local SEO signals (address, phone, embedded map)
- Slow page speed on mobile — a major vulnerability as mobile usage dominates

**Step 4: Find uncontested keywords**
Search your target keyword clusters systematically. Note which keywords return:
- No well-optimised pages in the top 5
- Results dominated by directories (Yellow Pages, LinkedIn) rather than service sites
- Results with high bounce rate signals (thin content, no images, poor formatting)

These are your quickest wins — create a well-structured, well-written page targeting the keyword and you will rank.

### 8.3 Competitive Gap Analysis Method

1. List 10 competitors' URLs
2. Run each through Ahrefs or SEMrush (or use free tools: Ubersuggest, Moz)
3. Export their top-ranking keywords
4. Filter for keywords where YOUR site does not appear in the top 20
5. Cross-reference against your service and geographic targets
6. Prioritise by: (commercial intent × search volume) ÷ competition strength

The resulting list is your keyword gap — keywords with proven demand that you are not yet capturing.

## Part IX: International and Multilingual Keyword Research (Jones, Chapter 13)

### 9.1 The Core Challenge

International keyword research is fundamentally different from single-market research because:
- The same concept may have entirely different vocabulary in different markets (and even in different cities of the same country)
- Search volume data is unreliable for many non-English, non-US markets
- Local search behaviour patterns differ significantly by country and language
- Cultural context affects which keywords signal trust vs. which signal low quality

### 9.2 The Francophone Africa Special Case

Francophone Africa is a unique keyword opportunity for three reasons:

**1. Near-zero competition:** Virtually no professionally structured websites target search queries in French for ICT services in West or Central Africa. A well-optimised French-language service page can rank first in Google.fr results for terms like "consultant informatique Côte d'Ivoire" with minimal effort.

**2. Large, underserved market:** The Francophone Africa internet user base spans 400+ million people across 29 countries. The French-speaking internet user base in Africa is growing faster than any other region.

**3. Vocabulary specificity:** Francophone African business vocabulary includes terms used in France but also terms specific to the regional context (OHADA business law, SYSCOHADA accounting standards, BCEAO banking regulations). Pages that use this specific vocabulary rank as authoritative for regional buyers.

### 9.3 French Keyword Research Principles

**Do not machine-translate English keywords:** "IT consultant" does not translate to "consultant IT" in standard French professional vocabulary. The standard terms are:
- Consultant informatique (most common)
- Conseil en systèmes d'information
- Expert informatique
- Consultant en transformation numérique
- Ingénieur conseil

**Do not use France-centric vocabulary:** Francophone African buyers use variants that differ from Metropolitan French. Research vocabulary specific to target countries.

**Geo-modify by Francophone African cities:**
- Abidjan (Côte d'Ivoire — economic capital of West Africa)
- Dakar (Sénégal — West African business hub)
- Kinshasa (DRC — largest Francophone city in the world, 17M+ people)
- Douala (Cameroun — commercial capital)
- Lomé (Togo), Cotonou (Bénin), Bamako (Mali)

**French-language voice search patterns:**
- "Où trouver un bon consultant informatique à Abidjan?"
- "Quel est le prix d'un site web en Côte d'Ivoire?"
- "Comment choisir un développeur logiciel en Afrique?"
- "Qui peut m'aider à informatiser mon entreprise à Dakar?"

## Part X: Voice and AI Search Strategy

### 10.1 Voice Search Characteristics

Voice queries differ from typed queries in five key ways:
1. **Length:** 5-9 words average (vs. 2-3 for typed)
2. **Form:** Question-based (who, what, where, when, why, how)
3. **Register:** Conversational, natural speech — not keyword shorthand
4. **Location:** Often include "near me" or a named location
5. **Intent:** Strong local and immediate intent

### 10.2 Voice Keyword Patterns by Service

| Service | Typed Query | Voice Query |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| IT Consulting | "IT consultant Uganda" | "Who is the best IT consultant in Kampala?" |
| Software Dev | "software developer Uganda" | "Where can I find a software developer in Uganda who builds ERP systems?" |
| Web Design | "website designer Kampala" | "How much does it cost to get a website built in Uganda?" |
| Business Planning | "business plan writer Uganda" | "Can someone write a business plan for my company in Kampala?" |
| Property Tech | "property management software Uganda" | "What is the best software for managing rental properties in Uganda?" |

### 10.3 AI Search and Featured Snippet Targeting

AI search tools (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews) pull answers from content that:
1. Directly answers a specific question within the first 50 words
2. Uses the question itself (or close variants) as a heading
3. Is from a credible, well-structured page with appropriate schema
4. Covers the topic comprehensively (long-form content with clear sections)

**Featured snippet format:** Question as H2 → direct 40-60 word answer → detailed explanation below. This format captures both AI search citations and featured snippets.

**AI search keyword targeting:** Create content pages that answer the specific questions AI tools are likely to answer. Example: a page titled "What does an IT consultant cost in Uganda?" with a direct answer in the first paragraph will be cited by AI tools when users ask this question.

## Part XI: Keyword Quality Scoring Framework

### 11.1 The Composite Score

Use this framework to score any candidate keyword from 1-20. Prioritise the top scorers for page creation.

**Scoring dimensions:**

| Dimension | Score Range | Criteria |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Commercial Intent** | 1-5 | 5 = strong buying intent ("hire", "cost", "services + location"); 1 = pure information seeking |
| **Relevance to Services** | 1-5 | 5 = exact match to a service Peter offers; 1 = tangentially related |
| **Competition Strength** | 1-5 | 5 = very weak competition (thin pages, low DA); 1 = dominated by large authoritative sites |
| **Search Volume** | 1-5 | 5 = solid volume for the market (relative); 1 = near-zero |

**Composite Score = Commercial Intent + Relevance + Competition + Volume (max 20)**

### 11.2 Priority Tiers

| Score | Tier | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| 16-20 | Tier 1 — Immediate | Create dedicated page. Optimise fully. Target within 30 days. |
| 12-15 | Tier 2 — Important | Create page or section. Target within 60 days. |
| 8-11 | Tier 3 — Develop | Create blog content. Target within 90 days. |
| 4-7 | Tier 4 — Opportunistic | Include in existing content as secondary keyword. |
| 1-3 | Tier 5 — Deprioritise | Monitor only. Do not create dedicated content. |

### 11.3 Quick-Win Multiplier

A Quick-Win keyword has a high composite score AND one additional factor: low existing competition on the specific geo-modified term. Apply a 1.5x multiplier to the score for any keyword where a search on Google returns no well-structured, well-optimised pages in positions 1-5.

**Formula:** Quick-Win Score = Composite Score × 1.5 (if uncontested) or × 1.0 (if contested)

A keyword scoring 14/20 that is uncontested gets an effective priority score of 21 — higher than any standard Tier 1. These are your fastest ranking opportunities.

## Part XII: Measuring Keyword Success (Jones, Chapter 14)

### 12.1 The Right Metrics

Most people measure keyword success by ranking position. Jones argues that ranking is an intermediate metric — it matters only insofar as it drives traffic, and traffic matters only insofar as it drives conversions. The ultimate keyword metrics are:

1. **Organic sessions from target keyword** (GA4 + GSC)
2. **Conversion rate from organic traffic** (form fills, calls, WhatsApp initiations)
3. **Revenue influenced by keyword** (did the person who found you via Google eventually become a client?)

### 12.2 Measurement Framework

**Weekly:** Check Google Search Console impressions and clicks for target keywords. Note any new keywords appearing in GSC that you did not intentionally target (these are signals of new content opportunities).

**Monthly:** Review ranking positions for all Tier 1 and Tier 2 keywords. Check organic traffic trends. Review which blog posts are getting organic traffic and trace back to which keywords are driving it.

**Quarterly:** Full keyword performance review. Compare rankings against baseline. Identify new keyword opportunities from GSC data. Update priority list. Check competitor keyword changes.

**Annual:** Full competitive re-analysis. Are new competitors entering? Have search volumes shifted? Are there new keyword categories emerging (e.g., new technology terms that buyers have started using)?

### 12.3 Success Benchmarks for African ICT Markets

Given the relatively low search volume of African markets, success looks different from Western market benchmarks:

- **T1 Uganda keyword:** Ranking in top 3 for "IT consultant Uganda" → target 50-200 organic visits/month from that keyword cluster
- **T2 East Africa keyword:** Ranking in top 5 for "software developer East Africa" → 20-100 organic visits/month
- **T4 Francophone Africa:** Ranking first for "consultant informatique Abidjan" → even 10 visits/month from this keyword can generate high-value enquiries given the virtually zero competition

**The key insight for African markets:** One qualified visitor from "IT consultant Uganda" is worth more than 100 visitors from a broad international keyword. Volume is not the primary metric. Quality and intent are.

## Appendix B: Quick-Reference Checklist — New Keyword Evaluation

Before committing to targeting a keyword, answer all of these:

- [ ] Does this keyword match how a real buyer in my target market would describe their problem?
- [ ] Is there genuine search intent behind it (not just academic curiosity)?
- [ ] Can I create content that genuinely answers this keyword's implied question?
- [ ] Does this keyword map to a specific page or piece of content on the site?
- [ ] Is the competition weak enough that I can realistically rank for this keyword?
- [ ] Is the search trend stable or rising?
- [ ] Does targeting this keyword attract the right type of buyer (not wrong-fit visitors)?
- [ ] Have I checked for commercial intent signals (ads appearing, CPC data)?
- [ ] Is this keyword distinct from other target keywords to avoid cannibalisation?
- [ ] Can I write a page title, H1, and meta description that naturally include this keyword?

If you answer "no" to more than two of these, reconsider the keyword before creating content for it.
