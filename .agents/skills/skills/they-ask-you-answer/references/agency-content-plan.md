# Agency Content Plan

The architecture for the agency's own website and authority engine. This is the
agency eating its own cooking — applying every framework we sell (Sheridan's
Big 5 and Selling 7, Pulizzi's audience-first model, Kleon's
show-your-work discipline, Brunson's Hook-Story-Offer) to its own marketing.

If we cannot demonstrate the system on ourselves, we cannot sell it to a
$22,000 buyer.

## The agency website at full scope

Every premium agency site has six layers, each serving a different stage of
the prospect's journey from stranger to qualified enquiry.

```
1. Authority layer    →  proves the agency is the category authority
2. Service layer      →  defines the offer with conviction
3. Sector layer       →  proves competence in specific industries
4. Proof layer        →  shows the work and the outcomes
5. Audit/lead-magnet  →  converts educated visitors into paying audits
6. Conversion layer   →  the discovery-call funnel
```

Every page on the site belongs to one layer and serves one job. Pages that
straddle layers underperform.

---

## Layer 1 — Authority pages

These pages establish the agency as a category-defining voice. They earn the
right to charge premium fees.

### 1.1 Home page

The single most important page. Hook–Story–Offer applied at maximum density.

- **Hero (the hook).** One sentence stating the new-opportunity category and
  the buyer ("The Authority Website System for [specific buyer] generating
  [specific outcome]"). One supporting subhead. One CTA.
- **The case for the system (story 1).** Why most agency-built websites fail
  to drive revenue. Names the pattern, names the consequences.
- **The system explained (story 2).** What an Authority Website System is —
  4 to 6 components, each with a single sentence.
- **Proof (story 3).** Three case study cards with outcomes, not screenshots.
- **The principles (story 4).** 5 to 7 published principles (see
  `expert-authority-framework.md` section 11).
- **The team (story 5).** Named senior people with bio videos (Selling 7,
  video 2).
- **The offer.** The discovery call. One CTA, repeated twice.

Length: ~2,000 words across all sections. No carousels, no testimonial
sliders, no stock imagery.

### 1.2 About page

The Epiphany Bridge applied to the agency. Three paragraphs, 600 to 1,200
words. See `expert-authority-framework.md` section 2.

Includes:

- The founder's 8-beat origin story.
- The mission and the one belief we advance.
- The principles we operate by.
- The team (with photographs and bios — real, named, no stock).
- A signature talk recording embedded.
- The reading list and acknowledged influences (per Kleon).

### 1.3 Our Principles page

A standalone page listing the 5 to 7 principles the agency defends publicly.
Each principle gets 2 to 3 sentences plus a link to a long-form argument
elsewhere on the site.

Linked from the home page header and the proposal template.

### 1.4 Why we publish (the manifesto)

Single page explaining why the agency invests in long-form authority content.
Cites Pulizzi, Sheridan, Kleon. Function: convert the rare visitor who
notices the depth and asks "why bother with all this?" into a subscriber.

### 1.5 The signature talk page

A standing page with the latest signature talk recording, transcript, and a
calendar of upcoming live deliveries (the monthly Authority Website
Roundtable). Gated email opt-in for the next live session.

### 1.6 The team / studio page

Per Kleon (work behind the work) and Sheridan (Selling 7, video 2). Real
photos of the team and studio. Bio videos for each customer-facing person.
Studio photo, tools photo, "a day at the studio" photo essay.

## Layer 2 — Service pages

The offer defined with conviction. One page per tier.

### 2.1 The Authority Website System overview page

A single page that defines the category and orients the prospect across all
three tiers.

- The category claim (one sentence).
- Why it exists (the problem with conventional agency engagements).
- The three tiers in a comparison block (see
  `agency-positioning/references/premium-website-offer-playbook.md`).
- Who each tier is for; who is is not for.
- A single CTA: book a discovery call.

### 2.2 Foundation tier page

$12k–$15k engagement.

- Headline: outcome the tier produces.
- Who it is for (3 specific buyer profiles).
- Who it is not for (2 explicit disqualifiers — see Sheridan's "fit" video,
  Selling 7 video 3).
- The 7-week scope.
- 2 case studies.
- Pricing range stated; factors explained (per Sheridan / CarMax Effect).
- Embedded fit video (Selling 7 #3).
- Embedded pricing video (Selling 7 #5).
- A pre-call CTA: "Watch the 80% video, then book a call."

### 2.3 Growth tier page

Same structure as Foundation tier page. $18k–$28k.

### 2.4 Authority tier page

Same structure. $30k+.

### 2.5 Audit pages (4 separate pages)

One page per audit offer (Website Authority, Trust & Conversion, SEO
Foundation, Messaging Clarity). See
`agency-positioning/references/premium-audit-offers.md` for the exact
structure of each landing page.

### 2.6 Retainer / on-going work page

For after-engagement support. See `agency-client-retention/`.

## Layer 3 — Sector pages

One landing page per target sector. Each functions as a hub for the sector's
research, case studies, articles, and tailored offer.

### Sector page structure

- Hero with sector-specific headline ("The Authority Website System for
  Boutique Law Firms").
- The 5 sector-specific symptoms that mean the firm needs us.
- Sector-specific positioning explanation (what makes a law firm site
  different from a clinic site).
- Case studies from the sector (3 to 5).
- Sector-specific articles (the cluster of long-form content).
- Sector-specific testimonials.
- Sector-specific FAQs.
- A sector-specific lead magnet (the audit, or a sector benchmark report).
- A single CTA.

### Initial sector pages to build

In order of agency priority:

1. Boutique law firms.
2. Specialist financial services firms.
3. Multi-location medical practices.
4. Premium ecommerce in target geographies.
5. Sector to be added based on case study volume.

Each sector page must have at least 3 case studies and 6 long-form articles
before the sector page is published. Otherwise the page reads as aspiration,
not authority.

## Layer 4 — Proof layer

Case studies, the Wall of Love, the proof library.

### 4.1 Case studies index

Ordered chronologically (newest first), filterable by sector and tier. Each
card shows:

- Outcome (one sentence).
- Sector and tier badges.
- Year.
- Featured image (no stock).

### 4.2 Individual case study pages

Built using `agency-positioning/references/case-study-template.md`.

Each case study links forward to:

- The relevant sector page.
- The relevant service tier page.
- 2 related articles.
- The discovery-call CTA.

### 4.3 Wall of Love page

Per `proof-library.md` section 8. Every testimonial collected, in
chronological order, with links to each case study.

### 4.4 Process and rationale gallery

Per Kleon. The "work behind the work" — moodboards, IA sketches, design
direction explorations, post-launch retrospectives. Organized chronologically
or by project. Updated monthly.

## Layer 5 — Audit / lead-magnet layer

Each audit (see Layer 2.5) has a paid landing page. Free lead magnets are
limited to:

- The reading list.
- The 40-point launch checklist (see `proof-library.md` section 5).
- The annual sector benchmark report (the cornerstone asset, see Pulizzi).
- The "What to Expect When Hiring Us" 80% video (Selling 7 #1).

Each gated lead magnet adds the visitor to the email list, which triggers the
5-part Soap Opera onboarding sequence (see
`expert-authority-framework.md` section 9).

Free lead magnets are gateways. Real qualification happens at the paid audit.

## Layer 6 — Conversion layer

The discovery-call funnel.

### 6.1 Discovery call page

A single page, not buried in a footer. Includes:

- The Selling 7 landing-page video (video 4): direct address explaining
  exactly what happens after the form is submitted.
- A short qualification form (5 to 7 questions, no more — see
  `qualification-guide.md`).
- The Assignment Selling pre-call materials, listed: "Before our call, please
  watch [80% video] and read [pricing article]."
- A clear timeline: "We respond within 4 business hours. Calls are 30 to 45
  minutes. You will not be pitched. We will tell you whether we can help."
- Testimonial from a recent first-call.

### 6.2 Thank-you / next-steps page

Triggered after form submission. Includes:

- Confirmation email is on its way.
- The calendly link for booking the call.
- The Assignment Selling pre-call materials, embedded directly.
- A reminder of what happens on the call.

## Article architecture (the blog that isn't really a blog)

The agency does not run a blog. It runs an authority library.

### Cornerstone pieces (the pillars)

5 to 8 long-form pieces (3,000 to 6,000 words each) that anchor the entire
content engine. Each cornerstone:

- Advances the Big Domino (see `expert-authority-framework.md` section 4).
- Maps to one of the Big 5 topics (see `legacy-guidance.md`).
- Has a bespoke design treatment (custom illustrations, embedded video,
  pull-quotes, case-study sidebars).
- Is updated annually.
- Has at least 6 supporting cluster articles linking to it.

Initial cornerstones to build:

1. **The Authority Website System** — what it is, how it differs from
   conventional web design, who it's for. (The category-defining piece.)
2. **The True Cost of a Premium Website in [year]** — Sheridan's "How much
   does X cost" applied to us. The single article that drives the most
   traffic and the most qualified enquiries.
3. **Why Most Agency-Built Websites Fail to Drive Revenue** — the diagnostic
   piece that names the pattern and positions the solution.
4. **The Difference Between a Website and a Revenue-Generation System** —
   the conceptual piece that establishes our vocabulary.
5. **Authority Website Systems for [Sector 1]** — sector-specific cornerstone
   per Layer 3.
6. **Authority Website Systems for [Sector 2]** — sector-specific cornerstone.

### Cluster articles

Each cornerstone is supported by 6 to 12 cluster articles that explore
sub-topics and link back to the cornerstone. The classic pillar-and-cluster
SEO model.

Cluster article types:

- **Big 5: Cost.** Variants — pricing range articles for specific buyer
  segments.
- **Big 5: Problems.** Articles naming the problems with our own approach,
  competitors' approaches, and adjacent options (Webflow, Squarespace,
  in-house teams, freelancers).
- **Big 5: Versus.** Comparisons (Astro vs WordPress vs Webflow; agency vs
  freelancer vs in-house; this tier vs that tier).
- **Big 5: Reviews.** Reviews of tools, platforms, and approaches in our
  category.
- **Big 5: Best of.** Best-of lists for our sector (best CMS for law firms,
  best appointment booking tool for clinics).

### Original research / benchmark reports

One per quarter (per Pulizzi's diversification rhythm). Examples:

- The State of Law Firm Websites in [region], [year].
- Conversion benchmarks for B2B services websites.
- The 30 most common conversion failures on agency-built sites.

These become the most-cited assets in the entire library and provide the
backbone for sector-specific signature talks.

### Teardowns

Public teardowns of well-known sites in our sectors. Always credit, always
fair, never punching down. These are some of the most shareable assets we
publish.

Two per quarter minimum.

### Process posts (the daily dispatch)

Per Kleon. Short posts (200 to 400 words) showing process artefacts:
moodboards, wireframes, the strategic memo, the design direction we
rejected. Published 2 to 3 times per week on the site (cross-posted to
LinkedIn).

minutes. All hosted on YouTube (per CLAUDE.md), embedded via
youtube-nocookie.com.

## Email list architecture

The single most important asset (per Pulizzi). The agency runs three lists:

1. **Main weekly newsletter.** Everyone who opts in via any lead magnet,
   blog post, or audit. Receives the 5-part Soap Opera onboarding then the
   weekly anchor.
2. **Sector-specific lists.** Subset opt-ins (e.g. "send me law-firm-specific
   content"). Receive the weekly anchor plus monthly sector-specific
   commentary.
3. **Roundtable invite list.** Senior subscribers invited to the monthly
   Authority Website Roundtable.

ESP: pick one and stay. ConvertKit, Substack, or self-hosted. Avoid
list-management tools that don't support proper segmentation (e.g.
Mailchimp's older free tier).

## Build order (the agency's own 18-month plan)

The agency cannot build all six layers and 25 videos and 20 articles in
month 1. The build order:

### Months 0 to 3 — Foundation

- Home, About, Principles, Service overview, 1 service tier page, 3 case
  studies (using existing client work).
- 80% video (Selling 7 #1).
- Bio videos for senior team (Selling 7 #2).
- Pricing video (Selling 7 #5).
- 3 cornerstone articles (Authority Website System; True Cost; Why Most
  Sites Fail).
- 8 cluster articles spread across the 3 cornerstones.
- Newsletter signup with 5-part onboarding sequence.
- Discovery-call page with landing video (Selling 7 #4).
- One sector page (the agency's strongest sector).

### Months 4 to 6 — Authority and rhythm

- 2nd and 3rd service tier pages.
- Service fit videos (Selling 7 #3) for each tier.
- Sector page #2.
- Customer journey video (Selling 7 #6) for the flagship case.
- Claims-we-make video (Selling 7 #7).
- 3 more cornerstone articles.
- 12 more cluster articles.
- Weekly anchor publishing rhythm now established (4 posts/month minimum).
- Daily LinkedIn dispatch rhythm established (3–4 posts/week).
- Process gallery launched.
- All 4 audit landing pages live.

### Months 7 to 12 — Compounding

- First quarterly cornerstone benchmark report.
- First monthly Authority Website Roundtable signature talk recording.
- Sector page #3.
- 2 more cornerstones; 24 more cluster articles.
- 2 to 4 teardowns per quarter.
- First refresh of cornerstone articles (annual update cycle begins).

### Months 13 to 18 — Diversification (if base is healthy)

- Podcast launches (1 episode every 2 weeks).
- Second-language version of the site begins (per CLAUDE.md, multilingual
  is expected).
- Annual benchmark report's first edition published.
- Newsletter list at 1,500+ subscribers.

## Anti-patterns (do not do these)

- **Publishing without a Big Domino target.** Every cornerstone must
  advance the one core belief.
- **Writing in a tone that doesn't match the buyer.** Bro-marketing,
  hustle-culture register, exclamation marks, "let's dive in," "what's
  up rockstars" — all wrong for the audience.
- **Treating the blog as a publishing schedule with no architecture.**
  Posts must belong to a cornerstone cluster or to a sector page. Orphaned
  posts dilute the library.
- **Hiring an outsourced content team to "produce content."** Per Sheridan,
  this never works. The senior agency people must contribute
  substantively, even if a content manager runs the production.
- **Publishing 5 cornerstones with no supporting cluster articles.** A
  cornerstone without clusters does not rank.
- **Adding a podcast in month 4.** Pulizzi: extend only when the base is
  earned.
- **Running ads to the home page.** The home page is for warm visitors. Ads
  go to specific landing pages with specific offers.
- **Hiding pricing.** Per Sheridan, the absence of pricing plants the seed
  of doubt regardless of affordability.
- **Generic case studies.** See `case-study-template.md` — no portfolio
  screenshots posing as case studies.
- **Quitting at month 6.** This is the single most common failure. The
  curve is invisible until month 12 to 18.

## Success criteria at 18 months

Audit the agency's own engine at month 18 against:

- [ ] All 6 site layers exist with at least minimum content per layer.
- [ ] Newsletter list at 1,500+ named subscribers from the target niche.
- [ ] At least 8 cornerstone articles published.
- [ ] At least 40 cluster articles supporting the cornerstones.
- [ ] All 7 Selling 7 video types produced.
- [ ] At least 3 sector pages with case studies and articles each.
- [ ] At least 6 case studies on the site.
- [ ] At least 4 testimonial videos.
- [ ] First quarterly cornerstone report published.
- [ ] Monthly signature talk being delivered live.
- [ ] At least 50 per cent of inbound enquiries cite a specific article or
      video as how they found us.
- [ ] At least 1 premium engagement per month attributable to the content
      engine.

If month 18 hits 8 of these 12 markers, the engine is working. If it hits
fewer than 6, the strategy needs serious diagnosis (likely tilt, cadence, or
distribution).
