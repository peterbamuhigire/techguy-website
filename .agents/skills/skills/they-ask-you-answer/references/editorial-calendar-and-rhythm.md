# Editorial Calendar and Lead-Generation Rhythm

The weekly, monthly, and quarterly cadence that runs the agency's authority
engine and lead-generation system. Without a documented rhythm, content
programmes drift, sales follow-up slips, referrals are never asked for, and
case studies are never collected.

This file installs the recurring habits that make Phase 7 a system rather
than a series of one-off pushes.

Read alongside:

- `agency-content-plan.md` (the architecture being executed against this
  cadence).
- `content-inc-audience-model.md` (Pulizzi's annual/quarterly/weekly rhythm).
- `show-your-work-practices.md` (Kleon's daily-dispatch principle).
- `legacy-guidance.md` and `assignment-selling-templates.md` (Sheridan's
  publishing tactics).
- `agency-positioning/references/expert-authority-framework.md` (the Dream
  100 weekly ritual).

|---------|--------|-------|----------------|

| Weekly | Dream 100 outreach ritual (1 hour Friday) | Founder | 60 min |
| Weekly | Sales follow-up cycle | Founder + sales support | 90 min |
| Bi-weekly | 1 short video (Selling 7 cluster) | Content manager + SME | 3 hours |
| Monthly | Signature talk / Authority Roundtable delivered live | Founder | 4 hours (incl. prep) |
| Monthly | Editorial planning + content review meeting | Whole team | 90 min |
| Monthly | Case study collection + production | Content manager + client | 6 hours |
| Monthly | Pipeline review + referral asks | Founder | 2 hours |
| Quarterly | 1 cornerstone deep asset (benchmark report, deep teardown) | Founder + content manager | 20 hours spread |
| Quarterly | Dream 100 list refresh + warmth review | Founder | 3 hours |
| Quarterly | Proof library audit (per `proof-library.md`) | Content manager | 3 hours |
| Quarterly | Sector page audit + cluster gap analysis | Content manager | 4 hours |

| Annually | Full proof library refresh | Content manager | 2 days |

Total founder time on publishing + lead-gen rhythm: 12 to 16 hours per week.
This is the cost of running an authority engine. It is non-negotiable.

## The weekly rhythm

The week is the basic operating unit. Get the week right and the year takes
care of itself.

### Monday — Surface and plan

**Morning (45 min).**

- Sales–content standup. Founder + content manager + any sales support.
  Surface the 3 to 5 customer questions heard on calls last week (per
  Sheridan's "obsession" test). These become candidate topics for the
  upcoming weekly anchor or short posts.
- Review the editorial calendar for the next 14 days. Confirm what ships
  this week and next.

**Afternoon.**

- Weekly anchor article — first draft. Founder writes if it's their topic;
  content manager interviews founder (the 30-minute interview-to-draft
  workflow from Sheridan) if otherwise.

### Tuesday — Produce

- Founder records 1 short video (cycling through Selling 7 cluster).
- Content manager edits the weekly anchor draft. SME review if needed.
- 1 short LinkedIn dispatch post (process artefact, observation, lesson).

### Wednesday — Polish and schedule

- Weekly anchor article: edit, fact-check, OG image, schema, sector tagging,
  internal links to relevant cornerstones and case studies.
- Schedule the weekly anchor for Thursday 8:00 local time.
- 1 short LinkedIn dispatch post.

### Thursday — Publish + amplify

- Weekly anchor article publishes on the site.
- Email newsletter goes out simultaneously — same article, summarised + link.
- Founder posts the LinkedIn version (200-word excerpt + link).
- Content manager handles cross-distribution (sector LinkedIn groups, partner
  shares, etc.).
- 1 short LinkedIn dispatch post.

### Friday — Relate and review

**Morning (60 min).**

- **Dream 100 ritual** (per `expert-authority-framework.md` section 10).
  Founder works through one segment of the Dream 100 list. One genuine touch
  per contact (thoughtful comment, useful link sent with context, intro
  made, private teardown shared).

**Midday (90 min).**

- **Sales follow-up cycle.** Review every active prospect in the pipeline.
  - For each: when did we last touch them? Is there a useful piece of new
    content to send (Assignment Selling)?
  - Send no more than one follow-up per prospect per week.
  - Move stale prospects (no response in 30 days) to nurture.
  - Update the CRM with each touch.

**Afternoon.**

- Capture the week's process artefacts (screenshots, photos, sketches,
  notes) into the process gallery (per Kleon).
- 1 short LinkedIn dispatch post if appropriate.
- Light week — no new long-form output. Recovery and reflection.

### Total weekly output

- 1 long-form anchor article (newsletter + LinkedIn).
- 4–5 short LinkedIn posts.
- 1 short video (recorded; published bi-weekly).
- 1 Dream 100 segment worked.
- 1 full sales pipeline pass.
- Process artefacts collected.

## The monthly rhythm

Run on the first business week of each month.

### Editorial planning meeting (90 min)

- Whole team: founder, content manager, designer, dev lead.
- Review the previous month's metrics (see metrics section below).
- Confirm the editorial slate for the upcoming month: 4 weekly anchors, 2
  short videos, the signature talk topic.
- Identify any cornerstone or sector gaps to address.
- Approve the publishing calendar.

### Signature talk delivery (4 hours including prep)

- Founder delivers the monthly Authority Website Roundtable signature talk.
- Live to the Dream 100 invite list and the roundtable subscriber list.
- Recorded and edited; recording sent to attendees within 48 hours.
- Topic rotates quarterly (the same talk delivered 3 times before it is
  refreshed, per the "stick around" principle).

### Case study collection (6 hours)

- Content manager identifies any engagements at launch + 30 days, + 60
  days, + 90 days.
- Drives the case-study production cycle per `case-study-template.md`.
- For each engagement at launch + 90 days: full case study published, added
  to proof library, distributed to the relevant sector page.

### Pipeline review and referral asks (2 hours)

- Founder reviews the closed-won deals from the previous month.
  - For each: was a case study captured? Was a testimonial requested?
- For every client engagement in good standing for 60+ days:
  - Send a referral ask (template: "Working with you has been some of the
    best work we've done this year. Three new firms are coming to us each
    month who could benefit from a similar engagement. If you know one or
    two who might fit, would you be willing to make a one-line introduction?
    No pressure either way.").
- Track referrals received and outcomes.

### Metrics review

Pull and review the monthly numbers (see metrics section below). Decisions
about what to amplify, what to fix, and what to retire are made here.

## The quarterly rhythm

Run in the last week of each quarter (e.g. last week of March, June,
September, December).

### Cornerstone deep asset publication

The quarterly cornerstone (benchmark report, deep teardown compilation,
sector study, original research). Per Pulizzi's diversification rhythm.

Example quarterly cornerstones:

- Q1: Annual sector benchmark report.
- Q2: 30-site teardown compilation.
- Q3: Original survey of decision-makers in target sector.
- Q4: "Year in review" cornerstone showing the agency's evolution.

Each cornerstone gets its own promotion arc (4 weeks of supporting weekly
anchor pieces, 2 podcast appearances secured around the launch, 1 paid
LinkedIn campaign to the Dream 100).

### Dream 100 list refresh

- Review the Dream 100 list. Promote those who advanced (warmth level
  raised), demote those who went cold for 6+ months, retire and replace.
- Add new entries based on the previous quarter's market signals (firms that
  appeared, new principals appointed, funding events, public RFPs).
- Maintain the list at exactly 100 names (clients) + 100 names
  (influencers).

### Proof library audit

Per `proof-library.md` quarterly maintenance:

- Retire stale components (case studies older than 3 years; testimonials
  from clients with whom the relationship ended).
- Rebalance sector coverage based on next quarter's pipeline.
- Update the standard diagram set if process has evolved.

### Sector page audit + cluster gap analysis

For each sector page:

- How many cluster articles exist? Should be 6+ per cornerstone.
- Are any of the Big 5 topics missing for this sector?
- Have the case studies on the page been refreshed?
- Are there any new sector trends to write about?

Identify the 6 to 10 article briefs that will fill gaps in the following quarter
and add them to the editorial calendar.

## The annual rhythm

Run in the first week of each calendar year (or any other anchor week the
agency picks — but the same week every year).

### Editorial strategy review (2 days, off-site if possible)

Per Pulizzi's annual cadence:

- Is the content tilt still distinctive? Or has the market caught up?
- Is the sweet spot still right? Or has the agency drifted?
- Is the niche still served? Or has the audience shifted?
- What did and didn't work last year?
- What is the editorial focus for the year ahead?

Output: a 2-page editorial strategy memo committed to writing, shared with
the team. Defended for 12 months unless something material changes.

### Annual benchmark report (60 hours, spread Q1–Q2)

The single biggest content investment of the year. The data-driven asset
that becomes the most-cited piece of work the agency produces. Drives
inbound, sponsorship invitations, podcast appearances, sales conversations.

### Full proof library refresh

Every component re-read. Broken links fixed. Screenshots refreshed. Founder
story and authority assets updated. Outdated case studies retired.

### Annual ritual: the year-in-review post

A long-form, personal post from the founder reviewing the year. Per Kleon's
"sell out" and "stick around" principles — quietly proud, factually
accurate, no hype. What worked, what didn't, what we learned.

This is one of the most-read pieces of content the agency publishes each
year.

## Metrics — what to track and when

### Weekly metrics

- Newsletter open rate (target: 35–45%).
- Newsletter click rate (target: 5–8%).
- New subscribers (target: 5–15/week in year 1; 15–40/week in year 2+).
- Inbound enquiries (any source).

### Monthly metrics

- Total subscribers (Pulizzi's primary metric).
- Top 5 articles by traffic; top 5 by time on page.
- Self-reported lead source on every form ("How did you hear about us?").
- Conversion rate: site visitors → newsletter subscribers.
- Conversion rate: subscribers → discovery call requests.
- Discovery calls held.
- Audits sold.
- Engagements signed.
- Average engagement fee.
- Referrals received from existing clients.

### Quarterly metrics

- First-touch URL per closed-won deal (per Sheridan).
- Pages-viewed per closed-won deal (target: 30+ per the River Pools data).
- Time-on-site per closed-won deal.
- Revenue attributable to specific cornerstone articles (the Sheridan
  attribution method).
- Cost per qualified enquiry from the content engine vs other channels.
- Dream 100 warmth movement.
- Cohort retention of subscribers (90-day, 180-day, 365-day stickiness).

### Annual metrics

- Total subscribers (year-over-year growth).
- Total inbound revenue attributable to content.
- Average engagement fee year-over-year.
- Content team time invested per dollar of revenue produced.
- Net Promoter Score from clients (annual survey).
- Number of speaking invitations received.
- Number of inbound podcast invitations received.
- Number of journalists / podcasters who cited the agency in their work.

## Lead-generation rhythm — the six recurring tasks

Phase 7 names six specific cadenced activities. Each maps to a slot in the
calendar above:

| Activity | Cadence | Slot in calendar |
|----------|---------|------------------|
| Publishing | Weekly + bi-weekly + monthly + quarterly + annually | All cadence layers |
| Outreach | Weekly | Friday Dream 100 ritual |
| Follow-up | Weekly | Friday sales pipeline pass |
| Referral asks | Monthly | First-week pipeline review |
| Case study collection | Monthly | First-week case study cycle |
| Pipeline review | Monthly | First-week pipeline review |

The discipline is not about doing these things — most agencies do them
sporadically. The discipline is doing them on the same day every week and
the same week every month, recorded in `project-log/` (see
`templates/project-log-template.md`), with the previous month's numbers
visible during the next planning meeting.

## The two failure modes to watch for

### 1. Rhythm collapse during a busy delivery period

The week the agency is launching three sites is the week the content rhythm
silently slips. The next week, no one notices. By the end of the quarter,
the rhythm is broken and the trend is visible only to the analytics
dashboard.

**Defence.** Block the founder's content time as immovable calendar
appointments. Treat them like client meetings. The content rhythm is a
client meeting with the audience.

### 2. Drift into vanity metrics

The team starts celebrating LinkedIn impressions instead of newsletter
subscribers, or social engagement instead of pipeline.

**Defence.** Pulizzi's rule: the only metric that matters is subscribers.
Hold the team accountable to it. If a tactic generates impressions but not
subscribers, it does not get repeated.

## The 30/60/90-day proof of life

A new agency adopting this rhythm should hit these markers:

**Day 30.**

- Weekly anchor publishing rhythm established (4 articles published).
- 8 to 12 short LinkedIn posts published.
- First short video produced.
- Newsletter signup live with onboarding sequence working.
- Editorial calendar populated for the next 60 days.
- First Dream 100 list drafted (first 30 names).

**Day 60.**

- 8 weekly anchors published.
- First sector page live with at least 1 case study.
- First signature talk delivered (live or recorded).
- Newsletter list at 100+ subscribers.
- First inbound enquiry citing a specific article.

**Day 90.**

- 12 weekly anchors published.
- 2 cornerstone articles complete.
- All 4 audit landing pages live.
- Newsletter list at 250+ subscribers.
- First quarterly cornerstone in production.
- First case study published using `case-study-template.md`.
- Pipeline review and referral asks have been done at least twice.

If the agency hits these markers, the rhythm is installed. Compounding now
takes over.

If the agency misses more than half these markers at day 90, the rhythm has
not actually been installed — it has only been planned. Diagnose what got
in the way (capacity, ownership, conviction) before adding more output to
the calendar.
