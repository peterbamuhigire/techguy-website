# Show Your Work — Authority-Building Practices

Ten practices from Austin Kleon's *Show Your Work!* (Workman, 2014),
translated for a premium website agency building an authority engine. Kleon's
premise: self-promotion is the wrong frame. The right frame is *be findable*
— share process, influences, and learning generously, every day, on ground you
own. The audience finds you.

For an agency selling $12k–$45k engagements, the practices in this file sit
alongside Sheridan's Big 5 (see `legacy-guidance.md`) and Pulizzi's
audience-building model (`content-inc-audience-model.md`). Sheridan teaches
*what* to publish (the questions buyers already ask). Pulizzi teaches *who* to
publish for (a specific audience in a chosen niche). Kleon teaches *how to
show up*, day after day, without burning out or posturing as a guru.

## The ten practices

### 1. You don't have to be a genius (position inside a scenius)

Brian Eno's "scenius" — an ecology of peers, amateurs, curators, and
contributors who copy, steal, and build on each other — produces good work.
The lone-genius myth does not.

**Practice.** Pick a scenius (the designers, strategists, SEO practitioners,
developers, and writers whose work overlaps with ours). Follow them. Read
them. Credit them. Fill the voids they leave with our own work. Embrace the
amateur's posture of curiosity — in love with the work, unafraid to learn in
public.

**Quotes.**

- *"Creativity is always, in some sense, a collaboration, the result of a
  mind connected to other minds."*
- *"That's all any of us are: amateurs. We don't live long enough to be
  anything else."* — Chaplin
- *"If your work isn't online, it doesn't exist."*

**For the agency.** We do not posture as the lone expert dispensing wisdom.
We position inside a visible community of practitioners, crediting the
teachers who shaped our approach, and publishing as enthusiastic learners.
Authority comes from being visibly curious and generous, not from claims of
genius.

### 2. Think process, not product

The finished site is a tiny fraction of the value the agency generates. The
*process* — the inspirations, sketches, decisions, mistakes, iterations — is
where audiences actually connect and trust forms.

**Practice.** Document everything. Work journal. Moodboards. Photographs of
screens at intermediate stages. Short videos. "Turn the invisible into
something other people can see."

**Quotes.**

- *"A lot of people are so used to just seeing the outcome of work. They
  never see the side of the work you go through to produce the outcome."* —
  Michael Jackson
- *"People really do want to see how the sausage gets made."* — Provost and
  Gerhardt
- *"No one is going to give a damn about your résumé; they want to see what
  you have made with your own little fingers."* — David Carr

**For the agency.** Every live build generates a trail of process artefacts:
moodboards, wireframe iterations, sector-research notes, failed colour
experiments, pre-launch Lighthouse scores. That trail is the real marketing
asset — not the glossy case study written six months late. Publish it as it
happens.

### 3. Share something small every day

The daily dispatch. One short artefact per day, pulled from whatever was
documented that day.

**Practice.** After the day's work, pull one thing and share it. Form is
flexible (short post, image, note, video clip). Run every post through the
"So what?" test — is it helpful, entertaining, or revealing?

**Quotes.**

- *"A daily dispatch is even better than a résumé or a portfolio, because it
  shows what we're working on right now."*
- *"Don't show your lunch or your latte; show your work."*
- *"Ninety percent of everything is crap."* — Sturgeon (so don't gatekeep
  yourself into paralysis)

**For the agency.** A single short daily artefact (LinkedIn post,
behind-the-scenes image, 200-word note) beats a perfect monthly essay. The
daily dispatch is the pulse that signals "we are alive, working, and
thinking." It feeds the top of the lead-gen funnel. It makes the weekly
anchor piece easier to write because the material is accumulating.

**Sustainability rule (our adaptation).** "Daily" for a senior founder in a
busy agency is aspirational, not mandatory. The achievable cadence is: 4 to 5
short posts per week on the primary platform (LinkedIn), plus the weekly
long-form anchor. That is the realistic expression of Kleon's daily-dispatch
principle for a B2B agency founder.

### 4. Open up your cabinet of curiosities

Share tastes, influences, reading lists. They are as revealing as finished
work.

**Practice.** Publish the reading list, the record collection, the designers
and writers we steal from. Always credit meticulously (attribution = museum
label: what it is, who made it, when, why it matters, where to find more).
**Don't share anything you can't properly credit.**

**Quotes.**

- *"You're only as good as your record collection."* — DJ Spooky
- *"Do what you do best and link to the rest."* — Jeff Jarvis
- *"Your influences are all worth sharing because they clue people in to who
  you are and what you do — sometimes even more than your own work."*
- *"Don't share things you can't properly credit. Find the right credit, or
  don't share."*

**For the agency.** Publish:

- The reading list that shaped how we work (with short notes on why each
  book matters).
- The designers, typographers, and agencies we learn from.
- The sector books informing current engagements.
- Teardowns of sites we admire (with credit to the original team).

This is the fastest way to attract clients whose taste aligns with ours, and
to repel mismatched prospects before a call ever happens.

### 5. Tell good stories

Work does not speak for itself. The story attached to it determines how it is
valued. Three-act shape: where you've been → where you are → where you're
going.

**Practice.** For pitches (proposals, case studies, About page, cover
letters), the third act hands the listener the ending — they become the hero
who decides. Strip adjectives from bios. Never "aspiring," "amazing," "ninja,"
"guru," or "rock star."

**Quotes.**

- *"'The cat sat on a mat' is not a story. 'The cat sat on the dog's mat' is
  a story."* — le Carré
- *"Our work doesn't speak for itself."*
- *"Every client presentation, every personal essay, every cover letter,
  every fund-raising request — they're all pitches. They're stories with the
  endings chopped off."*

**For the agency.** Case studies follow the three-act shape (see
`case-study-template.md`). Agency bios are factual and short, adjective-free.
Every proposal ends with the prospect as hero — our role is the
mentor/guide.

### 6. Teach what you know

Teaching doesn't create competition; it creates interest and closeness.

**Practice.** Share playbooks, tutorials, reference materials. Explain
techniques step-by-step. Publish recipes and cookbooks.

**Quotes.**

- *"The impulse to keep to yourself what you have learned is not only
  shameful, it is destructive. Anything you do not give freely and abundantly
  becomes lost to you."* — Annie Dillard
- *"Teaching doesn't mean instant competition. Just because you know the
  master's technique doesn't mean you're going to be able to emulate it right
  away."*
- *"Teaching people doesn't subtract value from what you do, it actually adds
  to it."*

**For the agency.** Publish playbooks openly:

- How we pick a sector palette.
- How we audit for conversion.
- How we structure a bilingual site.
- How we write a headline.
- How we price an engagement.
- How we run a discovery call.

This is the heart of the authority engine — and it echoes Marcus Sheridan's
Big 5 exactly (see `big-5-article-templates.md` and `legacy-guidance.md`).
Prospects who learn from us arrive pre-sold.

### 7. Don't turn into human spam

Broadcasting only your own work = human spam. Be a good citizen of the
community first.

**Practice.** Read before you write. Listen before you speak. Genuinely
engage with people whose work you care about. Never ask "follow back?" Run
the **Vampire Test** — if someone leaves you drained, banish them.

**Quotes.**

- *"If you want fans, you have to be a fan first."*
- *"If you want to be interesting, you have to be interested."*
- *"Make stuff you love and talk about stuff you love and you'll attract
  people who love that kind of stuff. It's that simple."*

**For the agency.** Outreach that leads with "check out our latest post" is
spam. Outreach that leads with "I read your piece on X and it made me
reconsider Y" is credibility-building.

Our social presence should be 70 per cent amplifying others and genuinely
engaging, 30 per cent our own work. Comments on other people's work should be
substantive, not emoji. Connect to Brunson's Dream 100 work (see
`expert-authority-framework.md`) — most Dream 100 touches are acts of
genuine interest, not pitches.

### 8. Learn to take a punch

Public work attracts criticism. You cannot avoid it; you have to condition
yourself to absorb it.

**Practice.**

1. Relax and breathe — bad reviews don't kill you.
2. Strengthen your neck — take punches by publishing *a lot*.
3. Roll with the punches — criticism is material for the next piece.
4. Protect vulnerable areas — keep work that's too personal offline.
5. Keep your balance — your work is something you do, not who you are.

Block trolls, delete nasty comments, consider turning comments off entirely.

**Quotes.**

- *"Compulsive avoidance of embarrassment is a form of suicide."* — Colin
  Marshall
- *"Having your work hated by certain people is a badge of honor."*

**For the agency.** Premium work attracts "that's too expensive" / "I could
do that in Wix" noise. Treat the noise as a filter, not a wound. Keep the
Wall of Love (see `proof-library.md`) visible; keep trolls off the site
entirely (comments closed, moderate aggressively).

### 9. Sell out (charge properly)

Money is not corruption. Charging fairly is honourable.

**Practice.** Build an audience by giving generously, then offer something
specific to buy. Buy-now and hire-me buttons, not just tip jars. **Always
collect email addresses** — email outlasts every social platform. Say yes to
opportunities that let you do more of the right work; say no when more money
means less right work.

**Quotes.**

- *"We don't make movies to make money, we make money to make more movies."*
  — Disney
- *"The real risk is in not changing."* — Coltrane
- *"Don't be afraid to charge for your work, but put a price on it that you
  think is fair."*

**For the agency.** The generosity is upstream (the published teaching,
playbooks, process). There is no apology needed for downstream pricing.
Premium engagements are the business model.

### 10. Stick around (chain-smoke for a decade)

The compound effect of showing up year after year separates careers.

**Practice.** Chain-smoking — light the next project with the end of the
last one; don't wait for feedback or permission. Practical sabbaticals
(commutes, exercise, nature) refill the tank. When you've mastered something,
begin again.

**Quotes.**

- *"Work is never finished, only abandoned."* — Valéry
- *"You gotta play till the ninth inning."* — John Croslin (via Kleon)
- *"Anyone who isn't embarrassed of who they were last year probably isn't
  learning enough."* — Alain de Botton
- *"Every career is full of ups and downs."*

**For the agency.** Year 1 of publishing is invisible. Year 3 is a library.
Year 5 is a moat. Chain-smoke case studies — the day a build ships is the day
a case study starts. Schedule sabbaticals to prevent the burnout that
silently ends most agency content programmes.

## What to share and what to hold back

**Share freely.**

- Influences, tastes, reading lists, tools.
- Works in progress, sketches, drafts, iterations.
- Methods, recipes, techniques, tutorials.
- Failures and what you learned from them.
- The everyday work journal.
- What you're struggling with right now.

**Hold back.**

- Things too personal or unhealed for public critique.
- Anything you can't properly credit.
- Things that fail the "So what?" test (lunch, latte, life minutiae).
- Anything you'd regret in the morning (use the 24-hour drawer test).

**On "trade secrets":** Kleon's position is that there really aren't any.
Aaron Franklin gives away his entire barbecue method on YouTube and still
sells out daily — because teaching the method doesn't produce a competitor
overnight. "Teaching doesn't mean instant competition." Apply to the agency:
publishing our playbooks does not create instant competitors. It creates
buyers.

**Rule of thumb.** *"Post as though everyone who can read it has the power
to fire you."* — Lauren Cerand

## The "work behind the work" concept

The core operating idea of the book. There is the **artwork** (finished
product) and the **art-work** (the daily labour behind it). Traditionally
artists were taught to hide the latter. Kleon's entire argument is that this
is backwards: the work behind the work is more valuable to share because:

1. It's what audiences actually want to see.
2. It can be shared continuously, not just at launch.
3. It lets people form a relationship with the maker, not just the made
   thing.
4. It builds authority before you have finished masterpieces to show.
5. It makes you findable while you're still learning.

### Concrete artefacts of the work behind the work — for an agency

- Sketches, wireframes, IA diagrams.
- Drafts (including abandoned ones).
- Moodboards and influence boards.
- Screen recordings of the process.
- Reading highlights and book notes.
- Studio photos, desk photos, tool shots.
- Post-mortems: what we learned, what we'd do differently.
- The three design directions we considered and the two we rejected.
- The research brief written before a sector engagement.
- The internal strategy memo (redacted where needed) that committed to a bet.

These artefacts flow into the proof library (`proof-library.md`, section 6:
Proof of Strategic Rigour) and into the daily dispatch rhythm.

## The daily and weekly publishing rhythm (our adaptation)

Kleon prescribes daily dispatches. That's right for an individual creator
publishing on their own time. For a B2B agency with founder-level content
production, the sustainable rhythm is:

| Frequency | Format | Channel | Time per week |
|-----------|--------|---------|---------------|
| 4–5×/week | Short post (100–250 words) | LinkedIn + site updates | 2 hours |
| 1×/week | Long-form anchor (1,200–2,500 words) | Site + newsletter | 4–6 hours |
| 1×/month | Signature talk / roundtable recording | Site + YouTube | 3 hours |
| 1×/quarter | Cornerstone asset (benchmark report, teardown compilation) | Site + gated newsletter | 20 hours spread across the quarter |

Total founder time on publishing: 8 to 12 hours per week. Sustainable.

**Work then share:** *"I like to work while the world is sleeping, and share
while the world is at work."* Sharing comes after the work, not instead of
it.

**The 30-minute rule:** If sharing is crowding out work, set a timer; when it
dings, kick yourself off the internet.

**Flow → Stock:** Social posts (flow) feed durable assets (stock — blog
posts, then cornerstone reports, then eventually a book). The tweet becomes
the blog post becomes the chapter.

## The Show-Your-Work audit for the agency content engine

Every quarter, audit against these 10 questions:

- [ ] Are we publishing at least 4 short posts per week on our primary
      platform?
- [ ] Is there a weekly long-form anchor being published consistently?
- [ ] Have we shared at least one "work behind the work" artefact this week?
- [ ] Are we crediting sources meticulously on everything we share?
- [ ] Is our About page adjective-free and story-shaped (3 acts)?
- [ ] Have we taught at least one new technique in detail this month
      (playbook, tutorial, how-to)?
- [ ] Is at least 50 per cent of our social engagement amplifying others'
      work?
- [ ] Do we have a clear mailing list and are we growing it?
- [ ] Have we refused the temptation to chase trends outside our niche?
- [ ] Are we still publishing in month 18, 24, 36?

A quarter where fewer than 7 of these are "yes" is a quarter where the
authority engine stalled. Diagnose the gap before the following quarter begins.
