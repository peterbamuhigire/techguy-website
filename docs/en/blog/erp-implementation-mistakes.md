# ERP Implementation: What SMEs Get Wrong and How to Get It Right

In 2019, I landed in Dakar to take over regional ICT operations for a health products company operating across three West African countries. The previous ERP system had been installed eighteen months earlier. It cost the company over $40,000 in licensing and setup fees. When I opened the system for the first time, I found that warehouse staff in Freetown were still recording stock movements on paper. The finance team in Conakry had never logged in. The Dakar office used the system only for generating invoices — ignoring every other module they had paid for. Eighteen months and $40,000, and the company was running on spreadsheets.

This is not an unusual story. Across the continent, ERP implementation in Africa fails at rates that should alarm every business owner considering the investment. Research from Panorama Consulting suggests that over 50% of ERP projects experience cost overruns, and roughly 60% fail to deliver expected benefits. For SMEs in Uganda, Kenya, Senegal, and across the region, the stakes are even higher — because the budget for getting it wrong simply does not exist.

[IMAGE: blog-images-1.jpg — ERP systems connect every function of a business, from finance and inventory to HR and customer management]

Over the past 15 years, I have designed, implemented, and rescued ERP systems across more than 10 African countries. I have seen the same mistakes repeated by businesses of every size, in every sector. The good news is that these mistakes are entirely avoidable. Here is what goes wrong — and how to get it right.

## The Promise Versus the Reality of ERP Implementation Africa

The sales pitch is always compelling. A single system that connects your inventory, finance, HR, procurement, and sales. Real-time dashboards. Automated reporting. No more chasing spreadsheets across departments. For any business owner drowning in disconnected systems, the promise of an ERP is irresistible.

The reality is more complicated. An ERP system is not a product you install. It is a transformation you undertake. The software is perhaps 30% of the equation. The remaining 70% is process redesign, data preparation, staff training, change management, and ongoing support. Most vendors will not tell you this, because their business model depends on selling licences, not managing change.

[IMAGE: blog-images-18.jpg — A well-configured ERP dashboard gives business leaders real-time visibility into operations across every department]

For African SMEs, additional challenges compound the difficulty. Internet connectivity is unreliable in many locations. Power outages interrupt work. Staff digital literacy varies enormously, sometimes within the same office. Multiple currencies, multiple languages, and multiple regulatory environments add layers of complexity that off-the-shelf systems rarely anticipate.

None of these challenges are insurmountable. But they require honest planning, not optimistic assumptions.

## Mistake 1: Buying the Software Before Planning the Implementation

This is the most expensive mistake, and the most common. A business owner attends a trade show, sees a demonstration, and signs a licensing agreement. Or a board member recommends a system that worked at another company. The software arrives. Nobody has mapped the business processes it needs to support.

I have walked into organisations where an ERP had been purchased twelve months prior and was still not operational. The reason was always the same: no implementation plan. No one had documented how purchase orders flow from request to approval. No one had defined who authorises what. No one had mapped the chart of accounts to the system's financial module.

**The cost of this mistake is staggering.** A mid-range ERP licence for an SME in Kampala runs between $5,000 and $25,000 annually. Add implementation consulting, and the total investment can reach $50,000 or more. When that investment sits unused for a year because nobody planned the rollout, the business has effectively burned cash.

**How to avoid it:** Before you speak to a single vendor, document your business processes. Map every workflow that the ERP will touch — procurement, sales, inventory, finance, HR. Identify the pain points you need the system to solve. Define your requirements in writing. Only then should you evaluate which software fits those requirements. The planning phase should take four to eight weeks for a typical SME. It is the most valuable time you will spend on the entire project.

## Mistake 2: Treating Staff Training as an Afterthought

Here is a pattern I have seen in every country I have worked in: the system is configured, the data is loaded, the go-live date arrives — and the staff cannot use it. Training was a two-hour session the week before launch. Half the team missed it. The other half forgot everything by Monday.

During my time managing ERP operations across Senegal, Sierra Leone, and Guinea for Dynapharm Africa, training was the single most important factor in determining whether a country office succeeded or failed with the system. In Senegal, where the team was based in the same office as the IT function, we could provide daily support and reinforcement. Adoption was strong within three months.

In Sierra Leone, the situation was different. The Freetown office operated with limited internet bandwidth, meaning remote support was unreliable. Staff had varying levels of computer literacy. The previous approach — a single training session followed by a user manual — had failed completely. We had to redesign the entire training programme. We ran hands-on workshops over two weeks, covering one module at a time. We created simplified quick-reference guides in plain language, with screenshots for every step. We assigned a local champion — a staff member who received advanced training and became the first point of contact for questions.

In Guinea, the challenge was compounded by language. The Conakry team operated primarily in French, and the ERP interface had been configured in English. Before any training could be effective, we had to localise the system — translating menu labels, form fields, reports, and error messages into French. Only after the interface matched the language people actually worked in did training begin to stick.

**The lesson is clear:** training is not an event. It is a process that must be planned, budgeted, and sustained over months.

**How to avoid it:** Budget 15-20% of your total ERP investment for training. Plan a minimum of two weeks of hands-on training before go-live, structured by module. Identify and train internal champions who can provide peer support. Produce written quick-reference materials in the language your staff actually use. Schedule refresher sessions at 30, 60, and 90 days after launch. Measure adoption by tracking system usage — if a module shows low activity, that is a training problem, not a software problem.

## Mistake 3: Ignoring Data Migration Until the Last Minute

[IMAGE: blog-images-2.jpg — Database infrastructure forms the backbone of any ERP system — migrating data requires careful planning and validation]

Every business moving to an ERP has existing data: customer records, product catalogues, financial histories, supplier details, employee files. That data lives in spreadsheets, paper ledgers, old software systems, and sometimes in people's heads. Moving it into the new ERP is one of the most technically demanding and time-consuming parts of any implementation.

Yet it is routinely left until the final week before go-live.

I once worked with a retail business in Kampala that had 4,500 product SKUs in their inventory. The data lived across seven different Excel files maintained by three different staff members. Product names were inconsistent — the same item appeared as "Paracetamol 500mg," "PARA 500," and "Paracetamol tabs" in different files. Pricing had not been updated in two of the files for over a year. Supplier codes did not match between purchasing and accounts payable.

Cleaning and standardising that data took three weeks of dedicated work. Had we attempted it in the final days before launch, the go-live would have been a disaster — orders pulling wrong prices, inventory counts that did not match reality, financial reports that could not be trusted.

**How to avoid it:** Start data migration planning in the first week of the project, not the last. Audit all existing data sources and assess their quality. Assign a data owner for each module — someone responsible for cleaning, standardising, and validating the data before it enters the new system. Build a data migration checklist: field mapping, deduplication, validation rules, test imports, and final verification. Run at least two test migrations before the live cutover. Budget four to six weeks for data migration on a typical SME implementation.

## Mistake 4: Choosing Complexity Over Fit

Many SMEs fall into the trap of selecting an ERP system designed for large enterprises. The logic seems sound — buy the most powerful system now so you will not outgrow it. In practice, the result is a system that is over-engineered for your needs, expensive to maintain, and intimidating for your staff.

I have seen businesses with ten employees running SAP Business One, paying annual maintenance fees that exceed their entire IT budget. I have seen companies licensing modules they will never use — manufacturing resource planning for a service business, or advanced warehouse management for a company with a single stockroom.

The opposite mistake also occurs: choosing a system so basic that it cannot handle multi-currency transactions, does not support your tax reporting requirements, or lacks the integrations you need with mobile money platforms and local banking systems.

**How to avoid it:** Match the system to your business, not your ambitions. Start with the modules you need today — typically finance, inventory, and sales. Ensure the system can grow with you, but do not pay for functionality you will not use for three years. For SMEs in Uganda and across East Africa, consider solutions that understand the local business environment: multi-currency support for regional trade, mobile money integration, offline capability for unreliable connectivity, and compliance with local tax authorities like URA. If a global enterprise system is the right fit, ensure the implementation partner has specific experience deploying it in African markets.

## A Better Approach: The Phased Implementation

[IMAGE: blog-images-4.jpg — A structured, phased approach to ERP implementation dramatically improves success rates for African SMEs]

The most successful ERP implementations I have managed follow a phased approach. Rather than attempting to deploy every module simultaneously — the "big bang" approach that fails more often than it succeeds — we roll out the system in stages.

**Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-4)**
Map business processes. Document requirements. Evaluate and select the system. Assemble the implementation team, including an internal project sponsor with decision-making authority.

**Phase 2: Core Configuration (Weeks 5-10)**
Configure the first modules — typically finance and inventory. Begin data migration for these modules. Start training the core team.

**Phase 3: Pilot (Weeks 11-14)**
Go live with the core modules in a single department or location. Monitor usage daily. Fix issues immediately. Gather feedback from users and refine configurations.

**Phase 4: Expansion (Weeks 15-22)**
Roll out additional modules — procurement, sales, HR. Extend the system to additional locations or departments. Continue training and support.

**Phase 5: Optimisation (Ongoing)**
Fine-tune reports and dashboards. Automate routine processes. Review system performance against the original business objectives. Plan for the next phase of growth.

This approach takes longer than a big bang deployment — typically five to six months for a full rollout versus the eight to twelve weeks that aggressive vendors promise. But it succeeds. In my experience, phased implementations achieve full user adoption within six months, compared to the 18 months or more that big bang deployments often require — if they achieve full adoption at all.

When we restructured the Dynapharm West Africa rollout using this phased model, we went from a system that three country offices were largely ignoring to one that all three offices used daily for core operations. The key was patience. We started with the Dakar office, proved the approach, refined the training materials, and then replicated the model in Freetown and Conakry — adapting for local conditions each time. In Freetown, we configured the system to handle Leone alongside the US Dollar, which the company used for regional reporting. In Conakry, we ensured all reporting templates met Guinean regulatory requirements in French. Each office had its own go-live date, its own training schedule, and its own local champion.

## When to Bring in Outside Help

Not every SME needs an external consultant for ERP implementation. If your business has a competent IT team, well-documented processes, and a system with strong vendor support, you may be able to manage the project internally.

However, consider bringing in experienced help if any of the following apply:

- **Your business operates across multiple countries or currencies.** Cross-border ERP deployments introduce complexity in tax compliance, currency conversion, language localisation, and regulatory differences that require specialist knowledge.
- **You have no internal IT team.** Attempting an ERP implementation without dedicated technical resources is a recipe for failure.
- **Your previous ERP attempt failed.** If you are on your second try, the problem is likely not the software — it is the approach. An experienced consultant can diagnose what went wrong and design a recovery plan.
- **You need to integrate with local systems.** Mobile money (MTN MoMo, Airtel Money), local banking APIs, URA tax systems, and industry-specific platforms require integration expertise that ERP vendors rarely provide out of the box.
- **Your data is in poor condition.** If your existing records are scattered across spreadsheets, paper files, and multiple disconnected systems, professional data migration support will save you months of pain.

## Getting ERP Implementation Right in Africa

ERP implementation in Africa does not fail because the technology is inadequate. It fails because businesses underestimate the human, organisational, and contextual factors that determine success. The software is the easy part. The hard part — and the part that separates successful implementations from expensive failures — is the planning, the training, the data preparation, and the sustained commitment to change management.

If you are considering an ERP system for your business, start with three questions: What business problems am I trying to solve? Are my processes documented and ready for a system to support them? Do I have the internal capacity to manage this change, or do I need help?

Answering those questions honestly will save you more money than any vendor discount ever could.
