# What a Website Actually Costs in Uganda (And Why It Varies So Much)

Ask five developers in Kampala what a website costs, and you will get five wildly different answers. One quotes UGX 500,000. Another says UGX 20,000,000. A third offers to "do it for free" if you buy hosting through them. It is no wonder that business owners walk away more confused than when they started. The truth about **website cost in Uganda** is that every number you hear could be legitimate — but only if you understand what you are actually paying for.

## The Real Price Ranges: What Websites Actually Cost in Uganda

Let me cut straight to the numbers. These are realistic ranges for the Ugandan market in 2026, based on what credible developers and agencies actually charge.

**Template or DIY website:** UGX 500,000 – 2,000,000 (USD 130 – 530)

This is your Wix, Squarespace, or pre-made WordPress theme. Someone installs a template, swaps in your logo and text, and hands it over. You get a functional site, but it will look like thousands of others. If your business is brand-new and you need an online presence tomorrow, this can work as a starting point.

**Customised WordPress site:** UGX 2,000,000 – 8,000,000 (USD 530 – 2,100)

A developer takes a premium theme or builds a custom theme, tailors the design to your brand, and sets up proper pages for your services. You get something that looks and feels like yours. Most established small businesses in Kampala fall into this category.

**Custom-built professional site:** UGX 5,000,000 – 20,000,000 (USD 1,300 – 5,300)

This is a site designed and coded from scratch — no templates, no bloated page builders. Technologies like Astro, Next.js, or hand-coded HTML with modern tooling produce sites that load in under two seconds, score 95+ on Google Lighthouse, and stand out from every competitor. If your website is a core part of your business strategy, this is where you should be.

**Web application or SaaS platform:** UGX 15,000,000 – 80,000,000+ (USD 4,000 – 21,000+)

This is not a website in the traditional sense. This is software: user accounts, dashboards, payment processing, databases, APIs. Think booking systems, inventory platforms, or client portals. I have built platforms like Maduuka and Aqar at this level. The price reflects months of development, testing, and iteration.

These ranges overlap because every project is different. A simple custom site for a law firm costs less than a custom site for a logistics company that needs real-time tracking. Context matters more than category.

## What Actually Drives the Price Up (and Down)

When a client asks me why one quote is UGX 3,000,000 and another is UGX 15,000,000, I walk them through these factors. Every single one affects the final number.

**Design complexity.** A five-page site with a clean, consistent layout costs far less than a twenty-page site with custom animations, interactive elements, and a unique design for every section. Design is not decoration — it is problem-solving. Good design guides your visitors toward action. That takes skill and time.

**Content creation.** Many clients assume the developer will write their website copy. Some do, but most charge extra for it — and they should. Professional copywriting that speaks to your audience and performs well in search engines is a separate skill. If you provide your own well-written content, you save money. If you need the developer to create it, expect the quote to rise.

**Number of pages and features.** A five-page brochure site is straightforward. Add a blog, a portfolio gallery, a contact form with email notifications, a client testimonial slider, multi-language support, and an events calendar, and you have tripled the work. Every feature takes time to build, test, and make responsive across devices.

**Responsiveness and cross-browser testing.** Your site must work on a Tecno phone in Gulu just as well as it works on a MacBook in Kololo. Responsive design is not optional — it is the majority of the work on most modern projects. Developers who skip this step deliver sites that look broken on half the devices your customers use.

**SEO and performance optimisation.** A site that loads in eight seconds on a 3G connection is a site that nobody will wait for. Proper image optimisation, clean code, fast hosting, and search engine optimisation all take effort. Some developers include this as standard. Others treat it as an add-on. Always ask.

**The developer's experience.** A junior freelancer charges less than a senior developer with fifteen years of experience, and that is perfectly reasonable. But experience shows up in the details: cleaner code, fewer bugs, better advice on what you actually need, and a site that does not fall apart when you try to update it two years later.

## The Template Trap: When Cheap Becomes Expensive

I want to tell you about a client I worked with in 2023. A medium-sized tour company based in Kampala had paid UGX 1,500,000 for a WordPress site. On the surface, it looked decent — attractive photos, a booking enquiry form, the usual pages.

Within a year, the problems started. The site loaded in twelve seconds on mobile because the template included fifteen JavaScript libraries that nobody needed. The contact form broke after a WordPress update. The SEO was so poor that searching the company's exact name barely brought up their site. And because the template's code was tangled and poorly documented, fixing any of it cost nearly as much as starting over.

They came to me, and we rebuilt the site from scratch. Custom code. Optimised images. Proper SEO. Fast loading. The new site cost more upfront, but it has run smoothly for over two years with minimal maintenance. Their enquiries from Google tripled within six months.

The lesson is not that templates are bad. They have their place. The lesson is that the cheapest option often costs you more in the long run — in lost customers, in repair bills, and in the opportunity cost of a website that does not work for your business.

## Five Questions to Ask Your Developer Before You Pay

Before you commit to any developer or agency, ask these questions. The answers will tell you whether you are about to make a good investment or an expensive mistake.

**1. "What exactly am I getting, and what is not included?"**

Get a written scope of work. How many pages? Does it include mobile responsiveness? Content writing? SEO setup? Image sourcing? Training on how to update the site? If it is not in the agreement, assume it is not included.

**2. "Who owns the site after you build it?"**

This is critical. Some developers build your site on their own hosting account and hold it hostage if the relationship sours. You should own your domain name, your hosting account, and all the source code. Get this in writing before any work begins.

**3. "What technology are you using, and why?"**

A good developer can explain their technology choice in plain language. WordPress is a reasonable choice for many projects. A custom static site is better for performance and security. A web application framework is necessary for complex functionality. If the developer cannot explain why they chose what they chose, that is a warning sign.

**4. "What happens after launch?"**

Websites need updates, security patches, content changes, and occasional fixes. Ask whether maintenance is included, what it costs, and what the response time is if something breaks. A site without maintenance is a ticking clock.

**5. "Can I see three live sites you have built?"**

Not screenshots. Not mockups. Live, working websites that you can visit on your phone right now. Check how fast they load. Check how they look on mobile. Check whether they rank on Google for relevant terms. A developer's portfolio tells you everything their sales pitch will not. ## The Costs Nobody Mentions Until It Is Too Late

Here is where many business owners get caught off guard. The website itself is only part of the expense. These ongoing costs are real, and any honest developer will discuss them upfront.

**Domain name registration:** UGX 80,000 – 200,000 per year for a .com or .co.ug domain. You must renew this every year, or you lose your web address. I have seen businesses lose their domain because they forgot to renew, and someone else bought it.

**Web hosting:** UGX 200,000 – 800,000 per year, depending on the type of site and the hosting provider. A simple static site can run on affordable hosting. A WordPress site with heavy traffic needs more resources. A web application needs even more.

**SSL certificate:** Most hosting providers now include this for free with Let's Encrypt. But some still charge for it. Your site must have SSL (the padlock icon in the browser). Without it, Google penalises your search ranking, and visitors see security warnings.

**Annual maintenance:** UGX 500,000 – 3,000,000 per year. This covers software updates, security monitoring, content changes, backup management, and fixing things that break. WordPress sites need more maintenance than static sites because WordPress and its plugins require regular updates to stay secure.

**Content updates:** If you want to add blog posts, update your service pages, or change photos, someone has to do that work. Some businesses handle it themselves. Others pay their developer a monthly retainer. Budget for this from the start.

**Email hosting:** If you want professional email addresses (info@yourcompany.co.ug), that is a separate cost. Google Workspace starts at around USD 6 per user per month. There are more affordable options, but avoid the free email services for business communication.

Add it all up, and a typical small business website costs UGX 700,000 – 1,500,000 per year to keep running after the initial build. This is not a hidden fee — it is the reality of owning digital property. Any developer who does not mention these costs is either inexperienced or hoping you will not ask.

## What You Actually Need (An Honest Assessment)

After fifteen years in this industry, here is my honest advice on what different businesses in Uganda actually need.

**You are a solo professional or very small startup.** You need a clean, fast, mobile-friendly site with five to seven pages: home, about, services, portfolio or testimonials, and contact. A customised template or a simple custom build will serve you well. Budget UGX 2,000,000 – 5,000,000 for the build and UGX 700,000 per year for maintenance and hosting.

**You are an established SME with ten or more staff.** You need a professional custom site that reflects your brand, ranks on Google for your key services, and converts visitors into enquiries. You probably need a blog for content marketing and possibly multi-language support if you serve clients across East Africa. Budget UGX 5,000,000 – 15,000,000 for the build.

**You are building a product or platform.** You need a web application, not a website. This requires proper software architecture, user authentication, database design, and likely integration with payment systems like MTN Mobile Money or Airtel Money. Budget UGX 15,000,000 and above, and plan for ongoing development costs as the platform evolves.

**You are not sure what you need.** That is perfectly fine. A good developer will have an honest conversation with you, understand your business goals, and recommend an approach that fits your budget and your actual requirements. If a developer immediately jumps to the most expensive option without understanding your business, find someone else.
